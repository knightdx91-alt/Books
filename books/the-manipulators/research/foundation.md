## 0. PREMISE (structural)

Kevin Jones can take a power from one person and give it to another — theft that cripples the
victim, gifts that cost him the power itself, and nothing he holds lasts: every borrowed power
decays and must be re-taken before it fades. He escapes a facility that manufactures reality-
benders with his memory beaten out of his skull — and must relearn the rules of his singular
gift, and recover what he did before the injury, before the organization that once *owned* him
uses him to rewrite the world for good.

**Concept Pitch Test:** "A man who can steal superpowers and give them to others — but every
power he holds is on a clock, fading unless he keeps stealing it back, and the only reason he's
free is that he can't remember he used to do it for the enemy." Passes. Shareable, contains the
irony engine (the escaped victim is the facility's own monster), retellable in one breath.

**Engagement Type:**
```
engagement_type:
  primary: intellectual (45%)     # the system is the plot; reader reasons WITH the decay economy
  secondary: fascination (35%)    # what-did-he-do mystery + moral vertigo of theft/sacrifice
  tertiary: empathy (20%)         # Kevin & Jake's dependency-bond; the man vs. who he used to be
```
Tension to manage: intellectual (system puzzles) can go cold and mechanical; empathy needs
Kevin to bleed. Rule of thumb — **never resolve a system-puzzle without a person paying for
it.** Every clever use of the rules costs someone a power they'll never get back, or costs Kevin
the power he gave away — the economy is zero-sum and always on a clock.

## 1. THE POWER SYSTEM (the engine — HARD CANON)

### 1.1 What Kevin actually is
Kevin's baseline — his **irreducible** power, the one thing that can never be stolen or
given away — is the meta-power itself: he is a **Vessel**. He can hold powers that belong to
other people, move them between bodies, and reclaim what he has given. Stripped of every
borrowed power, Kevin is an ordinary 22-year-old man. That ordinariness is his true self and
his central vulnerability: the moment his last borrowed power decays, he is just a person.

### 1.2 The three operations (contact-based unless noted)

**ABSORB (theft).** Skin-to-skin contact, held for a sustained beat (roughly a slow ten-
count; fear/adrenaline shortens it, resistance lengthens it). The power transfers into
Kevin. **Zero-sum: the original owner loses it, permanently, unless Kevin later gives it
back.** No consent required — this is predatory, and Kevin knows it. Range: contact only. He
cannot steal from someone he cannot touch. (This is why a flying enemy, an armored one, a
crowd — all are hard problems.)

**GIFT (self-sacrifice).** Contact plus intent. The power leaves Kevin **entirely**; he no
longer has it. Giving Jake flight means Kevin cannot fly. Giving is a wound he chooses.

**RECALL (reclaim your own gift).** The ONE ranged operation. Kevin can pull back a power he
personally gifted, at a distance, through the "thread" that connects a giver to his gift. It
requires desperation (a mortal-pressure trigger — the body reaches for survival) and it
**strips the recipient instantly**: recall flight from Jake and Jake falls out of the sky.
This is the seed's "Kevin remembers how to fly" — he is not remembering, he is *recalling
his own gift from Jake mid-escape*, and it is a small betrayal every time. Recall cannot
take powers Kevin never gave; those need contact-theft.

### 1.3 Impermanence — the ticking clock (the tax that runs the plot)
Every power inside a body that does not own it **decays**. Concretely:

- A freshly taken/given power holds **full charge**. Idle, it bleeds to nothing in roughly
  **ten to fourteen days**. Hard use burns it faster — a day of heavy flight, hours.
- **Renewal** resets the clock: Kevin must re-touch the source or the carrier (contact) and
  push charge back in. Renewing is cheap in effort but expensive in exposure — it requires
  proximity, so an ally on borrowed power is **leashed to Kevin's location**. Jake can fly
  only as long as Kevin is close enough, often enough, to top him up.
- **[PROPOSAL] Jake's watch = the decay meter.** The watch is not the source of flight
  (Kevin's gift is). It is a device Kevin built/acquired that reads the charge of a gifted
  power and counts down to zero — Jake glances at it to know how many hours of flight he has
  left before he needs Kevin. It literally puts the ticking-clock economy on the page. Keep
  the watch, demystified. Recommendation: KEEP.

### 1.4 The ONLY cost — IMPERMANENCE (decay is the whole tax)
There is no secondary penalty. Absorbing, gifting, and recalling carry **no** hidden toll — no
identity bleed, no physical backlash, no cosmic recoil on Kevin. The single, total cost of the
power is that **nothing he holds is permanent.** Every drop of drama comes from scarcity,
timing, the decay window, and the zero-sum nature of theft — not from a second tax.

- **Decay window.** A freshly taken/given power holds full charge and, left idle, fades to
  nothing in roughly **ten to fourteen days.** Hard use burns it faster — a day of heavy flight,
  hours. When it fades, it is simply gone from the world (it does NOT return to the original
  owner). To keep a power, Kevin must re-take/renew it before the window closes.
- **The multi-hold ceiling — derived from decay alone.** Kevin has a fixed pool of "maintenance"
  — the attention/contact-time he can spend keeping powers charged. Split it across many powers
  and **each one decays faster**: hold two and each lasts near its full window; hold six and each
  burns out in a fraction of the time, so he spends every waking hour re-touching sources just to
  keep them lit. The practical ceiling (**~3–4**) is therefore economic, not a hard cap — you
  *can* hold more, you just can't hold them long enough to matter. Scarcity of sources makes this
  brutal: to renew, he needs the source (or carrier) within reach, again and again.
- **The zero-sum edge (not a "cost," a consequence).** Theft permanently cripples the victim
  (unless later gifted back). Gifting means Kevin genuinely loses the power. Recall strips the
  ally instantly. These are the moral/tactical weights of the system — the price others pay, or
  that Kevin pays in capability, never a metaphysical penalty on his body or self.
- **The amnesia is unrelated to the power.** Kevin's memory loss is mundane blunt head trauma
  (§4, §3) — the bandaged head from the seed. It is NOT a cost of the power. What he forgot is a
  plot mystery (content, §4); how the power works is pure impermanence (this section).

### 1.5 Kevin's baseline vs borrowed — quick reference
| | Nature | Can be lost? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vessel meta-power | irreducible baseline | never | absorb/gift/recall; renews others |
| Any combat/utility power | borrowed | yes — decay, gift, or theft | no extra toll to hold; only cost is it decays and must be renewed before the window closes |
| "His" flight | borrowed, gifted to Jake, recallable | yes | seed's flight is a recalled gift |

### 1.6 Worked examples — the system making dilemmas
1. **The flying enemy.** An airborne Manipulator's shifting walls trap Kevin. He cannot
   steal (no contact) and cannot fly (he gave flight to Jake). To get airborne he must
   RECALL flight from Jake — dropping Jake into the collapsing corridor. Solve one problem,
   create another. (This is the opening.)
2. **The three-power ceiling.** Kevin holds flight, a shield, and Reach-suggestion. To take
   a fourth power he needs to survive a fight, he must first *dump* one — gift it to an ally
   or let it decay — and every dump is a tactical hole. Choosing what to carry is choosing
   what he can't do next.
3. **The renewal leash.** Nadia (a burning-out Manipulator) survives only if Kevin holds her
   Manipulation for her and keeps re-taking it before it fades — which means he must stay within
   contact range of a source, constantly, spending maintenance he needs elsewhere, AND every day
   he holds hers is a day he can't hold the powers that keep them alive in a fight. Keeping her
   breathing costs him the loadout he needs to survive. Let it fade and she dies.
4. **The price of winning.** The cleanest way through the Vault is to carry six powers. Do it
   and every one of them burns out fast (split maintenance, §1.4) — he'll reach the bottom with a
   deadly kit that's already guttering, forced to spend the fight re-stealing to stay lit, or to
   dump powers he still needs. Overload doesn't hurt him; it just won't LAST. He can go in loaded
   or go in able to sustain — not both.
5. **Recall as betrayal.** In the climax Kevin needs a power he long ago gifted to an enemy
   turned reluctant ally. Recalling it strips them at the worst moment. The "self-sacrifice"
   operation, run in reverse, is the most predatory thing he does.

### 1.7 Edge cases (locked)
- **Death of a source:** if a source dies, powers Kevin already took remain (they're his to
  decay now); powers still in the source die with them.
- **Consent:** irrelevant to theft (predatory). Relevant only emotionally.
- **Layering:** Kevin cannot hold two copies of the same power to "double" it; a power is a
  single object that moves.
- **Renewing at range:** impossible. Renewal is contact. This keeps allies leashed and keeps
  Kevin exposed — a deliberate pressure valve.
- **The meta-power cannot be stolen and cannot be copied.** Kevin is SINGULAR — the only being
  who can move powers between people. Meridian cannot make Vessels, partial or otherwise. What
  they have instead is crude **reliquary tech** (§3): machines that can strip a power into an
  object and later implant it into a person — slow, imprecise, and the stored/implanted powers
  decay even *faster* than Kevin's do. Their tech is a bad forgery of what Kevin does natively,
  which is exactly why they need the real thing: him.

## 2. THE COSMIC LAW (binding the Manipulators — SOURCE mysterious, EFFECTS legible)

Manipulators bend local reality — reshaping matter, space, and structure within a bounded
volume (the shifting hallways). They are bound by a metaphysical force larger than any
organization. In-world it has many names; the researchers call it **the Weight**; older
powered people call it **the Ledger**. Nobody knows its source. Its effects are hard law:

1. **Conservation / debt.** Nothing is created from nothing. A Manipulator can only *reshape*
   matter that already exists within reach — raise a wall by taking floor, seal a door by
   thinning another. Every alteration is borrowed, never minted.
2. **Volume & attention cap.** A Manipulator can only hold changes within a radius they can
   actively perceive and concentrate on. Break the concentration and the change starts to
   fail. This is why the Vault is a saturated, windowless maze — a bounded arena a Manipulator
   can flood.
3. **Persistence decay.** Manipulations are NOT permanent. Reality reasserts its "true state"
   over time unless the Manipulator keeps paying attention. (Thematic rhyme with Kevin's
   power decay — both the hero and the villains fight the same entropy.)
4. **The Recoil (backlash).** Overreach is paid by the Manipulator's own body — nosebleeds,
   premature aging, tissue that "corrects" toward some truer state. Push far enough past your
   limit and the Weight unmakes you: a Manipulator who tries something too big is *edited out*,
   leaving those nearby with the eerie sense a person's outline used to be there.
5. **No one owns the Law.** Meridian can *make* Manipulators; it cannot control the Weight.
   Its Manipulators die young or go mad. This is the engine of the whole conflict.

**Why this legibly surfaces in scenes:** the shifting building is thrilling AND lawful —
walls that rise mean walls that fall elsewhere; a maze that reforms faster than Kevin runs is
a Manipulator burning her own body to hold it; when she overreaches, she bleeds and the maze
stutters. The reader can *reason* with the danger.

**How it's leveraged/feared:** veterans fight Manipulators by *outlasting* them (persistence
decay), by *moving* (attention cap — get out of the perceivable volume), or by *forcing
overreach* (bait a change too big and let the Recoil do the work).

## 3. THE ORGANIZATION

**Public shell:** **Meridian Continuity** — a private "continuity and risk" research
consortium; disaster-proofing, actuarial futures, quiet money.

**Internal program:** **PALIMPSEST** — the project to manufacture Manipulators. (A palimpsest
is a page scraped clean and rewritten; the name is chosen deliberately — it is what they do to
reality and to Kevin's mind.) Facilities are called **Vaults**; Kevin was held in **Vault 7**,
the "down there" he resolves to return to.

**Goal — why manufacture reality-benders.** Meridian believes the world is a bad draft. Their
end is not conquest but **revision at scale**: to remake reality — erase a war, a famine, a
death — permanently. But the Weight kills anyone who bends too big, and manipulations decay.
So Palimpsest's real problem is a *durability* problem, and the missing piece is **Kevin**.

**Why they need Kevin.** He is the only being who can move powers between people cleanly. If
Kevin can distribute a single Manipulation across many carriers, or hold a Manipulator's power
and thereby *spread the Weight's debt* instead of one body paying it, then reality could be
rewritten big and made to *stick* without the Recoil unmaking anyone. Their reliquary machines
can't do it — stored powers decay too fast. **Kevin was their instrument for years** (see §4:
the buried truth) — their power-thief, emptying the unwilling and gifting abilities to Meridian's
chosen — until he turned. They don't need him blank; the amnesia is an accident of the beating
that stopped his defection. They need him because he alone knows where his defection hid the one
thing that could complete Palimpsest, and because a working Kevin is worth more than any machine.

**Structure & scale.** Not global-dominant — a well-funded, deniable network of a few Vaults,
a research directorate, contracted powered "assets," and a small enforcement arm. Scale is
*intimate and metastasizing*, not army-sized. Keeps the book standalone-solvable while seeding
a larger runway.

**Key figures:**
- **Dr. Silas Marrow** — Palimpsest's director and the book's antagonist. Cold, courteous,
  genuinely grieving something (a revision he wants badly enough to justify everything). Sees
  himself as a surgeon of history, not a villain. Believes emptying Kevin was a *mercy* — you
  can't be tortured by a past you don't have.
- **Wren** — Meridian's most successful Manipulator and enforcer; the architect of Vault 7's
  shifting halls. Loyal not from belief but because Meridian's renewals hold her Recoil at
  bay; she is dying by the Weight and they are her insulin. A tragic antagonist.
- **The scalpel doctor** (seed) — **Dr. Aldous Pym**, Kevin's intake technician; Jake kills him
  in the escape. Minor but establishes the Vault's cruelty.
- **The Collector** (the mirror antagonist — see §4) — Meridian's field agent who moves powers
  with **reliquary tech**: devices that strip a power into a stored object and implant it into a
  chosen carrier. He has NO innate power himself — he is a human wielding machines, not a copy of
  Kevin. He is Kevin's dark parallel by METHOD and CONSCIENCE: he takes and reassigns powers at no
  cost to himself, hoarding and selling what Kevin can only give at a loss. He is what "moving
  powers" looks like with a price tag and no soul, and he was BUILT to replace Kevin — a fact he
  resents, because the machines are inferior and he knows it.
- **The Reach** (backstory ONLY) — the Panama beggar-gang mind-controller Kevin robbed six years
  ago to save Jake. Appears in the memory interlude as how Kevin met Jake. NOT a present-day
  antagonist.

## 4. CHARACTERS

### Kevin Jones — protagonist
**Surface:** A calm, watchful 22-year-old who looks like he's always doing arithmetic. Wakes
with a bandaged, beaten skull and no memory; competent in ways that unsettle him ("how do I
know how to do that?").
**Wound / buried truth:** For years Kevin was Meridian's own power-thief — the human original
their reliquary machines only imitate. On orders he emptied the unwilling and gifted their
abilities to Meridian's chosen; he was good at it, and for a long time he let himself be. Then he
turned. His last act as a defector was to steal the one thing that could complete Palimpsest and
HIDE it where Meridian can't reach — a gift/sacrifice that cost him everything — before they
caught him and beat the memory out of his skull. The amnesiac "victim" escaping in Chapter 1 is
the facility's own monster, half-redeemed and running from a past he can't yet see. (Amnesia =
mundane head trauma from the beating; the guilt is what the returning memories deliver.)
**Lie:** "The blank is a mercy. Whoever I was, I'm better as no one." (He clings to the amnesia
as a clean slate — because part of him senses the man underneath was worse.)
**Want (external):** Get back down into Vault 7 and recover his past — his name, his history,
what he did and what he hid.
**Need (internal):** To own that he is the sum of what he took FOR them AND what he gave to stop
them — to refuse the comfort of the blank (a fresh start bought by not-knowing is a debt handed
to someone else) and to refuse becoming the thief again, even now that he could.
**Arc:** From treating the beaten-blank mind as a second chance → recovering the *content* of who
he was (their enforcer) and what he did (hid the keystone) → choosing to finish the giving he
started, rather than retake the taker's life Meridian offers back to him. Theme carried by CHOICE,
not by any mechanic. Lie → truth, at real cost.
**Voice markers:** Concrete, spare, ledger-minded — counts things, tracks charge and time,
narrates in costs and trades. Under-describes his own feelings.
**Contradiction:** The person who steals for a living is scrupulously honest about small
things — will not lie to Jake's face even when a lie would help.

**CHARACTER CHAOS (Literary/epic — full profile):**
- **Irrelevant obsession:** He counts stairs, and is genuinely offended by staircases with an
  irregular number of steps. Surfaces 2–3× with zero plot justification.
- **Cognitive distortion:** Sunk-cost reasoning applied to *himself* — "I've already lost this
  much of me, might as well spend the rest" — a habit of treating his own erosion as a budget.
- **Unprompted memory:** A yellow kitchen and a woman humming a song he can't name — genuine
  autobiographical memory surfacing in fragments as the head injury heals, at wrong moments (a
  sterile Vault corridor, a fight), slightly different each time. **[PROPOSAL]** It is the
  safehouse where he hid the keystone, and the woman is its guardian (or the keystone herself) —
  the memory is the buried map to what he stole. Recommend.
- **Failed emotional management:** He laughs — wrong, helpless — at the worst possible moment
  (over Pym's body; at a funeral-quiet beat), and cannot explain it.

### Jake Hobb — deuteragonist
**Surface:** Long blond hair, green eyes; loud, loyal, physical; the one who acts while Kevin
calculates. Flies via Kevin's gift.
**Wound:** Was "normal" and helpless the day Kevin saved him in Panama; has organized his
whole identity around never being helpless — i.e., around being *powered*, which means around
*Kevin*.
**Lie:** "The flight is who I am now." (It is borrowed, decaying, and recallable — it is the
least stable thing about him.)
**Want:** Keep Kevin safe and keep flying — he does not distinguish these.
**Need:** To be a person, not a power — to matter to Kevin as more than the man he gifted.
**Arc:** From defining himself by the borrowed power → to choosing, at the climax, to *give it
back* (let Kevin recall it) or to stand as just a man.
**Voice markers:** Fast, warm, deflects with jokes, over-shares; the emotional narrator to
Kevin's ledger.
**Contradiction:** Reckless flier, terrified of the ground — of being ordinary again.

**CHARACTER CHAOS:**
- **Irrelevant obsession:** Fantasy-football-style rankings of *everything* — best gas
  stations, worst clouds to fly through — maintained aloud during crises.
- **Cognitive distortion:** Mind-reading — decides he knows what Kevin feels and acts on it,
  usually wrongly.
- **Unprompted memory:** The exact weight of a fish he caught as a kid, invoked at solemn
  moments.
- **Failed emotional management:** Goes silent and over-controlled — MORE composed — when
  genuinely terrified; the tell that he's about to do something stupid.

### Dr. Silas Marrow — antagonist (see §3)
Chaos applied per NF-narrativo rule only lightly (he's the villain, not comic): irrelevant
tenderness for a dying office plant he over-waters; the mercy-logic that reads as sincere.

### Wren — antagonist / tragic Manipulator (MINI+)
**Role:** Vault 7's architect; the shifting-halls set piece is her body at work.
**Her own problem:** She is being unmade by the Weight one renewal at a time and knows the math
— counted in weeks. Meridian is the only thing slowing it.
**Irrational behavior:** Rebuilds the *same* corridor detail (a particular window that was never
real) every time she reshapes — a room from before she was a Manipulator, that the Law keeps
trying to erase.
**Voice marker:** Speaks in the past tense about the present, as if everything has already been
corrected.

### Nadia — supporting, the theme made flesh (MINI PROFILE)
**Role:** A young manufactured Manipulator burning out; the person Kevin can save only by holding
her power for her and re-taking it before it fades — spending maintenance and loadout he needs to
survive (§1.6 ex.3).
**Her own problem:** Wants to use her power for one ordinary thing (fix a specific wrong at home)
before the Weight takes her — a want unrelated to Kevin's mission.
**Irrational behavior:** Refuses to be carried; walks everywhere though it exhausts her.
**Voice marker:** Blunt, clinical about her own death, tender about strangers.

### The Collector — the MIRROR antagonist (FULL-ish profile)
**Surface:** Meridian's field agent for power-acquisition; precise, unhurried, a connoisseur;
speaks of powered people the way a dealer speaks of stock — "acquired," "placed," "in the right
hands." Carries a case of **reliquaries** (the stored-power objects).
**What he is:** A human with NO innate power. He moves powers with Meridian's reliquary machines
— strip a power into an object, implant it into a chosen carrier. He is NOT a Vessel or any kind
of copy of Kevin; he is a man with a toolkit and a customer list. His tech is crude: stored and
implanted powers decay even faster than Kevin's, which he covers with volume and cruelty.
**The mirror (by method + conscience, never by nature):** He does with machines and no cost to
himself what Kevin does at a loss and hates himself for. His line to Kevin: *"You and I are in the
same trade. The only difference is you insist on bleeding for it, and I've automated that away."*
Kevin's every gift wounds him; the Collector never gives anything he isn't paid for.
**Wound / why he's like this:** He was Meridian's answer to losing Kevin — built up as the
replacement, handed the machines, told he was the future. He knows the machines are a bad forgery
of the man who defected, and he *hates* Kevin for being the irreplaceable original. Envy dressed
as contempt.
**Personal stake:** He has spent months trying to reconstruct what Kevin can do and to track what
Kevin hid; catching Kevin alive is his one shot at proving he was worth building. He may hold, in
a reliquary, a power stripped from someone Kevin cares about — leverage, not kinship.
**Chaos / contradiction:** meticulously catalogues his reliquaries yet cannot bring himself to
implant one particular power he's carried for years — the one thing he keeps and won't sell.
**Voice marker:** the language of dealing and appraisal; never says "steal" or "cost" — only
"acquire," "place," "value." The words he refuses are the tell.

## 5. THEME

**Theme as question (never answered outright):**
*"Is a person the sum of what they've taken, or what they've given away — and can you be either
if you refuse to remember which?"*

Beneath the action the saga is about **power as debt** and **identity as an unpaid ledger**.
Kevin's every operation is a transfer with a victim: theft leaves someone permanently lessened,
gift leaves *him* lessened, and nothing he takes ever lasts — the ledger never balances, it only
runs down. There is no free power in this world: the Weight taxes the villains and decay taxes the
hero, and nothing is minted from nothing. The book asks whether an identity bought by *not looking*
at your debts — Kevin clinging to his blank slate, the Collector calling theft "acquisition,"
Meridian calling erasure "revision" — is freedom or the final theft. **The theme is carried by
choice and consequence** (who Kevin chooses to be now that he could be the thief again), **not by
any power mechanic** (the amnesia is mundane head trauma; the only power-cost is impermanence).

**Theme presence rule:** present in ~80% of chapters; explicitly RECEDES in at least the
Panama-memory breathing chapter and one mid-book action beat, where survival and spectacle
carry the weight instead.

**Emotional residue (10 minutes after the last page):** *Unsettled by the suspicion that a
fresh start is just a debt handed to someone else — and quietly taking inventory of what they
themselves have chosen to forget.*

**Cultural vocabulary [PROPOSAL]:** the in-world verb **"to empty"** (someone) — to absorb a
person's power and, by extension, to take what makes them themselves. Introduced plainly early,
deepened until by the end "who emptied you?" carries the whole moral weight. Recommend adopting.

**Identity effect:** the reader feels like someone unafraid to look at their own ledger — "I'm
the kind of person who counts the true cost." Affirmed ~every 3–4 chapters via Kevin's honest
accounting.

## 7. VOICE DNA

### 7.1 Global narrative voice
- **Vocabulary level:** 5/10 — plain, precise, occasionally a hard clean image. Not Nabokov.
- **Sentence rhythm:** MIXED/varied — this is load-bearing (see anti-patterns). Grounded
  declaratives that open into one longer clause when the world tilts.
- **Formality:** Conversational-measured. Close third on Kevin (past tense).
- **Humor:** Dry, mostly Jake-carried; disappears entirely under real threat.
- **Emotional register:** Restrained — Kevin under-reports feeling; the reader infers cost from
  what he *counts*, not what he emotes.
- **Distinctive features:** The narration thinks in **transactions and charge** — time
  remaining, what a thing costs, who pays. Physical and kinetic in action; the system is felt in
  the body, never lectured.
- **VOICE UNDER PRESSURE (how it breaks):** at high stress Kevin's voice goes MORE clipped and
  MORE literal — inventory-mode, present tense bleeding into past, counting out loud — the numb
  flat register, not florid collapse. Jake inverts: he goes quiet and over-controlled.

- **Anti-patterns (NEVER):**
  - **The dream-seed staccato** — one-sentence-per-line drumbeat. Budget hard against it (below).
  - **Dialogue-as-infodump** — no character explains the mythology in a monologue. The system is
    revealed through USE, cost, and consequence, dripped across the book. The seed's sky-lecture
    is explicitly forbidden as a template.
  - Analytical/explanatory similes ("it was as if…"), metacognitive narration ("he realized he
    was realizing"), emotional-temperature reports ("a wave of dread washed over him"),
    competence cascades.

### 7.2 Per-character voice cards
**Kevin** — Vocabulary band: plain, numerate, a mechanic's precision. Syntax: short declaratives;
subordinates only to weigh a cost. Rhythm: statements over questions. Tics: counts things; states
prices; "That'll cost." Never says: gushing emotional self-description; never names his feeling
directly. Sample line: *"Flight was Jake's now. I had a shield for maybe nine more days and
nothing that could touch the wall between me and the door."*

**Jake** — Vocabulary band: casual, warm, slangy, sports-brain. Syntax: run-on when excited,
comma-spliced; clipped when scared. Rhythm: questions, jokes, rankings. Tics: ranks things aloud;
deflects; over-shares. Never says: anything cold or calculated; won't discuss the watch's meaning
seriously until forced. Sample line: *"Okay, top three worst ways to die, and getting folded into
a wall is now number one, congrats to this building."*

### 7.4 Anti-pattern budget (genre: superhuman sci-fi-fantasy; per 1,000 words)
- Explanatory/analytical similes (Pattern #11): **≤4/1k**.
- **One-sentence-per-line paragraphs (the dream tic): ≤1 such paragraph per 1,000 words**, and
  never 3+ consecutive one-line beats. Enforced in style_check ALLOWLIST review.
- Infodump: **zero** mythology-lecture passages >120 words of pure exposition. Break all
  worldbuilding into use/consequence.
- Adverbs in dialogue tags: near-zero. "It was as if / almost as though": ≤1/2k. Metacognitive
  narration: ≤1/3k. Emotional-temperature reports: ≤1/3k.

## 8. OPENING STRATEGY
**Chosen: In medias res (the escape) + the ordinary made strange.** Open inside the Vault
escape from the seed — but with ZERO exposition dump. Kevin wakes not knowing the rules of his
own power, so the READER learns them exactly as he re-discovers them, in the body, under fire.
The flight "detonation" is the hook; the mythology is withheld. Explicitly NOT the seed's sky-
lecture. The strangeness is Kevin's own competence he can't account for.
