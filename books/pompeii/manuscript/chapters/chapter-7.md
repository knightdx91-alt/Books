# Chapter 7 — The Roman With the Mules

The first time I met the man I would spend my life with, he told me to get off his land, and he did not look up from the harness he was mending to do it.

I have gone back to that morning more times than I can count, in the years since, turning it over the way I once turned a corroded fitting under the lamp to find the maker's mark. I was looking, I think, for the moment the door opened. There wasn't one. That is the joke of it, the joke the whole rest of my story would turn on: the door I had crossed the world to find had made a great deal of noise, and this one, the one that mattered, opened on a man saying no to me across a mule-yard, and I walked toward it thinking it was a wall.

But I am ahead of myself, and I owe the morning its order, because the order is the point. Before there was a man to say no, there was the wall I walked into that taught me I would need him; and before the wall, there was the fool I still was, who thought that coin and competence were enough to buy a road in that world, as they had been in mine.

---

I found out otherwise at the first market of the turning year, on the first fair-weather day, from a wagoner out of Amphissa with a good team and space in his cart and no reason on earth, I thought, to turn down my money.

He was carrying oil-jars down toward Kirrha and the coast, three days' hire of a wagon and a guard, and I had heard him in the inn's yard the night before saying he had room and a mind to fill it, and I had lain awake costing it out, my clever plan half-built, and in the morning I put on my hardest face and went to him with the coin already counted into my hand. I told him plainly what I wanted. Passage south, one woman, to the coast, at whatever he thought fair.

He looked at the coin. Then he looked at me, and then, worst of all, he looked past me, over my shoulder, left and right along the market lane, and I did not yet understand what he was looking for, so I stood there like a simpleton holding out money while a man searched the air behind my head.

"Where's your man," he said.

"I've coin enough for the fare," I said, "and a tenth over."

"That's not what I asked." He wasn't unkind. That was the part I could not have prepared for; I had braced for cruelty and got instead a sort of weary practicality, a man declining to buy a thing that would only rot in his cart. "Where's the man. Husband, father, the one whose errand this is. Who hired you out, who meets you at the bottom, who comes asking if you don't arrive." He shook his head slowly, and I watched the fare go out of his eyes. "A woman with coin and no man in front of her and no man at the far end is a wagon-load of trouble I can't sell at Kirrha. Anything happens to you, it's my road it happened on, and there's no one to pay and no one to swear you got on my cart whole. And if nothing happens to you." He let that sit, and did not finish it, and did not need to. He shifted his weight and looked, deliberately, away from me, at the jars, closing the door with his shoulder the way men there closed doors, by turning their bodies into walls. "Go home. Find whoever it is you belong to and have him hire it. I don't sell rides to nobody's woman."

*Nobody's woman.* He said it the way you'd say an ownerless dog, not a slur, only a fact of the world, a category with no protection and therefore no price, and he turned back to his jars and I stood in the lane with my counted coin gone slick in my fist and the whole market flowing around me not looking, all of them not looking in the practised way of people who have watched a woman be told she is no one and have decided it is none of their affair. I had run an inn, beaten a fever off a dying woman, learned to buy fish in a dead language so that no one turned around. And I could not buy a seat on a cart, because the thing being sold was not the seat. The thing being sold was the answer to *who comes asking if you don't arrive,* and I had no answer, and no coin in the world buys you an answer you cannot give.

I walked back up the sanctuary road with the taste of it in my mouth, and I did the thing I do, and by the time I reached the house the humiliation had cooled into a problem, which is the only form of grief I have ever known what to do with. A problem I could work. I only needed, first, to understand its true shape, and for that, as for everything, I had Kallia.

---

Kallia gave me the shape of my problem the way she gave me everything, sideways and too late to argue with.

It was the tail of winter, the light coming back thin and grudging over the shoulder of the mountain, and I had said, over the loom, the plainest sentence of my life. I said I would need to go south when the roads opened. To the coast, and then a ship, and then across to Italy. I said it the way you say a thing you have decided, flat, as though the deciding settled it.

She did not stop her hands. That was the first answer, her hands not stopping. Then she said, "Whose widow."

"What?"

"You heard me." She fed the shuttle through and beat the weft down, tock, the sound I heard in my sleep for years after. "A woman goes south. A woman. Alone. To the coast, and then a ship." She said the words back to me the way you hand a child a thing they have broken, to let them look at it. "Say the rest of it. Say the man's name. Whose widow, whose daughter, whose wife sent on whose business. Say the name that lets a road-master put you on a mule without a second man asking why."

I had no name. I had crossed half the width of the world and nineteen hundred years, I had learned to say *bread* so that no one turned around, I had run an inn and kept a fever off a woman by main force, and Kallia had already taught me, weeks before, the thing the wagoner now proved on my skin: that in this world I was not a person. I was a gap where a person's owner should be. A woman here was a sentence with the subject left out, and the world read the blank and put its own word there, and the word it put was *unprotected*, which was to say *available*, which was to say *prey*.

"There has to be a way a woman travels," I said. I heard my own voice do the thing it does, go up at the end, questioning; I heard it and could not stop it. "Women travel. I've seen them. On the road, at the inn."

"With a man. Behind a man. Ahead of a man's coin, carrying a man's message." She looked at me then, over the loom, and her face had gone gentle in the particular way that frightened me more than her sharpness, because she was only sharp when she thought I could take it. "Or old. Old and ugly and past being worth the trouble, and even then she goes in a group and prays. You are not old. You are not ugly. You are worth a great deal of trouble to the wrong man on an empty road, and you have no one who would ask after you, and every hard man on that coast will read that off you before you've said good morning." She beat the weft down. Tock. "So. Whose widow."

I understood her. I was slow, that winter, but I was not stupid, and I had spent my whole life reconstructing the plausible lives of vanished people out of a comb and a hinge and a scatter of coins, building the person the objects implied. Now I would have to build one for myself. I would have to become an object that implied a man.

"A widow," I said, trying it. "A widow can travel. On her dead husband's business. If she has coin, and a reason, and a story that holds."

"There," she said, as though I had finally found the floor with my feet. "There is your road. Now build him."

---

So we built him, Kallia and I, over three nights, the way other women might have built a loom or salted a winter's meat, with the same practicality and rather more argument.

I wanted him complicated. I always want the true thing complicated, it is the flaw in me, the completionist itch; I had reconstructed too many real lives to trust a simple one. I gave my dead husband a name and a trade and a bad chest and a dispute over a boundary wall, and Kallia listened to all of it with her mouth going flatter and flatter, and when I was done she said one word.

"No."

"Why no. It's a good story. It's got a wall dispute, no one invents a wall dispute, it's the boring detail that makes it real, it's the—"

"Take it out," she said over me.

"It is a story you love," she said. "That is the wrong kind. A story you love, you tell too well. You lean on it. You put in the wall because it is a good detail and you are proud of it, and the clever man on the coast hears you be proud of a detail and knows, the way I know when a girl at market has practised her price, that the thing is made and not lived. Grief does not tell you about the wall." She reached over and, with two fingers, quite gently, tapped the middle of my breastbone. "Grief goes short here and stops. A widow says his name and looks at the floor and says he is three years dead and she is going to Puteoli to see about his brother's debt, and if you push her she gets angry, not detailed. Angry, Mara. Not detailed. You give them the wall, you are a liar. You give them nothing and a hard face, you are a widow." She sat back. "Take out the wall."

I took out the wall. It was, I think now, the best editorial note I ever got, and I have had a few. She stripped my husband down over those three nights the way I had once stripped a false patina off a bronze, taking away everything I had loved and added, until what was left was almost nothing and therefore true: a name, Lucius Caedicius, common enough to be no one; a trade, a small dealer in dyed wool, dull enough that no one would ask; three years dead of a chest complaint, because the dead need a plain door out too; and a reason to go south that was made entirely of debt and duty and had not one interesting thing in it. No wall. No grief I could perform. Only the short, closed, angry-if-pushed shape of a woman with a name to shelter behind and no wish to discuss it.

There was one thing the widow could not solve, and it sat under all my planning like a stone I kept my foot on so as not to look at it, which was that I did not, in fact, know exactly where I was going. South was not a destination. Italy was barely one. All I had of the where was a line in a dead man's papers, Fenn's, the treasure-hunter's, the marooned fool whose notes had brought me across in the first place, saying only that the door home lay somewhere under a mountain by the Roman sea; and a mountain by the Roman sea is a hundred mountains, and I could spend the rest of a short and friendless life walking the coast of Campania asking peasants which of their mountains ate travellers.

I had heard, that winter, the way to close the gap, and I had been putting off hearing it, because it frightened me worse than the road did. A pilgrim had come through the inn in the black of the year, footsore and certain, bound up the sacred way, and he had talked, the way pilgrims talk, of the thing he was walking toward: that the god still spoke at Delphi, whatever the clever men in the cities said, that the Pythia still sat on her tripod on the given days and gave the given answers, and that a question asked rightly, with a coin and a beast for the altar, came back answered. A morning's walk up the mountain. A door with a voice, the pilgrim had said, not knowing he was describing my whole life, and I had sat by Kallia's lamp with my heart going hard and thought: *a dead man's notes gave me a mountain and a sea and no name. A living god might give me the name.*

So the plan had two locks, not one, and I had been counting only the second. Before I bought my way south to a mountain I could not name, I would climb the sacred way and put the naming to the one voice in that world that might have it. I did not tell Kallia this part; I told her only that I had a question for the sanctuary before I left her country, and she looked at me a long moment and did not ask what it was, because she was a woman who had lit a lamp for twenty years for an answer she would not walk uphill to hear, and she knew better than most that some questions you carry to the god precisely because you cannot bear to carry them anywhere else.

"Now," Kallia said, on the third night, filling the lamp, setting it in the window for the boy who never came up the road, "the road itself. You have a husband. You still have no way down the mountain. A widow with a dead husband and no living man to hire her passage is a lamp with no oil. She is a very respectable nothing."

"Then I hire the passage," I said. "I have coin now. I've earned coin. I pay a road-master like anyone pays a road-master."

Kallia looked at the lamp and not at me, and I have understood since that she was being kind, giving me the small mercy of not watching my face while she said the next thing.

"There is one road down that a woman might buy her way onto and keep her skin," she said. "One road-master in these hills honest enough, or hard enough, that a widow could ride his string and arrive with her coin and her body both her own. Only one I would send you to. The others would take the fare and sell the rest." She set the lamp straight. "The Roman."

I knew who she meant before she said the next word. I had heard the shape of him for weeks in the inn's talk without knowing I was gathering him, the way you gather the outline of a room in the dark by the things you bump. A freedman. Roman, or Roman enough. Scarred past the ordinary. Ran mules and good horses down to the coast and back and was said to be honest, or at least too dangerous to cheat, which in that country was the same coin with a different face. Trusted no one. Helped no one. Kept to his yard outside the sanctuary road with a boy and a string of animals and a silence that people crossed the lane to avoid walking through.

And once, on the road below the treasuries, in the first bewildered hour of my life in that world, a big scarred man had called off two men who meant me harm and did not once trouble to look at my face.

"Him," I said.

"Him." Kallia sat down heavily, her knees gone in the damp. "Valens. Was a fighter, they say, before he was freed, or before he freed himself, the story changes with the wine. Some scandal down south, a great man's household, a woman's name in it. He came up into the hills to be no one, and he is very good at it, and he will not thank you for needing him." She fixed me with the look. "He will say no."

"Everyone says no, at first."

"He will say no," she said again, and there was something in it that was almost a warning and almost, I understood only later, a blessing, "and you will have to make him mean something else. But hear me, girl. That is a man the world has used hard, and you will smell it on him. A man used past bearing does not want your story, however good it is; a good story is how he was ruined. He wants the true thing or nothing. So." She turned a proverb inside out then, tiredly, mercifully wrong, and handed it toward me the way she turned the lamp toward the empty road. "The locked door opens to the one who does not push."

I did not understand her. I sat in the lamplight with my dead husband freshly built and my hard face on and my whole clever plan assembled, and I thought she meant *be gentle*, and I could not have been more wrong, and I would not learn how wrong for another day.

---

I went out to the yard on the first morning the road was dry enough to walk, which is to say the mud had gone from swallowing to merely clinging, and I dressed for it like a woman going to a hearing, which I suppose I was.

I wore the widow. I need this remembered rightly, because it matters to everything after: I did not wear a costume. Kallia had taught me better than that. I wore the plainest of my two good tunics and the dark mantle and a face I had practised in the black of the byre until it held without my holding it, the closed, tired, unimpressed face of a woman past being surprised by the world's unkindness, and I walked the three miles of sanctuary road down toward the coast turning with it in my head, not the story, Kallia had cured me of loving the story, but the shape underneath it: *closed. Short. Angry if pushed, not detailed. You are Caedicia. You bury your dead and you settle their debts and you do not explain yourself to muleteers.*

The yard sat where the road bent south, in a fold of ground with a stream and a stand of poplars gone silver-budded with the turning year, and I heard it before I saw it, the deep interrogative sound of horses, the stamp and blow of large animals in a small space, the ring of a hoof on stone. It smelled of them a hundred paces off, that thick clean animal smell of dung and hay and warm hide that I have loved ever since without ever once telling him why. There was a low house, and a longer stable, and a paddock of churned mud with a dozen mules and three horses in it, and everywhere the evidence of a trade kept by a careful man: harness oiled and hung in graded ranks under the eaves, a mending-bench with its awls and waxed thread laid in a row, a water-trough scrubbed pale, a muck-heap squared off at the corners as though even the dung were kept to a standard. Nothing was rich and nothing was slovenly. It was the yard of a man who owned little and lost nothing, and I read it the way I read a well-kept toolkit in a dead man's estate, and what it told me, before I had properly seen him, was that whoever ran this place trusted no one to do a thing right but himself and had made a kind of peace out of the doing.

A boy of maybe twelve was forking hay with the total absorption of the young. And a man sat at the bench under the stable eaves with a harness across his knees, doing something to the leather with his hands.

I knew him at once and my body knew him before my mind caught up, which should have told me something and told me nothing, because I read the lurch under my ribs as fear. He was very big. Sitting, he was still somehow the largest thing in the yard, larger than the animals, a stillness that the eye kept returning to the way it returns to a standing stone in a field. The scars I had heard about were real and worse than the telling, a country of them up one forearm and the old seam down the side of his jaw that pulled his short grey beard askew, the one I had seen at a distance on the sanctuary road and not let myself look at, and his hands, which never stopped, which were working a knot or a buckle or a length of stitching the entire time I knew him whenever the air went tight, were enormous and precise and gentle on the leather, a killer's hands doing a saddler's work.

He did not look up.

"Yard's closed to walkers," he said. He had a low voice, level, entirely without heat, the voice of a man reading a notice he has read out a thousand times. His Greek was accented and exact, a tool used well and loved not at all. "Road's a mile that way. Follow the wall."

"I'm not lost," I said.

"No." He drew a stitch tight. The leather creaked. "You walked here on purpose. That's worse." And still he had not looked up, and I understood that this was deliberate, that not-looking was a thing he did *to* people, a door he closed in your face without troubling to build a face to close it in, and I felt the first flare of something that was not fear and not yet the other thing, only the old contrary heat of a woman being made to feel she was nothing, which I had felt a great deal that winter and had run out of patience for.

"I'm told you run animals to the coast," I said. Closed. Short. "I want to buy passage. In the spring. For myself, one string, to Kirrha or wherever your road ends near a ship, and I pay the going rate and a tenth over for the trouble, in coin, half now."

That was the pitch. I had built it hard and dry, all business, no story, exactly as Kallia had drilled me, and I was proud of it, and I braced for the haggle.

He set the harness down.

He set it down slowly, across his knees, and he wiped his hands once on the cloth at his belt, and then, for the first time, he looked up, and he looked at my face, which I had been told he would not do, and I felt the look go over me the way a wave goes over you at the shore, complete and impersonal and taking your feet out from under, and it was not the look of a man weighing a fare. It was the look I had given a thousand objects across a lamp. It was appraisal. He was reading me the way I read a hinge, for the maker's mark, for the age, for the false patina and the true wear, and he did it in about the time it takes to draw a breath, and then he said the thing that undid a week's work.

"No," he said. "Go home, widow. You're not one."

---

I have had, in my life, a number of moments where the floor of a story I was standing on turned out to be painted on air, and I went through. This was among the worst, and I want to record honestly what I did, because it is not to my credit and it is the whole of why he trusted me later.

I lied harder. That is the shameful truth of it. My first instinct, faced with a man who had seen straight through the widow, was to reach for a better widow, and I felt the story come up in me, the detail, the wall, all the reconstructive apparatus of a lifetime, and I opened my mouth to give him a dead husband so specific he'd have to believe it.

And I saw his hands.

They had gone back to the leather. The moment he'd said *no*, before I'd even reacted, his hands had picked the harness back up and found a strap and begun, unhurried, to work a knot loose that did not need loosening, and I watched them do it, and something in me that had spent thirty years reading the wear on things read the wear on him. Kallia's voice came up out of the memory clear as a struck bowl. *A man used past bearing does not want your story. A good story is how he was ruined.* And I understood, standing in the cold mud of his yard with the better lie dying in my mouth, that the widow was not a key to this door. The widow was the exact wrong shape. Every clever plausible turn I could give her was another proof I was a person who built stories to get past men, and this was a man that stories had been built to destroy, and he could smell the workmanship on me like smoke.

So I did the hardest thing I did that whole first year, harder than the crossing, harder than the fever. I put the story down. I let him see me put it down.

"No," I said. "I'm not."

He didn't stop his hands, but something in the set of his shoulders changed, a fractional stilling, the way an animal stills when a sound it was braced for doesn't come.

"I'm not a widow," I said. My voice had stopped going up at the ends. It does that, I have noticed, when I finally tell the truth; the questioning goes out of it. "I made her up. Three nights ago, with the woman who took me in, because she told me a lone woman can't buy her way down this mountain without a dead man's name to hide behind, and she was right, and the widow was the best I could build, and you saw through her in less time than it takes to spit. So." I spread my hands, the gesture of a woman laying her tools on the bench for inspection. "I've nothing left to sell you but the fact that I've stopped selling. I want passage south in the spring. I have real coin, earned real, at Kallia's inn on the sanctuary road, you can ask her. And I am exactly what you think I am, which is a woman alone with no man behind her and nowhere she can prove she comes from, and I'd have lied to you about all of it if you'd let me, and you didn't, so now you have the truth, which is worth less than the lie would have been if it had worked. That's my whole offer. Coin, and the truth, and no story."

He worked the knot. The boy had stopped forking hay and was watching us with his mouth a little open; the man said something to him without turning his head, three words in Latin, and the boy went back to the hay. For a long moment there was only the sound of the animals and the stream and the small creak of leather in his hands.

"Where do you come from," he said at last. Not gently. Flat. A question that was a test and not an interest.

And here was the thing I could not do, the thing that would ride under my whole life in that world like a stone under a mattress: I could not tell him the one truth that mattered, because the one truth that mattered was insane. I come from a place nineteen hundred years from here. I come from a basement with a scalpel and a tray of solvents. I walked through a door in a headland and the door shut. I could give him the truth about the widow and I could not give him *the* truth, and the gap between those two, the true woman with the impossible provenance, was the loneliest country I ever lived in, and I lived in it for years, and it began there in his yard with the only answer I had.

"Somewhere I can't go back to," I said. "That part's not a lie either. It's just not a place you'd know."

He looked at me a while longer. Then he laid the harness aside and stood, and standing he was a wall, he blotted the thin sun, and I made myself not step back, and I think not stepping back was the second thing that day that mattered, though I did it out of stubbornness and not courage and there is, in the end, not much difference between them when you're cold and out of options.

"I'll tell you what I told the last three," he said. "I run animals. I don't run women, or messages, or contraband, or anyone's business but the animals'. You want to go south, there's a fair coming, wait for it, travel in a crowd, keep your coin in three places, and don't ride any string but the one with the most Roman women already on it, because a man won't do to a widow in front of witnesses what he'll do to her on an empty road. That's the whole of what I have for you. It's better advice than passage. Take it and go home."

"The fair's not safe and you know it," I said. "You just told me to hide in a crowd, that's not safe, that's *survivable*, those aren't the same word, I've had a whole winter of the difference. I'm not asking you to run me. I'm asking to buy a place on a string you're already running, one paying rider among however many, with the most respectable company on the mountain, which everyone from here to the coast says is yours. That's not doing me a favour. That's a fare."

"It's a fare that ends with a body found in the poppies below Kirrha and a road-master answering questions to a magistrate about the woman with no people who paid him half in front and was never seen again," he said, and it was the longest thing he'd said, and it came out level and cold and complete, a man who had thought the whole thing through to its end before I'd finished my sentence. "You have no one who'd ask after you. You said so yourself; it's the truest thing you've said. A rider with no one to ask after her is not a passenger. She's a liability with legs. If anything happens to you on my road, and on that road something happens to someone every second trip, there's no father, no brother, no master to pay off or fear or answer to, and there's just me, holding a woman with no name that holds, explaining myself to men who'd love a reason to look hard at the Roman who came up from a scandal to hide in the hills." His hands, I saw, had stopped. They had gone still on the harness, and that stillness, I would learn, was the loudest thing he ever did. "I have spent six years being no one. You are the most someone I've had walk into this yard, and I want you gone before the wanting to help you costs me the only thing I've got left, which is that no one knows my name. So. No. And I'm sorry, which is more than I said to the last three, and you should take *that* and go, because it's the most I've given anyone since I came up this road."

"You keep saying the poppies," I said. I did not mean to. It came out of the part of me that reaches, always, for the true detail under the plausible one, the part that had cost me and would go on costing me. "Below Kirrha. You said it twice, both times like a place you've stood. Most men, warning a woman, they say *something could happen.* You said where the body turns up. That's not a warning. That's a memory."

His hands stopped on the harness. I have told you what his stillness was; I felt it then for the first time full on, the way the whole yard seemed to lower its voice around it, the boy going quiet at his hay, the animals shifting, and I understood I had reached past the door and touched something behind it, and I braced, because a man does not thank you for that.

He did not throw me out. He did something worse and better; he answered.

"Two springs back," he said. His voice had not changed at all, and that was the horror of it, the same level tradesman's Greek he'd have used to price a mule. "A widow. A real one. Coin, a story that held, people at the far end who'd ask. Everything you don't have. She went down with a party out of Krisa, a good party, guards, other women, everything I'd have told her to want." He drew a strap through his fingers, slow, found the buckle, worked it. "The guards were the ones. You understand. The party was the danger. No one stood behind her to answer to, because the men she'd have run to were the men who did it, and they knew the road, and they knew the poppies, and it was three days before a goatherd found what there was to find, and by then the party was at the coast selling oil and swearing she'd left them at the fork of her own will." The buckle. His thumb, going over and over the worn place in the leather. "I carried what the goatherd found up to Krisa myself, on my own beast, because someone had to, and the men who'd have paid to bury her were three days gone, and I have never wanted my own namelessness so badly as the morning I stood in that magistrate's yard being asked, very politely, what my business was on that road."

The yard was silent. The stream ran. I found I had nothing to say, none of my questions, none of my spirals; the reading part of me had gone quiet at last, humbled, the way it should have been humbled years before.

"So when you stand in my yard," he said, and now he looked up, and looked at my face, "with no coin-count in the world that fixes what's wrong with you, which is that you are exactly her but without even the party to lie about it after, and you ask me to put you on my string, you are asking me to carry you up to Krisa in two springs' time. That's what I'm saying no to. Not your fare. Your funeral." He lowered the harness across his knees, done, and his hands, having nothing left to work, lay open and still and enormous on the leather, and I could not yet read what open still hands meant on him, though I would learn, though I would spend the last hour of both our lives learning it. "Now go home."

---

I should have gone. Any sensible woman goes, there. He had told me no in the one way that could not be argued with, which is the way where the no is *for your sake and his both* and true on every count, and he had even, at the end, done the thing I would come to know as the whole of him, the kindness turned sideways so you'd not catch him at it, the *I'm sorry* that was worth more than a yes from a lesser man. I should have thanked him and walked my three miles back up the mountain and waited for the fair and hidden in the crowd.

Instead I did the thing that is either my worst flaw or the reason I am the woman this whole story happened to, depending on the day you ask me. I stayed, and I told him a truth I had not planned to tell, because I had run clean out of plans and there was nothing under the plans but the actual thing.

"You're the first person in this country who's told me the truth about what I am," I said.

His hands didn't move.

"Everyone else lies to me about it, kindly. Kallia calls me her niece. The women at the harvest call me the one who came down from the hill country. The trader at the inn pretended to believe I was from anywhere. Everyone's been so gentle with the fact that I'm nobody that I'd almost stopped hearing how much danger I'm in, and you just stood there and said it out loud, *a liability with legs, a body in the poppies,* and do you know, it's the first time I've felt safe since I got here." I laughed, and it came out wrong, cracked, and I let it. "Which is insane. You've just refused me and frightened me half to death and you're the safest thing that's happened to me, because you're the first one who won't pretend. Kallia said you'd want the true thing or nothing. She was right about that the way she's right about everything, sideways and too late. So here's a true thing you didn't ask for and can throw away: I'm not afraid you'll hurt me. I've been read by hard men all winter and I know the difference, and whatever you are, you're a man who's telling me no *so that* nothing happens to me, and that's not the same species as the men I'm frightened of, and I think you know it, and I think that's exactly why you want me gone. Not because I'm a liability. Because you'd help me. And helping people is how they got you last time."

I did not know, when I said it, how close to the bone I'd cut. I was guessing, the way I guess at the life behind an object, from the wear. But I saw it land. I saw it go into him, the seam through his eyebrow deepening on that permanent question he didn't feel, and his hands came up off the leather and pressed flat on his thighs, spread, still, a man holding himself down onto a bench, and when he spoke again the level had a crack in it, hairline, but there.

"You want to be careful," he said, low, "reading a man you've known the length of one bad conversation."

"I know," I said. "It's a terrible habit. It's cost me. I read the dead for a living, where I come from, and the dead don't mind being wrong about. The living do." I pulled the mantle straight, gathering myself to go, because I had felt the thing tip and I was wise enough, even then, not to push it past the tipping. "I'll not trouble your yard again. You said no and you meant it kindly and I heard both halves. I'll wait for the fair and hide in the crowd and keep my coin in three places, and if I end up in the poppies below Kirrha it won't be your name they trouble over it. Thank you for the truth. It's rarer than passage."

I turned to go.

I had gone perhaps six steps, out past the paddock rail into the churned mud of the lane, the boy watching me go and the mules swinging their heads to follow, when he said it. He said it to my back, in Greek, level, as though it cost him nothing, which was how I would learn he said everything that cost him most.

"The fair's not for six weeks."

I stopped. I did not turn around, because I had learned, that winter, when a thing was being handed to you sideways and must not be looked at head-on or it would go back in the pocket.

"I know," I said, to the poplars.

"Come back in three days," he said. "Not for passage. I'm not running you south; that stands. But there's a widow woman goes down with the wool in a proper party, twice a year, guards and wagons and a factor from Corinth who answers to real people, and she's short a companion this spring, an educated woman to keep her books and her company, and she pays, and no one on that road will lay a hand on a factor's party without starting a war they can't afford. It's the safe crossing you asked for. It's just not on my string." A pause, and the creak of leather as his hands found something to do. "I'll speak for you. If Kallia vouches your coin's earned honest and your hands are what you say, I'll tell the factor you're steady and you keep your mouth shut, which I've seen enough of in one morning to swear to. That's not helping you. That's putting a steady clerk in front of a man who needs one and taking my finder's cut like anyone would. Business."

I stood in the lane with my back to him and I felt the thing move through me that I did not have a name for yet and would spend nine years learning, and I was careful, so careful, to keep it off my face and out of my voice, because I understood already, six steps into knowing him, that if I turned around and let him see it he would take it all back to protect the version of himself that helped no one.

"That's a great deal of business," I said, to the trees, "for a man who runs only the animals."

"Come back in three days or don't," he said, and I heard the harness go back across his knees, the conversation closed the way he closed everything, with a task. "It's nothing to me either way."

It was, I would learn, the largest lie he told in all the years I knew him, and the only one, and he told it looking at a knot instead of at me, and I let him have it, and I walked back up the mountain three miles in the clinging mud with something lit in me that I kept turning away from and turning away from, calling it relief, calling it a plan coming good, calling it anything but what it was.

---

Kallia was at the door when I came up the last of the road, though she pretended she was only throwing out the wash water, and she read my face before I'd got the gate open, because she read faces the way I read hinges and had been doing it longer.

"So," she said. "He said no."

"He said no," I agreed. I came into the warmth and the smell of the loom and the lamp not yet lit because the day still held, and I sat down heavily on the bench where I had built a dead husband three nights before, and I found I was shaking a little, the way you shake after cold water, and could not have said whether it was the three miles or the man.

"And then?"

"And then he found me a better road than the one I came asking for," I said, "and swore he was doing it out of business, and told me it was nothing to him, and could not look at me while he lied about it."

Kallia was quiet a while. She had picked up the shuttle without sitting, and she turned it over in her fingers, that small dark polished thing that had passed through the warp of her whole life, and when she spoke she was not misquoting any proverb. She said the plainest thing she ever said to me, and she said it looking at the shuttle and not at me, the two of us having learned, I think, the same trick from the same hard country.

"That is a more dangerous no," she said, "than any yes you were fishing for. A yes I could have prepared you against. Guard your coin, guard your body, expect the worst, all the old armour." She set the shuttle down in the warp. "There is no armour against a man who says no to keep you safe. That is the one that gets in." She looked at me then, and her face had gone gentle in the way that frightened me, and she did not turn it into a lesson, which was how I knew she meant it entirely. "Go back in three days, girl. Take the safe road he found you. Keep your books for the factor's widow and get yourself to the coast and to your ship and to whatever is under your mountain in the south that you will not tell me about." A breath. "But know, going, what you are walking back down that hill toward. I have buried a husband, and as good as lost a son, and a great deal else, and I have never regretted the loving, only the pretending I could keep it. You are already pretending. You called it business coming up the road; I saw it on you at the gate. Don't." She turned the lamp, though it wasn't lit, toward the window and the empty road out of long habit, the gesture that was her whole heart. "The oil burns whether you admit it or not. Better you know it's lit."

I did not answer her, because I had no answer, because she was right, sideways and too late and completely, as she was about everything. I sat in the failing light of a house I had come to belong to, on the bench where I'd built a lie to get me down a mountain, and I thought about a man with a killer's gentle hands who had told me no to keep me alive and then, unable to help himself, found me a road anyway, and I did the thing I do. The cold working part of me, the conservator, the one that survives, woke up and began, quietly, to plan the three days.

But under it, for the first time since the door had shut behind me on the headland and stranded me at the far end of the world, something that was not planning had lit, low and steady and unasked for, like a lamp set in a window for someone still a long way down a dark road, and coming.

I lay awake that night in the byre with the goat and the old cold enormity, and for the first time since the door had shut I found I was not planning the road at all. I was planning the yard. I was arranging the words I would use in three days on a man who had told me no, and catching myself arranging them, and turning the lamp of my attention away from what that meant the way Kallia turned her lamp toward an empty road: out of habit, and hope, and the refusal to say the hope aloud in case saying it blew it out.

I went back in three days. Of course I went back. But that is the next part, and the factor, and the yes I had to earn and not merely be given; and this part, the part where the man I would die beside told me to get off his land and could not manage it, ends the way it began, with his hands on a knot and his eyes anywhere but on me, and me walking away up the mountain toward a life I thought I was arranging and was, in fact, only just beginning to be surprised by.
