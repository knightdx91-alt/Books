# Chapter 3: Coordinates & the Survey

<!-- Word count: ~6,500 | Target: 7,000 | Anchor: the kit laid out on a foreign rock in the good light (the wristwatch wound, the film loaded, the date written in her own hand) and the small joy of it going wrong under her the way it never had before -->

There is a particular happiness in decoding the handwriting of the dead, and I am ashamed of how well I know it. It is not grief and it is not respect; it is closer to appetite. You sit with the loops and the cramped ascenders and the places where the pen dug in, and slowly the marks stop being a stranger's hand and start being a stranger's mind, and there comes a moment when the thing resolves, when the smear becomes a word and the word becomes a meaning, and something in the back of your skull says *there, got you,* the way it does over a scraped-clean inscription in the basement, and you are pleased. I was pleased, that first night, in a guesthouse on the English coast with the sea working away in the dark beyond a window that would not shut properly, bent over August Fenn's coordinates by the light of a bedside lamp with a scorched shade. I want to be honest about that before I tell you anything else. I was not frightened yet. I was happy. It is the last uncomplicated happiness in this account and it belonged to a man who had been dead for thirty years, and I took it from him gladly, and I would do it again.

The numbers themselves were simple. My father had copied them out of Fenn's ledger in his own ink, precise to the second of arc, and Fenn had taken them from somewhere I could not see, because a coordinate does not tell you how it was got. What took me the evening was the rest of it, the marginalia, the muddle of scripts on the map itself. I had the map spread on the candlewick bedspread with a torch and my reading loupe, the one that lives in my coat with the notebook. And I went over the ringed cross square by square the way I would go over a corroded plate, quadrant by quadrant, and the place-names came up slowly out of the fold-wear, and they were Greek. Not modern Greek. The lettering under Fenn's copperplate was older, the transliteration of a man showing off his schooling, but the coast it named was a coast I could find in an atlas, and I did find it, in the road atlas I had bought at a petrol station two towns back because I am the kind of woman who buys the map before she admits she is going.

It was not England. It was not even the same sea. And the second I was sure of it, the second the numbers and the names agreed and pointed at a stretch of southern shore two aeroplanes and a country away, I felt the whole sensible weight of my life shift onto its back foot, because I understood that going to look was no longer a drive up a coast road. It was a passport and a fare and a lie told to a bank about traveller's cheques. It was a decision with an airport in it.

I should have stopped there. I want to be fair to the woman on the bedspread and say that she knew it. She sat back against the cold headboard with the map on her knees and she thought, clearly and in her own voice, *this is the place a reasonable person turns round.* And then she reached for the notebook and copied the airport into the back of it, under the coordinates, in the same neat hand, and did not turn round. Because I have a fault I have spent my life mistaking for a strength, and the fault is this: I cannot leave a thing at the interesting part. Show me an inscription with the last three lines still under the mud and I will dig them out though the light is gone and the site is closing and I have a train to catch. It has cost me every good exit I was ever offered. That night it walked me, quite calmly, past the last cheap one.

I slept badly and dreamed of nothing I could keep, and in the morning I ate a fried breakfast I did not taste and rang Halloran from the box outside the post office with a fresh fistful of coins.

He was surprised to hear from me, and then he was pleased, and then, when I told him what I meant to do, he went into the careful voice men use when they suspect they are being made complicit in something. The permit, he said, was for Campania. For the Italian coast, for a survey season my father had never lived to run. I was proposing to go somewhere else entirely. I said I understood that. I said I meant to make a preliminary reconnaissance of a related site before the season proper, that my father had left coordinates, that it was the sort of thing one did. I heard myself building the sentence out of true words arranged to mislead, which is the only kind of lie I have ever been good at, and Halloran, three time zones away and tired and fond of a dead man, let himself be built. He would write me a letter of association on university paper, he said, so that if anyone asked I was somebody rather than nobody, a woman alone with a camera and a purpose. He asked me to be careful. He asked, at the very end, in a smaller voice than he had started with, whether I believed my father had been chasing something real, or only chasing.

I told him I would let him know. I am, it turns out, a woman who tells people she will let them know, and does not, and I have a long ledger of it now, longer than Fenn's, and no one to keep it but me.

---

I flew out three days later, and here the account has to brighten whether I like it or not, because the truth is that I was happy again, and more than happy; I was somebody I had not been in years. I had forgotten. I sat by the window of an aeroplane with the map in the overhead locker and my kit under the seat and I watched England go under the wing, grey field and grey field and the grey sleeve of the Channel. And then somewhere over France the cloud broke and the light came up off the land like something being turned on. I put my forehead against the plastic and looked at Europe going gold and brown beneath me and felt my chest do a thing it had not done since before the February heart, the bad one. I was going into the field. Not the basement. The field, at last, at thirty-four, on a stranger's numbers, with no father to take me and no father to tell. I laughed once, out loud, at the window, and the man beside me looked over, and I did not explain, because there is no explaining it, the awful happiness of finally doing the thing you refused the only person who ever asked you to do it.

The country when I landed was hot in a way England is never hot, a dry white heat with the sea in it, and the airport smelled of jet fuel and cigarettes and, underneath, of something green and resinous baking on a hillside I could not see. I hired a car from a man in a booth who was in the middle of a quarrel with someone on the telephone about a delivery of tyres, and who processed my paperwork one-handed while conducting the quarrel with the other, jabbing a finger at an invoice on his counter as though the person on the line could see it. He slid my keys across without once looking at me, still saying *no, no, Thursday, you said Thursday.* I took them and left him to his tyres and his Thursday, and I like to think he is out there still, or was, a man whose worst trouble that day was a lorry, and I envied him it later more than I can tell you.

The car was a small white diesel that smelled of hot vinyl and someone else's holiday. I drove it out of the city on the wrong side of the road for the first half hour, gripping the wheel and talking to myself. I stalled it twice at the same junction and a man behind me leaned on the horn and then gave up and went round me, and that was the whole of it. And then the city thinned and the road climbed and I stopped talking to myself and started, without deciding to, to sing, badly, along with a station I could not understand, a woman's voice going up and up over a bouzouki. And the olive trees went by in their dusty silver ranks and the light came through them in bars across the road. I drove into it happy, genuinely happy, a woman on a road in the sun with a purpose in the back of her book. I want that on the record too. Whatever came after, I had that afternoon. Nobody can take the afternoon.

I stopped once, at a taverna at a crossroads where two old men sat under a vine doing nothing with great commitment, and I ate bread and oil and a salad and drank a small bitter coffee, and the bread was extraordinary. I want to note it because I noted it then, sitting in the shade with Fenn's coordinates forty minutes down the road and my whole old life two aeroplanes behind me. I tore the bread and the crust cracked properly and the crumb inside was open and irregular and smelled of the fire it was baked in. And some part of me that will not be quiet at any graveside or on any threshold sat up and said *there, that, that is what bread is,* and thought with brief and total contempt of the leather loaf going hard in my dead father's kitchen, and then was ashamed, and ate the good bread anyway, all of it, wiping the plate. The woman who brought it watched me do it and said something I did not understand and looked pleased. I paid her too much and she did not correct me and I did not mind. It is possible to be, at one and the same hour, the happiest you have been in years and forty minutes from the end of your entire life. Nobody tells you that. They should put it on a sign.

---

The coordinates did not fall on a road, which I had known and had chosen not to fully think about. They fell on a headland, a fist of pale rock pushed out into the sea past the last of the olives, with a footpath of sorts worn into it by goats or fishermen or the few people who had ever had a reason to walk out to the end of the land. I left the car pulled off the tarmac in a scrape of gravel and I walked the last of it on foot in the long gold light of the late afternoon with my kit on my back and the map in my hand. And this was the happiest twenty minutes, and I have gone back to it more times than to almost anything, so I will do it slowly, the way I did it then.

I navigated by the numbers and by dead reckoning off the map, which is a skill my father had, and which I had, though I had never before had a reason to use it under an open sky; I had only ever used it on a grid over somebody else's trench. Now I paced it out along the spine of the headland with the sea falling away white on both sides of me, counting my strides, correcting, and the pleasure of it was clean and enormous and had nothing wrong with it yet. I was good at this. That is the thing I had never let myself find out. My whole life I had told the story that the field was not for me, that I was a basement creature, better with the scalpel than the spade, and it was a lie, and I found it out too late to tell the one person the truth would have meant something to. I paced the headland and I was good at it, and my father was three days and thirty years and one impossible thing away from knowing. And then I did a foolish thing, the first purely happy foolish thing I had done in a year: I walked back along the spine to where I had started and paced the whole line over again from the beginning. I checked the second count against the first, for no reason on earth but the pleasure of a thing done well and done twice. There was no one out there to see a grown woman pace a rock twice and grin at the sea over it, and if there had been I would not have cared.

The place, when I reached it, was nothing. I want to be exact, because I have heard people describe such places since, in the way of people who need the world to have warned them, and they always make it something; a stone circle, a blasted tree, a hush. It was nothing. It was a shallow saddle in the rock near the seaward end of the headland, a dip perhaps ten feet across where the pale stone was smoother than around it and a little greener, where something, wind or water or feet, had worn it. There was thyme growing in the cracks of it and a great deal of light coming off the sea beyond, and it smelled of hot stone and thyme and salt. And a single kestrel hung in the air off the point, working the updraft, going nowhere with tremendous concentration. That was all. I stood at the edge of the saddle with my heart going and I checked the numbers again against my watch and my pacing, and they agreed, and I thought, with the flat let-down of the professional finding the marked spot exactly where the mark said it would be, *well. Here it is, then. A dip in a rock.*

And I felt, standing there, the first thing I could not account for, and it was not grand. It was that something in me balked at stepping into the saddle. There was no reason. It was rock and thyme. But I found I had stopped at the exact lip of it, the way you stop at the top of a stair in the dark when your foot expects a step that is not there, and I stood at the edge with one boot forward and did not put it down. The kestrel hung, and the sea went on, and I told myself I was tired from the walk and the flight and the whole insane week, and I made myself step in, and nothing happened, because of course nothing happened. It was five o'clock in the afternoon and I was a grown woman standing in a hollow in a rock, and the only thing in that hollow with me was thyme, and my own good sense telling me, too quietly to hear, to go home.

So I fell back on my one reliable instinct. I decided to work.

---

I took the kit off my back and I laid it out on the flattest shelf of rock at the edge of the saddle, in the order I always lay it out, the order my father laid his out, which is the order I learned before I learned to read a section. I knelt beside him on a Sunday floor while he showed me the tools of a trade he would spend the next twenty-five years not quite taking me into. This is the part I have to do carefully, because it is the part that matters, though I did not know then that it mattered, and because it is the nearest thing I have to a prayer and I have said it since in places where prayer was the only kit I had left.

First the notebook, flat, spine down, opened to a fresh page. It is a field notebook, a proper one, stiff waterproof cover, the pages faintly gridded, a pencil bound into the spiral because pens fail in the wet and in the cold and a pencil will write on anything and underwater if it has to. My father taught me that, a pencil is the only honest instrument, it does not run and it does not lie about the weather. I opened it to a clean page and I did the first thing you do, before the light meter, before the compass, before anything, the thing so automatic that I did it without thought, the way you sign your own name: I wrote the date at the head of the page, and the place, and the time. The date in my own hand, in pencil, at the top of a gridded page on a rock above the sea. I did not look at it twice. Why would I. It was only the date. I have looked at it a great many times since, in memory, that ordinary line of pencil, because of what it would come to mean, and because of where I would one day see it written again in the same hand and not remember writing it. But that is far ahead, and I owe the woman on the rock her ignorance, so let it stand for now as what it was, a date at the top of a page, the most ordinary mark a working day can hold.

Then the watch. I took it off my wrist to check it against the meter, which is not necessary but is ritual, and it was his, the wristwatch he gave me, cheap and accurate, a steel case gone dull at the lugs and a second hand that swept rather than ticked, and I wound it three half-turns the way you do a watch you mean to trust for a day's work, and I felt the small resistance of the mainspring and heard the faint grain of it and set it down on the rock face-up, running, its second hand sweeping its clean smooth circle. My father wore it thirty years and was never late a day of them and never trusted a clock he had not set himself. I had worn it eleven days.

Then the camera. This is the heart of the ritual and I will not hurry it. It is a manual camera, all metal, heavy in the hand as a good tool should be, no battery to speak of but the one that runs the meter, and I loaded it there on the rock the way I have loaded it a thousand times, which is a thing the hands do while the mind is elsewhere. Mine was on the woman from the car radio, the song I could not understand and could not put down, going up and up; I was humming it, I realised, two notes of it, over and over, on a rock at the end of the land, and I did not stop. My hands did the rest without me: the cassette seated, the leader drawn across the gate and caught in the take-up spool, the back closed and clicking home, two blank frames wound off the fogged leader to bring fresh film to the gate. I watched the rewind knob turn as the film pulled taut, which is how you know it is truly loaded and not spooling free, connected to itself all the way through. The frame counter came up off S to one. Ready. I set the film speed from memory off the cassette and lifted the camera and put my eye to it and swung it across the saddle and the sea and the hanging kestrel to read the light.

And the meter was wrong.

I want to be careful here, because everything that mattered turned on my being a woman who does not say *the meter was wrong* and mean *I felt strange.* I mean the meter was wrong. The needle in the finder, the match-needle that tells you the light, swung up hard against its pin at the top of the scale and stayed there, pegged, the way it does when you point a camera straight at the sun, and I was not pointing it at the sun. I was pointing it at a hollow of grey rock in the shadow of the headland with the sun well off to the side and dropping. There was not that much light in the whole afternoon. I took the camera down and looked at the sky and looked at the rock, and the light on the rock was the ordinary gold going-down light of five in the afternoon. And I lifted the camera again and read again and the needle was pegged again, hard against the top pin, reading a brightness that was not in the world in front of it.

The professional in me was, God help me, delighted. This is the shameful thing and I will set it down. My first response to the wrongness was not fear; it was the pure clean joy of an anomaly, the same joy as the resolving of the handwriting, the *there, got you.* A stuck meter. A dead cell, condensation on the CdS window, a needle come off its jewel; some ordinary fault with an ordinary name, and I was pleased to have a fault to solve, because a fault is a thing you can fix, and fixing is the only faith I have. I sat down cross-legged on the rock with the camera in my lap and I began, happily, to diagnose it. And I did not notice for some minutes that the light around me had not changed at all while the sun went visibly further down. Which is a thing I could not have named then and can only just name now, that the shadow was moving and the brightness was not.

---

I took a reading with the meter off the camera, the handheld one, the old selenium meter in its cracked leather case that I carried as a check because my father never trusted a camera's own eye. It is a beautiful obsolete thing, no battery at all, a selenium cell that makes its own tiny current out of the light that falls on it and pushes a needle across a dial, honest as a sundial, nothing in it to fail but the cell itself dying slowly over decades. I held it out flat in the saddle at arm's length, incident to the sky, the way you do, and I read it, and it agreed with the camera. Pegged. Full deflection. The needle laid over hard against the stop and quivering there, reading a flood of light that my own eyes told me was not falling. Two meters. Two different meters, one electric and one that ran on nothing but the light itself, both reading the same impossible brightness in the same grey hollow. And I sat very still with the beautiful old thing in my two hands and felt the joy go out of me between one breath and the next and something colder come in behind it. Because two independent instruments do not share a fault. One meter is broken. Two meters reading the same lie are not broken. Two meters reading the same lie are telling you the truth about something you cannot see.

I put the selenium meter down on the rock, face-up, beside the watch. I did it gently. I was aware of handling all of it gently now, the way your hands change the instant a thing stops being an object and becomes evidence, when you no longer own it and are only its first witness.

The watch had stopped.

No. Not stopped. I picked it up and it was worse than stopped. The second hand was sweeping, but sweeping slowly, dragging, the smooth even sweep gone syrupy, taking what my own counted pulse told me was three seconds to cross one. And then, while I held it in the flat of my hand and watched, not believing, doing the arithmetic of a thing that cannot happen, it slowed further and hung, trembling, at a second-mark near the top of the dial. It strained against something, the way the meter needle strained. And then it came off the mark and swept on, fast, too fast, catching up, and then evened out to its ordinary honest sweep as if nothing had happened, as if I had imagined the whole of it, a cheap accurate watch keeping perfect time in the palm of a frightened woman on a rock. I held my own wrist with my other hand and counted my pulse against it and the watch was right. The watch was right now. But I had seen it drag. I had seen the second hand strain and hang and I do not, whatever else is wrong with me, imagine the behaviour of instruments; it is the one thing in the world I can be trusted to report.

I got the compass out. My hands were not quite steady and I noticed that they were not steady and noted it, filed it, the way I note oxidation, the way I note a crack in a guard, because when I am truly afraid I stop feeling and start cataloguing, and I was starting to catalogue. Compass. Prismatic, a good one, oil-damped, the needle floating on its jewel in the fluid so that it settles fast and true and holds. I laid it flat on the rock in the centre of the saddle and I let it settle and it did not settle. The needle swung to north and sat, and then drifted, slow, a smooth continuous drift off north with nothing to drive it, no iron near it, no current, the whole headland bare pale stone. It drifted through east and south and west and back to north and past it, going round, not spinning wild the way a compass spins near a magnet but turning slowly and evenly and continuously. It went once round in the time it took me to draw and let out four counted breaths, as if the north it was looking for were somewhere below the rock, and moving.

I knelt over it and watched it go round. I did not touch it. Behind me the sea went on and the kestrel hung and the light did not change, and in front of me on a slab of warm rock my father's watch kept perfect time beside a dead man's meter reading a light that was not there. A compass turned in slow circles pointing at a north that was under the world. And I understood, kneeling there, in the exact language I trust, in condition and behaviour and instrument, that I had walked out onto a headland and laid my kit down on the lid of something.

---

Here is where I have to tell you the truth about what I felt, and the truth is not creditable. I have said I catalogue when I am afraid, and I was afraid; my hands had gone to inventory and my pulse was up and there was a coldness climbing my spine that had nothing to do with the warm evening. All of that is true. But under it, below the fear, in the place where the appetite lives, the place that is happy over a dead man's handwriting, there was a thing I can only call exultation, and it frightened me more than the compass did, because I recognised it and I knew it would not let me leave. Fenn had felt this. I understood that, kneeling on the rock; whatever else the man had been, a fraud or a fool or a lonely liar priced into his own grave, he had knelt somewhere like this over instruments behaving like these and felt the whole solid world turn treacherous under his hands, the way sound rock rings wrong under the hammer the moment before it gives, and he had not walked away. Eleven times he had not walked away. And I knelt on the twelfth headland of my life, or the first, over the same impossible readings, feeling the same forbidden joy, and I knew myself for the first time completely, and did not like the knowledge, and did not, God forgive me, put down the compass and run.

I did the opposite. I reached for the camera.

I lifted it and I put my eye to it, and the meter needle was still pegged hard against its pin, reading the flood. I overrode it, which is the whole reason you carry a manual camera and the whole reason my father would trust nothing else, because a manual camera lets you tell it the truth when its own eye is lying. I set the exposure off the handheld reading I had taken before the meters went mad, off the real light, the light I could see, and I framed the empty saddle of rock with the sea beyond it and the going-down sun laying a track across the water. And I put my finger on the shutter, and I did not press it.

I want to be exact about why, because I have gone over this more than anything, this one held breath with my finger on the release, and I do not fully understand it and I will not pretend to. Part of it was the ordinary superstition of the frightened, that to photograph the thing was to provoke it. But that was not the main part. The main part was smaller and stranger and truer, and it was this: that if I pressed the shutter I would use the frame, and once I had used the frame I would want to see the frame. And to see it I would have to leave the headland and find a darkroom in some town two aeroplanes from home and develop the roll, and I could not make myself leave the headland. That was the whole of it. I had walked to the end of the land and found the lid of something and I flinched from going off to get the picture developed, because getting the picture developed happened somewhere else and I could not bear, in that moment, with the compass turning at my knee, to be somewhere else. I took my finger off the shutter. I never pressed it. And the frame counter stayed on one, one frame, the whole rest of that undeveloped roll still waiting behind the gate. And I have wondered ever since whether, if I had pressed it, if I had spent the frame and gone down the headland to find a darkroom, I would have been off the rock by nightfall and in a hotel by dark and on an aeroplane home inside a week. A woman with a strange photograph and a strange story and a whole ordinary life still in front of her. The frame I did not take is one of the doors I did not go through. There are not many of them and I have counted them all.

Instead I did a small thing, and it is the ugliest thing in this whole account and I put it here on purpose, in the quiet part, so you will believe the rest. I got hungry. There, on the lid of the impossible, with the compass going round and the light lying about itself and my father's watch straining against the shape of time, I became suddenly and stupidly aware that I had eaten nothing since the bread at the crossroads. I sat back on my heels and thought about the bread, the good open crumb of it, and wished I had bought a second loaf for the walk. And that was the thought, that was the whole thought, *I should have bought bread,* while the fabric of everything I believed about the world came quietly apart on a slab of rock in front of me. The body does not care what you have found. The body wants bread. I have come to think that is the most human sentence I know, and it arrived at the worst possible moment, and it left without apologising, the way they do.

---

The light began to go at last. Not on the rock; the rock held its false steady brightness. But the true light, the honest going-down light off the sea, began to redden and lower the way an evening does. I became aware that I had knelt on that headland for the better part of an hour and that if I meant to get off it before real dark I had to move. And everything in me refused, and I made myself gather the kit, which is when the last of it happened, the small one, the one I have never told anyone in the plain words, so I will tell you.

I reached for the watch to put it back on my wrist. And as my hand closed over it, for no reason, uninvited, from thirty years down and two aeroplanes away, I heard the *click.* His glasses. The little plastic sound of the arms folding in against the frame, the sound that lived below language in the body of me, the sound the drawer three hours south of an English coast would never make again. It came up out of nowhere, in the hot thyme-smelling dark-coming air of a foreign headland, so clear I turned my head, actually turned my head to the empty rock as though he were sitting there having just finished the page. And there was no one, there was the kestrel gone now and the sea going purple and my own kit laid out in its ritual order on the stone. And I knelt there with the running watch in my closed hand and my dead father's most ordinary sound in my ear and I did not weep, because I do not, but my hand would not open. I want to be accurate. My hand simply would not open to put the watch on. I knelt over my own kit on the lid of the world and could not make my fingers let go of a cheap steel watch, and I stayed like that longer than I will admit, until the sound faded the way it always fades, uncommented, gone, and my hand came back to me, and I put the watch on my wrist and did up the strap.

I packed the kit in reverse order, which is the other half of the ritual, camera and meter and compass and last of all the notebook. I did not tear out the page with the date on it, I closed the book on it with the pencil marking the place, and I stood up in the near-dark on legs gone stiff from kneeling. The compass, when I lifted it last, had stopped turning. It sat true on north, oil-damped, honest, a good instrument doing its one job, as if it had never done anything else, as if I had dreamed the hour. The watch kept perfect time. The meters, when I would look at them later in a hotel room with the lamp on, would both read true, would both work flawlessly for years, would both outlive far more than they should. Every instrument I carried onto that headland worked perfectly before I reached it and worked perfectly after I left it, and I have never had one of them examined, because I know what an examination would say. It would say there is nothing wrong with them. And there was nothing wrong with them. That was the entire horror of it, standing on the darkening rock: not that my instruments had failed, but that they had, for one hour, all together, told me the truth.

I walked back down the spine of the headland in the last of the light with the sea black on both sides of me and the lights of the town coming on far off along the coast, small and warm and human. I did not run, and I did not look back at the saddle, and I told myself as I walked all the sensible things, the way I had nodded at all the sensible things in the weeks after my father died and done the opposite. I told myself sunspots, I told myself a magnetic anomaly in the rock, iron ore, a seam of something, a local field strong enough to swing a compass and, who knew, to fool a selenium cell, that these things happen, that the world is full of ordinary strangeness for which we have not yet found the ordinary name. I have always had a gift for the reasonable explanation. But I did not believe it, walking down in the dark, and the proof that I did not believe it is the thing I did when I reached the car.

I did not drive to a hotel. I sat in the small white hire car at the top of the headland with the door open and the interior light on and the moths coming in, and I got the notebook out again, and I opened it to the page with the date at its head, and under the date, under the place and the time, in the pencil bound to the spiral, in the steady hand that is the only steady thing about me, I began to write down exactly what the instruments had done. Meter, pegged, both, incident and reflected. Watch, retardation then acceleration, self-correcting, approx. duration unknown. Compass, continuous rotation, period approx. four respirations, no local iron observed. I wrote it the way I would write the condition report of an object I meant to come back to, because that is what it was, and because a person who writes a condition report is a person who intends to return to the object, and I knew, writing it, in the plain cold language I trust more than my own heart, that I was going to come back to the headland, and that I was going to step into the saddle, and that the only real question left, the one I did not write down because writing it would have made it true too soon, was when.

I closed the book on the pencil. The moths turned round the little light. Out past the headland the sea went on doing the one enormous thing it does, and somewhere under the pale rock at the end of the land a north that was not on any of my instruments went on turning, patient, indifferent, waiting to see whether I was the sort of person who could leave a thing at the interesting part.

We both already knew that I was not.
