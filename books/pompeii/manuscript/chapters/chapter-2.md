The shop was called Verrall & Son, and there was no son, and by the time I understood why that mattered I was already three hours up the coast with a dead man's map in my bag and no good reason to be anywhere.

I had driven since first light. The road ran along the top of the cliffs for the last hour, the sea flat and pewter on my left and the fields going by grey-green on my right, and I passed through three towns that were the same town and pulled up at last in the fourth, which was smaller and older and had the particular closed look of a place that had once expected to be a resort and had made its peace with not being one. The tide was out. The front smelled of weed and diesel and the specific cold that comes off the North Sea in a spring that has not yet decided to be spring. I found the shop halfway up a side street that climbed away from the water, wedged between a shut café and a chapel that had become a carpet showroom, and I sat in the car for a while looking at it before I went in, because I am a person who reads the caption before she looks at the picture, and I wanted to know what I was walking into.

The window told me nothing I could use. A brass carriage clock stopped at twenty past four. A regimental drum. A shallow tray of medals gone the colour of old teeth. And, propped at the back where the light did not reach it, a barometer in a broken case, its needle fixed forever on *Change*, which I noticed and thought nothing of, because I did not yet believe that objects commented on the lives passing them. I have had a great deal of time since to revise that opinion, and I have not revised it, and I want that understood before I tell you the rest: I do not think the barometer knew anything. I think I was a frightened woman looking for a shape to put my fear in, and the shop was full of shapes.

The bell over the door was the old kind, an actual bell on an actual spring, and it announced me the way such bells do, gracelessly, so that whatever composure I had brought in came apart on the threshold.

"With you in a moment," a voice said, from somewhere in the back, over the top of a wireless playing the shipping forecast at a volume meant for one man alone. *Dogger, Fisher, German Bight.* I stood among the furniture of other people's deaths and waited, and I catalogued, because it is what I do in a room I cannot control. Provenance was the word for what the place had none of. A Regency card table with a Victorian repair. A ship's chronometer that had been civilian for a hundred years pretending to a naval past it never had. Everything a little wrong, a little married from two histories, the honest muddle of a shop that bought what came through the door and asked few questions. It calmed me, which tells you something about me I would rather it did not.

He came out wiping his hands on a cloth, an old man, not frail but pared down, the way men go who have spent their lives lifting things heavier than themselves. Seventy, perhaps more. He had a face like a walnut and pale intelligent eyes and he looked at me the way a dealer looks at anyone who comes in without a clear intention to buy, which is to say kindly and with the shutter already half down.

"If it's the drum," he said, "the drum's spoken for."

"It isn't the drum."

"Then you're a rare visitor." He folded the cloth and set it on the counter, precisely, corners squared, and I liked him for it against my will, because a man who squares a cloth is a man who cannot leave a thing crooked, and I know that man; I shave in his mirror. "We don't get many just looking. Not up here. Looking's a town pastime and we're not quite a town."

I had rehearsed this in the car and the rehearsal deserted me, as rehearsals do, and so I did the thing I always do when the prepared words fail, which is reach for an object. I took the trade card out of my coat and laid it on the counter between us, foxed at the corners, my father's *Fenn?* written on the back in the hand I had spent a whole day learning not to weep at.

He looked at it without picking it up.

"That's an old card," he said. "That's twenty years old, that card. I stopped printing them when the printer died and I couldn't be bothered to find another." He turned it with one finger to read the back, and something moved behind the pale eyes, not surprise exactly; recognition doing its slow arithmetic. "Where'd you come by this?"

"My father's things. He died in April."

"I'm sorry to hear it." He said it the way people up there say it, flatly, meaning it precisely because he would not decorate it. "Was he a customer?"

"I don't think so. He came here once, last spring, I think. A tall man, thin, glasses. He'd have been asking what I'm asking."

"And what's that?"

I had come six hours to say the name and now that the moment was here I found I did not want to, the way you do not want to say a word aloud that you have only ever read, in case you have been saying it wrong your whole life inside your head. But I had driven up a cliff road to do exactly this, and I have never in my life been able to walk away from a thing I have started, and so I said it.

"August Fenn."

The shipping forecast went on in the back room. *Humber, Thames. Southerly, veering southwesterly, four or five.* The old man did not answer at once. He reached under the counter and brought out a pair of spectacles and put them on, and my chest did something I was not prepared for, because they were the wrong shape and the wrong colour and belonged to a stranger and it did not matter in the slightest; a man had put on his reading glasses in front of me and my whole body had lurched toward a drawer three hours south that would not open. I put my hand flat on the counter to steady it. He did not notice, or he was too courteous to.

"Now there's a name," he said, "I've not been asked for in a long while. And twice in two years, which up here is a rush." He looked at me properly now, the shutter up. "You favour him. Your father. Round the eyes."

"You do remember him, then."

"I remember everyone who asks me about Fenn. It's a short list." He took the glasses off, and folded them, one-handed, the arms in against the frame, and I heard the small sound they made and I have to tell you that I did not breathe for the length of it. "You'd best come through. I'll not tell it standing up. My hip won't have it."

---

The back room was where he lived, more or less, a paraffin heater and a kettle and a chair worn to the shape of him, and stock in tea-chests to the ceiling, and I sat where he pointed me, on a piano stool that had lost its piano, and he made two mugs of tea I did not want and put one in my hands anyway, because that too is a thing they do up there; the tea is not a courtesy, it is a structure, it gives the hands something to do while the mouth does something hard. There was a cut loaf on the chest by the kettle, gone stale in its paper, and some cold clerk in me sized it up and found it wanting, a poor shop loaf with the crust gone to leather, and I was privately appalled by it, there, in that room, while a stranger got ready to tell me about my father. There is a part of me that will not switch off, not for grief, not for anything. It appraises the bread at the graveside.

"August Fenn," he said, settling, "was a fraud. I'll say that first, so you don't go building him into something. I've watched people build him into something. Your father, God rest him, was well on the way."

"A fraud how?"

"The ordinary way. He called himself a treasure hunter. That's a fraud that's mostly a fraud on yourself; the marks are thin on the ground for it and the disappointment's your own to keep. He came up here after the war, took a damp house on the front, and he spent thirty years telling anyone who'd stand still that he was close." He blew on his tea. "Close to what changed. Some years it was a Roman wreck off the point. Some years it was a hoard in a barrow the ministry wouldn't let him dig. Toward the end it was..." He stopped. "Well. You'll have read the map."

"I've read the map."

"Then you know what it was toward the end." He said it without inflection, the way you report a diagnosis you have made your peace with. "A door. He'd got onto doors. I'll be honest with you, that's when I stopped having him in for tea. A man can be close to a wreck for thirty years and it's a hobby, it's a sadness, you feel for him. A man who's close to a door, and can't tell you what's on the other side of it, and looks at you sideways when you ask, that's a different thing, and I'm too old to have it in my back room."

"And then he vanished."

"And then he vanished." He set his mug down. "Autumn of '68. Didn't pay his milk, didn't pay me for a barometer he'd taken on tick, didn't tell his landlady, didn't take a coat, and it was October, mind, so a man who walks out without his coat is a man who either drowned himself off the point or thought he wasn't going far. They dragged for him. Nothing. No note that anyone found, though God knows the man left enough paper for ten notes. Just gone, the way people are gone, and the house sat, and eventually the county cleared it, and I bought the contents, because that's my trade and someone has to, and because I'll tell you the truth, I wanted to know."

"Know what?"

He looked at me. The pale eyes were not unkind but they had gone somewhere careful.

"Whether there was anything in it," he said. "After thirty years of a man telling you he's close, you'd like there to have been something, even a little something, even if it's only so the poor devil wasn't wrong his whole life. So I bought his boxes and I went through every page, and I'll tell you what I found." He held up one finger, the auctioneer's habit, the man who weighs a thing before he names its price. "Rubbish, mostly. Fever. The map you've got, and forty like it that were nonsense. Letters he wrote and never sent. And one thing, one single thing in thirty years of paper, that I could not explain and have not explained, and it's the reason I remember your father, because your father found it too, in about a minute, the way you're going to find it, because you've the same disease in you the pair of them had."

He got up, his hip audible, and went to a particular tea-chest as a man goes to a drawer he has gone to a thousand times, and I understood that he had shown this to himself on winter nights, alone, and I did not blame him, because I have a study three hours south full of things I go to that way.

---

What he brought out was a ledger.

Not the map; I had the map. A ledger, cloth-bound, the accounts of a small failing enterprise, and I did not understand at first why he was handing me a book of sums. Then he opened it to a page he did not have to search for, and I saw that Fenn had kept his madness the way a bookkeeper keeps a business, in double columns, dated, and my skin did the thing it does over a genuine artifact, the small lift at the back of the neck that thirty years in a basement has taught me not to distrust.

"He logged them," Verrall said. "The attempts. Like a man logging the tides. Read the left-hand column."

I read the left-hand column. It was dates. A column of dates running down three years, most of them crossed through with a single flat line, and beside each a word or two in the copperplate I already knew, the wedding-invitation hand that came down off its own confidence as it went, and I read: *4th. Nothing. Cloud.* And: *11th. Nothing. Stood an hour. Fool.* And, further down, no longer crossed through, the line drawn under instead: *4th of the month. It opened. God forgive me, it opened, and I went through, and here is where I am writing this.*

"Now the right-hand column," he said.

The right-hand column was money. Because of course it was; a man keeps a ledger to know what he can afford, and Fenn had priced his impossible thing the way you price a season's digging, tools and passage and the bribe of a landowner, and it was this, this idiot ordinary arithmetic of a man budgeting for a miracle, that convinced me of him more than any soaring note could have, because you do not do sums for a lie you are telling other people. You do sums for a thing you mean to actually pay for. I know the difference. I have paid for both.

"You see it," Verrall said, watching me. It was not a question.

"I see a man keeping accounts."

"You see a man keeping accounts for going through a door." He said it flat, and then, because he was honest, he said the rest. "And here's the part I hate, and here's the part your father seized on, and here's why I stopped inviting either of you for tea." He turned to the front of the ledger, to the flyleaf, where Fenn had written something in a steadier hand, earlier, when the confidence was still up. "Read that."

I read it.

*The map is honest,* it said. *I have checked it against the Admiralty survey and the depths are true and the coast is true and a boy could sail by it. The map is honest. It is the door that lies. The door showed me true country and then would not show me the way I came, and I have concluded that the two are not the same instrument at all; the map tells you where, and something else entirely decides when, and whether, and for whom. I have stopped trusting the door. I have not stopped believing it. A wiser man would find that a contradiction. I find it Tuesday.*

I read it four times. My father had trained me to it. And on the fourth reading I did the thing I had promised myself in the car I would not do, which was believe it a little, and I felt the belief arrive not as wonder but as a kind of vertigo, a floor going soft, and I set the ledger down on my knees very carefully, the way you set down something you have just realised is genuine and therefore no longer yours to be casual with.

"That," said Verrall, "is why the map's not for sale, and never was, and why I told the county it wasn't in the boxes when they asked was there anything of value. I don't hold with lying to the county as a rule. I made an exception. Because a map that's true is worth money and a map that's true and mad is worth nothing to anyone but a man who wants ruining, and I'd had thirty years of watching it ruin one man and I wasn't going to sell it to the next." He looked at the tube-shaped absence in my bag, though I had not taken the map out. "And then, of course, I did. Didn't I."

"You sold it to my father."

"I gave it to your father." The correction cost him something. "Last spring, near enough a year gone. Tall thin man, glasses, exactly as you say, and a way of reading a page that I recognised from Fenn himself, God help me, that lip-moving carefulness. He came in for the drum, would you believe, he actually came in for the drum, and he saw the ledger out on the counter because I'd had it out, I have it out more than I should, and he read one page and went white and quiet and sat down on that stool you're sitting on and didn't get up for two hours."

I looked down at the stool I was sitting on. I have sat in many places since that I would give a great deal to have never sat, and that stool is on the list, and I did not move. For one lunatic moment I wanted to apologise to it, the way you do to a thing that has held more than its share, and I understood even as I felt it how far past sense I had already gone.

"He asked me the same as you'll ask me," Verrall went on. "Was it real. And I told him what I'll tell you, which is that I'm a dealer and not a fool and I don't know, and that's the honest answer and it's no use to anyone. And he asked could he take the map, and I said no, same as I've said no for years, and he came back three times, three separate times, drove up here from wherever it was you say, and the third time he didn't ask about the map at all. He asked about Fenn. Where Fenn had gone. When. He'd got a notion in him by then." Verrall's mouth went tight. "And I gave him the thing eventually, the map, because I saw he'd have it in the end one way or another and I'd rather it went to a man who'd read it careful than left in a box for the next clearance. I've been sorry since. When I heard he'd died I was sorrier still. I thought, there, that's the map done its work again, that's another one."

I drank the tea then. It was cold and over-stewed and tasted of the paraffin and I drank all of it, because my hands needed the doing of something and there was nothing else in that room to do.

The paraffin heater ticked. The shipping forecast had ended; the wireless in the front murmured something else now, a phone-in, a stranger's grievance about a roundabout. And I sat holding a mad ledger in a cold back room and understood, for the first time entirely, that my father in the last spring of his life had been driving up a cliff road to sit on this stool and go white over the accounts of a vanished man, and had told me nothing, and had underlined *START THE LIST* twice at the end, and that the list was this, and that I had, without meaning to, without believing a word of it, taken up the exact route he died on.

"He'd have taken you," I said. I had not meant to say it aloud. "If he'd lived. He'd have taken me up here and shown me this and I'd have told him he was being ridiculous."

"Would you," Verrall said.

"No," I said. "That's the trouble. I'd have told him he was being ridiculous and then I'd have copied the coordinates into the back of my notebook while he wasn't looking."

He gave a short laugh that had no cruelty in it. "Aye," he said. "There it is. The disease. I told you you had it."

I asked him the questions then, the sensible ones, the ones I had driven up to ask before the ledger had made sense a thing I could not afford to have make sense. Whether Fenn had family. He had not; a man who leaves without a coat leaves without a mourner too, mostly. Whether the police file had anything. It had a photograph and a height and a closed conclusion. Whether anyone else had come asking in the thirty years between. One woman, once, in the seventies, whom Verrall thought a medium and threw out. And my father. And me. Three people in thirty years had wanted to know what became of August Fenn, and one of them was dead, and one of them was me.

"Why'd you keep it out," I said. "The ledger. If it's ruined two men and a business you keep it out on the counter."

He considered that with the seriousness it did not deserve and entirely did.

"Because I'm eighty-one," he said, "and there's no son, whatever the sign says. I put *and Son* on the sign in nineteen fifty-nine because a shop wants a future in its name to get credit, and then the future didn't arrive, we weren't given one, my wife and I, and I never took it down because by then it was cheaper to be a liar than to repaint. And a man with no son and no future in his name gets to my age and he finds he doesn't mind so much whether the door's real. He minds whether it's there. You'll not understand that yet. Come back at eighty and you will." He drank his tea. "Fenn believed there was a way out of the one life you're given into another one. I don't believe it. But I'd not throw the ledger on the fire, and I've had thirty years and a cold enough grate to do it in, and I think about that, that I've not done it, more than I think it's healthy to."

I have thought about that speech more than almost anything a stranger ever said to me. I have thought about it in rooms he could not have imagined, under a sky he would have called a liar, holding children he will never know were born. *He minds whether it's there.* Verrall was the last person from my own century to say a wise thing to me and I did not know it, and I would not have valued it if I had, because I was thirty-four years old and I still thought wisdom was a thing you collected, like slides, like anything, and stacked in a tower, and kept.

"I'd like to see the photograph," I said. "The one from the file. If you have a copy."

I did not know why I wanted it. I have learned to trust the wants I cannot explain; they are the only part of me that ever knew where I was going. He got up again and went to the tea-chest and came back with a manila envelope soft as cloth with handling, and he did not open it, he handed it to me and let me, which I understood was a kindness and a test both, and I undid the flap and slid out a single photograph, a police copy of a copy, grey and grained.

It was Fenn.

He stood on a shingle beach in a heavy coat with the point behind him, a big man run to stringiness, hatless, his hair blown flat, and he was not looking at the camera; he was looking off down the beach at something out of frame with an expression I knew, that I had seen that very week held up to a dying window in another man's slides, the expression of a person looking at a thing they intend to reach. And under the photograph, in a clerk's careful hand, not Fenn's, the file's own summary, the height and the eyes and the distinguishing marks and the age.

*Age at disappearance: 34.*

I sat with it a while. Verrall let me. The heater ticked and the phone-in murmured its small modern complaint and outside the tide was coming back over the flats, and I looked at a photograph of a man who had walked off a beach without his coat when he was exactly the age I was now, and who had logged the fact of a door in a bookkeeper's hand and priced his way through it and never come back. I did the arithmetic I would spend the next nine years refusing to do, and did not yet know that I was refusing it, and I felt the cold come up through the soles of my feet as if the tide had reached the shop.

"Thirty-four," I said.

"About that," said Verrall. "Younger than you'd think, for a man who'd been close to things for thirty years. He started young. Started at your age, near enough, I should think, whatever age that is; I'll not ask a lady." He took the photograph back, gently, and slid it into its soft envelope, and put the envelope away, and the ledger after it, into the tea-chest, into the dark, where he went to visit them, and where, I understood, they would stay when he was gone, until the next clearance, the way everything up there was always one death from a clearance.

He gave me a copy of the flyleaf line before I left. He wrote it out for me himself, in his own square hand, on the back of a shop receipt, *the map is honest, it is the door that lies,* and I have it still, I had it still, it went into the field notebook with my father's coordinates and it was one of the things I carried across, though I was told to carry nothing, and I will tell you before the end what became of it. He did not charge me for the tea or the writing or the two hours of a lonely man's afternoon. He walked me to the door and the graceless bell rang me out and he stood on the step in the cold with his cardigan buttoned wrong and said the last thing he ever said to me, which I have kept.

"If you find him," he said, "or find what became of him. You'll not come back and tell me, will you. Nobody ever comes back and tells me. I've made my peace with that. But I'd have liked to know." He looked out at the returning sea. "It's the only thing I ever wanted from any of you. Just to be told, at the end, whether there was anything in it."

I said I would come back and tell him.

I meant it when I said it, and so little of what I said in my old life was meant. I got in the car and drove back down the cliff road with the ledger's arithmetic in my head and a receipt in my pocket and a photograph of a thirty-four-year-old ghost behind my eyes, and I did not go home. It was the last honest choice I made as the woman I had been. There was a turning, south of the third town, where the road forked, one way inland toward the motorway and my father's cold study and the drawer I could not open and the ordinary grief that was waiting there for me with its casseroles and its black veil, and the other way on down the coast, toward a set of coordinates I had copied into a notebook while telling myself I was only tidying.

I took the coast road. I told myself it was to think. I have always been able to tell myself anything.

I never did go back and tell Verrall. He is one of the debts I carry, and there are heavier ones now, God knows, there are debts on the other side of this that would make an old dealer's disappointment look like the small change it is. But I think of him sometimes in the last dark, buttoning his cardigan wrong on a cold step, wanting only to be told at the end whether there was anything in it. And I want to answer him, across the whole impossible distance, in the one language he would have trusted, which is the plain one.

There was, Mr. Verrall. There was something in it. I am so sorry. It took the same from me it took from Fenn, and I would not undo a day of it, and both of those are true, and I never came back, and I am telling you now, too late, in the dark, the way we tell each other everything that matters.
