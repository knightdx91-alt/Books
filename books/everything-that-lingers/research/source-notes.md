## Original premise (author's handwritten seed — kept verbatim)

John Maxwell runs a bed and breakfast. He's always been able to see ghosts, so when his wife died it
was no surprise her ghost stayed with him. His wife had never truly believed him when he told her he
could see ghosts, but once she was dead she obviously believed him. There is a wraith or some other
entity not only draining energy from the living but the dead as well. So when his dead wife's ghost
disappears, he goes hunting. He has put other ghosts to rest before, so he thinks he knows what to
do, but this is something he has never encountered before.

## LOCKED STORY DECISIONS (author, 2026-07-13)

**Genre / positioning:** Supernatural horror — grief-driven, "elevated but accessible." Standalone.
~80,000 words. Comps: *The Sun Down Motel* (Simone St. James), *A Head Full of Ghosts* (Paul
Tremblay), *The Hollow Places* (T. Kingfisher). Note (market): a middle-aged widower lead puts this
in the quiet/literary-horror lane rather than the female-led haunted-place lane — a craft standout
more than a mass-market one; that's an accepted trade for the emotional core.

1. **The drive — STOP it, not rescue her.** The wife's ghost is devoured and gone for good. John
   isn't trying to get her back; he's trying to stop the thing from taking anyone else. This makes
   the real arc a **letting-go story**: the man who could never let his dead move on (his whole life
   is keeping ghosts) must finally let *her* go.

2. **It's REAL — no unreliable-narrator ambiguity.** The sight is real, the wraith is real. Scares
   come from the *rules* and the dread, not from "is he cracking up." We commit to a coherent
   supernatural cosmology.

3. **The antagonist — "the Starved Dead."** It was once a ghost like any other — but instead of
   moving on, it discovered it could feed on other spirits to sustain itself, and over years it grew
   monstrous and crossed over to draining the living too. **It is a dark mirror of John:** a ghost
   that refused to let go and clung until clinging became hunger. It is the thing John could become.
   His normal method (resolve the unfinished business, release the ghost) *almost* works — same laws,
   perverted and stronger — which is scarier than methods that don't apply at all.

4. **The partner — a ghost-hunter who SENSES but can't SEE. Platonic found-family, NO romance.**
   She's the professional (reputation, method, equipment, and a history of hunting this thing from
   the outside); she works off cold spots, dread, direction — but she's essentially *blind*. John is
   the amateur (no framework, self-taught, alone for decades) but the *true seer*. He's her eyes; she's
   his training; neither is complete alone. Built-in friction (she can't verify what she can't see; he's
   never worked with anyone). She brings the wraith's history — it has eaten before, elsewhere, which is
   why she's hunting it. **Handle the relationship as platonic / found-family** — he's a fresh widower;
   no romance in this book.

5. **Origin — LOCAL.** The Starved Dead was a specific dead person tied to John's own house/town.
   Consequence: the **investigation and the weapon are the same thing** — you don't out-muscle a
   soul-eater; you uncover *who it was and why it clung*, and resolving that human wound is how you
   unmake it. The mystery of the house is the plot engine. Awful irony: the home he and his wife built,
   his livelihood, the place his guests sleep, **is the lair** — he's been living on top of it, and
   unknowingly feeding it, for years.

6. **Scope — STANDALONE.** One man, one wraith, one grief, a contained gut-punch. (A seer + a sensing
   hunter is an obvious series team, but this book is built to end.)

## THE THREE LOAD-BEARING MECHANICS — they collapse into ONE climax

The clock creates the trap, the cost is the wife, and the wife's payoff *is* the cost. Build them
as a single climactic beat.

### The clock — his own grief set the table
The wraith has been here for years, feeding *lightly* and unnoticed (the guest who "just didn't sleep
well," the local who quietly wasted away). What woke and accelerated it is **the wife's death**: a
fresh, strong, beloved, lingering spirit was a feast, and eating her tipped it from slow parasite to
active hunter. So the horror is recursive and personal — John, the seer, unknowingly *fed* it the one
soul that mattered; his grief is what made it dangerous.
**Hard deadline:** as it eats it crosses over further, toward a **point of no return where there is
not enough *person* left in it to reach** — it becomes pure, unmakeable hunger. Every soul it takes
burns down the last shred of its humanity, and that shred is the only handle anyone has on it. Stop it
before it stops being someone. Bodies rise (guests, townsfolk, the town's own dead going missing); it
circles **John** — the one soul that can see it is either the threat it wants gone or the ultimate meal.

### The cost — the sight was the flaw
His normal method fails because this thing chose hunger over rest and does not *want* release. To
release something that refuses release, John must do the one thing his gift is *for*: **see** it —
force it to remember it was a person, confront the grief that made it cling, so that in the moment of
being truly seen it can finally let go. But John has spent his life *keeping* his dead; he cannot teach
a soul to let go while he still clings to his wife. **So the cost is: to unmake it, he must let her go
first — release her echo, by his own will, permanently.** The monster can only be *released*, not
killed, and he has to prove he knows how by doing it to her.
**Resonant consequence — his sight goes quiet.** Not burned out as punishment: the gift and the flaw
were always the same thing. He saw the dead because he could never let them go; once he does, he stops
needing to hold them, and the seeing stops. He ends the book **able to live among the living for the
first time** — a gain wearing the clothes of a loss.

### The wife's payoff — the monster wears her; the real her gives permission
At the climax the Starved Dead uses what it ate: it puts on her face and voice and **begs** — offers
John the illusion of keeping her if he'll let it live (keep feeding, become complicit, become the thing
himself). That's the temptation, and it's the whole theme in one image: *keep your dead and rot, or let
go and live.* Then the one true beat — the real fragment of her surfaces and **tells him to do it, to
let her go, to stop it.** This closes the core irony: *she never believed his sight in life; in death
she finally trusts it enough to let him end her.* She gives permission. **Goodbye, not rescue.** He
releases her, and with her the thing that wore her; the sight goes quiet; he is free and bereft in the
same breath.
→ Temptation, permission, letting-go, and cost are **one beat**. That is the standalone ending.

## KEEP-IT-SIMPLE GUARDRAILS (do not violate in the architect pass)
- **The wife's death is NATURAL** (illness/accident) — **NOT** the wraith. The tragedy is purer and
  crueler if the thing didn't kill her; it just ate what love left behind. The moment the wraith
  murders her, this becomes a revenge thriller and the grief is lost. Resist it.
- **No unreliable-narrator game** — it's real (see decision #2).
- **No romance** — platonic found-family (see decision #4).
- **Standalone** — build the ending to close; do not leave series hooks (see decision #6).
- **Investigation = weapon** — the middle is uncovering *who the Starved Dead was*, not researching a
  generic monster; the human wound behind it IS the method of stopping it (see decision #5).
