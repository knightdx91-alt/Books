## 1. THE DEVOURER — adult-romantasy superfan

*Reads fast for the couple, the slow burn, the dangerous-devoted lead, the heat, momentum. Judges by shelf expectations: Bishop, spicy romantasy.*

Third time and the front third still owns me, and this pass I got the one thing I'd been begging for since round 1: a **mid-book couple beat**. The Ch.19 pre-dawn shieling open — her cold hand flat on his heart "not to gentle it, only to feel it go," and "I've had the alone. I'm not trading my cage for a man who builds himself one out of love and calls that different. Take the term out of the sum" — that's *my* scene, that's the intimacy-in-the-quiet-hour beat the middle was missing, and it's earned and it's devastating and it made the eventual separation hurt in the good way instead of the frustrating way. It's not a heat scene and it doesn't need to be. It's *couple time*, and there wasn't any between Ch.18 and Ch.26 before, and now there's a real one.

And Ch.26 is still the best spice in the book — the inversion where *she* runs the whole scene pays off Ch.18 instead of repeating it. Nobody's getting bait-and-switched on heat.

My round-1/2 gripe is still there but it's smaller now: the couple's still apart Ch.21–26, still only two on-page scenes, both back-loaded. But the Ch.19 beat took a real bite out of "the middle runs on lore and yearning" — the middle now has one scene of *them*, together, wanting the same thing out loud, before it all comes apart. That's the lever I asked for two rounds running and somebody finally pulled it. The ending's still bittersweet-not-HEA (throne / mews), but the Ch.29 mews scene does the physical-togetherness work and I've made my peace with it.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.21–26 — couple still separated for a fifth of the book.** The Ch.19 beat helps a lot, but the structural separation (dragon alone / war-road alone / Kell near-miss) is intact. Still the ceiling.
2. **Ch.18 & Ch.26 remain the only two explicit beats, both late.** Cadence unchanged; the Ch.19 beat is warmth, not heat, so the spicy-shelf "long dry middle" note stands, softened.
3. **Ch.29 close — together in the *scene*, not the same *life* (throne / mews).** Same as R2; a Devourer wants claimed-and-together on the last page.

**Verdict:** Buy. **8.5/10** (R2: 8, **+0.5**). The mid-book beat is exactly the fix I asked for; it's off a 9 only on the structural separation and the two-scene cadence.

## 2. THE CRITIC — developmental editor / agent repping adult romantasy & fantasy

*Structure, originality, prose discipline, market position. Judged against adult-romantasy comps.*

Still the most accomplished manuscript I've had across my desk in a long time, and this round it fixed the thing I'd have hammered hardest in an acquisitions meeting. **The plot-timed power-limit is gone.** In R1/R2 Amelia "couldn't" save her mother and Maren via a limit that appeared for the first time exactly when two deaths were required, then evaporated to save a stranger the same chapter — a plot demanding casualties and calling it a law. That's now been rebuilt into a *skill/experience gap*, and crucially it's **seeded backward**: Ch.20's child-save establishes her power *exceeds* the law ("the law had not been the edge of her power… a fence her mother built well short of the edge"); Ch.24's six hearts is "a small precise motion… a girl learning a craft"; so when Ch.28 lands "The power was equal to it. She was not. Not yet," it reads as the arc paying off, not a rule invented on the page. And it does real thematic work — the court feared what she could *do*; the horror at the end is "not the harm she could do, but the good she could not, yet." That's a genuine upgrade. Letting Selwyn die closes the death-fake-out at the same stroke.

Two things still to raise, and they're the same two, both softened, neither closed.

**First — the narrated-thesis tic + "did the sum" cross-POV cadence is *reduced again but still the dominant signature.*** The de-tic pass keeps helping (Ch.27 now ends on a pure image — "And Amelia put back her hood"). But the scene-end meaning-statement is still how a large share of beats close ("the whole of what she had left to hold," Ch.28; "choosing… each other," Ch.29), and *"did the sum / the arithmetic"* is still in both POVs and thick in the changed chapters — Ch.19 alone runs "I did the sum on it," "Take the term out of the sum," "I've been doing your arithmetic," "the sum," and Ch.29 pairs "she had done the sum" with the "both are true and they don't cancel" payoff. The team's defense (Korvan *consciously inherits* Amelia's counting) is real and signposted, and it largely holds on a close read — but a fast reader still hears one narrator. Softer than R2. Not gone.

**Second — positioning. Unchanged and authorial.** Lyrical literary-political epic fantasy with two explicit scenes, pitched as spicy romantasy; romance is a B-plot by architecture. The new Ch.19 beat pulls a little couple-weight forward but doesn't move the spine. Decide which book it is and position honestly.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Passim — narrated-thesis endings + "did the sum" cadence.** Reduced, still dominant, thick in Ch.19/29.
2. **Positioning (Ch.19–26).** Romance-as-B-plot + literary register vs. the romantasy/Bishop pitch. Open, authorial.
3. **Ch.20 ↔ Ch.28 — the skill-gap seam is *almost* seamless.** The child's leg-bleed in Ch.20 holds "easily… about as much as breathing"; Selwyn's is "wide and fast." The distinction (scale + speed of loss, not domain) is drawn, but a sharp reader can still feel the line falling exactly where the plot needs it. One more early beat of a bleed she *can't* catch in time would make it airtight.

**Verdict:** Maybe → revise, closer still. **7/10** as pitched (8.5 as literary epic fantasy; R2: 6.5, **+0.5**). The power-limit repair is what moved it; the tic and positioning are what's left.

## 3. THE HOSTILE READER — predisposed to dislike it; hunts plot holes, convenience, main-character shield

*Came back with the red pen. My two loudest unfixed catches from R2 both got fixed. I'm annoyed about it.*

Fine. Credit where it's earned, twice over.

**The Selwyn death-fake-out is dead, because Selwyn is dead.** In R1/R2 the whole moral cost of the climax — Korvan holding still while his one friend drowns reaching up a hand (Ch.27) — got rented back twenty pages later when the Queen-to-be conveniently knelt in the exact ford and saved him. You can't have the gut-punch *and* the rescue. Now she kneels, she reaches, "the power came… enormous," and **he dies anyway** — "the boy went out under her hands regardless" (Ch.29). The cost stands. The main-character shield is off the fool. That was my single loudest unfixed item across two rounds and it's genuinely gone.

**And the plot-timed power-limit is repaired at the root.** It's no longer an on/off "too open to hold" limit that flips exactly when two deaths are needed. It's a skill gap — vast power, no craft-hours — and it's planted earlier (Ch.20: power exceeds the law; Ch.24: "a girl learning a craft"). So the rule that killed her mother and Maren is the *same* rule that fails Selwyn (a wide fast bleed she can command but not yet catch in time), and it doesn't get contradicted a page later — she reaches for Selwyn with the same too-slow craft and *loses him too*. Consistent. I can't call it a cheat anymore.

What I still won't let go: **convenience still ferries the cast to the exact right spot.** Maren walks a country and — now — turns up at the Queen's shoulder at the top of the one hill (Ch.28); it's *explained* this round ("she had spent a life walking toward the thing she was most afraid of when someone she loved was inside it"), which is a good line, but it's still the warmest character teleporting into the kill-box on cue. The chief still materializes exactly where Korvan is (Ch.21, Ch.22). And Amelia still happens to work down the *exact* shelf where Selwyn is bleeding — the death now defuses the shield, but the geography's still convenient. These are smaller than what got fixed, but they're the residue.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.28 — Maren's convergence.** Explained but still a country-length walk into the precise kill-box at the precise minute.
2. **Ch.21/22 — the chief keeps appearing exactly where Korvan is.** Unaddressed since R1.
3. **Ch.20 ↔ Ch.28 skill-gap seam** — I'll grant it's seeded now, but the child in Ch.20 bleeds slow and lives "easily," Selwyn bleeds fast and dies; the line between "catchable" and "not" is drawn precisely at the plot's need. Close, not airtight.

**Verdict:** Put back — but grudging, and upgraded again, because my two biggest holes are both closed. **7/10** (R2: 6, **+1.0**).

## 4. THE CASUAL READER — low investment, will put it down if bored or confused

*Picked it up again. Better, with the same two soft spots.*

The opening still hooks me and any chapter with the two of them I read fast — and there's *one more of those now*, the shieling morning early in the back half (Ch.19), which I liked, because it's just the two of them being a couple for a page before it all goes wrong, and I don't get many of those.

The thing that gutted me and I didn't see coming: **the friend dies.** I'd half-braced for a rescue and it didn't come, and honestly that landed harder *because* the book had been letting people scrape through. When she kneels in the water and the power comes and it *still* isn't enough — "The power was equal to it. She was not. Not yet." — I got it instantly, no re-reading. That's the clearest that rule has ever been for me.

Where I still drifted, same as both prior reads: the **court/politics chapters**. Ch.17 feels a touch tightened but it's still a long conversation among people I don't fully hold in my head, far from the couple, and I coasted through stretches of Ch.20 and Ch.23. And I *still* took the **whale-iron "reach"** rule (Ch.21) on faith — it pays off big in Ch.27 (the "short… she'd had the reach of it from four seconds of looking" callback is satisfying once you're there), but the first time it's asserted more than shown, and the whole Ch.27 climax leans on it.

One nag left over: people still say **"a year"** as a figure of speech — "not ten words of him in a year" (Ch.25), "A year of having no word for you" (Ch.26), "one word in a year" (Ch.11) — and since the real span is a couple of months, those made me blink once or twice, same as last time. Small. It's the last splinter.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.17 (and stretches of 20/23) — court/politics still drags for me.** Lightly tightened, still the skim zone.
2. **Ch.21 — whale-iron "reach"** still asserted the first time; the whole Ch.27 escape rides a rule I took on trust.
3. **Residual "a year" idioms** (Ch.11, 25, 26) vs. the now-clear weeks span. Minor, unchanged.

**Verdict:** Maybe, leaning finish. **7/10** (R2: 7, **—**). Selwyn's death is the clearest beat in the book for me; the two soft spots are exactly where they were.

## 5. THE DEVOTED READER — emotionally invested in the couple, the cost, the ending

*Cared about all of them again. Reports what wrecked me and what I'll mourn.*

It hurt me the right way, again, and this time it took the one mercy I'd been holding onto. In R2 I *defended* Selwyn living — "the one mercy I got," the fool who gets to live in a book with so few survivals. He doesn't live now. And I want to be honest: I grieved that on the page, the loss of the survival I'd loved. But I also can't argue it's not better. His Ch.27 drowning — "he did not go to him," the dragon holding still while his one friend goes under reaching up a hand — is the whole moral cost of the climax, and letting him actually die means the cost is *paid*, not rented. And "Here's the thing about a downed one—" going unfinished, "the way the important things do," wrecked me clean. So I mourn the mercy and I bow to the choice.

Ch.28 is still the cruellest perfect page in the book: the Queen dying with her whole attention on Amelia, "you're always cold… I never could keep you warm," never once looking at Loric, his whole life's vengeance beneath her notice to the end — and Loric's grief cracking on "She never even—" and being buried mid-sentence. And Maren's button-count death, coming out even, "I do like it when they come out even," is the most beautiful death in the book.

What I still rage about, structural and unchanged: **the ending holds me at arm's length from the biggest grief.** She loses her mother and Maren in one iron-cast and "did not weep… there was nothing in her yet that could," and by the end of Ch.29 she still hasn't. Canon, I know — grief held inward. The Ch.29 mews scene comes closest: her voice "went, for the length of one word, thinner than she had told it to be," and I was grateful for that crack and I still wanted the full break.

And **Maren still shares the Queen's iron-cast** (Ch.28). Her death is gorgeous and now it's the *only* warmth-death that gets its own held moment (the buttons) — but it's still one blow, two losses, and the book's warmest character deserves her own stroke.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.28–29 — Amelia never breaks.** Emotionally true; still keeps the grief-reader outside the largest loss. (Softened a hair by the Ch.29 "thinner than she told it to be" beat.)
2. **Ch.28 — Maren shares the Queen's cast.** Beautiful, still a shared stroke.
3. **Selwyn's death** — I lost the one survival I loved. I concede it's the right call; I'm logging the cost, because the book now has almost no mercy left in it, and a devoted reader feels that absence at the close.

**Verdict:** Buy and press it on everyone, crying about the ending. **8.5/10** (R2: 8.5, **—**). The Selwyn death is better craft and a real emotional loss for me both at once; it nets to the same score, sadder.
