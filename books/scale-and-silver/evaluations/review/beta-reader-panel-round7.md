## 1. THE DEVOURER — adult-romantasy superfan

Not my lane, mostly — I read for the couple, and they didn't touch a single couple beat, which is the
first thing I checked. The garden's still there whole, the grain-cart hiding, the tarn, the reunion,
the claim. Good. What I *did* notice, and I'll be honest it surprised me: I got to the garden faster.
The first two chapters used to make me wait a beat longer than I wanted before the story picked me up,
and this time Amelia's decision to go down felt like it arrived sooner and the banquet didn't make me
work as hard to get through her taking in the crowd. I never skim the front of a book I love, but I
notice when a book stops *asking* me to, and this one stopped asking. Nothing I love is gone; it's the
same book with less throat-clearing at the top.

- **Ch.5** — the banquet reads quicker now; the "she was in the room / ached to be in a loud bright
  room" loop that used to say the same feeling three times is down to once, and the feeling's just as
  strong. Good trade.
- **Ch.2** — the new bit where the Queen tells her *holding a life in is the slow craft, strength
  stops, only craft catches* — I clocked that as a couple-relevant plant the second I hit Selwyn's
  death later. It makes the ending's worst beat land as *her failure earned*, not bad luck.
- **Ch.1** — Della calling her "lamb" is tiny but it's the exact warmth I want from the woman who
  raised her; she reads as a different person from Maren now, which she didn't quite before.

**Verdict:** Buy. **9/10** (R6: 9.5 → I settle back to **9**, and that is NOT a knock on the pass —
it's that the R6 yearning beat was a couple-heat bump and this pass is a craft-tightening pass, which
holds my number rather than raising it. The front reads faster; nothing I love was cut.)

## 2. THE CRITIC — developmental editor / agent repping adult romantasy & fantasy

**This is the pass I have been asking for since round one, and it's the one an outside reader (Grok)
named too, so I'll give it full credit.** For six rounds my two standing structural notes were (a) the
front act is over-narrated — the interiority is a feature the audience wants but the book *restates*
its emotional beats, which is a different and fixable problem — and (b) the gift's power sat too close
to convenient. Both are addressed here, and addressed correctly: at the *sentence* level, not by
gutting scenes.

The front-act cuts are the right cuts. I went looking for over-reach — a surgical pass in a writer's
own hands usually shaves muscle along with fat — and I did not find it. Every cut I checked (Ch.3's
age recap, Ch.4's tripled father/Selwyn/Loric triad, Ch.5's "she was in the room" loop, Ch.6's
re-done Loric-unease) removed a *second or third statement of a thing already shown*, which is exactly
the drift I named. The scenes are intact; the voice is intact; the book simply stops telling me twice.
And the discipline shown by **not** cutting Ch.7–9** — recognizing the exposure/war/escape as load-
bearing and leaving them — is the mark of someone editing with judgment rather than to a word target.

The gift-cost seed is the higher-value change. My "power sits too convenient" note has haunted this
book since the tarn (Ch.13, seven hearts, no apparent limit). Planting the *named, consistent* limit
in the Ch.2 ritual — strength is easy, craft is time, and she has none of the hours — retroactively
disciplines every use of the power that follows, and turns the Ch.28 Selwyn death from "the plot
needed him dead" into "the limit the book established in chapter two came due." That is how you kill a
deus-ex charge: not by weakening the power on the page it's used, but by pricing it in early. It's done.

Caution, per my standing note: the de-tic/restatement sanding is now genuinely at its floor. Two
passes back I said stop; this pass was the *right* additional cut because it targeted front-act
over-narration specifically and the Grok read corroborated it, but the manuscript has now given up all
the fat it has to give without becoming a colder book. **Stop cutting.**

**Verdict:** Yes. **8.5/10** as pitched (R5: 8.0, **+0.5**). The two structural notes I could still
name — over-narrated front, convenient-feeling gift — are both closed, and closed at the sentence
level without touching the architecture or the voice. The remaining item is the middle-book separation
*architecture*, which is authorial taste, not a defect.

## 3. THE HOSTILE READER — hunts plot holes, convenience, main-character shield

Grudging movement, and I want it minuted that it's grudging. My longest-standing *mechanical* gripe
was never really Maren's cross-country walk or the chief's homing instinct — those are convenient but
arguable. It was the **gift with no ceiling**: a girl who can stop seven trained men's hearts at a
lake with a turn of the hand, and then five more on a mountain, is a main-character cannon, and a
cannon with no stated limit is the definition of the shield I hunt. The Ch.2 seed doesn't remove the
cannon, but it bolts a governor onto it *before it fires*, in the character's own mother's mouth:
strength stops, only craft catches, and the girl hasn't the craft. That's a rule I can hold the book
to — and the book then *keeps* it, all the way to Selwyn dying under her hands because she's strong and
green. A power that fails at the exact moment it matters most is the opposite of a shield. Credit,
and I don't give it easily.

What's **unchanged** is unchanged, and I'll list it so no one thinks I've gone soft: Maren still
surfaces at the Queen's shoulder on the one hilltop (Ch.28); the chief still materializes exactly
where Korvan is (Ch.21/22); the whale-iron "reach" is still asserted from four seconds of looking
(Ch.21). None of that was in scope for a pacing pass and none of it moved. But the pass introduced
**no new hole** — I checked every cut for a severed antecedent (a "this"/"that" pointing at a deleted
sentence) and found none; the cuts are clean.

**Verdict:** Maybe, leaning yes. **8/10** (R5: 7.5, **+0.5**). The gift now has a governor seeded
early and honored late — that's a real answer to my oldest structural gripe. My *named* convenience
residue (convergence, chief homing, whale-iron) stands exactly where it did; a pacing pass was never
going to touch it.

## 4. THE CASUAL READER — low investment, will put it down if bored or confused

Better start for me, which is the part of the book that matters most to whether I finish. The first
few chapters used to be where I nearly set it down — a lot of a girl in a room thinking about being in
a room — and this time I got to the party and the garden with less of that. The banquet chapter (Ch.5)
in particular stopped circling; I felt like I was moving through the party *with* her instead of
watching her narrate the same feeling about it a few times. That's the difference between me finishing
the first fifty pages and me putting it on the nightstand "for later," which we both know means never.

The Della bit (she calls the girl "lamb") is small but it did the thing — I can now tell the two
servant women apart, which I genuinely couldn't a couple rounds ago; there was a warm-loyal-tragic one
and *another* warm-loyal-tragic one and I kept them straight by which chapter I was in. Now one's the
soft nurse and one's the dry one who counts. Helps.

My old splinters are where they were: the **court chapters** (Ch.17, stretches of 20/23) are still my
skim zone — this pass actually left Ch.17 alone on purpose and I understand why (it's the counting
woman's chapter and the counting is her), but it's still the stretch I speed through. And the "a year"
idioms (Ch.11, Ch.25) and the whale-iron rule are untouched. Those cap me. But the *front* got easier,
and the front is where you were losing me.

**Verdict:** Maybe, leaning finish. **8/10** (R5: 7.5, **+0.5**). The front act stopped asking me to
wade; that's the single most valuable thing you could have done for a reader like me. Court skim and
the "a year" splinters unchanged.

## 5. THE DEVOTED READER — invested in the couple, the cost, the ending

I came to this pass braced, because a "cut for pace" pass is where a book like this one usually loses
the exact quiet interiority I love it for — and I am relieved to report it didn't. I went to my
tenderest pages first (the garden, the grain-cart hand-on-the-board, the tarn, "the clan of one is now
two") and every one of them is whole. What got cut was the book saying a feeling a second time after
it had already landed it in the body — and Bishop's whole register is *land it in the body once and
trust it*, so the cuts actually move the prose **toward** the comp, not away from it.

Two things I loved this round. First, the Ch.2 seed. I have always found Selwyn's death the hardest
page in the book, and now the mother plants the reason for it in the second chapter — *strength stops,
only craft catches, and craft is time* — so that when Amelia kneels in the ford and cannot hold him,
it's the oldest promise in the book coming due, not a cruelty the plot arranged. That deepened a grief
I already carried. Second, Della. She's finally *herself* — the "lamb," the slow bolt, the bedside
voice — and because Ch.24 reaches back for exactly those, the reach lands harder now that I actually
heard her say them in Chapter One.

Nothing I love was flattened. The book is a hair leaner and a shade closer to its own voice comp.

**Verdict:** Buy, evangelize. **9.5/10** (R6: 9.5, **—**). Held at the top: the pass protected every
beat I read this book for and quietly strengthened the one death that hurts most. No couple-heat delta
(this wasn't that kind of pass), so no further rise — but no fall, which for a pacing pass is the win.
