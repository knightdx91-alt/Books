## 1. THE DEVOURER — adult-romantasy superfan

*Reads fast for the couple, the slow burn, the dangerous-devoted lead, the heat, momentum. Judges by shelf expectations: Bishop, spicy romantasy.*

Second time through and the front third still owns me — the garden (Ch.6), the grain-cart hand pressed to the plank (Ch.10, Ch.12), "we're a well-matched pair of monsters, which is rarer, and I'll take it" (Ch.13). And Ch.26 is even better than I remembered: the *inversion* of it, that in the shieling he leads and here she runs the whole scene — "I have wanted the sounds. You gave me none worth keeping the first time" — pays off Ch.18 instead of repeating it. That is a textbook second scene, and the deepthroat / held-down / then she strips and climbs on top and rides to her own finish before he turns her — that's the graphic register the ADULT shelf actually wants, and it's still deep-POV and emotionally loaded, not anatomy for its own sake. Nobody's going to feel bait-and-switched on *heat*.

But my honest gripe from round 1 is exactly where it was, because nobody moved it: **my couple is apart from Ch.21 to Ch.26.** Five chapters. He's a dragon flying alone, she's walking a war-road, and the Kell near-miss (Ch.25) is gorgeous and it is *also* the book choosing, on purpose, to keep them a mile apart in the dark one more time. I came for them *together*. And there are still only **two** on-page scenes, both back-loaded (Ch.18 ~62%, Ch.26 ~90%), with the whole middle running on lore and yearning. I wanted a mid-book beat and there still isn't one.

The ending still doesn't give me the same-room HEA my shelf trains me for — she's crowned, he's housed in the mews behind a wall, last image "alive on the two sides of the same water." It's beautiful and it's earned and I still closed it wanting them in one room. But I'll note the Ch.29 mews scene now does more work than I gave it credit for — she crosses the court, puts her cold face on his throat, he says the "clan of one is now two" — so they *are* physically together at the close, just not in one household. That took a half-point of my complaint off.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.19–25 — couple separated for a quarter of the book, unchanged since round 1.** By Ch.25 the romance is carried by "we threw each other away one more time. In the dark. A mile apart." Still the thing holding it off a 9.
2. **Ch.18 & Ch.26 remain the only two heat beats and both are late.** Thin, back-loaded cadence for the spicy shelf; the long dry middle still risks losing heat-readers.
3. **Ch.29 close — together in the same *scene* now, but not the same *life* (throne / mews).** Better than round 1's "opposite banks," still not the claimed-and-together last page a Devourer wants.

**Verdict:** Buy — caveat unchanged. **8/10.** (The leads and the two scenes are great; the separation structure is still the ceiling.)

## 2. THE CRITIC — developmental editor / agent repping adult romantasy & fantasy

*Structure, originality, prose discipline, market position. Judged against adult-romantasy comps, not YA and not lyrical closed-door epic.*

Still the most accomplished manuscript I've had across my desk in a long time. Ch.1's acoustic-rating conceit, "you must not be," the almond cakes as unspoken apology — a masterclass open. Loric is a genuinely fresh antagonist (the *heads-I-win* trap in Ch.7; the father's-laugh crack in Ch.23; dying "beneath her notice" in Ch.28). The father-son spine (Ch.4, the river in Ch.14, the third door in Ch.22) is better than most literary fiction. Craft is well above any gate, still.

Two things to raise, and here's where the re-read matters, because one of them moved and one didn't.

**First — the narrated-thesis tic is *reduced but not resolved,* and it's still the manuscript's dominant stylistic signature.** The de-tic pass did real work: the front-act codas are cleaner (Ch.8 no longer ends on a spelled-out meaning-statement), "both are true and they don't cancel" is now planted once (Ch.16) and paid off once (Ch.29 dialogue) instead of ringing three times, "it was not nothing" is de-doubled, and "looking back was a hand reached out for a thing that was not going to be there" is varied down to its Ch.21 anchor. Good. **But** the scene-end meaning-statement is still how a large share of beats close — "Hidden and seen were the same wall. She had only got round to the far side of it" (Ch.24), "The touch was only tidying" (Ch.28), "The clan of one is now two" (Ch.29), "reaching the thing in front of you was the only mercy the world had ever let her be good at" (Ch.28). And *"did the sum / the arithmetic"* is still everywhere — 28 instances across 14 chapters, in **both** POVs. The team's own note (§8) makes the case that Korvan *consciously inherits* Amelia's counting ("the arithmetic voice, come into his mouth at last," Ch.22) and that it's load-bearing arc, not bleed — and read closely, that defense largely holds; it is signposted. But "largely holds on close reading" is exactly the problem for a *fast* reader, who just feels two narrators sounding the same. It's softer than round 1. It is not gone.

**Second — positioning. Unchanged, and it's the acquisition-meeting question.** You're pitching Bishop / spicy romantasy; what's on the page is *lyrical literary-political epic fantasy with two explicit scenes.* The romance is, by architecture, a B-plot: leads apart a third of the book, spine is war/politics/Loric, register is McKillip/Priory-interiorised more than Bishop-plain. This wasn't a round-2 target and it didn't change. Decide which book it is and position honestly, or rebalance couple page-time forward.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Passim — narrated-thesis scene-endings + the "did the sum" cross-POV cadence.** Reduced, still dominant. End more beats on image/action.
2. **Positioning (Ch.19–26).** Romance-as-B-plot + literary register vs. the romantasy/Bishop pitch. Open.
3. **Ch.28 — the plot-timed power limit, still unrepaired.** "There was too much gone and too much open" saves the author two deaths, then she immediately holds a stranger's vessel shut in the same chapter. Still reads as a plot demanding casualties dressed as a rule.

**Verdict:** Maybe → revise-and-resubmit, but closer than round 1. **6.5/10** as pitched (8+ as literary epic fantasy). The de-tic pass moved it; positioning and the power-limit are what's left between this and a clean yes.

## 3. THE HOSTILE READER — predisposed to dislike it; hunts plot holes, convenience, main-character shield

*Came back with the red pen. Some of my round-1 catches got fixed. I found the ones that didn't.*

Credit where it's due. **The feeding plot no longer dies of neglect.** Round 1 my loudest complaint was that the hunger clock — the near-dry vial, the near-kill of the drover, the stolen goat, the knife-pact — just evaporated after Ch.19. It doesn't now. Ch.23 explicitly closes the loop: "the empty vial cold against her breast, dry these two weeks… taken the little she needed off what the road left dying — a snared hare, a broken-legged ewe, once a gull with a smashed wing — and not one warm human throat, and gone cold and slow between the times the land gave her nothing." That's the accounting I said was missing. It even reframes the rule as a *won* thing ("the first thing she'd kept whole by her own will"). Fine. That one's answered.

But the two machinery problems I flagged are **exactly where I left them.**

**The power does precisely what the plot needs.** Ch.28: she can't save her mother or Maren — "There was too much gone and too much open" — and that limit appears, for the first time in the book, at the one moment two deaths are required. Then, *in the same chapter,* she kneels in the ford and holds a stranger's torn vessel shut and he lives. So the rule that killed the two people she loves is inoperative one page later on a man she's never met. That's a plot demanding its casualties and calling it a law. Seed the limit earlier or don't have her save Selwyn — you still can't have both.

**Selwyn's death-fake-out is still the anchor undone by coincidence.** Ch.27 makes his drowning the whole moral cost of the climax — "he did not go to him," the dragon holding still while his one friend goes under a foot of water reaching up a hand. Genuinely gutting. Then Ch.28 the Queen-to-be happens to kneel in the *exact* ford where he *happens* to be bleeding out and saves him. Either he's dead and it costs, or he lives and the gut-punch was rented. You've kept both. (I'll grant the coincidence is slightly softened — she's working down the whole shelf holding wounds, so him being one of them isn't quite lightning — but he's still the *named* one, so it still reads as the shield.)

Convenience still ferries the cast: Maren walks a country and lands in the exact Dunmoor square at the exact minute (Ch.23); the chief keeps materialising precisely where Korvan is (Ch.21, Ch.22).

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.28 — plot-timed power limit.** "Too much gone and too much open" saves two deaths, then she saves a stranger the same chapter. Unfixed.
2. **Ch.27→28 — Selwyn death-fake-out.** Climax's moral cost undone by a convenient rescue. Unfixed.
3. **Ch.23 — convergence convenience.** Maren finds Amelia in the exact square at the exact minute after a two-hundred-mile walk.

**Verdict:** Put back — but grudgingly upgraded, because the feeding hole is genuinely closed and that was my biggest one. **6/10** (was 5).

## 4. THE CASUAL READER — low investment, will put it down if bored or confused

*Picked it up again. The thing that confused me most last time is mostly fixed.*

The opening still hooks me — the locked room, the mother, the party she's not allowed to go to — and any chapter with the two of them together I read fast.

**The timeline reads much better now.** Round 1 I genuinely could not tell if a year had passed or a season — everyone kept saying "a year" at the reunion and then Ch.29 said "Twenty-two days after the Elver" and I kept re-reading to check. This pass, the "a year of wanting / a year of being the loneliest thing alive" lines are gone or turned into *similes* — "every week of it had felt like a year" (Ch.26) — which actually helps, because now it reads as *it felt long,* not *a year literally elapsed.* The bones are weeks now (six days, four days, "dry these two weeks," twenty-two days after) and they line up. I could follow it. **One nag:** people still say "in a year" as a figure of speech a lot — "the only one who made sense to him in a year" (Ch.6), "not ten words of him in a year" (Ch.25), "more than he had managed in a year" (Ch.28) — and since the real span is a couple of months, those idioms still made me blink once or twice. Small, but it's the last splinter of the thing.

Where I still drifted: the **court/politics chapters** (Ch.17 especially, the Verrin-vs-Corenne council debate; and stretches of Ch.20, Ch.23). Beautifully written, but I don't know these lords and I'm waiting to get back to the dragon and the girl. Ch.17 is a long argument among people I hadn't met, far from the couple, and I skimmed it same as last time.

And I *still* didn't fully absorb the **whale-iron "reach"** rule (Ch.21) — the escape hinges on "twenty feet of height past the alder and the line's spent," and I sort of took it on faith. It comes back in Ch.27 so at least it pays off, but the first time it's asserted more than shown.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.17 (and stretches of 20/23) — court/politics chapters still drag for me.** Ch.17's council debate is the one I skimmed both reads.
2. **Residual "in a year" idioms** clash faintly with the now-clear weeks timeline (Ch.6, Ch.25, Ch.28). Minor, but it's the last confusing thread.
3. **Ch.21 — whale-iron "reach"** still feels asserted the first time; I took the big escape on trust.

**Verdict:** Maybe, leaning finish. **7/10** (was 6.5). The timeline fix is why.

## 5. THE DEVOTED READER — emotionally invested in the couple, the cost, the ending

*Cared about all of them again. Reports what wrecked me and what would make me rage.*

It hurt me the right way, again. The garden. The grain-cart hand, too-hot and too-cold with a plank between (Ch.10, Ch.12). Selwyn spending his name — "You did just exactly what one of ours does. Twice now" (Ch.14, Ch.19) — destroyed me twice now. And Ch.28 is still the cruellest perfect stroke in the book: the Queen dying with her whole attention on Amelia, "you're always cold… I never could keep you warm," never once looking at Loric, so that his whole life's vengeance is *beneath her notice to the end* — and Loric's grief cracking on "She never even—" and being buried mid-sentence. That's the best page in the manuscript.

What I still rage about, and it's structural so it didn't change: **the ending holds me at arm's length from the biggest grief.** She loses her mother and Maren in one iron-cast (Ch.28) and "did not weep… there was nothing in her yet that could," and by the end of Ch.29 she still hasn't. I understand it's the point — grief held inward, the cold register, canon. But as the reader who came for the *feeling*, I'm kept outside the largest loss in the book. I wanted one crack. The Ch.29 mews scene comes *close* — her voice "went, for the length of one word, thinner than she had told it to be" against Korvan's throat — and that's the nearest I got, and I was grateful for it, and I still wanted the full break.

And **Maren still dies in the same blow as the Queen** (Ch.28). She's the human heart of the whole book — the buttons, "I'll not be made sorry I came" — and she gets a *beautiful* death (the buttons coming out even, "I do like it when they come out even"), better than round 1 gave her credit for, honestly. But she still shares the iron-cast with the Queen, so it still lands fast, two losses in one stroke, and I wanted her to be her own blow.

One thing that's *better*: Selwyn living. Round 1 I forgave the rescue; this read I'm glad of it, because the book has so few survivals that letting the fool live — "Here's the thing about a downed one—" from the ford — is the one mercy I got, and I'll defend it even though the Hostile reader is right that it's a bit convenient.

**Blindspots/issues:**
1. **Ch.28–29 — Amelia never breaks.** Emotionally true; still keeps the grief-reader outside the ending's largest loss. (Softened a hair by the Ch.29 "thinner than she told it to be" beat.)
2. **Ch.29 — the couple ends together-but-apart** (throne / mews). "The clan of one is now two" earns it; I still wanted them in one life, not one visit.
3. **Ch.28 — Maren shares the Queen's iron-cast.** Her button-count death is gorgeous but still a shared beat; the book's warmest character deserves her own stroke.

**Verdict:** Buy and press it on everyone, still crying about the ending. **8.5/10** (unchanged).

# CHANGE SINCE ROUND 1 — did the four consensus problems move?

**(1) Scene-end narrated-thesis tic + cross-POV repeated phrasing → IMPROVED, not resolved.**
The de-tic pass did measurable work. Verified in text: "both are true and they don't cancel" now appears as a single plant (Ch.16 chief/Amelia) and a single dialogue payoff (Ch.29) rather than three cross-POV rings; "it was not nothing" is de-doubled and kept to origins; "looking back was a hand reached out for a thing that was not going to be there" is varied down to its Ch.21 anchor (survives once, as Amelia's, deliberate); the front-act codas (Ch.8 ¶46) end on image now. **But** the scene-end meaning-statement is still the dominant closing move across the back half (Ch.24 "hidden and seen were the same wall," Ch.28 "the touch was only tidying," Ch.29 "the clan of one is now two"), and *"did the sum / the arithmetic"* still runs **28× across 14 chapters in both POVs.** The team's judgment call (keep it as Korvan's conscious inheritance of Amelia's counting) is defensible and signposted — but a fast reader still registers a single narrator voice. **Verdict: improved; still the #1 craft note. Partial.**

**(2) Feeding / hunger arc resolution → RESOLVED.**
This was round 1's loudest plot hole and it's closed. Ch.11's contingency was recalibrated toward the animals-not-people rule, and Ch.23 now explicitly pays the arc off: the vial "dry these two weeks," survival on "a snared hare, a broken-legged ewe, once a gull," "not one warm human throat," reframed as "the first thing she'd kept whole by her own will." The Hostile reader's dropped-thread complaint is answered and the Casual reader's lost-rule confusion is gone. **Verdict: resolved.**

**(3) Timeline consistency (compressed to a WEEKS scale) → IMPROVED, one minor splinter left.**
The literal "a year of wanting / a year of being the loneliest thing alive" assertions in Ch.25/26 are gone or converted to *similes* ("every week of it had felt like a year," "worn like a year"), and the concrete markers now line up on a ~6–8-week span plus "Twenty-two days after the Elver" (Ch.29). No remaining literal "a year elapsed" clash at Ch.27/Ch.29. **Remaining:** ~6 idiomatic "in a year" figures of speech survive (Ch.6, Ch.11, Ch.19, Ch.25, Ch.28) and read faintly oddly against a two-month span; and Amelia's Ch.9 promise of "one word in a year" implies a year-long expectation, though as a *plan* that's internally fine. **Verdict: improved; a cheap final mop (swap the idioms) would close it. Minor-open.**

**(4) Romance structurally a B-plot vs. the romantasy pitch → UNCHANGED (open, authorial).**
Not a round-2 target; the structure is intact. Leads still apart Ch.19–26, spine still war/politics/Loric, register still literary-interiorised more than Bishop-plain. The one micro-improvement is incidental: the Ch.29 mews scene gives the couple a beat of physical togetherness at the close that round 1 said was missing. But the B-plot architecture and the positioning question are exactly where they were. **Verdict: open / structural.**
