# Proposal — The Human-Revolt Thread ("Loric's Wrecking Ball")

> **Status:** DEVELOPMENTAL PROPOSAL for author (Terry) review. Nothing in the
> finalized 29-chapter manuscript has been changed. This file maps *where* and
> *how* a human-uprising thread would weave into the existing structure, what it
> costs, and the decisions Terry has to make before any of it is written.
>
> **Origin:** Terry's original conception was a "semi three-way war" — Loric
> working with a human to start a revolt among the food source (humans), trying
> to cause as much damage as possible. That thread was never expressed in the
> canon docs, so it never got built. This maps adding it back.

---

## 1. The core idea, stated cleanly

Loric works with a human collaborator to ignite an uprising among the human
underclass — **not as a strategy with a political goal, but as a wrecking ball.**
He does not care whether the humans win, the vampires fall, or the whole
treaty-world burns. The revolt is the biggest lever he can find for *damage*,
because damage is the point: he wants the Queen to watch everything she built
come apart.

**This is the whole reason it works.** See §2.

---

## 2. Why it makes the book better (and doesn't break Loric)

The bible's load-bearing line about Loric is:

> *"Cannot be negotiated with or bought off because no political outcome
> satisfies his real goal… his true motivation is emotional, not strategic:
> he wants the Queen specifically to suffer."*

A villain with a clever *political* scheme would contradict that and weaken him.
But a villain who hands the food source a torch **not to free them, but to burn
the house down around the Queen** is the *same* man, escalated. The revolt is an
instrument of grief, not a plan for power. It deepens him instead of muddying him.

Three concrete gains:

1. **Raises the ceiling on stakes.** The war is currently vampires-vs-shifters
   over one exposed girl. A human uprising turns it into "the entire order is
   coming apart," which makes the Queen's impossible choice cost more and makes
   Loric feel like a force, not a court schemer.
2. **Fires a gun the world already loaded.** The bible literally says humans are
   *"vulnerable to manipulation if someone questions the legitimacy of the
   protection pact"* and that *"the entire vampire economy depends on them."*
   That sentence has sat unused. Pulling it makes the worldbuilding pay off
   instead of decorate.
3. **A face for the third side, with built-in poignancy.** The one character
   with a *just* cause (humans are livestock) is being *used* by the one
   character with the ugliest one. That irony is Bishop-flavored and thematically
   rich.

**Thematic fit:** the book's thesis is already "truth is offered and changes
nothing" (Ch.23). A human revolt with a legitimate grievance that gets
weaponized, co-opted, and spent by Loric — and that the "victory" of Ch.28 does
NOT resolve — reinforces that thesis. It also hands Amelia (who inherits the
throne in Ch.29) the single most interesting unfinished problem in the world:
the food-source pact the revolt exposed. **That is a sequel engine.**

---

## 3. The prime directive (the guardrail that keeps it from eating the book)

> **The revolt is PRESSURE, not PLOT. It is Loric's instrument, seen mostly
> through its EFFECTS on the leads. It never gets its own POV chapters or its
> own arc.**

The emotional spine stays Amelia/Korvan and the Queen's choice. If the revolt
grows a full third-faction narrative with its own throughline, it will unbalance
a book that is already 29 chapters, ~150k, and beta-tested. Every insertion
below is a *seam into an existing scene* or a *short beat*, never a new chapter.
**Chapter count stays 29. No renumbering.**

---

## 4. The collaborator (new minor character)

**Working name: BRAM** *(placeholder — Terry to name; fits the world's register
alongside Maren/Selwyn)*.

- **Who:** A human of the pact underclass — proposed as a **blood-tithe steward
  or a "protected" village headman's son** — someone who has lived the feudal
  arithmetic of the pact from the inside and has a *specific* dead: a family
  member bled past recovery, or taken in a tithe and not returned.
- **His cause is real.** He is not a fanatic or a fool. He has looked at the
  "protection" and correctly seen a slaughterhouse with manners. The reader
  should half-agree with him. That is the point.
- **His tragedy:** Loric finds him, recognizes the grievance, and *aims* him.
  Bram thinks he is using Loric (a highborn shifter who can arm and shield the
  rising). Loric is using Bram. Bram never quite sees that he is a match, not a
  general — and by the time he might, it's spent.
- **Tie to an existing character (strongly recommended):** connect Bram to
  **Maren's world** — same home region, or known to her, or from the very
  village named on the casualty list in Ch.20. This does two things: it gives the
  revolt an emotional conductor we already care about (Maren), and it lets us
  *feel* the food-source injustice through the human we've had since Ch.2 without
  making Maren herself the collaborator (she stays loyal to Amelia). Maren
  becomes the lens; Bram is the match.

**Hard line:** Maren is NOT the collaborator. Her loyalty is to Amelia; making
her the instigator would wreck her Ch.28 death. Bram is a separate figure whom
Maren's thread *touches*.

## 5. The surgery — where it seeds, turns, and detonates

Structure of any good subordinate thread: **seed early, turn at the midpoint,
detonate in the back third, pay off in the residue.** Mapped onto the existing
chapters:

### SEED — Act I (make it latent, cost ~+150–300 words each)

- **Ch.4 "The Man Who Only Asks" [K]** — Loric is already built here as
  *reasonable, patient, "only asks questions."* **Add one beat:** among the
  people he quietly cultivates in the camp is a human — not shown as a plot
  move, just Loric being the one man who treats a human servant as a person too.
  Plants that Loric's reach already extends *under* the pact, not just across it.
  *This is the single most important seed and it costs almost nothing — it's
  Loric doing exactly what the chapter already shows him doing, pointed one
  degree differently.*
- **Ch.5 "Among People" [A]** — the banquet already puts "vampires, humans, the
  pact" on display. **Add a half-beat:** Amelia, rapt at simply being among
  people, brushes past the *underside* of the pact — a human being fed-upon or
  tithed as ordinary furniture of the party. She barely registers it (she's
  happy); the reader does. Plants the injustice as a fact of the world before it
  becomes a fire. **Optional:** this is where the reader (not Amelia) could first
  glimpse Bram, in service, watching.

### TURN — Act II-A / II-B (the ember catches; cost ~+300–500 words each)

- **Ch.12 "The Burned Village" [K]** — currently "the war's first cost made
  concrete." **Repurpose, don't add a chapter:** let the reader see that the
  raid's survivors are being *spoken to* by someone — the first whisper that the
  humans here are not only victims of the war but being *organized*. Korvan
  registers it as one more wrongness; he doesn't understand it. **Bram can appear
  here** (or his work can), unnamed to Korvan.
- **Ch.17 "Banner" [C] (Queen/Maren, court)** — the court is already spinning
  Amelia/Korvan into a war-banner. **Add the mirror:** intelligence reaches court
  that the human tithe-regions are *stirring* — and the Queen understands, with
  dread, that a human rising would collapse the economy the vampire order rests
  on. This is where the *stakes* of the revolt land for the ruling side. Maren
  hears a name from home (seed Bram to her here).
- **Ch.19 "A Dragon Can't Hide" [K]** — Loric "makes his first move to claim or
  destroy the dragon-asset." **Add the tell:** we see, briefly, that the dragon
  is not Loric's only iron in the fire — he is *also* feeding the human unrest.
  The reader clocks that Loric is running two wrecking balls. Korvan does not.

### DETONATE — Act III (the third front flares; cost ~+400–700 words each)

- **Ch.23 "The Cause of Every Death" [C/A]** — Loric's public framing of the
  leads as "the cause of every death." **Fold in:** the human rising is now
  *visible*, and Loric folds it into the same frame — "see what the witch and her
  dragon have unleashed" — using a fire *he* lit as proof against them. The
  revolt becomes another death laid at Amelia's feet. Reinforces the thesis
  (truth changes nothing). **Bram's use is near-complete here.**
- **Ch.27–28 "Outlasted (I/II)"** — the climax. The war is *"redirected, not
  undone."* **The revolt is part of what cannot be undone.** When Loric is
  contained at unrecoverable cost, the human rising he lit does not politely end
  with him — it is loose in the world. **Bram's fate pays off here:** proposed —
  he dies, or is broken, having been spent as a match; a short, bleak beat that
  lands the tragedy of the used just-cause. Consider: a moment where someone
  (Amelia? Maren, before her death?) realizes the humans were *right* even as
  they were *used* — which is the cruelest possible reading and the most Bishop.
- **Ch.29 "What We Choose" [A]** — the residue. **The payoff that makes the whole
  thread worth it:** Amelia inherits the throne as the first natural-born Queen —
  and inherits, unresolved, the food-source pact the revolt tore the skin off of.
  Her final "choose how to wear the crown" beat now carries a real, specific,
  world-sized question: *what does she do about the humans?* This converts the
  book's ending from "personal cost + crown" to "personal cost + crown + the one
  reform that could redraw the world" — **without resolving it**, which is the
  strongest sequel runway the book could have.

## 6. What this costs (honest accounting)

- **Chapters touched:** ~8 (Ch.4, 5, 12, 17, 19, 23, 27–28, 29). All are *seams
  into existing scenes* or short beats — no new chapters, no renumbering.
- **Net words added:** rough estimate **+3,000 to +4,500** across the book (well
  within the ~150k → 140k-floor margin; if anything it uses headroom the book
  has).
- **New character:** one minor (Bram) + a strengthened use of an existing one
  (Maren as lens). No new POV.
- **Risk level:** **moderate, and front-loaded onto discipline, not craft.** The
  only real failure mode is scope creep — the revolt demanding more room than
  §3 allows. Mitigation: treat every insertion as capped, and if a beat wants a
  scene of its own, cut it back to an effect-on-the-leads.
- **Beta impact:** the book has been through 7 beta rounds. Re-inserting a thread
  means those chapters would want a fresh consistency + continuity pass
  (continuity-guardian) and at least a spot beta re-read on the touched
  chapters, because the propaganda/casualty/framing beats (Ch.12, 17, 20, 23)
  interlock.

## 7. Canon-compatibility check (does it violate anything?)

| Canon element | Compatible? | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Loric's motive is emotional, not strategic | ✅ | Revolt reframed as a *wrecking ball for damage*, not a political plan. Deepens, not contradicts. |
| Loric can't be bought off / no outcome satisfies him | ✅ | He doesn't want the revolt to *succeed*; he wants it to *hurt*. |
| Humans = food source, economy depends on them | ✅ | This is the exact lever the bible names as exploitable. |
| Maren is the loyal human keeper | ✅ | Maren is the LENS, not the collaborator. Bram is separate. |
| Treaty is vampire↔shifter (humans subordinate, not signatories) | ✅ | A human rising is *outside* the treaty — which is precisely why it's destabilizing and why no one planned for it. |
| War is "redirected, not undone" (Ch.28) | ✅ | The loose revolt is part of what's not undone. Strengthens it. |
| Ch.29 inherit-vs-choose resolution | ✅✅ | The pact question makes "choose how to wear the crown" concrete. Net gain. |

**No canon violations found.** The thread slots into space the world already
left open.

## 8. Open decisions for Terry (before anything is written)

1. **Loric's degree of involvement.** Recommended: he *lit and fed* the unrest
   but does not command it (keeps him a wrecking-ball, not a general). Confirm,
   or do you want him more hands-on?
2. **Bram's tie to Maren.** Recommended: same home region / known to her, seeded
   Ch.17, so the revolt runs through a character we love. Confirm, or keep Bram
   wholly separate?
3. **Bram's fate.** Recommended: spent and broken/dead by Ch.27–28 — the used
   just-cause. Confirm, or does he survive as a live sequel thread?
4. **How loud in Ch.29.** Recommended: the pact question is *raised, not
   resolved* — sequel runway. Confirm you want it left open (vs. Amelia making a
   first move on it in the residue).
5. **Name for Bram.**
6. **Scope ceiling.** Confirm the §3 prime directive (pressure, not plot; no POV
   chapters; 29 chapters preserved). This is the guardrail I'd hold the line on.

Once these six are answered, this becomes a concrete revision task: ~8 chapters,
capped inserts, one continuity pass, and a spot beta re-read on the interlocking
war-front chapters.
