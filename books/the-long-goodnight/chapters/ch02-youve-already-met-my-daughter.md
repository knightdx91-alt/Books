# Chapter 2 — You've Already Met My Daughter

He woke to the smell of coffee and floor wax and something else under it, older — the smell a building gets when it's been the same thing for fifty years, dry goods and paper sacks and the ghost of every winter's woodsmoke soaked into the boards.

The man had put him up on a cot in the storeroom the night before without much discussion. He'd knocked, and the screen door had opened on a big-shouldered old man in a canvas apron who'd looked at the duffel first and the face second, and said, "We're closed," and then, "You eaten?" in the same breath, so that the closing and the feeding arrived together and canceled each other out. Xavier had said he'd eaten. The man had said there was a cot in back if he wasn't particular, and Xavier — who had not been particular about a place to sleep in three years — had said he wasn't.

Now it was morning and the man was out front already, and Xavier lay a minute on the cot with his boots off and his socks on and listened to the store wake up. Somewhere a cooler kicked on and hummed. A truck went by outside and the front glass took the sound and let it go. Then the old man's voice, low, unhurried, talking — and Xavier came up on an elbow before he understood the man wasn't talking to anybody. He was shelving. Xavier could hear the small clap of cans set down in a row, and under it the voice, easy and continuous.

"—no, you go up front, you've been in the back too long, nobody's going to buy you in the dark—" A can, set down. "There. Now you're somebody."

He was talking to the soup.

Xavier lay still and took the storeroom in by ear and nose the way he'd learned to take a strange building, which was the way you take the measure of a man by his house. Everything within reach of the cot was paper or wood or cloth. Flour in paper sacks stacked to his shoulder, crackers in waxed boxes, a bolt of oilcloth stood on end in the corner, cardboard soft with age, the dry-goods smell of a dry old room under dry old beams that had been holding the second floor up since before anybody now alive had a say in it. A box of kitchen matches on the shelf by his head, and under the window a squat can of lamp oil, because the power went out here and George was the kind to be ready for it. It was all so quiet and so kept. Somebody wound this clock. He hadn't slept in a wound building in a long time, and his body had noticed before he had, and stayed down an extra hour on the strength of it.

Xavier lay back down and looked at the ceiling, which was pressed tin gone the color of weak tea, and for a moment — a bare moment, gone before he could be ashamed of it — the store held still around him like a held breath, and it was the first honest quiet he'd been inside of in longer than he wanted to count.

He got up and put his boots on.

---

"There he is." The old man didn't turn around. He had a clipboard hooked in one arm and was working down a shelf with a grease pencil, marking prices, and he said it to the shelf. "Sleep?"

"Yes, sir. Thank you."

"George." He turned then and put out a hand, and Xavier took it, and the grip was dry and enormous and careful, a big man's grip that had learned to hold back. "George Holmes. It's my name on the sign, so." He looked Xavier over, unhurried, the way Anna had looked him over on the road but slower, an older instrument. "You came in on foot."

"Got dropped off."

"Mm." George turned back to the shelf. "You do this a lot?" A can went up, got turned so the label faced front. "Come into a town at night — there, that's your good side — knock on the first lit window?"

"It was the last one lit."

That got him a sound out of the old man, not quite a laugh — a breath through the nose, an acknowledgment. "Fair enough. Fair enough." He capped the grease pencil. "What do you do, when you do something?"

"Whatever's around. I'm handy."

"Everybody says they're handy. My brother-in-law says he's handy. My brother-in-law has cost me two doors and a good sink." George set the clipboard down on the counter and jerked his chin toward the far corner, where the screen door hung a little proud of its frame, the top hinge pulled half out of the jamb. "That's been catching for a month. Anna keeps at me about it. You want to be handy, that door'd be a place to start. There's a box of tools under the register been there since Eisenhower."

Xavier looked at the door. He was glad of it — of a thing with a clear inside and outside, a thing that was either fixed or not fixed and told you which. He found the toolbox. The old man went back to his shelving and his soup and left him to it, which was its own kind of courtesy, and Xavier took the hinge down and saw the screws had stripped their holes and the wood had gone soft, and he plugged the holes with matchsticks and glue the way you did and reset the screws into fresh bite, and rehung the door, and it swung true and the latch caught with a small clean sound.

He stood there holding the door a second longer than the job needed, working it, listening to the latch, and something in his chest did the thing it had done in the diner — the small unlocking. He shut the door. He shut the feeling with it.

"Now that," George said from behind him, not turning around, "is the first time that door's told the truth in a month."

Xavier meant to say it was nothing, which was the true thing and the easy thing and the thing that closes a moment clean. What came out of him instead was the matchsticks. He heard himself explaining them, how the screws had chewed their holes out and the wood behind had gone punky, how you plug it and give the threads something green to bite into, how it'd hold now till the frame itself let go in a few more winters — explaining it past where George had stopped needing to know, and then past where he himself knew to quit, the words just running on because the other option was to stand there having done a decent thing in front of a man and be looked at for it. He got to the frame letting go in a few more winters and there was nowhere left to take it. He stopped. He looked at the door instead of at George.

"Anyway," he said. "It was catching."

"It was," George agreed, and let him off it, and turned back to the shelf, and that was a kindness too, though a different-shaped one than the sandwich.

---

They ate lunch at the counter around noon, when the store went quiet between the morning and the after-school. George made two sandwiches out of the case — ham, store cheese, a tomato he cut on the counter with a pocket knife — and set one in front of Xavier without asking whether he wanted it, the same way he'd fed him the night before, so that the kindness kept arriving before Xavier could get his guard up in front of it. The bread was a day past fresh and Xavier ate all of it.

"So you walked in from the county road," George said around the sandwich. "That's what, six miles from the turnoff. You didn't walk six miles. You'd have come in later and worse."

"Got a ride most of it."

"Somebody local?"

"Don't think so." Xavier turned the sandwich a quarter turn on the paper and didn't eat it yet. "A woman picked me up out past the line. Drove me all the way in. Wouldn't take gas money."

"That so." George kept eating. "She have a name?"

"Said it was Anna."

The old man chewed. He didn't stop chewing, exactly, but something in the machinery of it changed, a hitch, a gear finding another gear, and he swallowed and set the sandwich down on the paper and wiped his mouth with the back of one big hand, and Xavier watched a thing move up through him from the belly, slow, and come out as a laugh.

It wasn't a big laugh. It was a low one, private, the laugh of a man enjoying something at nobody's expense but the situation's, and it went on a little, and Xavier sat there with his sandwich in front of him not knowing which part of the last ten seconds had been funny.

"What," Xavier said.

George shook his head. He picked the sandwich back up. He took a bite of it and made Xavier wait through the whole of it, chewing, his eyes bright over the bread, and then he said it the way you'd mention the weather.

"Well— looks like you've already met my daughter."

---

The floor didn't move. Xavier would think about that afterward — that the floor stayed where it was, that the cooler kept humming, that nothing in the room did anything, and yet something in him dropped clean through all of it like a stone through water. He felt it in his stomach first, the drop, and then his neck, and then his face, and he understood he was doing something with his face and couldn't have said what.

"Your—"

"Daughter. Anna." George was frankly delighted now, watching it happen, kind about it but not about to help. "One and only. Lives out a mile past the church, though she's here more than there. She does that, you know. Picks people up. Gets a notion about somebody on the side of a road and can't leave it be. Her mother was the same, God rest her — couldn't pass a stray anything." He took another bite. "She drove you all the way in and never once said whose store she was dropping you at?"

"No, sir." Xavier heard his own voice come out level and was distantly grateful to it. "She didn't."

And now the whole ride ran backward through him at once, the way a thing does when you've been handed the trick of it. *I'm nobody you've met yet.* The smile she'd thought about first. *In a way.* Every coy sideways thing she'd said, every question she'd asked and every one she'd dodged — she'd been sitting on this the entire three hours, steering him to her father's own front door, and she'd let him get out and stand on the sidewalk asking *who are you* with the answer already parked under his feet. She'd known exactly the shape of this morning. She'd built it.

"She set me up," he said, and didn't mean to say it out loud.

"She surely did." George looked pleased down to his boots. "Don't take it hard. It means she likes you. She wouldn't waste the trouble on somebody she didn't." He balled up the sandwich paper and dropped it under the counter. "The ones she can't stand, she just tells them where the bus stops."

Xavier looked down at his sandwich. He picked it up because his hands wanted something to do. He'd been played across three hours and six miles by a woman he'd known for one meal, and the strange thing — the thing he turned over and couldn't set down — was that under the drop in his stomach there was that other feeling again, the unlocking, the almost-laugh, coming up his throat uninvited for the third time in a day, and this time he was too caught off guard to shut the door on it fast enough, and a little of it got out. Not much. A short sound, through the nose, a lot like the one George had made at the shelf.

George heard it and grinned and didn't say anything, which was the kindest thing he could have done.

---

"So," the old man said, when they'd finished and Xavier had cleared the counter without being asked and wiped it down and found where the rag went, "you can hang a door. You can bus a lunch. You slept in my storeroom and didn't take anything, or if you did it was that soup can I can't account for—"

"I didn't take your soup."

"I know you didn't, son, I'm making a point." He said *son* and didn't notice he'd said it, and it went into Xavier and lodged somewhere and stayed. "The point is I've got a store that's too much store for one old man and a girl who's got her own ideas about her time, and I've got a room upstairs I've been using to keep the winter coats in, and I've got a run of work around here that's been waiting on somebody handy for about two years." He came around the counter and stood next to Xavier and looked out the front glass at Jamestown doing its slow afternoon. "I don't hire strangers. Never have. But then I don't figure you're a stranger anymore, exactly. You've been introduced." He put out the big careful hand again. "We hire people who haven't been introduced yet. You have been. Grab an apron. There's one on the hook that'll fit you."

Xavier looked at the hand. Three years he'd taken work the way you take weather, one town's worth at a time, careful never to be anywhere long enough to be missed leaving it. He knew how to say no to this. He'd said no to gentler things than this and walked out into the dark and been glad of the dark.

Behind the counter, through the front glass, a green hatchback that had seen some winters slowed at the curb, and gravel ticked, and a woman he'd known for exactly one day put it in park and left it running.

She looked in through the butter-colored window to see how the morning had gone, and Xavier looked back at her, and there it was, the thing she didn't have yet. She'd built his whole morning and driven off pleased with the building of it, and now she'd come back around to watch it land on him, and she didn't know it had already landed, that her father had handed him the whole trick of her across a ham sandwich an hour ago. She was reading his face for a stranger's surprise. He had a day on her now that she didn't know he had, one turned card she hadn't seen him turn. It settled into his chest next to the other thing, the unlocking, and the two of them did not sit easy together, the warmth and the small cool weight of knowing something about her before she knew he knew it.

She lifted a hand off the wheel. Not quite a wave. Just letting him see she was there.

Xavier took the old man's hand.

"All right," he said. "I can do that."

He watched her through the glass while he said it, and kept what he knew off his face, and it came to him that he'd just said yes to two people in the one breath, and only one of them had heard it.
