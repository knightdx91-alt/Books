# Chapter 1 — The Ride

The car slowed before he'd decided to want it to.

He was standing at the county line with his thumb out and his duffel between his boots, and he'd been standing there long enough that the standing had stopped being about a ride and started being about the standing itself. Two hours. Maybe closer to three. He'd stopped counting the trucks that didn't slow. That was the thing about a road like this — you could stand on the shoulder of it and it would agree to let you disappear, and there was a comfort in that he wasn't going to say out loud to anybody.

Then the car slowed, and gravel ticked under the tires, and a green hatchback that had seen some winters rolled up beside him with the passenger window already coming down.

"You look like a man who's decided to live out here," the driver said. "There's nothing out here. I'd rethink it."

He bent to the window. A woman, maybe mid-twenties, one hand loose on the wheel and the other coming up to knock the hair out of her eyes. She wasn't looking at him the way people looked at a man on the shoulder of a road. She was looking at him the way you look at a price tag.

"Going east," he said.

"Everything's east of here. You'll have to do better."

"Anywhere with work."

She let that sit. A semi went by behind him and rocked the air and she waited it out, and then she reached over and popped the door.

"Get in before I think about it," she said.

He thought about it for her — the way you were supposed to, the way the pamphlets and the mothers all said. Woman alone, stranger on a road, a duffel that could hold anything. He almost said something about it, some version of *you shouldn't do this,* and then he understood that she'd already thought it and picked him up anyway, and that the thinking wasn't his to do for her. He put the duffel in the back seat because he didn't want to hold it in his lap like a man on a bus. He got in.

The seat was too far forward. His knees were up near the dash. He found the bar underneath and slid it back, and the seat gave that long complaining groan they all give, and he adjusted it to the second notch and then, without meaning to, reached across to the empty air on his right where a hand would have been and moved to do the other seat too.

There was no other seat to do. There was a woman driving it.

He put the hand back in his lap.

She hadn't seen it. She was checking her mirror, pulling them back onto the road, and the tires found the pavement and the car settled into fifty-five like it was glad to. He looked out the windshield at the road doing what roads did.

"Xavier," he said, because you had to say something.

"Anna." She said it the way you'd set a cup down. Not offered. Just put where he could reach it.

---

They drove a while without deciding to talk. That suited him. He'd gotten good at riding in other people's cars — you learned the geography of a stranger's front seat fast, where to put your eyes, how to be a shape that wasn't a burden. He kept his eyes on the fields. Corn, mostly, and then not corn, and then a long low run of pasture with cattle standing in it the way cattle stood, like they'd been switched off and left there. One of them had its head through the fence and was eating on the other side, which was the same grass.

"You're not from around the last place either," she said. It wasn't a question, so he didn't answer it, and she didn't seem to need him to. "I can always tell. People from a place have a way of not looking at it. You look at everything like you're going to have to describe it later."

"Suppose I do."

"Where'd you come from?"

He named the last town. She hadn't heard of it. He named the one before, and she had, and made a face.

"That's four hours behind you."

"I've been going a while."

"Doing what?"

"Whatever's around." He turned his hand over on his knee, and she glanced at it, and he saw her read the calluses and the split thumbnail and the way the knuckles sat. "Fences. Roofs. Cleared a barn out for a man in the spring, he paid me in the barn." He heard how that sounded and let it stand. It was true.

"You're handy."

"With things."

She caught the shape of what he'd left off the end of that and didn't chase it. Instead she reached down and turned the radio on, and it came up on static, and she thumbed it along the dial and found a station that was mostly a preacher and turned it back off.

"There's a diner in about ten miles," she said. "It's terrible. You want to stop?"

"I'm all right."

"That's not what I asked."

He looked at her. She was watching the road, but there was something at the corner of her mouth, something set there on purpose for him to find. He hadn't decided yet whether she was easy company or the hardest kind, the kind that saw you.

"I could eat," he said.

---

The diner was terrible. She'd been honest about that.

It sat at a crossroads with a gravel lot and a sign missing two letters, and inside it smelled like a griddle that had been going since before either of them was born. There were four stools and a row of booths with the vinyl split on the seats and taped, and behind the counter a boy of maybe nineteen straightened up when Anna came in like a plant turning to a window.

"Anna," the boy said, and then couldn't think of the next word.

"Marcus." She slid into a booth and Xavier took the other side, and the boy came around with two laminated menus and set them down and stood there with his order pad like he was waiting to be told he could exist.

"Rosalie out?" Anna asked.

"She — called in. So it's. It's just me."

"Then we'll go easy on you." She didn't open the menu. She turned it face down and folded her hands on it and looked at the boy, and Xavier watched the boy get ready for whatever a pretty woman in a booth was going to be, and then Anna said, "He wants the double cheeseburger."

"I hadn't—" Xavier started.

"You want the double cheeseburger," she said, not to the boy now, to him, flat as a level. "Trust me. It's the most interesting thing that's going to happen to you today. The rest of that menu is a series of disappointments Rosalie is not here to prevent." Back to the boy. "Two. And coffee. He looks like coffee."

"I look like coffee," Xavier said.

"You look like a man who drinks it cold and doesn't notice."

The boy wrote it down like it was scripture and went. Xavier watched him go and then looked back at Anna, and something happened in his chest that he didn't have a good name for, a small unlocking, a hinge easing. She'd aimed the whole thing sideways, past the boy, so the boy got to feel chosen and the joke landed somewhere else. On Xavier. He saw the seams of it and it worked anyway.

He almost laughed.

The almost came up out of nowhere and got as far as his throat, and he caught it there, and the catching scared him worse than the road had, worse than the standing. Three years he'd been careful with himself the way you're careful with a bad tooth, learning where not to press. And here was this stranger's front seat, this terrible diner, this boy named Marcus, and his own throat trying to do a thing it hadn't tried in a long time, and he shut it down and looked at the table.

There was a jukebox in the corner, dead, unplugged, the cord looped over the top of it. There'd been a song. Years ago, some diner like this one, a song had been on, and he could get everything about it — the booth, the sun through the blinds in bars across the table, Selena's foot hooked over his under it — everything except the song. The name of it sat just past where he could reach. He worked at it while Anna talked about the town and he made the sounds a person makes, and he never did get the song, and it bothered him for reasons that had nothing to do with music.

---

The burger was, in fact, the most interesting thing that happened to him that day.

"Told you," Anna said, watching him eat it. She'd ordered one too and eaten half and pushed the rest away, and she drank her coffee black and watched him over it, and he had the sense of being tallied.

"You do that a lot?" he asked.

"Do what."

"Order for people."

"Only people who won't order for themselves." She set the cup down. "You'd have gotten the grilled cheese. Played it safe. Left hungry and polite about it."

She wasn't wrong, and it landed somewhere he didn't like, because it was a small thing and true, and the true small things were the ones that got under. He picked up his coffee. It had gone cold. He drank it anyway and didn't tell her she'd called that too.

Marcus brought the check and Anna took it before Xavier's hand was halfway across the table.

"I've got a ride to finish," she said. "You can get the next terrible diner."

"There's going to be a next one?"

She smiled at that, and it was the first smile she'd given him that she seemed to have thought about first. "In a way," she said.

He didn't know what to do with *in a way,* so he did nothing with it, which he would think about later.

---

Dusk was coming down blue when she turned off the county road onto a two-lane and the two-lane onto a street with a name, and the name was Jamestown, and Jamestown was one long block of storefronts with their lights coming on one at a time like they were being remembered. A feed store. A church with a marquee. A hardware place. And near the end, on a corner, a general store with a wooden sign gone soft at the edges — HOLMES GENERAL — and a screened front door and a light in the window the color of butter.

She pulled up in front of it and put the car in park and left it running.

"This is you," she said.

He looked at the store. He hadn't asked to come here. He'd asked for east, for work, for anywhere, and she'd taken all three and made a decision out of them, and here he was in front of a lit-up store in a town he'd never heard of, delivered.

"Why here?"

"Because you'll get work here, and because you look like you could use a place that closes at six." She reached across him — that unlooked-for nearness, the smell of coffee and cold air off her jacket — and popped his door the way she'd popped it three hours and a lifetime ago. "Go on. Tell them Anna sent you. Or don't. Suit yourself."

He got out. He took his duffel out of the back and stood on the sidewalk with it, and he leaned down to say a thing he didn't have ready — a thank-you, maybe, which he was bad at, or a question, which he was worse at.

"Who are you?" is what came out. "To this town, I mean."

And she gave him the smile again, the thought-about one, and put the car in gear.

"In a way," she said, "I'm nobody you've met yet."

Then she was a pair of taillights going down the long block and turning off it, and he was standing in front of Holmes General with his bag in his hand and a burger in his stomach and a song he couldn't name still going somewhere behind his teeth.

He made himself for two out of habit before he remembered. He'd have told her — Selena — about the woman on the road, the whole strange delivered evening, done the voices, gotten it a little wrong on purpose because she liked catching him at it. He got as far as opening the door of that in his head and then shut it, the way you shut a door on a cold room.

The light in the store window was the color of butter and somebody was moving around behind it, closing up.

He went to the screen door and knocked.
