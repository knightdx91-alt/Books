# Voice DNA — The Long Goodnight

> Executable voice spec. The Writer writes FROM this; Dialogue Polish and Hook
> Craft check AGAINST it; Editor and Evaluator hold the line with it.
> Built to DIVERGE from the author's other three books — see Section 6.

---

## 1. Global Narrative Voice

Strict single third-limited, **Xavier only**, for the entire main narrative line.
The narration is Xavier's consciousness rendered in close past tense. Within his
line the reader never knows a thing Xavier doesn't, and Anna's offscreen life
reaches the page only as report or aftermath. **The one exception** is the 3–4
concealed **Watcher interludes** (see the Watcher voice-card in §2 and the amended
POV lock in outline.md) — the sole non-Xavier POV, and even there the antagonist's
*identity* is withheld, never his menace.

- **Vocabulary level:** 4/10. Plain, concrete, worked-with-his-hands nouns.
  Xavier notices tools, weather, the give of a floorboard, the price of things.
  He reaches for a "literary" word maybe twice a chapter, and when he does it
  should feel like a man surprising himself.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Mixed, but weighted short. Grief makes him economical.
  Long sentences happen only when he's avoiding a feeling — the syntax runs on
  to keep from stopping at the thing that hurts. Then a short one lands on it
  anyway.
- **Formality:** Measured-plain. Not curt, not folksy. A decent man being
  careful with words because words have cost him.
- **Humor presence:** Dry, rare, mostly self-directed and mostly unspoken. He
  registers the funny thing and doesn't say it. When Anna is funny, the
  narration lets her be funny without competing.
- **Emotional register:** Numb-forward. This is the whole book. Xavier reports
  his own feelings at a delay, or geographically — he feels things in his hands,
  his chest, the back of his neck, not in named emotions. He almost never says
  "I was sad." He says the coffee had gone cold and he hadn't noticed drinking
  it.
- **Distinctive features:**
  - **Grief as absence, not presence.** Selena is rendered by the shape of
    where she isn't — the second toothbrush, the passenger seat, the way he
    still cooks for two and throws half out. Name her sparingly. The withholding
    IS the grief.
  - **Somatic displacement.** Emotion lands in the body or in objects, never
    announced. Counter to the "wave of X washed over him" default.
  - **The delayed clause.** He notices the important thing, then the mundane
    thing, then circles back. Memory and attention misfire the way real numb
    attention does.
  - **Present-tense grief-intrusions.** Two or three times in the book, mid
    past-tense scene, one short sentence slips into present tense when Selena
    surfaces. Deliberate. Flag each to the Editor so it isn't "corrected."
- **Anti-patterns (the narration NEVER does):**
  - Names the emotion it just dramatized ("he felt a deep sadness").
  - Explanatory similes that decode the image ("the silence was like X, which
    meant Y").
  - Metacognitive stacking ("he realized he was realizing").
  - Competence cascade — Xavier fixing everything smoothly to look capable.
    He's out of practice with PEOPLE; let him be clumsy with them even when
    he's deft with a hinge.
  - Romance-novel interiority: no cataloguing of Anna's beauty, no swooning
    inventory. He notices her the way he notices weather changing — something
    shifting that he can't stop.

### Voice Under Pressure (mandatory)

When Xavier is overwhelmed, the prose **goes flatter, not louder.** Sentences
get shorter and more literal. Description drops to bare function — he stops
seeing texture and starts seeing exits, distances, the time on a clock. Under
maximum stress (the fire) the syntax fragments and the present tense breaks in
and stays a beat too long before past tense reasserts. Humor vanishes entirely.
He counts things. That is his tell: when Xavier starts counting — steps, seconds,
dollars — he is barely holding on.

## 2. Per-Character Voice Cards

#### Xavier Steadman
- **Vocabulary band:** Trades and weather; blue-collar precise. Says "figured,"
  "suppose," "all right." Avoids emotional vocabulary — physically can't reach
  for it.
- **Syntax fingerprint:** Short declaratives. Answers questions with fewer words
  than asked. Trails off rather than lands when the subject nears Selena.
- **Rhythm:** Statements, not questions. When he does ask a question it matters.
- **Verbal tics:** "It's fine." (It is never fine.) Deflects thanks. Repeats the
  other person's last word back as a stall while he decides how much to say.
- **Never says:** Anything effusive. No "beautiful," no declarations, no naming
  of his own grief. Won't say Selena's name aloud until late in the book — track
  the first time he does; it's an event.
- **Sample line:** "Store needs a new door hinge. I can do that today." (What he
  means: I would like to be useful to you. I don't have other words.)

#### Anna Holmes
- **Vocabulary band:** Sharp, contemporary, quick. Small-town literate — she runs
  the register and reads the customers. Warm but with an edge she deploys on
  purpose.
- **Syntax fingerprint:** Complete, quick sentences with a turn at the end. Sets
  a man up and drops the floor out — the double-cheeseburger beat is her whole
  method: bland surface, deadpan payload.
- **Rhythm:** Questions as scalpels. She asks the thing you hoped she wouldn't.
- **Verbal tics:** "In a way." (Her coyness tell — used in Act 1 to hide being
  George's daughter; retro-loads as fair-play.) Deadpan menu/ordering deflations
  for men who flirt at her.
- **Never says:** Anything that mistakes her for decoration. Won't perform
  reassurance. Won't say she's scared until she's very scared — she downplays the
  stalking to stay in control of it, which is exactly what raises the stakes.
- **Under pressure:** Gets funnier and more clipped as cover, then goes quiet
  when the joke won't hold. The silence from Anna is the alarm.
- **Sample line:** "You want the double cheeseburger. Trust me. It's the most
  interesting thing that's going to happen to you today."

#### George Holmes
- **Vocabulary band:** Old-store folksy, unhurried, proverbs he half-believes.
  Talks to customers and product alike.
- **Syntax fingerprint:** Runs long and circles; buries the kind thing in the
  middle of a story about something else. Never says the warm thing directly —
  smuggles it.
- **Rhythm:** Storyteller cadence; the reveal is always at the end and always
  underplayed. ("Well, looks like you've already met my daughter.")
- **Verbal tics:** "Well—" to open. Calls Xavier "son" once, and it lands hard.
- **Never says:** A cruel word, or a rushed one. His warmth is the book's warmest
  register — protect it so his death costs.
- **Under pressure:** Doesn't change — that's the tragedy. He's mid-ordinary-
  sentence when the heart attack takes him. No dramatic last words.
- **Sample line:** "Well— we don't hire strangers here. We hire people who
  haven't been introduced yet. Grab an apron."

#### Derek Vance
- **Vocabulary band:** Charming-flat. Public register is smooth, reasonable,
  likeable — which is the danger. Private register (rare, Xavier only catches
  glimpses) goes possessive and cold.
- **Syntax fingerprint:** Reasonable-sounding sentences with an ownership word
  hidden inside ("my Anna," "she'll come around"). Present-tense certainty about
  a future that isn't his.
- **Rhythm:** Calm statements that are actually threats. Never raises his voice
  until the very end.
- **Verbal tics:** Refers to Anna in the possessive. The menace line — "we'll see
  when she breaks up with him" — said lightly, like a joke. Uses the private-
  memory detail (the fair-play clue) as if it's tenderness.
- **Never says:** Anything overtly menacing in public. His whole cover is
  plausibility. The reader should like him a little and hate themselves for it.
- **Under pressure:** The control that hides him all book **shatters** at the
  arson — that's why he slips and leaves evidence. Careful → raging → sloppy.
  Rendered only through Xavier's secondhand perception and the aftermath.
- **Sample line:** "She's just upset. She gets like this. She always calls me back —
  you can ask anybody who actually knows her."

#### Caleb Ruiz
- **Vocabulary band:** Local, earnest, a little stiff. Means well and says it
  badly. Not smooth like Derek — the contrast matters.
- **Syntax fingerprint:** Starts sentences he doesn't finish around Anna. Blunt
  and grudging toward Xavier — resentment worn openly, which (fair-play) is
  exactly why he's NOT the culprit: he hides nothing.
- **Rhythm:** Short, defensive, over-explains when nervous.
- **Verbal tics:** "I'm just saying." Mutters. Won't meet Xavier's eye.
- **Never says:** A smooth lie. He's a bad liar, and the reader can tell — his
  transparency is the tell that clears him.
- **Under pressure:** Gets loud and clumsy and immediately regrets it. Genuinely
  helps in the crisis, which completes his turn from suspect to ally.
- **Sample line:** "I've known her since we were nine. You've known her a month.
  I'm just saying."

#### The Watcher (concealed-antagonist interlude voice)
> This is the stalker's interior, used ONLY in the 3–4 set-off interludes (see the
> amended POV lock). It is Derek — but it must NEVER read as identifiably Derek, or
> as any single suspect. Treat it as a **fourth, deliberately anonymous voice.**
- **Vocabulary band:** Cold, flat, controlled. Plain words, short declaratives.
  No charm (that would fingerprint Derek), no fumbling (that would fingerprint
  Caleb), no grief-somatics (that would fingerprint Xavier). Ownership language
  kept abstract — "she," "the thing," "what's mine" — never "my Anna" (Derek's
  public tic must not leak into the interludes).
- **Syntax fingerprint:** Calm, patient, sequential. The horror is the *reasonable
  tone* applied to unreasonable content. Present-tense certainty about her
  feelings ("she remembers," "she'll understand"). Repetition as fixation.
- **Concealment rules (hard):** Never names himself, Anna's men, his job, his
  vehicle, or any single-suspect physical tell. Refers to himself as "he." Every
  line literally true in hindsight (withhold, never lie). May carry **reread-plant
  facts** (e.g. "you don't throw a thing back and have it stay thrown") only after
  the necklace history is planted in Xavier's line.
- **Never says:** His own name; "my Anna"; anything that decodes to one suspect
  before its fair-play window; any statement false after the Ch27 reveal.
- **Under pressure (late book):** The control fissures into rage right at the arson
  — this is the ONLY place the flat voice heats up, and the heat is *why* he slips
  and leaves the evidence that gets him caught. Reserve it.
- **Sample line:** "He had a good spot now, past the propane cage where the
  streetlight quit. She moved around the lit store not knowing she was watched.
  That was the part he liked. The not-knowing."

## 3. Voice Differentiation Matrix

| Character | Sharpest distinguisher | Contrast vs nearest |
|-----------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Xavier | Numb somatic displacement; won't name feelings or Selena; counts under stress | vs Caleb: Xavier withholds, Caleb overspills — both inarticulate, opposite directions |
| Anna | Deadpan payload after a bland setup; "in a way" coy tell | vs George: both fold meaning into surface talk, but Anna's turn is a blade, George's is a hug |
| George | Long circling stories that bury the kind thing at the end | vs Anna: George's turn warms, Anna's cuts |
| Derek | Reasonable sentences with a possessive word hidden inside; calm = threat | vs Caleb: Derek's resentment is CONCEALED and smooth; Caleb's is OPEN and clumsy — the whole misdirection lives in this contrast |
| Caleb | Unfinished sentences, "I'm just saying," can't lie | vs Derek: transparency vs concealment |
| The Watcher | Cold flat reasonable interior applied to obsessive content; abstract ownership ("the thing," "what's mine") | vs Derek: must NOT carry Derek's charm or "my Anna" — the interlude voice is deliberately un-matchable to any suspect |

**Cover-the-name test pre-solved:** the two men circling Anna are the collision
risk. Rule: **Derek is always smooth and never finishes short; Caleb is always
clumsy and never finishes his sentences.** If a line could belong to either,
it's wrong — sharpen until concealment vs. transparency is audible.

## 4. Anti-Pattern Budget (per 1,000 words — Romantic Suspense calibration)

- Explanatory/analytical similes (Pattern #11, the #1 AI tell): **≤4**. In
  Xavier's numb register, aim lower — ~2. Under-decode on purpose.
- Adverbs in dialogue tags: **near-zero.** Anna and Derek especially carry
  subtext in the words, not the tag.
- "It was as if / almost as though": **≤1.**
- Metacognitive narration ("he realized he was realizing"): **≤1.**
- Emotional temperature reports ("a wave of sadness washed over him"): **≤1** —
  and every instance is a Writer failure to use somatic displacement. Prefer the
  body or the object.
- Beauty-inventory of the love interest: **0.** Hard ban. Xavier notices change
  and presence, never a catalogue of features.
- Competence-cascade (protagonist smoothly fixing things to look capable): budget
  it AGAINST his social clumsiness — deft with objects, awkward with people.

## 6. Cross-Book Divergence Lock (why this voice is NOT the author's usual)

The author's other three protagonists must not rhyme with Xavier. The
differentiators to HOLD:

1. **Grief-numbness as the default register** — not a mood Xavier passes through
   but the water he swims in. Feelings arrive at a delay and in the body. No
   other book of the author's runs on this displaced, under-named affect.
2. **Withholding as method** — Selena is rendered by absence; Xavier won't say
   her name until an earned late beat. A romance protagonist who refuses the
   vocabulary of romance.
3. **Anti-swoon** — zero beauty inventory, zero declarations. The love story is
   told in hinges fixed and coffee gone cold, not in longing paragraphs.
4. **Counting-under-pressure tell** — a somatic signature unique to this book.
5. **Present-tense grief-intrusions** — a formal device (2-3 across the book)
   reserved for Selena's surfacings; do not use it anywhere else.

If a passage could be lifted into one of the other three books unchanged, it has
failed this section.
