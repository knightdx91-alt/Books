# Chapter One

## The Roof and the Temple

The roof of Saint Andre's orphanage was the one place Balthazaar could go to be away from everyone, and he had long ago decided that was worth the risk of dying for.

It was a flat roof, mostly, grey slate tiles laid in even rows that had gone soft and crumbling at their edges with two centuries of rain. None of the other children ever came up here. They weren't supposed to — a boy had gone off the north gutter and broken himself on the flagstones four years back, and after that the door to the stairs had been nailed shut and the story told to every new arrival as a warning. The nails had never stopped Bal. He'd learned which window let onto the kitchen lean-to, and from the lean-to it was an easy climb. He came up here when the noise of forty children in a building meant for thirty pressed too hard against the inside of his skull.

The orphanage had been built sometime in the seventeen hundreds, three storeys of dark stone at the end of a lane that no one ever seemed to walk down. It sagged in the way old buildings sagged, but it held. Behind it, separated from the kitchen garden by a low wall, stood the thing the children called the mausoleum — a windowless block of pale stone the size of a chapel, its doors banded in iron, its roofline crowded with carvings worn faceless by weather. Every child who had ever lived at Saint Andre's had tried those doors at one time or another. None had opened them. The building kept its secrets the way the dead keep theirs.

Bal sat on the slope of the roof with his knees drawn up and rubbed, without thinking about it, at the stump of his left wrist.

It itched. It always itched when he looked at the mausoleum, a deep crawling itch under skin that wasn't there, in a hand he had never owned. He couldn't remember the hand. He'd been born without it, or near enough — there were no records, only the matron's word that he'd come to them as an infant already missing it, wrapped in a blanket with no name pinned to the cloth. Bal had named himself, more or less. Balthazaar, because it sounded like someone who mattered, and Bal to anyone he liked enough to let close.

He watched the other children parade their sigils, sometimes. It happened around their twelfth or thirteenth year — the mark surfacing on the back of a hand or the inside of a forearm, a thin bright line of color that told the world what kind of magic lived in you. Fire ran orange. Water ran a deep tidal blue. The marks were a map and a passport both; certain factions wouldn't so much as look at you without one, and the schools wouldn't take you at all. Bal had no sigil and no hand to grow one on. He could still work magic — clumsier than the marked children, slower, every spell a thing he had to muscle through by force where they simply let it flow. But he could do it. There were children at Saint Andre's who couldn't do even that. He told himself that meant he was lucky, and on the good days he half believed it.

Anyway, he'd never much wanted to do things the way other people did them.

Which was the whole reason he was on the roof that afternoon with a rolled carpet under his arm, about to try something that could kill him.

Three days earlier he'd been up here after lunch, tracing the worn carvings along the mausoleum's cornice with his eyes, when the roof of the building had flickered. Just for a heartbeat — the solid pale stone had gone thin as smoke, and through it he'd seen, impossibly, the inside: a vaulted space lit warm and gold, high pillars, and at the far end a statue, enormous, its face turned away. Then the stone had been stone again, as if it had never been anything else.

He'd thought he was seeing things. The light, maybe, or hunger, or the particular boredom that made your eyes invent company. But he'd kept coming back, and the itch in his missing wrist had kept getting worse, and somewhere in those three days the question had stopped being *did I see that* and become *how do I get over there*.

There was no ladder tall enough. He couldn't jump the gap. That left magic, which left exactly one spell he had any business attempting — a levitation charm out of a book called *Sygldry, Year Two*, left behind by an older boy named Fredrick who'd finally gotten his sigil and gone off to the proper school. Fredrick had left Bal his whole shelf of beginner texts. It had been a kindness and a cruelty at once. Bal had read every one of them twice, knowing the entire time that no school would ever let a sigil-less boy through its gates, that self-study was the only schooling he was ever going to get.

He unrolled the carpet on the tiles — a threadbare runner, gold thread gone dull, a border of sky-blue that had once probably been bright. He knelt at its edge and set the stump of his left wrist against the weave, because that was the part of him that itched, and because some stubborn childish part of him still believed that if he pushed magic through the place where his hand should be, a sigil might finally bloom there.

"*Levimotorcorpus,*" he said, and shoved.

That was always the part the books didn't explain — the shove. For other mages the magic ran like water downhill. For Bal there was a blockage where his wrist ended, a wall he had to lean his whole will against until something gave. He leaned. Something gave. The words finished leaving his mouth and the carpet shivered under his arm, lifted, and rose a clean two feet into the air, steady as a shelf.

A grin pulled at his face before he could stop it. He grabbed his backpack, shrugged into the straps, and sat cross-legged on the floating runner, and then — gently, gently, he told himself — he willed it forward.

The carpet did not do gentle. It shot off the roofline like a startled cat. Bal's stomach stayed behind on the tiles. The mausoleum's roof rushed up to meet him and he braced for the bone-jarring slam of slate against his knees —

— and there was no roof.

His hands closed on nothing. The carpet dropped through the place the stone should have been as if the stone were a painting of stone, and Bal had one wild lurching moment of falling before he got hold of himself and willed the runner *down, slower, down* — and it carried him in a tightening spiral past warm light and high pillars and set him, soft as a sigh, on a floor of solid cold concrete.

The carpet, its charm spent, went limp and slid out from under him.

Bal sat where he'd landed and breathed.

The inside of the mausoleum was no mausoleum at all. It was a temple — there was no other word for it. Two braziers taller than a grown man burned at the far end, throwing a soft amber light that climbed the pillars and lost itself somewhere overhead. The pillars were old in a way the orphanage only pretended to be, fluted Greek columns gone gold in the firelight, and between them the open roof showed a square of bright afternoon sky. He'd seen no opening from outside. He'd seen a solid roof flicker once in three days. He looked up at that patch of impossible daylight and felt the back of his neck prickle.

At the far end stood the statue from his vision, and now that he was beneath it he could see it was a figure — a woman, robed, her stone face turned toward the braziers and worn smooth by something that wasn't weather, because no weather reached in here.

"Because it's magic," a voice murmured against his ear, warm and amused and very close.

Bal came up off the floor and spun, the dead carpet kicking out from under his heel, and there was no one there. The temple was empty. He turned a full slow circle, heart going hard.

"Hello?" he said.

For a moment nothing answered. Then a shape detached itself from the pooled dark at the back of the temple, where the braziers' light gave up, and stepped into the gold.

A girl. Older than him, he thought — a few years older, though there was something about the way she held the age that made the number feel beside the point. She was pale, properly pale, the white of someone the sun had never bargained with. Her hair was black past black and fell to the middle of her back, and small teardrop rubies hung from her ears and caught the fire and threw it. The black dress she wore was old-fashioned and very fine. She looked at Bal with her head tilted and an expression he couldn't read — curiosity, mostly, with something sharper underneath it.

"Hello yourself," she said. "Would you mind telling me how you got in?"

Bal, who had never once in his life been at a loss for something to say, found his mouth empty. He pointed at the ceiling.

The girl arched one perfect eyebrow. "You *flew* in."

He nodded.

"Huh." She came a step closer, and her bare feet made no sound on the concrete. "No one's ever tried that one. Six hundred years and not one of them looked up. What gave you the idea?"

So he told her — about the roof, the flicker, the glimpse of pillars and the statue, the three days of trying to think of a way across. About the carpet. The words came easier as he talked, the way they always did; talking was the one thing he'd never lacked a hand for.

"I honestly thought I'd cracked," he finished. "Seeing temples where there's only a tomb." He looked at her. "Who are you? And how did *you* get in, if no one ever has?"

The girl laughed — a bright, high sound that rang off the stone — and waved one hand. Two benches of pale stone rose up out of the floor between them as though they had always been waiting just beneath it.

"Sit," she said, "and I'll tell you."

Bal stared at the benches. He had pushed his whole will against a blockage in his wrist just to lift one carpet a single foot; she had raised furniture out of solid rock with a flick, the way another person might brush off a sleeve. He sat, slowly, on the nearer bench. The girl folded herself onto the other and arranged her skirts.

"My name is Amelia," she said. "And this is my temple."

"A temple to what?"

She frowned, and something in the frown was almost hurt. "You've truly never heard of me. The Queen of the Vampires."

Bal's mouth fell open.

"Wait." He held up his one hand. "You mean — the originator of the Blood sygl? *That* Amelia?" He could hear how the words sounded even as he said them. "I thought you were a story. A myth. Something they made up to scare little kids off the dark spells."

For a moment Amelia simply looked at him. Then her face came apart into outrage.

"A *myth*." She was on her feet without seeming to have stood. "They have made me a *myth*?"

The braziers flared. Bal scrambled backward off the bench, palm raised, every nerve telling him to be somewhere — anywhere — that was not here. Amelia saw it. He watched her see it, watched her pull the fury back into herself by main force, her shoulders coming down, her hands unclenching one finger at a time.

"I'm sorry," she said, and the high ring had gone out of her voice. "I wasn't expecting that. Though I suppose I should have been." She tilted her head, and her gaze dropped to his left arm. "What happened to your sygl?"

"My —"

"Your hand." She nodded at the stump. "Did the thought of carrying a myth on your wrist disgust you so badly you cut the whole thing off?"

Bal went still.

"What do you mean," he said. It didn't come out as a question.

Amelia's anger drained away entirely, and what replaced it was something slower and stranger — a kind of wondering amusement. "My," she said softly. "How the fates do love their work." She sank back onto her bench, watching him. "You lost your hand for showing my mark, child. And then you flew across a courtyard on a rug and fell into my temple by accident."

Bal sat down. He didn't decide to; his legs simply stopped holding him.

"You're saying I had a Blood sygl," he said.

"A strong one. A good clean connection." She studied the stump as if she could still see the mark that had been there. "Better than most who carry mine knowing what they carry."

And Bal, who had spent seventeen years being told the truth about himself in pieces by people who didn't much care how the pieces landed, heard the truth in her voice and felt it settle into him like cold water.

Blood sygls weren't outlawed. Not technically. But they were the marks people crossed the street to avoid, the ones that made grandmothers make signs against the evil eye. He'd heard the stories all his life — that Blood mages went mad, that the magic ate them from the inside, that it turned them into something that wore a person's face. There were doctors, he knew, who would take the hand off a newborn the moment a Blood mark surfaced, quietly, before the parents had a chance to grow attached. Bal had always suspected most of it was fear dressed up as medicine, a story told to keep people frightened of a thing they didn't understand. He'd never had reason to suspect it had been done to *him*.

"No one's even sure the Blood originator still exists," he heard himself say. "You. They thought maybe it was Dracula, except then somebody killed Dracula and the mark didn't die with him, so —" He stopped. "Every year all the originators come to the Sygldry School to claim their own. The students with their marks. There's a place set for the Blood originator. Every year. You never come."

"No," Amelia said. "I never could."

She didn't explain it yet. She only sat with her hands in her lap and let him work the rest of it out, and Bal found he could. He could see it the way you could suddenly see the shape of a word you'd been mispronouncing your whole life. He looked around the temple — the pillars, the impossible sky, the doors he had never been able to reach from outside because there had been no way *in* — and understood that it was not a temple at all.

It was a cell.

"They locked you in here," he said.

Amelia exploded.

Literally — Bal flinched as she came apart into a churning cloud of bats, hundreds of them, wings and small shrill voices filling the temple and wheeling around the braziers before they folded back, all at once, into the shape of a furious young woman standing exactly where she'd sat.

"They locked me in," she said, low, "and then they told the world I'd abandoned my followers. They made me into a bedtime story so that no one would come looking." Her red eyes found him in the firelight. "Why haven't you gone to school, child? You crossed a warded courtyard on a beginner's charm. You have more raw magic in that one hand than half the marked brats they let through those gates."

Bal lifted the stump. "No sigil," he said. "No school takes you without one. You know that better than anyone, apparently."

Amelia's eyes narrowed. The firelight slid red along them. And then, slowly, a grin spread across her face that Bal felt in the pit of his stomach.

"And if you *had* a sygl," she said. "Would you go?"

"Of course." It came out before he could weigh it. "I've wanted to go my whole life."

"Would you do anything to go?"

She rose as she said it and crossed the floor toward him, unhurried, her bare feet silent on the stone, her eyes never once leaving his.

Bal swallowed. "Uh," he said. "Within reason."

Amelia laughed, and the danger went out of the air, and she dropped onto the bench beside him close enough that he could feel the cold coming off her like a window in winter.

"You'll go to this school," she said. "I'll bind myself to you as your familiar. The bond will break this place open for me — I can't walk out of here on my own, but I can walk out at your side, as something that belongs to you and you to it. And then you and I will go and remind the whole sneering world that the Blood Bond is alive and very, very well." She turned her head and met his eyes, and there was something almost grave under the playfulness now. "I will never ask of you anything you aren't willing to give. You have my word, and my word is older than their lies."

Bal's heart was going hard again, for a different reason this time. "How," he managed. "How do I get a sygl back? You can't just — grow a hand."

Amelia smiled, slow and sharp, and the firelight found the points of her teeth.

"Are you thirsty?" she asked.

As it turned out, you could grow a hand. You grew it by drinking the blood of a thousand-year-old goddess from her cupped white palm, while the magic she'd been hoarding for six centuries poured down your throat like cold fire and remade you from the wrist outward. It hurt and it didn't; it felt like having a limb you'd forgotten you owned go suddenly, blazingly to sleep and then wake all at once. When it was done Bal sat on the stone bench flexing five new fingers and turning them in the brazier-light, and on the inside of his new left wrist a mark glowed faintly — a deep arterial red, threaded with something that pulsed like a slow heartbeat.

His sygl. The Blood sygl. His.

"I don't know how I'm going to explain this," he said. His voice came out rough. "A new hand. You. Any of it. Things like this don't just *happen* to people."

Amelia stood and stretched, unhurried, and her dress rode up her pale legs in a way that pulled Bal's eyes despite everything, and she caught him looking and laughed, low and pleased.

"Time enough for that later," she said. "I'll see to your hand — to the story of it, so the world doesn't faint. And to your school. But first there's one last part of the binding, and then you can take us both out of this tomb."

She knelt on the cold floor in front of him, between his knees, and looked up.

"Do you know the words?" she asked. "For the familiar binding."

Bal's heart climbed into his throat. He looked down into those soft red eyes — soft now, none of the fury left in them, only a kind of patient ancient hope — and nodded. He set his new left palm flat against her forehead. The mark on his wrist warmed against her skin.

"*Caro familiare,*" he said. "I offer you this soul bond. May I be worthy to hold a place in your heart." He felt his ears go hot on the last of it and was glad of the firelight.

Amelia smiled, and the smile was gentle, and she answered.

"*Caro maestro.* I accept your bond. May our blood sing together for a thousand years, and more."

A second mark surfaced as she finished — the Blood sygl, the twin of his own, blooming red on her brow beneath his palm. Bal felt something pull tight between them, a thread drawn through the center of his chest, and the strength went out of him all at once. He let his hand fall and rested his forehead against hers because he didn't trust himself to keep his head up otherwise. They stayed like that, breathing the same warm air, for a few long minutes.

"How do we get out?" he asked at last, opening his eyes to find hers already open and watching him.

"Through the front door, of course." She giggled — actually giggled, six hundred years of queen falling away — and stood, brushing the dust from her knees.

"But it's locked," Bal said. He looked back at the great golden doors at the temple's mouth, the ones he'd seen banded in iron from the outside his whole life. "Everyone's tried it. For two hundred years. Nobody's ever —"

"It's only locked one way." Amelia pulled him up by his new hand and started toward the doors, talking as she went. "Even without the bond, you could have walked right out of here whenever you liked — you came in through the roof; the door would have let *you* go. It was only ever locked to me. And now I'm yours." She glanced back at him, and there was wonder in it. "Now it has to let me through too."

She set both hands to the doors and pushed, and the great golden valves that had defeated two centuries of orphan children swung wide without a sound — and Amelia stopped on the threshold, the late afternoon falling full across her pale face, and simply stood.

"It's been a long time," she said, very softly. And for a moment she didn't look like a goddess or a myth or the Queen of anything. She looked like a girl who had been shut in the dark so long she couldn't quite believe the door had opened.

Bal took her hand. "Come on," he said. "Let's go outside."

Hand in hand they crossed the threshold into the sun, and the temple let them go.

---

They came around the side of the orphanage with the long shadows reaching across the garden wall.

"I live here," Bal said, nodding up at the dark stone bulk of the place. "My mom died having me, so they brought me here. It's been home as long as I can remember." He glanced sideways at her. "Do you know anything about — the modern world? Cars, electric lights, any of it? I don't want you fainting in the road."

Amelia made a small offended sound. "I can speak to certain creatures," she said. "Bats. Ravens. The clever ones that go everywhere and see everything and gossip worse than washerwomen. I've been locked in a box, child, not asleep. I know what a motorcar is. I simply haven't ridden in one." She considered. "I'd like to."

That was a relief, and not only because of the cars. The schools sat on a different plane entirely — a fold of the world with no engines and no electric light, where only those who'd attended a magical school, and their families, were allowed to live. He'd half feared dragging a six-hundred-year-old goddess across into all of that and watching her come apart at the seams. Apparently she'd come apart at fewer seams than he would.

As they rounded the front of the building the noise of the others reached them — shouting, the crack of a bat, the particular ragged cheer of a game nobody was keeping proper score in. The orphans had a softball game going in the front yard, all the long afternoon energy of children who'd been let outdoors. Amelia stopped to watch it, her head tilted, that wondering look on her face again, and Bal let her watch for a few minutes before he drew her in through the front door.

The front hall of Saint Andre's was a dull grey, its white floor tiles echoing under their feet the way they always did, so that you could never sneak anywhere in the place. Most of the children were still outside, which suited Bal — he needed to get to Mrs. Brown's office, and he needed to do it without an audience.

Mrs. Brown was one of the women who ran the orphanage. She was somewhere in her forties, always in the same uniform of light grey skirt and white blouse, her greying hair pulled back into a bun so tight it looked like it must ache. Plenty of the children were afraid of her; she handed out punishments that left an impression, and she caught nearly everything. Bal had worked out his own arrangement with the fear of her a long time ago — if a thing was worth getting caught for, then you'd decided in advance it was worth the punishment, and there was no sense being upset when the bill came due. And underneath all of that he'd always known, with a certainty he didn't examine too closely, that if he ever truly needed someone — if he ever genuinely, badly messed up — Mrs. Brown was the one door in the building that would open.

This was one of those times.

Five wooden chairs stood in a row beside her office door. Bal knocked twice, then sat, and motioned for Amelia to do the same.

"What exactly are we doing?" Amelia asked, dropping into the chair beside his and looking up and down the echoing hall as if cataloguing the exits.

"I need to talk to someone about the hand," Bal said. "And about school. There's a new orientation class starting in two weeks — three boys and two girls left a few days ago, so there's room. If I'm ever going to go, it's now."

"Come in," called a voice from behind the door.

Bal stood and tipped his head for Amelia to follow. Mrs. Brown's office had as much personality as a cell — two filing cabinets, a desk, a single framed photograph of her dead husband angled toward her chair. She was bent over some form, writing, and they waited in front of the closed door until she set down her pen and looked up.

And froze.

Bal watched it land on her — the new hand first, his left hand resting easy at his side where for seventeen years there'd been nothing, and then the pale girl in the old black dress standing at his shoulder. Mrs. Brown's pen-hand hung in the air.

Bal got in ahead of the questions. He laid it out fast and plain — the roof, the carpet, the temple that was really a prison, the goddess who'd been made into a myth, the blood and the bond and the mark on his wrist. He held the wrist up so she could see the sygl pulse there in the office light. By the time he finished he honestly couldn't tell which had rocked her harder: that he had two hands now, or that one of them belonged, in a manner of speaking, to the Queen of the Vampires.

Mrs. Brown opened her mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. For a moment she looked, Bal thought, exactly like a goldfish, and he had to bite the inside of his cheek.

"Well then," she said at last, and sat back.

They looked at each other across the desk for a long moment. Then Mrs. Brown laughed — a real laugh, startled out of her.

"You don't do anything by half measures, do you," she said.

Bal grinned despite himself. "I can honestly say it's not how I pictured my afternoon going." He turned the wrist again, sobering. "Is it going to be a problem, though? You know how the schools are about Blood sygls."

Mrs. Brown's smile faded. She picked her pen back up and tapped it against the desk, thinking it through.

"They won't like it," she said slowly. "But they don't actually have a choice — not if she shows herself." The pen pointed at Amelia. "When they call the roll for the Blood mark, you'll stand, and they'll ask for your originator to claim you. If no one stands for you, you don't enroll. Simple as that. It's how they've kept Blood mages out for years without ever having to put it in writing — there was never anyone to do the claiming." She set the pen down and leaned forward, her chair creaking under her. "But she can stand for you. And once she does, in front of the whole school and every other originator, they can't refuse you without admitting to the world exactly what they've done."

"They're not going to be glad I'm there," Bal said.

"No," Mrs. Brown agreed. "The only reason they haven't outright banned the Blood mark from those gates is that they want to be seen as fair. That's the same reason they locked her away instead of simply destroying her. A dead goddess is a martyr. A vanished one is just a story that stopped being told." She glanced at Amelia, and something careful came into her face. "It made it look like she'd abandoned her own."

Amelia inclined her head, and when she spoke the playfulness was gone and the six centuries were all there in her voice.

"That," she said, "and the fact that they knew they could not beat me in a fair fight."

She tucked a strand of black hair behind one ear and looked at Balthazaar — really looked at him, the new hand, the mark, the boy who'd flown a carpet into her tomb because he didn't know any better than to try.

"But I think," she said, and smiled, slow and certain and edged like a blade, "it's about time they were reminded what a true Blood mage can do."
