# Chapter Three

## The Claiming

An hour out from Verin's cottage the road began to fill.

Other buggies and carriages joined them one by one, falling into a loose procession through the thinning forest, each carrying students Bal's age and a little older, all of them dressed in the same dark school robes he and Amelia now wore. Verin slowed Thistle to a walk and fell in behind a buggy carrying two boys around Bal's age and an older girl, sixteen or so, with a sygl stitched across the back of her sweater that Bal didn't recognize at first — and then did, when it caught the morning light and threw off a thin crackle of actual lightning. The Tempus mark. Storm. The three of them shared a buggy and a certain set to the jaw, and Bal guessed they were kin.

And then the trees fell away, and Bal saw the school for the first time, and forgot the strangers entirely.

The Heir's School of Magykal Artes was the first school the originators had ever raised together, and no one alive remembered the year of its building. From a distance it was a castle — but a castle the way a cathedral is a building, which is to say technically. Five great stone towers ringed it, one for each originator, and between them the main keep sprawled so wide and high that Bal's eye couldn't take it in all at once. A curtain wall ran around the whole of it, and an iron portcullis stood raised above the gate, and beyond the gate, crowded into the inner courtyard, waited what looked like the entire incoming year.

They were gathered before a raised stone stage. On the stage stood five thrones of pale marble veined with blue, and on the front of each throne a sygl had been worked in gold, so that the marks gleamed and shifted in the sun. Bal counted them as the buggy rolled up to the wall: five thrones, five marks. One of them, he knew before he could read it, would be hers.

Verin drew them aside from the main flow and let them down. "Straight to the back of the crowd," he told them, "and don't wander. It won't be long now." He gave Amelia a long, considering look that he politely declined to put into words, lifted a hand in farewell, and turned old Thistle for home.

Bal and Amelia waded into the back of the crowd. The noise of it washed over him — fifty-odd voices all going at once, high and bright with nerves. The students had already sorted themselves by mark, Fire clustered with Fire, Water with Water, knots of strangers who shared a sygl finding each other and clinging on. Bal, marked with the one sygl no one else in the courtyard carried, stood at the edge of all of it with a goddess in disguise at his shoulder, and felt, for once, that being apart suited him fine.

"Why hold a ceremony at all," he asked under his breath, "if everyone already knows which mark they carry? It's not as though there's any suspense."

"Because it keeps a useful idea alive," Amelia murmured back. "The idea that we *chose* you. That an originator looked across the whole of the mortal world, saw you in particular, and reached down to set the mark on your hand by name." Her mouth quirked. "It isn't how it works, of course — the marks surface where they will, and we claim what surfaces. But for most of these children this is their first true step into the magical world, the morning they stop being ordinary. A thing like that stays with a person their whole life. We'd be fools to give it up." She glanced at the stage. "And we are not, whatever else we are, fools."

A gong sounded, deep and bronze, rolling out across the courtyard, and the chatter guttered and died.

"Can they sense you?" Bal whispered, the thought arriving too late to be any use. "Standing here? Can they feel you in the crowd?"

Amelia gave him a sidelong grin and shook her head, and a heartbeat later the air over the stage cracked white.

When Bal could see again, four of the five thrones were filled.

On the far left sat Aren, the Duality originator — an elderly man with a long white beard, brown robes, sandalwood sandals on his feet, the very picture of a storybook wizard. *That's the day-form,* Amelia breathed against his ear; *by night he's Nera, a young woman in white. Two faces, one self. The Duality mark is what it sounds like.* Beside Aren sat Kenmei of the Waves — tall and stately in robes that moved like water, brown-haired, her eyes a pale sea-green that seemed to look through the courtyard to some farther shore. Then Naisura of Terra, who looked like a man in his hardy thirties, bare-chested, in loose white drawstring trousers, as though he'd strolled up from working stone and hadn't bothered to dress for the occasion. And last, Raizen of Tempus — Storm — a middle-aged man in orange monk's robes, hands folded, face composed, the picture of serenity. Bal looked at him and thought of eight high priests dying in a ring of stone and found the serenity hard to forgive.

The fifth throne, the one whose golden mark Bal could now read plainly — the Blood sygl, twin to the one on his own wrist — sat empty.

Aren rose and walked to the edge of the stage, and the courtyard held its breath.

"Welcome," he said, and his voice carried without seeming to rise. "Most of you know that this was the first school we built, the five of us, together — and that we have always been particular about who is permitted to walk through its gates. Never forget it. Out of all the children of all the marks, you were chosen to come *here*, and not to one of the lesser schools. As of this morning you are Heirs, with everything that title carries." He spread his hands. "Now. It is time to begin. I will call my own first, and as I name you, you will come and stand behind my throne. Then Kenmei, and so on down the line, until every one of you stands claimed. When the last name is called, each of us will lead our own to their quarters, where your lead mage will give you your schedule. Any questions you hold for us — and hold them, for you may not see us again this year — must be asked then." He looked out over the upturned faces. "Is that understood?"

A wave of nods. Aren returned to his throne and began to read names.

There were fifty students, and so it took a while. After the first handful Bal stopped following it and leaned close to Amelia. "You all right?"

She nodded, but her eyes were too bright, fixed on the empty throne with her own mark on it. "You don't know how long I've waited for this," she said softly.

"About three thousand years, give or take?" Bal said.

That startled a laugh out of her, low and surprised. "Well — yes. I suppose you do know, at that." She wiped quickly at one eye. "I can promise you this much. Whatever else today is — it will be an initiation no one in this courtyard forgets."

The line of claimed students grew. Aren's, then Kenmei's, then Naisura's, then Raizen's — the storm-marked girl from the buggy and her two kin among them, falling into place behind the orange-robed originator. The crowd in front of the stage thinned and thinned, until at last the courtyard went quiet in a different way, and Bal looked around and understood that the quiet was about *them*.

He and Amelia were the only two left standing before the stage.

Raizen had stopped reading. There were no more names. And yet two students remained unclaimed, in plain view of everyone, and a low confused murmur began to rise behind the thrones as the realization spread.

Aren stood again. He crossed to the edge of the stage, and his old face had gone gentle and sorrowful in a way Bal trusted not at all.

"Young man," he called. "Come closer. Bring your companion."

They went. Bal kept his chin level and his pace even and was distantly proud that his legs obeyed him. At the foot of the stage Aren turned to the four seated originators.

"Are they yours?" he asked them.

One by one, each shook their head. Kenmei's eyes barely moved from the horizon. Naisura looked frankly bored. Raizen's serene face never flickered.

Aren turned back, and his expression arranged itself into compassion. "I'm sorry, young ones. If you carry a Blood sygl, then I fear you've come a very long way for nothing." He gestured, almost tenderly, at the empty fifth throne. "As you can see, Lady Amelia has not come to claim you. And unless an originator stands as your patron, you may not study here. It is not cruelty. It is simply the law we are all bound by — even us."

It was beautifully done. Bal had to give him that. Every word of it was true, and every word of it was a cage, and the old man had built the cage so the door looked like an open hand.

Bal stepped forward and pitched his voice to carry — not just to Aren, but past him, to the four on their thrones and the fifty students gathered behind them, so that there could be no taking it back later.

"But if she *did* claim us," he said. "Then we could attend. Couldn't we."

Aren nodded, sadly, generous in his defeat. "Of course you could. Any one of us may stand for any child of our own mark; that is the whole of the law. But alas — Lady Amelia has not been seen in a very, very long time. Longer than any of you have been alive. I am sorry, child. Truly."

Bal felt Amelia step up beside him.

She looked at Aren. Then she looked, slowly, down the line of the four originators on their thrones. And then she clapped her hands, once.

Aren stumbled backward as if struck. On their thrones, four faces that had not changed expression in three thousand years changed all at once — Kenmei's eyes snapping from the horizon to fix on Amelia, Naisura half-rising, Raizen's serene mask cracking clean down the middle into something Bal had not expected ever to see on the face of the man who'd ordered her chained.

Fear.

"I disguised myself," Amelia muttered to Bal, almost apologetic. "You could see me as I was, all along. No one else could." Bal nodded; it made a kind of sense he was getting used to. Amelia stepped past him and sprang lightly up onto the stage, and the four originators watched her cross it the way you watch a snake you'd thought was a stick — but she only walked to the empty fifth throne, the one with her mark in gold upon it, and laid her hand on its back.

"Balthazaar Norheart," she announced, and her voice filled the courtyard and silenced the last whisper in it. "I formally claim you. And I name you my High Priest."

Then she turned to face her family, and her red eyes narrowed, and the temperature of the whole courtyard seemed to drop a degree.

"Let the first of you who harms him do so at his peril."

No one moved. No one answered.

"There are things that must be said between us," Amelia went on, lower now, almost conversational, which was somehow worse. "But they will keep. You won't be returning to your domains until they're said, though — will you?"

One by one, quickly, the four originators shook their heads.

And in that small motion — four immortal beings, older than nations, agreeing without a word to stay exactly where a thousand-year-old woman told them to — Bal finally understood, in his gut and not just his head, why they had needed eight dying priests and three thousand years of stone to keep her where they wanted her. They hadn't beaten her. They'd only managed, once, to trick her. And she remembered.

Amelia turned back to Bal as though the matter were settled, which apparently it was, and beckoned him toward the stairs at the side of the stage. He climbed up and crossed behind the thrones, and felt the eyes of fifty students on him the whole way — confused, awed, a few openly thrilled, as if they'd come for a school ceremony and been handed a legend instead. Aren clapped his hands. The four claimed originators rose, swept down the stage's side stairs, and led their lines of new students away into the keep, and not one of them looked back at the Blood throne.

Amelia beckoned again, and Bal followed her into the school.

---

"I'll admit," Bal said, once they'd put a few corridors between themselves and the courtyard, "the furious Vampire Goddess look suited you. You wore it well."

Amelia laughed — the sound bouncing off the close stone walls of the passage, which had begun to slope downward. "They know what I *could* have done up there," she said. "If I'd really wanted to. They're grateful I didn't. That's most of what fear is, in the end — gratitude that hasn't been spent yet."

"Why didn't you?" Bal asked. The corridor opened into a longer one, hung with paintings he didn't have time to study. "Want to. You had them. Right there, all four of them, scared white. You could have —"

Amelia shrugged. "Could have. As angry and as hurt as I was when it first happened, three thousand years is a long time to hold a blade at the same heat. The anger cooled. It didn't go out — don't mistake me — but it cooled into something more useful." She didn't elaborate, and when Bal saw she wasn't going to, he let it lie.

"And the High Priest thing?" he asked instead. "You sprang that on me up there. 'I name you my High Priest.' First I'd heard of it."

Amelia winced. "Yes. I should have warned you first. I'm sorry — I did it to keep you safe, I swear to you that's the truth of it."

"Keep me safe how?"

"Any originator can interfere with any follower, to a degree — even a common priest. Push them, cloud them, bend their luck. It's part of what we are. But there is one rank they may not touch." She held up a finger. "A High Priest. Lay so much as a thought on another house's High Priest, and the offended originator may issue a formal challenge — and no one wants to be challenged. Least of all by me. I would be only too glad to go toe to toe with any one of them, and every one of them knows it." Her eyes glinted. "So now, if any of them moves against you, they aren't poking at a first year. They're poking at *my* High Priest, and through him, at me. This way I can stand behind you all the way — not just as far as a familiar's bond reaches. All the way."

Bal turned it over and found it sound. "Anything special I ought to know? About being a High Priest?"

Amelia hesitated, just for a breath. "We'll talk more about it tonight."

They came to a stop in front of a stone pedestal set into the wall, carved with the Blood sygl. "Put your left hand on it," Amelia said. Bal did, and felt a sharp prick against his palm — the stone drew a bead of his blood and used it, painting the sygl out in thin red lines across the carving. When the mark was complete the blood sank into the stone, and a section of wall ground aside to reveal a narrow flight of steps leading up.

Amelia led the way. The wall slid closed behind them.

"Every dorm is enchanted differently," she said as they climbed. "One of the workings on mine is that it has no fixed rooms. The space makes what it needs. Another student joins us — a new bedroom appears. We want a dining room — one's there. If you want a room to be permanent, you ask the lead mage." A grin. "Who is you. Or a High Priest. Who is also you." She laughed at her own joke, plainly delighted with the arrangement, and dropped onto one of the blood-red couches before a wide hearth, hooking her feet up onto the table.

"Will we get more students?" Bal asked, settling beside her.

"Eventually. Especially once word gets round that I'm back." She sighed, and then sat up straight, all business. "All right. The official part, before I go and deal with the family. Do you remember the way back up from here?"

Bal nodded.

"Good. I won't always be at your side, so you'll need to find your own way. You've four classes as a first year; I'll bring you the list once I've finished with them upstairs. There's a curfew — but as lead mage you're exempt, so don't trouble yourself about it. The library's on the second floor, since I know full well you'll go hunting for it the moment my back is turned. Make friends with the other Heirs if you can. If you can't, don't fret — they'll come round." She tipped her head, and a mischief came into her face. "And last. You like secrets."

"Cautiously," Bal said, "yes."

"So do I." She leaned toward him. "This place is full of them — old enchantments, hidden rooms, doors no one's opened since the stones were laid. If you find any I don't already know, anything the founders tucked away, I'll reward you for it. Consider it a standing offer."

Bal felt himself grin. "I can't wait."

"Neither can I," Amelia said. "Now — I really must go. If you need me, push your magyk into your sygl, and I'll come." She gave him a quick hard hug, snapped her fingers, and was gone between one breath and the next.

---

Bal spent the next few hours learning his new home, because that was simply what Bal did with a place he'd been told had secrets.

The dorm hadn't looked large when he'd come in — a hearth on the right with a couch and a wing-backed leather chair drawn up to it, a low table between them. But two doors stood in the far wall, and behind the first he found a bathroom, and behind the second a bedroom easily twice the size of the one he'd had at the orphanage. A four-poster canopy bed dominated it, broad enough to sleep five, and a great chest crouched at its foot. The bedclothes were soft and dark, a single deep-red rose embroidered on black across the coverlet. Bal stood in the doorway a moment, taking the unlikely size of it in, then closed the door, satisfied.

He thought, idly, about what else a dorm ought to have. A library, obviously. A kitchen. Somewhere to eat.

Three more doors appeared in the wall while he was still thinking it.

He opened the first to find a kitchen — a wood-burning stove, two chests off to the side that proved, when he lifted their lids, to hold meat and vegetables kept somehow cold, and a shelf of spices above them. Satisfied, he moved on to the second door and found the library: smaller than the common room, ringed with bookshelves that stood completely empty, five reading chairs gathered before yet another fireplace. The bare shelves stung a little — he'd been picturing them full the instant the room appeared — but he could feel already how good a place it would be to read, once he'd hunted down something to fill them with. The last door opened on a dining room, a long mahogany table with polished chairs marching down both sides.

Bal considered the kitchen and the dining room a moment, then willed them together into one large warm room, the way you'd push two thoughts into a single sentence. The wall between them simply wasn't, anymore. He went back out to the common room and, with a deliberate effort that felt a little like the shove he used for spells, fixed the new rooms in place. *Permanent.* They held.

By then he was hungry, and rather than test his luck on the unfamiliar stove, he decided to go and find the school's own dining hall — and to see, while he was at it, what the rest of the Heir's School looked like when it wasn't watching him climb onto a stage.
