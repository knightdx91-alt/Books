# Chapter Two

## What It Costs to Help

"So you'll do it," Bal said. "You'll get me enrolled."

Mrs. Brown nodded, and Bal felt something in his chest he didn't have a name for — relief, maybe, or the particular vertigo of wanting a thing so long that getting it felt like missing a step in the dark.

"I'll need to leave soon, won't I," he went on, already running the arithmetic. "To make it in time. And I'll need supplies — books, robes, whatever first years are meant to —"

Mrs. Brown waved that off the way she waved off most of his worrying. "Supplies are provided. First years get everything from the school — books, uniforms, the lot. You bring whatever personal things you want to keep, and nothing else. That's the whole of your packing."

"May I ask you something?" Amelia said.

Mrs. Brown sat back and folded her hands. "You may."

Amelia studied her across the spare little office, head tilted, and Bal recognized the look — it was the same weighing look she'd turned on him in the temple, the one that took the measure of a person and didn't pretend otherwise.

"Why are you willing to help a Blood mage?" Amelia asked. "Most people want them dead. The kindest of them simply want nothing to do with them. You've smiled at the boy and offered to fight a school full of originators on his behalf, and you haven't once looked at his wrist the way the rest of the world will. Why?"

For a long moment Mrs. Brown didn't answer. Her eyes drifted to the single framed photograph angled toward her chair — a man with a broad plain face and a slightly crooked smile, the kind of face you trusted on sight.

"Weak affinities don't always surface when they're meant to," she said at last. "Sometimes they come late. Sometimes not at all. My husband's didn't show until he was past thirty." She turned the frame a half-inch, as if to see him better. "A water mark, faint as a watermark in old paper. Harmless. He could barely fill a kettle with it. But it surfaced while he was working the floor at the textile mill, and the foreman saw it, and within the week they'd let him go. Said they couldn't be seen employing a marked man — couldn't risk the talk, the rumor that they were soft on mages." Her voice stayed level, which was somehow worse than if it had cracked. "After that he took what work he could find. It was never enough. There was always less of it, and always someone who'd heard about the mark before he arrived." She set the frame straight. "One evening he didn't come home. They found him three streets from the mill. He'd been shot. No one was ever charged. No one ever told me why." She looked up, and her eyes were dry and very old. "But I have my suspicions."

The office was quiet. Out in the yard someone hit the softball with a crack and a ragged cheer went up, very far away.

"So when a boy turns up in my hall with a Blood mark surfacing the day he was born," Mrs. Brown said, "strong enough that they took his hand off in his first hour to be sure of it — because that's what it means, Balthazaar, they don't cut that early for a weak mark — I find I'd like to help him outlast their hatred. If I can. I find I'm tired of watching it win."

Bal looked down and realized he'd closed his new left hand into a fist hard enough to ache, the red mark on his wrist burning against his own grip.

A cool palm settled over his fist. He didn't startle. Amelia's hand covered his, and the cold of it was steadying somehow, the way pressing a bruise can be.

"Don't," she said quietly. "They can't do it again. Not to you. Not while I'm yours."

Bal looked into her red eyes and found, to his own surprise, that he believed her completely.

He uncurled his hand.

"So what do I actually have to do?" he asked Mrs. Brown.

"Nothing but pack." She was already pulling a sheet of paper toward her, already the brisk administrator again, and Bal understood she'd shown them as much of the grief as she meant to and was folding the rest back where she kept it. "I'll send word to the school tonight. You'll likely leave in the morning." She glanced between them, and the brisk look turned thoughtful. "A first year with a familiar will cause talk on its own — most students don't bond one until their classes introduce the practice. A first year whose familiar is his own *originator* —" She shook her head. "I'll tell them you have a familiar. I'll leave the particulars out. Let them find the rest for themselves; you'll want the surprise, I expect, when it comes." She picked up her pen. "Now go and pack. I'll send Pierre to wake you."

---

Packing took Bal almost no time, which was its own quiet kind of sad.

Seventeen years in one building, and everything he wanted to carry out of it fit in a single backpack with room to spare. A few books. A brass compass he'd been given for a birthday he could no longer attach to a particular year. A pocketknife worn smooth at the handle. And — the one thing he'd have run back into a burning building for — his rune-engraving kit, the little roll of fine chisels and styluses he'd bought himself, dish by scrubbed dish, with the coins from a year of kitchen work.

The books were old and battered and not, by any honest measure, worth keeping, since the school would hand him better ones the moment he arrived. He packed them anyway. Fredrick had given them to him, and Fredrick had been a friend in a place that didn't hand those out free, and Bal wasn't in the habit of leaving friends behind.

Amelia had stolen one of the spellbooks the moment they'd reached his room and was now stretched out across his narrow bed on her stomach, ankles crossed in the air, turning pages with the lazy interest of someone reading a menu in a language they'd invented.

"You know," she said, "most of what they teach first years isn't difficult at all."

Bal rolled his eyes and tucked the chisel-roll down the side of the pack. "That's because it's *for* first years. The charm I put on the carpet isn't taught until second."

Amelia peered at him over the top of the book, and even in the dim light of the room he could see the laughter banked in her red eyes. "Oh," she said. "An overachiever."

It should have felt strange — bantering with a goddess older than half the buried cities of the world, a creature who'd come apart into a cloud of bats over being called a myth. Mostly it felt like talking to a girl who was very good at it.

"I like to read," Bal said, and shrugged. "And think about it — if I hadn't seen that flicker, or found that one spell, I'd never have found you at all." He surprised himself with how true it came out. "You'd still be in there. I'd still be down here."

Amelia's smile faded. She closed the book without marking her place, sat up, and set it carefully aside.

"I should warn you about something," she said. "And I want you to take it seriously, because you have a habit of taking dangerous things lightly that I find charming and would very much like you to survive." She folded her hands in her lap. "The others won't want you at that school. Not the students — they hardly matter. Not even the teachers. I mean the *originators*. My — siblings, you might call them, though it's a poor word for what we are to each other. They can't move against you directly. That's bound into the very stones of the place; we wrote it ourselves. But there are always others who can act for them. Always someone willing."

Bal sat down beside her on the bed. "Why did they lock you away in the first place? You keep circling it."

Amelia turned the question back on him, the way she seemed to turn most things. "Do you know why Blood mages are truly feared?"

Bal shook his head — he caught himself doing it and felt absurdly self-conscious, as if she could hear how little he had. "Not really. I know some of the magic runs on your own blood. That's about the whole of what anyone's allowed to learn."

"Of course it is." She didn't say it unkindly. "Listen, then, because no one else will tell you. Blood mages are feared because we can change a person's sygl." She let that sit. "Not against their will — never that; the bond won't take if the heart refuses it. But if someone *wants* to be remade, if they kneel and ask for it freely, a Blood mage can lift the old originator's mark away and set a new one in its place. We can move a soul from one house to another." Her mouth curved without much humor. "You can imagine how the others felt when they learned of it."

"They didn't know?"

"It was the most closely kept secret I had. For a long age, none of them so much as suspected." She drew one knee up and wrapped her arms around it, and for a moment looked younger than the ageless thing she was. "It came apart because of Raizen — the Storm originator, the Tempus mark. His followers and the others' had begun to go quiet, here and there, a high priest gone missing, a temple's lights gone dark. They grew frightened. So Raizen sent one of his own to take a high priest of mine and put the question to him with knives until he answered it." Her jaw tightened. "The truth is, I hadn't even known what was happening among my own. I was — occupied, with other matters, the way you forget a garden for a season and come back to find it's overrun. One of my high priests had taken it into his head to go converting, soul by soul, quietly, and I'd never noticed. So when the secret spilled out under Raizen's knives, the others didn't believe for a moment that I'd had no hand in it." She shrugged, a small bitter motion. "And to be fair to their suspicion — had I known, I'd not have stopped him. The thing that frightened them, you see, isn't only the converting. It's what the converts *keep*. When someone comes over to me from another house, they don't lose the gifts the old mark gave them. They carry them across. A storm mage who converts is a Blood mage who can still call the storm."

"Inherited gifts," Bal said slowly.

Amelia's grin came back, sudden and bright and a little wicked. "Don't fret about the particulars. You'll see for yourself, at the ceremony." She unfolded from the bed in one fluid motion and stretched, the spell of the dark story breaking. "Now. I'm hungry."

Bal's eyes went wide before he could stop them.

Amelia rolled her own. "*Normal* food," she said, with the long-suffering patience of someone who'd had this conversation across a great many centuries. "I can live off it well enough. But if you want me at my full strength — and tomorrow, you'll want exactly that — I'll need the other kind too, eventually. For now, take me down to wherever it is orphans are fed, before I waste away in front of you and Mrs. Brown has my throne to explain."

She caught his hand and pulled him off the bed, and Bal let himself be pulled, and didn't think too hard about the *eventually*.

---

Pierre woke him at six, which Bal had expected, and Mrs. Brown met him in the kitchen with a blueberry muffin and instructions to report to her office the moment he'd eaten, which he had not.

He was stuffing the last of the muffin into his mouth when he and Amelia stepped through her office door, and so it was around a cheekful of breakfast that he got his first look at the man who'd come to take them away.

He sat in one of the chairs as if he'd grown there — an old man in mage's robes worn soft with years, the red of the cloth faded to the color of dried roses but immaculately kept, every fray mended, every seam sound. He rose when they entered, and his smile arrived a half-second before he did, and his eyes were a kind faded blue that put Bal in mind, instantly and without resistance, of the grandfather he'd never had.

"You'll be Balthazaar and Amelia," the old man said. "My name is Verin. I'll be your escort to the Heir's School." He turned to Mrs. Brown with the easy warmth of long acquaintance. "Sarah — I'll set the portal in the front courtyard. The other children are kept to their rooms this morning?" At her nod he turned back to Bal. "I'll be waiting out front. Don't be long about your goodbyes; portals are patient but I'm not getting younger." And he was gone, the door closing soft behind him.

Bal turned to Mrs. Brown, swallowing the muffin. "I didn't think to ask yesterday — how do I explain her? Until everyone knows who she is. The familiar brand isn't something most people would see on a person, is it? She'll just look like a girl I've turned up with."

A tap on his shoulder. He turned, and the red sygl that had been glowing on Amelia's brow was simply gone — bare unmarked skin where it had been.

"Familiars can choose to show the brand or hide it," she said, enjoying his confusion. "Unless their master commands one or the other directly, it's ours to wear or not. I'll be a girl enrolling alongside you. Until it's time not to be."

"Besides," Mrs. Brown said, coming around the desk, "Verin knows better than to ask the questions he isn't meant to. He's been walking children through that portal longer than you've been alive, and he's learned which silences to keep." She caught Bal by the shoulders and pulled him into a quick, fierce hug, the kind that was over before he could be embarrassed by it. Then she held him at arm's length and looked at him the way she must once have looked at a man with a crooked smile. "Go on. And you —" to Amelia — "keep him out of trouble."

"On my honor," Amelia said, and behind her back she showed Bal her crossed fingers and a grin.

The portal stood across the main walkway, twelve feet wide and eight tall, a shimmering window cut into the morning air. Through it Bal could see a different world entirely: a horse-drawn buggy waiting in a clearing, a lantern swinging from an iron arm and throwing soft yellow light, and all around it a forest so dark and close it seemed to drink the light. Verin waited beside the buggy. Bal took one last look at the grey stone bulk of Saint Andre's — the only home he could remember, the roof he'd nearly died on, the nailed-shut door — and then he took Amelia's hand and stepped through.

The forest was cold and smelled of wet earth and pine. Verin was already handing Amelia up into the buggy. "We ride from here," he said, settling Bal beside her and taking the reins. "Portals don't work in or near the school. Not properly, anyway." He clicked his tongue and the horse leaned into its harness, and the buggy rolled forward into the dark trees. "When the school was raised, the originators each laid an enchantment on it for the children's safety. One of them turns portals aside — try to step through anywhere close, and you're shunted off to some other corner of this plane entirely. No telling where. I've heard of a boy who meant to arrive for autumn term and turned up on a mountainside two provinces east, in the snow, in his summer robes." He glanced back, eyes twinkling. "The only ones the enchantment doesn't touch are the originators themselves. They go where they please. As, I imagine, does your young lady, though I'll not pry into why a first year's familiar makes the trees lean away from her."

Amelia smiled and said nothing, and Verin, true to Mrs. Brown's word, didn't ask.

The forest swallowed them. It was a deathly quiet wood — no birdsong, no wind, only the soft drum of hooves on packed dirt and the long creak of the wheels, and after a while even Verin fell silent, and the three of them rode on each wrapped in their own thoughts.

This was all Bal had ever wanted. He turned the strangeness of it over and over. He'd dreamed of this school his whole life, lying on that off-limits roof, and never once in the dream had he carried a Blood sygl through these gates. But even that, he found, didn't trouble him the way it should have. Bal had always been a boy who went looking for the locked rooms, the off-limits roofs, the secrets people built whole stories to keep him away from. And of all the kinds of mage in all the world, the Blood mages were the most hidden — barred from every school, their goddess made into a myth, their craft reduced in the public mind to a rumor about doctors and newborns and madness. No one really knew what they could do. He was about to find out from the inside. The thought, when he let himself feel it honestly, was less like dread than like standing at the mouth of the one cave nobody had ever mapped.

He glanced over at Amelia. She was gazing out at the black trees, gone somewhere far away — to tomorrow, he guessed, and whatever waited on that stage. He reached over and took her hand.

She startled, looking down at their joined hands and then up at his face. Bal squeezed once, gently, and gave her a small smile — *I'm here; you're not going back in alone.* Amelia's expression softened. She returned the smile and looked back out at the forest, but she kept his hand.

They rode like that, in the quiet, for ten miles or more.

---

The cottage came up out of the dark with a fire already burning in the pit out front, a small bright warmth against the great black weight of the wood. Verin drew the buggy in and climbed down stiffly, motioning them to do the same.

"My home," he said, unbuckling the horse from the traces. The animal walked straight to a trough at the cottage's side and drank in long noisy pulls. "Normally I'd press on to the school the same day. But we've come late, and the gates aren't kind to travelers who arrive after dark, so we'll rest here and ride in fresh tomorrow." He nodded toward the door. "There's stew in the pot, and your uniforms are inside — you'll need to change into them in the morning before we leave. Don't fret that there's no sygl on the cloth; once you're claimed it'll surface on its own, school robes are spelled for it. Go on and warm yourselves. I'll see to old Thistle here."

Inside, the cottage was small and well-kept, a lifetime of small economies in every corner. A fire burned in a stone hearth with a heavy iron pot hung over it, and the smell that filled the room — savory, peppered, rich with something slow-cooked — made Bal's stomach growl loud enough to be heard. Two bedrolls lay side by side near the fire. Beside them sat two paper-wrapped parcels that Bal took, rightly, for their uniforms.

He went to one of the parcels and had it half-unwrapped before he noticed that Amelia hadn't moved from the doorway.

She stood with her arms loose at her sides, worrying her lower lip between her teeth, and the firelight couldn't quite reach whatever had come into her face. It wasn't a look Bal had seen on her yet. It took him a moment to name it, because it sat so oddly on a goddess: nerves.

He set the parcel down. "Are you all right?"

Amelia nodded, and crossed to him, and sat. "I am," she said. "There's only — something I need to ask you. And I find I don't quite know how, which is novel. I usually know how to ask for things."

Bal waited.

"Can I feed from you?" she said at last, softly, her eyes coming up to his through her black bangs and dropping again. "Truly feed. I told you I'd need to, before tomorrow. I haven't tasted human blood in a very, very long time, and I need it to stand at my full strength. If the others try something on that stage — and they may — I'm not certain I could stop them, as I am now. I won't risk you to my own hunger left unfed."

"If it's something you need," Bal said, "then of course you can. You're my familiar." He paused, then added, because it was true and he wanted her to have it: "And my friend, I'd hope, by now. Why wouldn't I help you?"

Amelia peeked up at him again, and there was something almost shy in it, which on a Vampire Queen was a strange and disarming thing to witness. "It doesn't frighten you? I am — what I am. A Vampire. I'll have my teeth in you."

Bal shrugged. "Honestly? It just makes you more interesting."

For a heartbeat she only stared at him. Then she grinned — a real grin, shy and pleased at once — and threw her arms around him in a quick hard hug. Bal, caught off guard, got his arms up and hugged her back before she let go and turned, suddenly busy, to her own parcel.

And then she was talking — fast and bright, the nerves burned off, telling him things he could never have learned anywhere else. How the originators had first dreamed up the schools. Why they'd built more than one — how they'd decided a single school would breed a single way of thinking, and so had scattered several across the plane to let different methods grow and argue and improve each other. It was history no book held, and Bal filed every word of it away out of long habit.

But mostly he was watching her. The shyness and then the rush of it; the way she'd gone from a goddess negotiating for blood to a girl too excited to keep still about the school she'd helped build, all in the space of a few minutes. Bal had never had a girlfriend, not really — there'd been a few stolen kisses behind the orphanage chapel, nothing that had outlasted the season. But the way Amelia was, in this firelit room, telling him secrets with her whole face — that was a thing he found he wanted. The Vampire part he hadn't seen coming. All the rest of it, though.

Amelia caught him staring. She tucked her hair behind her ear. "What are you looking at?"

"You," Bal said.

Amelia blushed — actually blushed, a faint rose under all that pale — and Verin came in from the dark at that exact moment, so that whatever she might have said was lost as the old man fixed himself a bowl of stew and lowered himself into the cottage's single chair.

---

Over supper, Verin told them what to expect.

He'd set them down at the gates and leave them to walk in with the other students, he said, spooning stew between sentences. There'd be a raised stone podium in the inner courtyard, and all the new students would gather before it. Once everyone had assembled, the originators would arrive on the stage, and the claiming would begin — each student called by name, each walking up to fall in line behind the originator who'd claimed them. When the last name was called and the last line formed, each originator would lead their new charges off to their dormitory.

"That may be the only time you lay eyes on yours all year," Verin warned them. "So if you've a question for your originator, ask it then, or hold it a long while."

Bal slid a sideways look at Amelia and had to fight down a grin. Amelia, across the room, was visibly losing the same fight.

Verin, mercifully, was bent over his bowl and missed the whole exchange. "Your schedule comes from the lead mage in your dorm," he went on. "All the first years take the same classes — only the hours differ. And they mix the sygls together in every class, on purpose. Fire beside Water beside Earth. It's meant to make you friends across the lines you'd otherwise never cross, because they know full well that the day you leave that school, most of you will fold straight back in among your own kind and never look out again." He scraped the bowl, set it on the table, and yawned hugely. "That's the shape of it. The rest you'll learn the way everyone does. By getting it wrong first."

He threw two more logs on the fire, crawled into his bed, and within a few minutes was snoring softly.

Bal gathered the bowls — his, Amelia's, the old man's — and carried them outside to rinse in the horse trough. Amelia followed him out into the cold and the dark. Thistle, grazing nearby, lifted his head an inch, decided they were no one worth worrying about, and went back to the grass.

While Bal worked the bowls clean in the icy water, Amelia stood with her face turned up to the sky.

"It's been so long since I stood under it," she said softly. "I'd forgotten how beautiful it is. You forget, in the dark. You make yourself forget, because remembering is worse."

"How long," Bal asked. "In the temple."

"Almost three thousand years." She said it the way you'd report a distance you'd walked. "A very long three thousand years."

Bal dried his hands and nodded toward a weathered bench at the cottage's side. They sat. Above them the unfamiliar stars wheeled, thick and bright in a sky no city light had ever spoiled.

"They won't try to take you back tomorrow, will they," Bal said. It came out more frightened than he'd meant it to.

Amelia shook her head. "No. Trapping me the first time was as much my own foolishness as their cunning — and the fact that I trusted them, which is the same thing in the end."

"What happened?"

She leaned back against the cottage wall and closed her eyes, and when she spoke the firelight made the planes of her face very old and very young at once.

"They commissioned a stone-crafter to build that temple for me. Not just any — a high priest of Naisura's, the Terra house, the finest hands for stone in all the world. They called it a peace offering. They were sorry, they said, for having suspected me over their lost followers. A gesture of good faith." Her mouth twisted. "Here is a thing the world has forgotten about my kind, Bal, because we took such care that it should. We — the immortals, the originators — we can only be bound in one of our own places of power. A temple. A shrine. Somewhere our nature is rooted into the very stone. And we go into such places rarely; I could count on one hand the times in an age that I'd set foot in a temple raised to me." She opened her eyes and looked at the fire. "Which they knew. So when Naisura's priest built me a temple of surpassing beauty, they knew I'd want to come and thank him with my own voice. That I'd walk in. And I did."

"And the priest —"

"The priest was the bait. What I didn't know was that they'd hidden their other high priests in a ring all around the temple, outside the stone. The moment I crossed the threshold, every one of them gave their life at once — poured themselves out entirely — to seal me in. It took eight of them. Eight high priests, dead in an hour, to close one door on one goddess for three thousand years." A tear slid from the corner of her eye, and Bal saw with a small shock that it ran red as the mark on his wrist.

He reached up and wiped it away with his thumb, gently, before it could fall.

"They chose their road," he said. "Every one of them. The priests who died and the ones who lied to your face. You didn't make them do it." He drew a breath. "And you're free now. So — listen. If you want it, I'll release you from the bond. I mean it. You're three thousand years old and bound to a boy who can barely levitate a rug, and that can't be anything like what you imagined for yourself. If you'd rather walk out of here free of me, I'll say the words tonight."

Amelia turned her head against the cottage wall to look at him, and slowly she smiled.

"Thank you," she said. "For offering. You can't know what it means that you offered — more than you do know." She reached over and laid her cold hand against his cheek for a moment. "But I'd like to stay, if you'll have me. Besides —" the wicked grin surfaced through the tears — "who else is going to teach you Blood magic?" And she stuck out her tongue.

Bal laughed, and the sound of it broke the last of the heaviness off the night. And then he remembered.

"Right — you said you needed to feed. If you're my familiar, then taking care of you is the whole job, isn't it?" He held out his arm. "How do you want to do it?"

At the word *feed* Amelia's eyes brightened, the grief gone as quickly as it had come, replaced by something keen and warm and old. She took his offered hand and drew his wrist toward her mouth.

"You're certain," she said. It wasn't quite a question.

Bal nodded.

She bent her head, and her fangs slid into his wrist — and Bal, who'd grown up with an unusually high tolerance for pain, who'd reset his own broken finger once rather than explain to Mrs. Brown how he'd broken it, felt almost nothing at all. A bright pinprick, and then a strange warm pulling, and the soft sound of her drinking in the dark.

He started talking. It seemed the natural thing, and the silence felt too large to leave empty. He told her how badly he'd wanted this, all of it — how, with no left hand and no sygl and no school that would have him, he'd long ago made his peace with being a laborer, hauling and lifting his whole life, hoarding what scraps of magic he could teach himself until maybe, someday, he'd know enough to be useful in some quiet way no one would have to be ashamed of. He told her how that future had felt like a coat a size too small that he'd just have to grow used to. He talked until Amelia drew back and ran her tongue once across the punctures, and Bal watched, astonished, as the two small wounds closed over and vanished, leaving the skin smooth and whole.

She caught his look and laughed. "It wouldn't do," she said, "to walk you into your first day of school with two holes in your wrist. People talk enough about Blood mages without the evidence handed to them."

"No," Bal agreed, turning his unmarked wrist in the firelight. "I suppose it wouldn't."

He stood and held out his hand. Amelia took it, and they went inside together, to the two bedrolls by the fire and the old man's soft snoring, and lay down in the warmth, and Bal fell asleep faster than he had in years — eager, for the first time in his life with no shadow on it, for the morning to come.
