# Chapter One: The Roof and the Flicker

The stump of his left wrist itched again, which it only ever did when he looked at the mausoleum, and the roof of the mausoleum had flickered three days ago, and the spell folded under his right hand could drop him forty feet onto the lane if he got a single syllable wrong. Bal lined those three facts up in his head the way he lined up everything, in order, and decided none of them was a good enough reason to stop.

He sat cross-legged on the slate with the carpet across his knees.

It was a small carpet, the kind that lived in front of a wash basin, gold thread gone dull and a sky-blue border that someone had darned in the wrong color. He'd taken it from the storeroom. He'd put it back later, cleaner than he found it, which was the deal he made with himself about borrowing things from a place that had never given him anything to begin with.

There were three small stones in his jacket pocket, smooth ones he'd pulled out of the gutter that morning, and while he sat working the spell over in his head he took them out and threw them. Up, across, catch with the right hand, around, catch with the right hand again, the left wrist coming up to meet each one out of pure habit and missing it, always, by the width of the hand that wasn't there. He could keep three going for a long time if nobody was watching. He'd taught himself this as he'd taught himself everything, by repeating it past the point where it had stopped being interesting, and it gave the part of his head that worried something to do with its hands while the rest of him got on with the dangerous thing. The stones went up and came down. He didn't think about them. He thought about the spell.

Saint Andre's roof was the one flat thing for half a mile. Grey tiles, evenly spaced, warm under him from a whole afternoon of sun. Off limits, of course. A boy had gone off the north edge years before Bal arrived, and the matrons told the story every spring so no one would forget, and every spring some child crept up to see the place where it happened. Bal came up for the quiet. From here the lane ran out toward the trees and nobody ever walked it, and behind the orphanage the great grey building sat closed, as it had sat closed his whole life.

The orphanage was old. Somebody had built it the better part of three centuries back and somebody else had patched it ever since, so the three stories of it stood crooked but sound, brick gone the brown of old bread, gutters that sang in a hard rain. Forty children slept in it on a full year. They came and they went, claimed and unclaimed, and the ones who got claimed left in a cart with a stranger and the ones who didn't grew old enough to be useful and were sent out to be useful somewhere. Bal had watched both kinds go. He was sixteen now, which meant he was running low on both kinds of future, and he tried not to count the months because counting them did no good and the doing-no-good was the part that kept him up.

He came up here for the quiet, and he came up here because from the roof he could not be the only boy in the building without a sygl. From the roof he was the only boy at all.

Boys had thrown themselves at those doors for two hundred years. Shouldered them, picked the locks, prayed at them once on a dare. The doors did not care. A mausoleum was a box you put the dead in and the dead did not answer knocking.

Below him, on the bald patch of yard the orphanage called a field, the older boys were playing a game with a flat stick and a ball wound from rags. He could hear the crack of it, the run of feet, somebody calling a name that wasn't his because none of them were ever his. He didn't look down. He'd learned a long time ago that watching the game from outside it only taught you what outside felt like, and he already knew.

He rubbed the stump with his thumb. It had no fingers to scratch the itch with, which was a joke he'd made to exactly nobody.

Bal could not remember a left hand. Other children grew theirs whole and then, somewhere around six or seven, woke with a sygl burned bright into the palm of it, a mark that told them what they were for. Waves. Terra. The fast bright crackle of the storm ones, who walked around like they'd swallowed a lamp. The sygl was a sort of compass. It pointed a person at the kind of magic their blood already wanted to do, and the schools and the factions read the mark the way a banker reads a coin, and they let you in or they did not.

He had seen it happen to the others, the marking. A girl named Posy had got hers at supper one evening, sitting two down from him, and she'd dropped her spoon and turned her hand over in front of her face like it belonged to somebody else, and a green coil of light had been burning itself slowly into the skin of her open palm. Terra. The whole table had crowded round her. By the next spring a steward of some land-holding house had come up the lane that nobody walked, and Posy had gone off in the cart, and that was the last anyone heard of Posy. A sygl did that. It told the world what you were good for, and the world came and collected.

Storm ones were the rarest and the loudest. There'd been a boy, once, before Bal's time, the matrons said, whose sygl had come in crackling, and he'd had to sleep apart from the others because the static off him stood the hair up on your arms in the dark. Waves and Terra and the storm-marked Tempus and the dueling double-marks they called Duality, and one or two more, and each one ran back to an originator, a god who'd first carried that magic into the world and still came once a year to the great school on the other plane to claim the new followers born under their sign. The factions read the marks like deeds of ownership. So did the schools. No mark, no door.

Bal had nothing to show them. A wrist that ended in a soft white knot, neat, old, the work of someone who'd known what they were doing. He'd asked Mrs. Brown about it once, when he was small enough to still think questions had bottoms, and she'd gone very still and said it had been done before he came to her, and changed the subject, a door closing quietly behind the words. So he'd done it the slow way. He'd taught himself out of cast-off books, pushing his magic through the blockage in his wrist by main stubbornness, and he could light a candle and lift a teacup and, three days ago, he had almost decided he could lift himself.

Almost. He went back over the spell one more time before he committed to it, because if he was honest the thing that had nearly killed the falling boy was not the roof. It was being sure.

He'd seen the flicker on a Tuesday. Or a Wednesday. He'd been up here after lunch tracing the carved figures that ran around the top of the mausoleum, vines and faces worn soft, when the whole roof of the thing went thin as breath on glass and he looked straight through it. Pillars. An open floor. A statue, pale and tall, standing in the middle of a room that had no business existing inside a sealed tomb. Then the stone closed back over it like water, and Bal had sat very still for a long time, his stump burning, certain he was making it up.

He had not been making it up. He'd gone over the angle a dozen times since, lying awake in the long dormitory while the other boys breathed, ticking the problem over the way you worry a loose tooth. The window in the roof, if it was a window, sat maybe sixty feet off, across air no ladder in the building could span and no boy could jump.

A ladder had been the first idea, because it was the safe one. There wasn't a ladder at Saint Andre's that reached past the second-floor gutters, and even the long painters' one the groundsman kept chained in the shed would have stood you in the lane staring up at a wall of dead stone with no foothold and no door. He'd counted the rungs anyway, because he didn't trust a thing he hadn't checked, and a plan you discarded without checking had a way of climbing back through the window at night to ask whether you'd been sure.

That left magic. That left the one road across, and it was the one that could kill him, and Bal had read the appendix at the back of *Sygldry Year 2* before he let himself read the spell at the front, because he did not start things until he'd finished them. Forty pages on the theory of sustained enchantment, dull as ditchwater, tables and tolerances and a long argument with a dead mage about load. He read all forty. There was a line in the middle of the dullness, almost an aside, that said a levitation laid on an inanimate object would hold for as long as the caster fed it or until the caster's attention broke, whichever came first. *Until the caster's attention broke.* It meant the carpet would do exactly what he told it and nothing more, and would stop being a carpet that could fly the instant he stopped believing it was one. It meant the most dangerous thing in the world he was about to do was let his mind wander.

Fredrick had left him the books. Fredrick had a sygl and a place at the Sygldry school and a trunk too full to carry, and on his way out the door he'd pushed three beginner spellbooks into Bal's chest and said *you'll get more out of these than I will*, which was a kind thing dressed up as a careless one. He hadn't been wrong, either. Fredrick had carried his sygl around for ten years like a coat somebody else had bought him, never once opening a book that wasn't set for class, and Bal had read these three to the spine inside a month and gone hunting for more. It was the oldest unfairness Bal knew, that the marking went to the ones who didn't want it and skipped the one who'd have crawled over glass for it, and he'd long since stopped expecting it to make sense. Sense was a thing you got from people. The world ran on something colder. The school those books came from would never take him. A school required a sygl, and a sygl required a hand, and Bal was a boy the world had decided about before he was old enough to argue.

He gathered the stones out of the air, one, two, three, and put them back in his pocket, and sat for a while not doing anything at all. The game went on below him, the crack and the running and the names. A bell rang somewhere inside the building, the four-o'clock one, which meant chores, which meant in an hour someone would notice the boy who was always at the edge of things had gone missing from the edge of things, and would look, without much urgency, and not find him.

He thought about that. He thought about a matron walking the lane at dusk calling a name into the trees, and the cart that wouldn't come for him because no cart came for the unmarked, and the long row of months he wasn't counting. He thought about Posy's spoon hitting the floor, and the green light on her upturned palm, and the whole table leaning in, and the cart. There had never been a moment like that with his name on it. The world looked a child over once at birth and once at the marking, and where his second look should have found a hand it had found a soft white knot, and it had written him down and moved on to a child it could use. He thought that whatever was on the other side of that flicker, it had wanted to be seen by exactly one person, and that the one person it had let see it was a boy the world had already filed away as a discard.

He could sit on this roof and agree with the verdict, be as right about himself as they were, until they sent him out to be useful somewhere. Or he could go and find out whether the verdict had missed something. That was the part the curiosity covered for, and he let it, because the other part was harder to say even to himself. He was tired of agreeing with them. If that wasn't a reason, it was the closest he had to one, and it was his.

He set the carpet flat on the slate and smoothed it with his right palm.

If this went wrong, it was going to be his own fault, and he wanted that clear in his head before he started so there'd be no surprise about it later. Nobody had told him to do this. Nobody would mourn it cleverly. He'd be the boy off the roof, like the last one, and they'd tell his story in the spring, and the kids would creep up to see the spot, and the spot would be the same spot. It was a tidy thing to be, he supposed. He pre-forgave himself for it, as he pre-forgave himself most things, since it saved time.

Then he knelt and put his left wrist down on the carpet. Not his hand. He could have used his good hand, his right, and the spell would have worked the same. He used the wrist on purpose. He always did. Some buried, stupid, hopeful piece of him still thought that one day he'd push the magic through that stump and the push would catch on something, and a mark would surface there like a face coming up out of dark water, and he would finally be a thing the world had a name for.

It was silly. It was the silliest thing about him. He did it anyway.

"Levimotorcorpus."

He shoved the word down through the blockage in his wrist, that old gristly resistance that had never once let go, and felt the magic squeeze past it and into the threads.

The carpet rose.

It lifted under him a few inches, gold and sky-blue, steady, and then it stopped. Stopped dead, hovering a hand's breadth off the slate, holding his weight as though nothing about that were strange. Bal looked down at the gap of air between the rug and the roof and laughed out loud, one short bark of a sound that surprised him more than the magic had. It was working. It was actually, ridiculously working, and the funniest part was that the spell had clearly decided a few inches was plenty and it was waiting to be told otherwise.

"Higher," he told it, like an idiot, out loud, to a rug.

He grabbed his backpack off the tiles and worked his arms through the straps, the compass and the pocketknife and the rune kit and the dull weight of *Year 2* shifting against his spine. Then he folded his legs and settled, one knee touching the cold border, and he silently asked the carpet to take him across.

It went faster than he expected.

It went a great deal faster than he expected. The roof's edge slid out from under him and then there was nothing under him at all, the whole drop of the yard opening up forty feet down, the boys with their stick and their rag ball gone small and bright and oblivious, and the wind pushed at his hair, and Bal felt his stomach lift clean out of his body and float somewhere up behind his ears. He should have been terrified. He waited politely to be terrified.

What came instead was the giddy, helpless delight of a child shoved too high on a swing. He whooped. He couldn't help it. The lane reeled by beneath him, the trees, the long-closed grey building rushing up to meet him, and he was flying, badly, on a wash-basin rug he hadn't even tested at speed, and it was the best thing that had ever happened to him. His face had pulled itself into something tight and wide and aching, and he let it. If he died doing this, he thought, at least it would have been *this*.

And there it was. That was the wandering. He felt his mind drift toward the thought and the carpet answer it, the threads under him going loose, the steady hum of the magic stuttering as his attention came off it for half a heartbeat, and the rug dropped a foot out of the sky before he caught himself.

*No.* He hauled his whole mind back onto the spell the way you grab a child by the collar. *The carpet. Only the carpet.* He stopped feeling the wind and the giddiness and the forty feet of nothing and shrank himself down to the single fact of the rug holding him up, gold and sky-blue, real, flying, and the threads pulled taut again and the hum steadied. He held it there by main force. He could be terrified later. He could be delighted later. Right now he was a boy who believed in a carpet, and he did not let himself be one thing more than that.

Then the mausoleum was right there, and the stump of his left wrist lit up. Not the old maddening itch but something with weight behind it, a hard hot pull deep under the soft white knot, a fishhook set in a part of him that had no nerves left to feel it. It dragged toward the tomb. He almost lost the spell to the strangeness of it. He hauled his mind back, and aimed himself at the place in the roof where the stone had gone thin, and braced his good hand on his knee, and prepared to land on solid slate as a person braces to land on a thing he is sure is there.

There was no roof.

He hit the place where the stone should have been and went straight through it, no jolt, no crack, only the light changing all at once from afternoon to something warmer and lower, and a wash of air that smelled of cold stone and old smoke. The carpet didn't stop. His heart finally caught up to his body and slammed, late, useless. He flung his weight back and the rug overcorrected and dipped, and below him a floor swung up out of the gloom, real and grey and close.

*Down,* he thought at it, fast, *take me down, gently, down,* and the carpet sank through the warm air past two pillars taller than the orphanage, and set him down on solid concrete with a softness it had refused to show him anywhere else.

The rug went dead the instant his weight came off it, the magic he'd forced through the blockage draining out all at once, and it slumped to the floor in a heap of dull gold. Bal stood very still and breathed.

He was inside.

Two braziers burned at either side of a long stone room, throwing a low steady warmth across columns that climbed up into shadow. The roof was open above him to a square of pale sky, except it wasn't, except it couldn't be, because he'd watched this building sit sealed and dead his whole life and there was no roof up there to be open. The light fell in anyway. At the far end of the room, where he'd glimpsed it through the flicker, stood the statue. Pale stone, tall, a figure with its face turned away from him toward the dark behind it.

The floor was clean. That was the thing his mind caught on first, ahead of the impossible roof, ahead of the statue. Two hundred years of a sealed building should have meant dust thick enough to write in, leaves, the small dry bodies of insects, the slow grey ruin that got into everything no one touched. There was none of it. The concrete under his boots was swept and cool and bare, the way the kitchen flags were bare after Mrs. Brown had taken a hard brush to them, as though someone walked this room and minded it. Someone in a tomb that no one could open. He filed that beside the roof and the braziers, in the growing column in his head of things that had no business being true, and he did not let himself think too hard about what kept two fires burning in a place the living couldn't reach.

He stepped off the carpet. His footstep came back at him off the stone, larger than it should have been, and he held still until the echo of it died.

His stump had stopped itching. He noticed it with the distant part of his mind that noticed mechanisms, the part that had already filed the dorm-rooms in Fredrick's stories growing on a thought and the rug settling for three inches until it was told otherwise. The itch was gone as a sound you have stopped hearing is gone, and the absence of it felt stranger than its presence ever had.

He turned a slow circle, taking it in. High pillars. An altar of some kind, low, near the statue, a long pale block worn smooth along its top edge the way a doorstep wears under years of feet. He didn't go to it. Something about it kept him at the center of the room, and he was glad enough to obey the something without examining it. The whole place put him in mind of the old temples in the picture books, the southern ones with their open colonnades, and he found himself wondering, with a curiosity that elbowed clean past the fact that he had flown through a stone roof, how the sun got in.

"Because it's magic," whispered a voice at his ear.

It was warm, and amused, and a hand's breadth from his skin, and there was no one there.

Bal whirled around. The room stretched empty behind him, braziers steady, statue facing away, the gold rug crumpled on the floor where he'd left it. The pillars climbed up into the dark and the dark held nothing he could see.

But the voice had been close enough to stir the hair beside his ear, and the temple that should have been empty for two hundred years was, very clearly, not.
