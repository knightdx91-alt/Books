## PART 0 — SERIES OVERVIEW

Planned series of 3–4 interconnected books in one fantasy world. Unifying
villain across the saga: **High King Alten**. Thematic spine through every book:
**power, sacrifice, and consumption.**

**Writing/publication order (≠ chronological):**
1. Land of the Guardians (write & publish FIRST — strongest hook, most accessible).
2. The Dragon Isle (potential second volume of Land of the Guardians — TBD).
3. Son of None (already drafted; revise LAST, once the world is locked).
4. The Origin (late prequel — structurally harsh, everyone dies, less accessible opener).

**Chronological order:**
1. The Origin — Kynes and the five elementals end the old world to birth this one.
2. Land of the Guardians (+ The Dragon Isle) — some years before Son of None.
3. Son of None — Ne'veran's story; the final reckoning with Alten.

Guiding principles: publishing vs. writing order deliberately separated;
retroactive worldbuilding (Alten's dragon-heart implants) deepens villain
coherence; Son of None revised last.

## PART 0.5 — COSMOLOGY (sibling worlds)

The Origin does **not** birth a single world — the five elementals' sacrifice
shatters the dying old world into a small **cluster of sibling worlds**, each
carrying a shard of the elementals' residue. (Draft support: Father says Alten's
gateway "frayed the very fabric of our universe, **and all the others along with
it**.") This is why gateways/rifts between worlds are possible at all.

**The old world is literally OUR Earth — real ~1700s England (Option A, CANON
2026-07-12).** *The Origin* is a secret-history / alternate-history APOCALYPSE: it
reads as real historical fiction until the ritual, then Earth is **consumed** (the
fixed total-sacrifice ending stands) and the sibling fantasy worlds are **born from
its remains.** So the Guardians' and Son of None's worlds are Earth's metaphysical
**offspring** — when Ne'veran reads the Origin as history in Father's temple, he is
reading about **Earth.** (This is alternate history by definition — our real timeline
does not continue; that is the point.)

- **World G — the Guardians world.** Setting of *Land of the Guardians* (+ *The
  Dragon Isle*). Home of dragons, elves, Moonwalkers. **Alten is native here** and
  rules as High King.
- **World N — the sacred world / Father's world.** Setting of *Son of None*. Built
  and shepherded by **Father** after the Origin; magic here is literally the residue
  of the five. Home of Ne'veran, the Elders, the Journey, Kysmer.
- **World C — the Gorlak homeworld.** "A world far different than ours" (draft); the
  four-armed winged **Gorlak** army is bred here and imported through Alten's rifts.

**Dragons exist across the sibling worlds** (both G and N), as the scattered living
remnants of the five elementals — one per element (Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Spirit).
This is why Father is *weakened by "the slow extinction of the dragons, his closest
remnant of the original five"* in *Son of None*: Alten hunts and eats them in **both**
worlds.

## PART 0.6 — ALTEN: HEART-GRAFTING, LONGEVITY & THE FLIGHT TO WORLD N

The unified villain engine. One device — dragon-heart implants — powers Alten,
keeps him alive, makes him hard to kill, corrupts him, and bridges the worlds.

**Heart-grafting.** A **dragon heart** is the crystal left when a dragon dies; it
still faintly *beats*, holding a shard of an elemental's near-immortal power. There
are five elements, so **five hearts exist at a time** (hard cap — no infinite power).
Alten **surgically implants** each heart into a specific site on his body; it grafts
to his flesh and feeds him that element. Placement (targetable in a fight — destroy
one implant to deny that element):
- **Fire → right palm**, **Water → left palm** (casting hands)
- **Air → throat / base of skull** (breath)
- **Earth → sternum** (grounding)
- **Spirit → over his own heart** (spirit = life; Spirit is the Origin's dream/vision element)

**Three effects of each graft:**
1. **Power** — full mastery of that element.
2. **Longevity + distributed life** — the hearts carry the elementals' near-immortality;
   while any heart still beats in him, a killing blow won't finish him — the hearts
   re-knit the body. **To truly kill Alten you must cut out or shatter the hearts, not
   the man.** (The heroes' real objective; why it takes a saga.)
3. **The cost (theme: consumption)** — stolen sacred power in mortal flesh slowly **eats
   him**: he greys, withers, calcifies (dragon-scale creep). This is why the prologue's
   Alten is a withered "kindly old man." He needs *more* hearts to outrun his own decay —
   the addiction spiral.

**The flight (climax of *Land of the Guardians*).** The heroes take/destroy **one
heart** — nearly killing him and cracking his distributed life. Cornered and
destabilized, Alten dumps the overload of his remaining hearts to **tear a gateway and
flee** — desperate and uncontrolled, not a planned conquest. He lands in **World N**,
**not knowing it is Father's sacred world**, and the gateway collapses behind him. The
Guardians heroes hold no heart-power and have **no way to follow** — he escapes; they
win the world but lose the man. (This is why the Guardians cast does not appear in *Son
of None*.)

**The doomsday tally.** The **heart count is the series countdown.** Default: Alten flees
with ~2–3 of 5 hearts; in World N he hunts *that* world's dragons too and climbs toward
the full set. **All five = effectively a god, gateway fully stabilized, all sibling
worlds doomed.** *Son of None*'s reckoning is stopping him before he completes the set;
Ne'veran, charged by Father, is the one who can. He is "far more powerful" in *Son of
None* precisely because of the hearts consumed in between.

**Open knob:** **Spirit** (rarest, hardest-to-hide element) may be the **last** heart —
the one nobody has found yet — a natural final-book McGuffin.

## PART 2 — LAND OF THE GUARDIANS (proposed first to write/publish)

Second book chronologically, some years before Son of None. Three converging
storylines + cold-open villain thread, all tied to Alten. Introduces world,
magic system, dragons, heroes.

**Cold open:** Alten watches a man torture another — "I know you know where they
are, just tell me and the pain will stop." Seeds the hunt for the twins' parents.

**Storyline One — Human twins Aeron & Selena (16):**  _(male twin renamed Knight→Aeron, canon 2026-07-12; "Knight"/"Xavier" are used in other books.)_
- Parents didn't wake them on their 16th birthday (coming-of-age/magic unlock).
- Dagger in the kitchen table, parchment underneath: "Gorlak."
- Pulling the dagger triggers a hologram message from mother: if seen, she & father
  are dead; they aren't the twins' real parents; the High King hunts the twins.
- Aeron finds a sword with a **black dragon** engraved (meaning unknown until he
  meets Reagen, who has a **blue dragon** sword).
- They flee with teachers **Alverin** (Aeron) and **Raveena** (Selena). Women's
  and men's magic differ (Wheel of Time–style) → same-gender teachers.
- Mission: find the elves (only ones who might know the dragons' fate); seek allies.

**Storyline Two — Elven twins Katy & Reagen (16):**
- Children of **Queen Rosalia**.
- Katy found crying in the crypt-library reading the main prophecy book; an
  hourglass has appeared beside the main-line prophecy (it has started).
- Bells ring — kingdom invaded.
- Rosalia portals Reagen in, instructs them, sends them to find the "flying twins."
- **Reagen's sword:** blue dragon; old stories say it can call a blue dragon. The
  black/blue meeting with Aeron is a major recognition moment.
- **Rosalia's sacrifice (hard-set, uncuttable):** summons the creature always with
  her — shaped like a mriswith (sinuous, reptilian, cloaked, ancient). "You know the
  cost." She lies on a stone slab; the whole kingdom falls into an irreversible
  slumber until the true heirs reassume the throne. He conjures a blue rose, lays it
  on her breast, "Long live the queen," and vanishes. Creature is bonded to the
  throne/kingdom, not Rosalia — likely the last visible **Guardian** (title source).
- Rosalia dies; must be replaced by Katy as queen in Son of None.
- Roles: Reagen is Katy's **consort**, not co-ruler. Katy is the diplomat.

**Storyline Three — Seiss & Wolf:**
- **Seiss** — apprentice trader; trains telepathic horses. Master is dying, passes
  on the master-trader medallion and his ship.
- **Wolf** — Seiss's best friend; head of the Moonwalker clan. "Wolf" is name +
  title, given to the one NOT cursed with the transformation (full control at will).
  Others change when angry or at full moon. Only 16 — remarkable control. Possible:
  can control other Moonwalkers mid-transformation (feared by his own people).
- Arc: leave for the big city (where Alten resides), get roped into Alten's army,
  sabotage from inside, help people escape.

**Convergence:** all four groups ally into a unified resistance. Late arcs: seek the
dragons (via elven knowledge); reclaim the elven throne to wake the kingdom.

**The dragons:** disappeared; only elves might know their fate. Last remnants of the
original elemental power. Alten hunts them for **dragon hearts** (crystals forming
after a dragon dies); each implanted heart = one elemental power. Stopping him from
getting more is a primary goal; heroes may take one back. Dragons in hiding,
traumatized, slow to trust.

**Magic system:** men's/women's magic differ; children can't control their abilities;
magic is feared; persecution is bottom-up (village mobs, witch-hunt), not top-down.

**Open questions:** Aeron & Selena's real parents (last dragon-bond bloodline?);
does Selena have her own artifact?; how many dragons (likely five, one per element —
black, blue, +3 TBD); what does calling a dragon cost?; does Alten know about the
swords?; where are dragons sleeping?; does Reagen become queen-shaped or stay consort?;
does the spirit dragon appear?

**Possible two-book split:** Volume One (Land of the Guardians) through the convergence
and setting sail; Volume Two (The Dragon Isle) the island, earning trust, calling
dragons, Wolf's payoff, taking back a heart, waking the kingdom, Katy crowned.

## PART 3 — THE ORIGIN (earliest chronologically; write/publish last)

How the worlds came to be — five elementally-marked people end their old world to
birth new ones where magic is no longer hunted. Their deaths become the substance of
the sibling worlds. Referenced in Son of None as history Ne'veran reads in Father's temple.

### Setting & register (CANON 2026-07-12)
- **The old world is our real Earth** — **rural England, the year of our Lord 1697**
  (Option A: literal Earth, alt-history apocalypse; see PART 0.5). Real geography,
  real monarchy, muskets-and-candles tech, period-accurate names, dialect, and voice.
  This demands real-world research; the payoff is instant immersion and the rug-pull
  from "historical fiction" into creation myth.
- **Register:** opens as historical/gothic; only detonates into fantasy at the ritual.
  This tonal jolt is why the Origin is published LAST (harsher, least accessible opener).
- **The persecution is the real witch-hunt** — and deliberately **bottom-up**: by 1697
  the Crown/courts are cooling on witch trials (last English executions, the Bideford
  witches, 1682; the 1735 Act will end prosecutions), but **village mobs and local
  zealots still hunt and hang "witches."** The establishment looking away while the mob
  still burns is the exact trap the marked live in. Trigger is loss of control.
- **Religion (handle with care):** **Father is the hidden true power behind creation.**
  The persecutors are the period's zealots and mobs — NOT a polemic against real
  believers. "Year of our Lord" and church texture stay as period flavor; the story
  makes no argument about a real faith. Father operates in secret, unknown to the Church.
- **Power is conserved (reconciliation, CANON):** an elemental mark cannot be destroyed
  — it always transfers to a new host on death (the old world's own rule; if Kynes died,
  Fire would pass to another). So at the ritual the five *people* die **totally** (no
  godhood, no reincarnation), but their **power** is conserved and precipitates into new
  vessels — the **dragons** (one per element) — in the newborn sibling worlds. The
  dragons are the five *elements* made flesh, NOT the five *people* reborn.

**Core premise:** in the old world, five people at a time are born with an elemental
mark (Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Spirit) granting absolute mastery; only one per element
at a time; the mark transfers on death. The marked are persecuted, hidden, isolated,
strangers to each other.

**Persecution:** bottom-up, witch-hunt; trigger is loss of control (children can't
manage power). The world is its own antagonist — the only way out is Father's spell.

**Kynes — protagonist:** **Fire crafter** (fire mark). Lives in fear. Discovers
Father's crumbling temple; receives a stone tablet with a spell. Does NOT know the
cost. Sets out to find the other four.

**Kynes — the inciting wound (CANON 2026-07-12):** the village hunt takes his
**mother** — accused of witchcraft because she is an **herbalist / cunning-woman**:
she knows plants, heals, likely midwifes. She is the textbook 1697 target (an older
woman with plant-lore and no man to shield her), and she has **no magic at all** — the
hunt kills the innocent one. The trigger is ordinary human cruelty: a child she
couldn't save, or a failed harvest, and gratitude curdles into "she cursed us."
- **It is a mob PYRE, not a court sentence (important, and consistent with canon).**
  England hanged witches; it did not burn them, and by 1697 the Crown/courts had
  largely stopped executing at all. So this is an **extralegal village burning** driven
  by mob fervor — which is exactly the book's bottom-up persecution (the mob, not the
  Crown). The stake makes the persecution's lawlessness literal.
- **The fire irony (the heart of the wound):** Kynes is a **Fire crafter** — he can feel
  the pyre, could quell it or turn it aside with a thought. To do so is to expose himself
  and burn beside her. He stands in the crowd, the fire *answering* him, and **refuses
  it.** He lets his mother burn in the one element he commands. (Rhyme: she healed with
  growing things and dies by fire; the son who owns fire does nothing.)
- Then he **flees in shame** — and while hiding, stumbles on Father's crumbling temple.

**How Kynes gathers the five (CANON 2026-07-12) — no orchestrator, he does the work:**
1. **Father gives the first rumor.** Not a name, not a magic compass — just a **single
   lead**: word of one uncanny soul, somewhere, to start him moving. (The stone tablet
   holds the *spell*, not a locator.)
2. **The witch-hunt is his map.** From there he tracks each mark by following the
   persecution's own tales — a fire no water could quench, a drowning on dry ground, a
   field grown overnight, a woman who says how you'll die. The uncanny gets reported as
   **curses**, so the hunt itself advertises where the marked hide. The machine built to
   destroy them is how he finds them.
3. **The marks resonate up close.** Within range, two marks *recognize* each other — a
   pull, a heat, a wrongness in the air. This is the confirmation and the first-meeting
   beat; short-range, so it never does the finding for him.
4. **The chain compounds.** Each crafter gathered adds their own instincts and rumor-
   knowledge, and **two or three marks together resonate more strongly than one** — so
   each is a little easier to find than the last. Natural escalation, no hidden hand.
- **Spirit is equal to the others** — their power (dreams, visions, hearing the dying) is
  distinct, but they are found and gathered like everyone else; NO special role in
  assembling the five.

- The engine is **grief + guilt**, not heroism. Father dangles the spell as the thing
  that ends the hunts *forever* — a world where no mother burns for nothing — which is
  exactly the bait that moves a broken man (and why the later reveal of the cost guts him).
- **Optional gut-twist (author to decide):** a near-exposure of Kynes's fire is what
  first turned suspicion toward the household — so his secret helped doom her. Heavier
  guilt; drop if too much.
- **The arc it sets up:** the spell's true cost is that the five must **die** to birth
  the new world. Kynes **refused to burn with his mother out of fear** (and let fire — his own element — take her); his atonement is
  to **burn with the other four out of love**, so no one's mother burns again. His sin
  and his redemption rhyme.
- **Alt:** swap the mother for a **sister** if youth/innocence is wanted over the
  widow-healer authenticity.

**The other four (CANON 2026-07-12 — names/incidents are proposals, tweak freely).**
Genders locked: Fire (Kynes) M, **Air M, Earth M, Water F, Spirit F.** Each survived the
witch-hunt by doing or losing something terrible — that is their *survival wound*. They
are five damaged people, not a hero + four helpers, which is why "die to end the hunts
forever" costs them so much. Found in this order (the pull compounds as the group grows):

1. **Alys Marsh — WATER (F, ~30s).** *Found first* (Father's one rumor points to her:
   an old tale of "a drowning on dry ground"). As a girl her power broke loose and
   **drowned someone** — a sibling / a household / a mill full of people. Since then she
   has **refused to touch her gift**, living beside water she will not command (laundress,
   fishwife). **Wound: guilt over a lethal loss of control; total self-denial.** She is
   Kynes's mirror — both suppress their power — but from *killing*, not cowardice. Clash:
   Kynes needs her to embrace the gift; she would rather die than use it again.
   *Rumor trail:* a woman who lives on the river but won't go near it; a drowning no water
   explains.

2. **Silas Vane — AIR (M, ~20s–30s).** *Found second.* Charming, quick, funny — and a
   **coward.** He survived by **informing on other accused "witches"** to turn the hunt
   away from himself, sending innocents (and maybe marked ones) to the rope and the pyre.
   **Wound: self-loathing betrayal.** He is Kynes's darker mirror — both bought their
   lives with someone else's death (Kynes passively at the pyre, Silas actively). When
   the group learns what he did, trust shatters. The spell is his shot at redemption: the
   man who saved himself by sacrificing others chooses to die to save everyone.
   *Rumor trail:* a run of accusations that always break *around* one lucky man; a
   windless storm that stripped a barn roof.

3. **Aldous Reeve — EARTH (M, ~50s, the oldest).** *Found third — the anchor.* A weathered
   farmer/reeve who kept his people fed through famine by quietly coaxing the soil — which
   eventually drew the hunt and **cost him his family** (taken, or sent away and lost). He
   survived by **burying his power, and himself, so deep he's gone numb** — endures, feels
   nothing, has outlived everyone he hid it for. **Wound: grief calcified into numbness.**
   Stone: patient, immovable, done believing anything can change. Kynes's hope irritates
   then moves him; he becomes the group's father-figure. Arc: feeling something again
   before the end.
   *Rumor trail:* a farm that never fails in a killing famine; an old man blight won't touch.

4. **Wren Holt — SPIRIT (F, ~early 20s).** *Found last — hardest to reach, not hardest to
   find.* Everyone has *heard* of the mad prophetess (Spirit leaks — she can't hide), but
   she's confined / exploited / dismissed as a lunatic, so reaching her is the hard part.
   She **hears the dying and dreams true**, and no one has ever believed her. **Wound:
   isolation and half-madness from a lifetime of unwanted visions.** Fragile, lonely,
   unsure what is real. Per the earlier note she may **glimpse the cost** in fragments she
   can't fully parse (leaked truth, NOT orchestration — she is equal to the others). Arc:
   the first people who ever believe her; and the horror that she may have already seen
   their deaths.
   *Rumor trail:* the notorious woman who tells you how you'll die.

(Kynes — FIRE (M) — has a wound too: **the pyre.** He survived by letting his mother burn.
So all five bought survival with a loss or a sin.)

**Father's role:** younger, hopeful, still has all his children. Gives the tablet but
withholds that the spell kills all five and ends their world. Complicates his character.

**The cost — the reveal & the choice (CANON 2026-07-12):**
- **Father withholds the cost; Kynes decodes it himself.** Kynes works out the tablet
  and learns the full price: the spell takes **all five of them** *and* **the whole world**
  (total end of Earth). The god who gave the tablet stayed silent about the price — the man
  who reads it will not.
- **Kynes does NOT hide it. He tells the group.** The instant he understands, he lays the
  whole truth before them — no deception, no manipulation. This is the deliberate INVERSE
  of his wound: the man whose sin was deadly *silence* at his mother's pyre refuses to be
  silent, and refuses to be Father, now. (His honesty is the point — it's how he begins to
  redeem the silence.)
- **The reveal lands ~60–70% in**, and turns the book from a *gathering* story into a
  **collective-choice** story: five damaged people, told the truth, each deciding whether to
  die to end the hunts forever. This is why the drafted ending has them "all in agreement" —
  it is *informed, willing* sacrifice, not a trick.
- **Every survival wound pays off in the choosing.** Kynes is the readiest (he has wanted to
  burn since the pyre — this is his atonement). **Silas (Air, the betrayer) is the hardest
  and most important yes:** the coward who bought his life with others' deaths must now give
  his own for others — his whole arc. Alys (who killed with her gift) may find her own
  atonement or her deepest fear; Aldous (numb) an end that finally means something; Wren
  (Spirit) may have already glimpsed it — her sight **corroborates** the tablet (leaked truth,
  not the source). The campfire persuasion (bible: "he convinced them… it was their duty") is
  the emotional climax.

**The ending (fixed & final):** all five die in a campfire ritual, pour their will into
Father's spell; the old world ends in light, all matter consumed; a new world is born
from their atoms and magic. No godhood, no reincarnation, total sacrifice. Father builds
the new world from his loss and from grief creates his children (Syl, Garzi, Rafel).

**THE CLIMAX — the campfire ritual (CANON 2026-07-12).** Built on one anchoring image:
- **The anchor — his hands.** At the pyre Kynes stood with his **hands at his sides** and
  let fire take his mother. The ritual has the five **raise their hands to the sky** to
  pour their power in. The whole book bends toward that inversion: the fire-crafter who
  once *refused* the fire now *reaches into it.* **This time he does not step back.** (Plant
  "hands at his sides" at the pyre so it pays off here.)
- **The fire, remote, at night.** Five hunted strangers, each of whom did or lost something
  terrible to be here. Last doubts surface — someone asks if there is *another way.* There
  isn't, and Kynes does not lie to soften it (his honesty again).
- **The choosing — Silas is the hinge.** The coward who bought his life with others' deaths
  is the last, hardest **yes.** He **nearly bolts and turns back on his own** — and it is
  **Kynes**, the man who once *did* run from a burning, who talks him back. The two mirrors
  resolve each other.
- **The ritual.** They join hands, raise them, pour will *and* power into the tablet's spell.
  The five elements go in together; the world ends in **light**, matter unmaking, total.
  They feel it. They simply **end** — no godhood, no reincarnation.
- **POV hinge to Father.** Ride it in **Kynes's POV to dissolution**, then hinge to **Father**
  for the birth — the one witness who remains. The book ends not on the heroes (gone) but on
  **Father, alone**, walking straight into Son of None's grieving god.
- **Seeding the mythology (final image).** As the five dissolve, **five points of light rise**,
  and (first in Wren's dying sight, then Father's) each light **becomes a dragon** — one per
  element — scattering into the **newborn sibling worlds** blooming from the released matter.
  This plants the whole cosmology on the page: *dragons = the five made flesh; the worlds =
  their death.* (Power carries on, not souls — per the conserved-power rule.)
- **Spirit's grace note.** **Wren**, who spent her life *seeing death*, gets the last mortal
  vision: as she dies she finally sees **birth** — the worlds her death buys. The only mercy
  anyone gets, and it's earned.
- **The last page — Father.** Alone with the loss, he **shapes the new worlds from grief and,
  from grief, makes his children** (Syl, Garzi, Rafel). The god who withheld the cost now pays
  it forever in mourning — the weakened, grieving Father of *Son of None*. Bookend closed.
- **Harshness held:** no reward, no ascension, no "they live on in the dragons." The only grace
  is Wren's glimpse and the worlds themselves — which none of them live to walk.

**Why total death is right:** sacrifice is total; Father's children born from grief
explains why losing more of them breaks him in Son of None; Ne'veran's world is sacred
ground nobody knows about; magic there is literally the residue of the five.

**Connection to Land of the Guardians:** dragons are living remnants of the elementals'
power (~five, one per element). Black (Aeron) and blue (Reagen) are two of five. Alten
consuming dragon hearts = cannibalizing the sacred legacy. Spine power/sacrifice/
consumption unifies all books.

## PART 4 — THE DRAGON ISLE (potential second volume; TBD)

Likely the back half of Land of the Guardians if split: journey to the island, earning
the dragons' trust, twins calling their dragons, Wolf's Moonwalker payoff, larger Alten
confrontation (taking back a heart), waking the elven kingdom, Katy crowned, Reagen consort.
