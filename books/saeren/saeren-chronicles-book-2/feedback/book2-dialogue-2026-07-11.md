# Book Two — Dialogue Assessment (report only)

**Date:** 2026-07-11
**Scope:** All 20 chapters, dialogue only. No prose changed; finished book on `main`, author sign-off required for any edit.
**Reference voice cards:** `research/book1-voice-dna.md`; character canon in `foundation.md`.
**Reviewer note:** This is a deliberately literary, close-third, atmospheric book. Most of what follows is refinement, not repair. The voices are, on the whole, strongly individuated — the manuscript passes the Cover-the-Name Test far more often than it fails it. The flags below are the exceptions and the patterns worth the author's eye before any future revision.

---

## HEADLINE FINDINGS

1. **Dialogue-to-prose ratio runs well below the stated target band (25–40%).** By eye the book sits nearer **10–20%** most chapters, dipping to ~5% in the interior-monologue and battle chapters (5, 8, 14, 16, 18). Only the two argument chapters (9, 11-adjacent, and 7) approach the low end of the target. This is a legitimate authorial choice (the quiet-book positioning), but it means dialogue carries less of the characterization load than the target assumes, and the few conversations that exist are doing heavy lifting. Not a defect to "fix" — a fact to own.

2. **The antagonist barely speaks in his own words.** Erik Meros — the book's engine of menace — has almost **no directly quoted dialogue**. His one big rhetorical moment, the battlefield offer (Ch.14), is *summarized* ("He said the capital had no wish to spill the blood of children misled…"), never quoted. This is the single largest missed characterization opportunity in the book. See per-chapter, Ch.14.

3. **The "flat/level voice" converges across three POV-adjacent characters.** Viridia, Jazen, and Amber-under-pressure all "go flat," and the narration repeatedly *names* it ("the flat voice," "level and stripped," "the levelness was the alarm"). It's a deliberate motif (Ch.9 even says they "taught each other the same tell"), but in the flat register the three become hardest to tell apart on dialogue alone. This is the chief Cover-the-Name weakness.

4. **Characters occasionally narrate their own thematic function (rule #8).** Strongest instance: Jazen's *"She's the question and I'm the warning, and we're both in the room"* (Ch.9). Beautiful line, but it is a character labeling the book's architecture. The whole Ch.9 argument edges toward on-the-nose by design; mostly earns it, but see fixes.

5. **A grave-elder tic is shared too widely: "Do you understand me."** Drake, Varissa, Jazen, Lor-ar, and Viridia all use it. Ch.13 lampshades the hand-off deliberately, but across the whole book it slightly homogenizes the ancient/grave voices.

## PER-CHARACTER VOICE NOTES

### Viridia (protagonist) — DISTINCT, with a caveat
Spare, single-word truths ("Yes."; "It holds."; "I read it."), the precise-true-thing-as-gift ("You look better"), never explains herself. This is pitch-perfect to the Book-One card and is the most consistent voice in the book. **Caveat:** her signature under-pressure move (flatten, catalogue) is *shared* with Jazen, and in her longer speeches late in the book (Ch.17 to the dead Jazen, Ch.20 at the fork) she becomes more openly eloquent than her Book-One card ("spare … never explains herself") would predict. This is arguably earned — the wall/well broke the careful register on the page — but the author should confirm the late eloquence is a chosen arc and not drift. Her best lines remain the short ones.

### Amber — DISTINCT (best-realized peer voice)
Ticks on fingers, "There's no three / There's a three," "I've got a degree in feelings from the woods," the bun-woman, deflects sincerity then drops the armor. Unmistakable. The Ch.9 challenge is her high point and it holds voice even while carrying the theme. One risk: when Amber turns fully serious (Ch.9, Ch.19) she loses the tics and can drift toward the same flat register as Viridia/Jazen — but she almost always re-grabs the joke ("congratulations, you've outdone a death"), which rescues it.

### Jazen — DISTINCT but LECTURE-PRONE
Level, iron-under-grief, plain-because-plainness-is-the-only-honesty. Strong. **Two issues:** (a) he shares Viridia's flat register (see Headline 3); (b) he is the book's biggest **lecture-mode** offender — the salt-road confession (Ch.3), the "mirror" speech (Ch.9), the Ch.10/Ch.11 "you did not choose the wall" absolutions. These are dramatized and mostly gripping, but several run 6–10 uninterrupted sentences of thematic exposition with Viridia only receiving. Consider (for any future pass) breaking one or two with her interjecting or deflecting.

### Lor-ar — DISTINCT
Formal, ancient, dryly amused, aphoristic, "little one" rationed hard (and the rationing pays off enormously in Ch.19). "I am three hundred years old and I have learned…" cadence is his alone. Excellent. Only shares the "do you understand me" tic.

### Drake — DISTINCT but LECTURE-PRONE
Gruff, from-a-height, grief-as-bluntness ("Doesn't look like much"; "You've got hands"). The clipped tags are superb. **But** Drake delivers the book's longest monologues (Ch.7 daughter/war/symbol; Ch.8 the door and the lesson; Ch.12 goodbye). They are load-bearing and voiced, but they are essentially *lectures*, and a reader could feel the author's hand delivering world-rules through him. The gruff one-liners are where he lives; the long speeches are where he risks becoming a mouthpiece.

### Varissa — DISTINCT
Fierce, present, "mine," close-to-the-ground, the repeated "Bring him back. Whatever shape." She is the sharpest emotional voice in the dragon material and never blurs with Drake. Her Ch.12 and Ch.19 speeches are long but stay in-voice because they're all heat, not rule-exposition.

### Raizen — DISTINCT (best comic voice in the book)
Wonder + more-words-than-needed + literalism. "You are *smaller* than I remember"; "They're at the *bottom*"; "This is warm," repeated hollow. Carries the book's only sustained relief. **One mechanical inconsistency:** in Ch.2 and Ch.7 his speech is rendered partly in *italics* (bond-speech) mixed with roman, then from later chapters he simply speaks in plain quotes. The italic-vs-roman convention for "bond" vs "spoken" wobbles once he "learns to speak." Worth a consistency decision (see Attribution).

### Alice (changed) — DISTINCT and deliberately altered
The calm, even, healer's-correction voice ("You shouldn't have come up here"; "You're not, but you can be cold next to me instead of cold by yourself") is a strong, sad contrast to the Book-One warm-runaway card, with the crooked tooth and "you always come through the wrong door" as the surviving warmth. This is exactly the intended arc and it lands.

### Meros (antagonist) — WEAKEST VOICE (mostly absent)
See Headline 2. He is characterized almost entirely through narration and staging, not speech. The "false grandfatherly warmth over clinical assessment" card from Book One is asserted, rarely dramatized in his own quoted words. This is the book's clearest voice gap.

### One-off / supporting voices — mostly DISTINCT
- **Councilor (Ch.10):** excellent one-chapter voice — "Both, in the same coat," cost-vs-right, weary bravery. A highlight.
- **Ferro (Ch.2):** scout's flat report, "I report what I saw," detail-as-trade. Distinct and does exposition cleanly (see Ch.2).
- **Brutus, Mirelle, Hiram, Coram, Mella, silver-bun woman (Ch.1/6/11):** each carries one distinguishing tag (Brutus counts in winters/"I built this"; Mirelle "you don't have to hover"; Hiram "the arithmetic, girl"; Mella's locket/grandchildren; silver-bun's cold arithmetic; Coram soft-and-last). The Ch.1 council scene passes Cover-the-Name well.

## PER-CHAPTER FLAGS

**Ch.1 — clean.** Council scene distinguishes four speakers by content and tag economy. Amber's "There is no three. I had a three. It drowned." is a model of voice. No action needed.

**Ch.2 — mostly clean.** Ferro's report is a good example of exposition delivered *through a character's trade* rather than "as-you-know." Flag: Raizen's first spoken lines mix italic bond-speech and roman ("*You came,*" vs "You," he said) — establish the convention here.

**Ch.3 — watch lecture length.** Jazen's salt-road confession is powerful but is ~2 long uninterrupted speeches with Viridia only saying "You don't have to." Consider (future) one Viridia interruption to keep it a conversation, not a monologue received.

**Ch.4 — clean.** Amber's "Not yet, and maybe not by us," imitating a voice she never heard, is excellent character-through-dialogue.

**Ch.5 — LOW dialogue / lecture.** Nearly all Lor-ar. His counsel is in-voice but this is essayistic. Amber's late entrance rescues the ratio. Note the chapter is ~5,400 words with perhaps 12% dialogue.

**Ch.6 — near-zero dialogue (battle).** Fine by design. Hiram's "the arithmetic, girl" and "Long enough. It was a lie, and the kind a brave man tells" are the standout lines.

**Ch.7 — DRAKE LECTURE + rule #8 watch.** Drake's daughter/war/death-symbol speech is the longest single monologue in the book. Load-bearing world-rules, but it is a lecture. Also Varissa's "you think the only life on your ledger is your own" is theme stated fairly directly — earned by her fury, but note it.

**Ch.8 — DRAKE LECTURE (the door/the lesson).** Same as Ch.7. Beautiful, but a reader gets two consecutive chapters of Drake-explains-the-world. If any pacing trim is ever wanted, this is where dialogue could become exchange rather than delivery.

**Ch.9 — THE ARGUMENT: on-the-nose by design; two rule-#8 lines.**
- Amber: *"That's the council, Vee. That's the chamber. … The not-asking is the whole crime and you'd commit it bigger than anyone in history."* — The book's spine, delivered through a friend, mostly earns it. But it is the theme stated almost as thesis.
- Jazen: *"She's the question and I'm the warning, and we're both in the room … we can't both be heeded."* — **Clearest instance of a character naming his own symbolic role.** Proposed softer fix (author's call): let him *show* the mirror without labeling it — cut "She's the question and I'm the warning" and let the salt-road retelling carry the function. As written it tells the reader what the scene means.
- This chapter also has the strongest dialogue in the book (Amber's "you've outdone a death"). Net: the finest and the most-exposed chapter at once.

**Ch.10 — clean, strong.** The councilor is a highlight. His cost-vs-right speech is long but stays in-voice because it's confession, not rule-delivery.

**Ch.11 — LOW dialogue (battle).** Brutus's death lines ("I built this. … Don't let them have the field too.") are spare and perfect. Hiram's "don't waste my holding by standing here being sorry at me" — model voice.

**Ch.12 — Varissa/Drake goodbyes; "do you understand me" tic peaks.** Varissa's long goodbye holds (all heat). Note the tic: Drake, Varissa, and Lor-ar all in one chapter reach for the grave-elder register. Lor-ar's "there is no absolving in me" is the best line.

**Ch.13 — the "do you understand me" hand-off is lampshaded.** Jazen and Viridia consciously trade the phrase ("she handed it back to him on purpose, his own words"). This *justifies* the tic locally — but it's also the moment to notice how load-bearing that one phrase has become book-wide.

**Ch.14 — MEROS: the missed voice.** *"The small bald man began to speak, and what he said was not for them … He said the capital had no wish to spill the blood of children misled."* The antagonist's single great oration is summarized, not quoted. **Proposed fix (author's call): give Meros 2–4 lines of actual quoted speech here** — the false-grandfatherly-warmth-over-clinical-assessment voice made audible once, so the reader has heard the man before Drake burns him. Even a single quoted sentence ("*No child of the light need die today; lay down the corruption and be taught the clean half of what you are.*") would convert the book's flattest antagonist-beat into characterization. Jazen's "No," handed down the line, is excellent as-is.

**Ch.15 — strong.** Alice's changed voice debuts here and is distinct and devastating. "He's not going to make it, Vir. You know that. You've known it longer than I have." — perfect.

**Ch.16 — near-zero dialogue (the hinge).** Correct. The silence is the point.

**Ch.17 — Viridia's long spoken elegy to Jazen.** In-voice but note it is her most *eloquent* speech in the book ("I couldn't be the leash… I was a wrong door, I was a hair short, all morning, every time"). Confirm this is the intended post-break register, not drift from the "spare" card.

**Ch.18 — no dialogue (rebirth).** Correct.

**Ch.19 — best dialogue chapter in the back half.** Raizen's buttons comedy ("There is a god somewhere who is laughing at me") + Drake's "You've got hands" + Varissa's "I know where they are" + Lor-ar's "Little one" final = four distinct voices, each unmistakable. This chapter is the Cover-the-Name high-water mark. Alice's "you can be cold next to me instead of cold by yourself" closes it. No notes.

**Ch.20 — mostly narrated; Viridia's fork "speech" is reported, not quoted.** Deliberate essayistic finale. The choice to *report* rather than *quote* her address to the survivors ("There is no home to go to, she told them, in not so many words") is consistent with the book's restraint; no change needed, but note it's the second time (with Ch.14) a big oration is summarized rather than voiced.

## SUBTEXT & ATTRIBUTION (quick pass)

**Subtext:** Generally excellent where it matters. Brutus's wall = "he was counting how many [winters] were left"; Drake's "Doesn't look like much" = blessing; Mirelle's chosen word "safe"; Alice's "you're cold" = the whole friendship. The argument chapters (5, 9) are *low-subtext by design* (surface = real), appropriate for the moral spine but the reason those chapters read as the least "quiet."

**Attribution:** Clean and disciplined — "said"/"asked" dominate; no creative-tag abuse; comma-inside-quotes convention held. Two minor items:
- **Raizen italic/roman inconsistency** for bond-vs-spoken speech (Ch.2, 7 vs later). Pick one rule.
- Mild over-reliance on **"observed"** for Raizen and Lor-ar ("Raizen observed," "Lor-ar observed") — a small verbal tic in the tags themselves. Trivial.

## SUMMARY FOR THE AUTHOR

**Weakest-voiced characters:**
1. **Erik Meros (antagonist)** — barely speaks in his own words; his one oration (Ch.14) is summarized. The book's clearest voice gap. *Highest-value optional fix: quote him once.*
2. **The shared "flat/level" register of Viridia + Jazen (+ Amber under pressure)** — individually strong, but they converge in the pressure moments the book returns to most, and the narration keeps naming the tell. This is the main Cover-the-Name softness.

**Top issues (in priority order):**
1. Give Meros **actual quoted dialogue** at Ch.14 (and optionally one earlier beat) — convert the flattest antagonist moment into characterization.
2. **Lecture mode** in Drake (Ch.7, 8, 12) and Jazen (Ch.3, 9): mostly earned world-rules/confession, but consider breaking one or two long monologues into exchange if a pacing pass is ever done.
3. **Rule #8 — characters naming their own theme:** trim Jazen's "She's the question and I'm the warning" (Ch.9); watch the Ch.9 argument's directness generally (it earns most of it).
4. **"Do you understand me"** shared across five characters — lampshaded in Ch.13, but book-wide it homogenizes the grave voices; thin by one or two instances.
5. **Raizen italic/roman bond-speech convention** — make consistent.
6. **Confirm Viridia's late eloquence** (Ch.17, 20) is an intended post-break arc, not drift from the "spare, never explains herself" card.

**What is working (do not touch):** Raizen (Ch.19 comedy), Amber (Ch.9), Lor-ar's rationed "little one" payoff (Ch.19), Varissa, Alice's altered voice, the Ch.1 council, Ferro's trade-based exposition, and Viridia's short single-word truths throughout. The dialogue's biggest strength is restraint and subtext; its biggest exposure is that there is *so little* of it that each conversation is load-bearing — and the one character who most needs a voice (Meros) was given the least.
