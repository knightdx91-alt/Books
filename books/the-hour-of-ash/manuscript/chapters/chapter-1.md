They tell you not to clear a dead man's house alone. They tell you a great many sensible things, in the weeks after, in voices gone soft and careful, and I nodded at all of them and did the opposite, because I was thirty-four years old and very good at being alone in rooms with things that had outlived the people who loved them. It is, in fact, my profession. I sit in a basement four days a week with a scalpel and a tray of solvents and I coax the grime of centuries off objects nobody living remembers, and my hands do not shake. I had assumed my hands would work in my father's study the way they worked over a corroded hinge. I had assumed a lot of things that April.

The first thing that defeated me was the drawer.

It was the bottom left drawer of his desk, the deep one built for hanging files, and it would not open. Not stuck in the negotiable way a swollen drawer is stuck, where you rock it and swear at it and it yields you an inch of apology. This one had decided. I knelt on the cold boards and worked the brass pull with both hands and got nothing, not the faintest give, and I said a word to the empty room that I will not write down, and I was not saying it to the drawer.

I knew what was in there. That was the trouble. If I had not known, I would have fetched a screwdriver and been a competent adult about it, the way I had been competent about the four drawers on the right, all emptied now into folders I had bought specifically in the wrong colour so that sorting his life would feel like starting something instead of ending it. But I knew. His reading glasses were in that drawer. Tortoiseshell, the cheap sort from the chemist, one-point-five he always said though they were plainly two, and I knew they were folded in there because I had watched him fold them and put them there roughly ten thousand times.

He read everything. Cereal boxes, other people's headstones, the museum captions I had personally written and which he read slowly, moving his lips, so that he could tell me afterward which comma was wrong. And when he finished a thing, when he had come to the end of the page or the chapter or the bad news, he took the glasses off one-handed, folded the arms in against the frame, and set them down, and they made a small sound.

*Click.*

The most ordinary noise a house can hold. A little plastic *click,* and then whatever came next. *Click,* and the dig had lost its funding. *Click,* and your mother is going to live with Alan now. *Click,* and it's nothing, sit down, you're hovering, the doctor is a worrier by trade. I had heard that sound so many times it had gone below language and settled in the body, the way a childhood staircase lives in your feet, and now the glasses were folded in a drawer that would not open and would never make the sound again, and I could not get the drawer to move, and I could not have opened it if it had.

So I gave up on it. An hour on my knees, brass-marks pressed pink into both palms, and I gave up, which is the truest thing I can tell you about grief and also, if I am honest, about the whole of my life until the year everything changed. When I finally stood, both legs had gone dead under me and I sat straight back down on the floor, hard, and had to wait for the feeling to come back before I could try being upright again. There is no dignity in it. Nobody tells you that one either.

The rest of the study I could manage. The rest of the study was not the drawer.

---

I worked through the afternoon with the wireless on low, a station I did not choose playing songs I half-knew and could not have named, which suited me exactly, because a song with a name attached would have had a memory attached, and I was rationing memories the way you ration water. Outside the window the garden went from grey to greyer. It was the sort of English spring that cannot commit, sleet at breakfast and a thin lying sunshine by noon and nothing you could call warmth, and I had the two-bar fire on and still worked in my coat.

My father was an archaeologist. I should have said that. His name was Emilio Reyes, and he spent his life on other people's centuries and precious little on his own, and the study was the sediment of it: three walls double-shelved, the second row of books hidden behind the first the way he hid the paperbacks he could not admit he loved behind the site reports he wanted you to see him loving. Behind a grave grey run of excavation monographs I found a thriller with a rocket on the cover, the spine cracked white in six places, read to death, a man who dug up the actual past unwinding at night in somebody's invented future. I sank down against the shelf with it and read the first page in his voice and could not get to the second.

There were slides. That was the find of the afternoon, if find is a word I am allowed. A whole carousel of them and three loose boxes besides, thirty years of dig seasons in little cardboard mounts, and I held them up one at a time to the failing window and they lit like small stained windows of trapped Mediterranean noon. A trench. A section drawing pinned to a baulk. A meter-stick against a wall, a wall, a wall; he photographed walls the way other men photograph their children, and I had been, as a child, occasionally and stupidly jealous of walls. Somewhere in the middle of holding his whole working life up to a dying window I caught myself wondering whether I had put the lid back on the solvent tray at the museum before I left on the Friday, and I could not remember. His thumb was in the corner of two of them. In one, taken against the light so the figures were only shapes, a young man crouched at the lip of a trench with his hand out, and I could not tell if he was steadying himself or reaching for something in the dirt, and there was no one to ask, and this is the thing nobody warns you about, that the dead take the captions with them and leave you the pictures.

I made a stack to keep and a stack to give away. The keep stack became a tower and then a second tower. The give-away stack held a duplicate atlas, a dictionary, and, for one brave minute, the rocket thriller, until I took it back. I was not clearing the room. I was rearranging it. I was a magpie in the costume of a removal van, and I had been doing versions of exactly this my whole life.

I found his datebook in the top drawer, the long shallow one that worked. He kept a paper diary to the end, a little black one from the stationer's on the high street, the same model every January for thirty years while the rest of the world went to its screens and its mobile telephones. It was 1998. I mean to be exact about the year, because exactness is the only thing I have ever reliably been able to offer the dead, and because later it would matter to me in ways I could not then imagine that I had lived at the tail end of a century that still ran on film and paper and the human voice down a wire. I opened the diary to April, out of a curiosity I would have called concern if I were being gentle with myself and nosiness if I were not.

The week he died held three lines. *Dentist, Tue 14.* He had not gone to the dentist. *Ring Halloran back re: permit, Italy, be quick.* And on the Sunday before, in his cramped forward-leaning capitals, underlined twice so hard the pen had nearly gone through: *START THE LIST.*

I did not know what list. There was always a list; the man made lists the way other men breathe, lists of finds and lists of shopping and lists of the small revenges he intended to take on colleagues and never did. But he had underlined it twice, at the end, in a hand pressed harder than a shopping list earns, and I sat on the floor of his study holding a dead man's diary and felt, for the first time all day, not grief exactly but its ugly cousin: the feeling of having been left a question with the answer torn off. For a stupid half-second I actually thought, well, I could start it for him, I am the one who is good at lists, it is the finishing that always... and then I remembered which of us was dead, and stopped being clever. I make the joke and then the joke turns round and looks at me. I have never been able to leave a thing unfinished. It is my worst quality and I have dressed it up as diligence for so long that the disguise fits better than the fault. Show me a half-excavated trench and I will finish it though the funding is gone and the permit is void and the man who started it is dead. It is, I have come to think, the closest thing I have to a religion, and like most religions it has mostly cost me.

But all of that was nine years and two thousand miles and one impossible thing away, and I did not know yet that any of it was coming, and I owe some fairness to the woman on the floor, who thought the worst had already happened to her and was only sorting through the wreckage of it. She had no idea. I have had all the years I was given to be tender toward her, and I am, mostly. There are nights I would go back and take her by the wrist and say don't. There are other nights I would not change a single step. I have never been able to hold both of those still at once for longer than it takes to say them.

There was a promise between us that I have not mentioned, because it is a thing you do not put down until you have to. He had been saying it since I was nine. *Next season I'll take you with me.* Next season, when the permit came through, when the money was there, when I was old enough, when I was done with school, with my training, with the doctorate I did not finish. There was always a next season and I was never in it. He went to Libya and Jordan and the parched hill in Turkey and I stayed in the basement with the magnifier, cleaning what other diggers pulled out of the ground, and I told myself I preferred it, that the field was heat and dysentery and men and I was better with the quiet and the scalpel, and none of that was untrue and all of it was a wall I had built one polite refusal at a time. He asked. He asked most years, right up to the February heart, the bad one, when he said it from the hospital bed with the tubes in and I laughed and said next season then, and he said yes, next season, and we both let it be a joke because the alternative was unbearable and we were, the two of us, world-class at choosing the joke. He died in April with a permit half-arranged and a daughter who had spent her whole life declining to go into the field with the one person who had ever asked her. I have thought, since, that the door did to me by force the thing my father spent twenty-five years asking me to do by choice. It took me into the field at last. He would have found that funny. He would have folded his glasses over it. I did not find it funny for a very long time.

---

By six the light was gone and I had not eaten and the not-eating had reached the stage where it feels like a small accomplishment, a thing you are getting away with. I went down to the kitchen to make tea and found the bread and that, absurdly, is where the day finally broke me, if broke is the word for something so quiet.

He kept a bread bin. In it was half a white sliced loaf, the industrial kind, gone hard at the cut edge, and I stood holding it and felt a contempt so pure and so misdirected that it frightened me. Because I have opinions about bread. I went through a phase, years back, a proper obsession, scales and a thermometer and a starter I named and fed and mourned when it died, hydration percentages, the whole ridiculous liturgy of crumb and crust, and then I quit it the way I quit most things, cleanly, without ceremony, the moment it began to matter to me. And here was my father, who had eaten flatbread baked in the ashes of a dozen countries and could tell you which century a grain pit belonged to by the char on the husks, buying the worst bread in England because it was cheap and it lasted and he was alone and could not be bothered, and I stood in his cold kitchen holding the stale evidence of how small his life had got at the end while I was in the city being calm over other people's ruins, and I did not cry. I want that on the record. I have never in my life cried when a moment called for it. I put the bread down and washed a mug that was already clean and dried it and put it away and went back up to the study, because the study was a problem I could work at and the kitchen was only a fact.

I decided I would do the shelves properly. I would take the second, hidden row out, book by book, because that was where he kept the things he actually was, and if I was going to be a magpie I could at least be a thorough one. And it was behind the second row, wedged flat against the back of the shelf where the hidden books hid, that I found the tube.

It was a mailing tube, the grey cardboard sort with black plastic ends, water-stained along one seam, and it did not belong to him. I knew his hand on sight, and the label on this was a stranger's, a looping confident copperplate more suited to a wedding invitation than a postal tube, addressed to a name I had never heard: *A. Fenn, Esq.,* and beneath it an address that had been crossed out and another written under, and that one crossed out too, a small history of a thing that could not find where it belonged. The postmark was too faded to read. The tube had clearly been opened long ago and its ends pushed back on, and it had been hidden, deliberately, behind the row of books my father hid, which meant it was a thing he could not bear to be seen keeping, and I sat down on the floor with it in my lap and worked the plastic end off, and that is the exact moment, though I did not feel the floor tilt, that my old life ended and the other one began.

Inside was a map.

It was old, but not antique-dealer old, not the flaking hand-inked kind you keep behind glass; it was the good sturdy linen-backed survey kind, a working map, folded and refolded so many times the creases had gone furry and one of them had parted and been mended from behind with a strip of gummed paper browned to the colour of weak tea. It was a stretch of coastline. Not English. The place-names, where I could read them under the fold-wear, were a muddle of scripts, some Roman-lettered and some not, and there were depths marked offshore in a hand that knew what it was doing, and contour lines, and a scatter of small crosses inland, the kind an excavator makes, and one of the crosses was ringed, twice, hard, the pen nearly through the linen.

Twice. Underlined twice, ringed twice. I thought of *START THE LIST* pressed into the diary with the same furious pressure and felt the back of my neck go cold in a way I told myself was the draught.

There was writing in the margins, and it was not my father's, it was the copperplate, the wedding-invitation hand of *A. Fenn, Esq.,* and it was the writing of a man performing confidence for an audience he did not have. *The door,* it said, in one margin, and I read it and thought, a door, some site feature, a sea-gate, a temple threshold, the kind of thing a coastal survey is full of, and read on and stopped thinking that rather quickly. Because further down, smaller, as if the confidence had leaked out of it between one line and the next, the same hand had written: *The door is not honest. It opened for me and would not open for the others and I have stopped pretending I understand the rule of it. Went through the fourth of the month. Have not been able to get back. This is the eleventh attempt and I am writing it down in case there is not a twelfth.*

I read that sentence four times. I am a careful reader; my father trained me to it, moving his lips over my commas. I read it four times and each time it declined to become the thing I wanted it to be, which was the diary of a madman, a treasure-hunter's fever, a ghost story a lonely old man had told himself in the tropics and typed up to feel less alone. Because a madman does not write *this is the eleventh attempt* in the flat exhausted voice of a man logging a failed experiment. A madman soars. This hand had started out soaring, up in the wedding-invitation letters, and somewhere in the writing of it had come down, and down, and gone quiet and numbered and precise. I know the sound of a person coming down out of their own story and landing on the hard floor of a fact. I make that sound myself. I heard it coming off the page in a stranger's hand.

My father had this. My father had hidden this behind the books he was ashamed to love. My father, who did not believe in a single thing he could not stratify and date and draw in section, who once made a child cry at a public lecture by explaining with real gentleness that no, there had been no flood, the layers did not lie, my father had kept a map with a door marked on it and a dead man's note saying the door was not honest. He had rung a man named Halloran about a permit. He had underlined *START THE LIST* twice in the last week of his life. And I had, four hours ago, been unable to open a drawer because I was afraid of a pair of glasses. Now I sat on his floor with a coastline in my lap and understood that I had never truly known what my father was actually doing.

I have wondered, since, on the long nights, whether he meant to leave it for me or meant to take it with him and simply ran out of Tuesdays. I have wondered whether the list I was to start was this one. I have wondered whether, if the drawer had opened, if I had held the folded glasses and heard in my memory the *click* and let the day finish breaking me the way it was meant to, I would have wept myself empty and gone back to the city on the Thursday train and left the tube behind the books for the house-clearance men to bin. I think about that more than I think about almost anything. I think the drawer stayed shut to keep me from the ordinary grief that would have sent me home. I do not believe that. I am a woman of solvents and section drawings and I do not believe the furniture conspires. But I have had a great deal of time to sit with the fact that the drawer that killed my father, or that I had decided killed him, was also the drawer that would not let me be finished with him, and both of those cannot be luck, and neither can they be anything else, and that is the shape of the whole story I am going to tell you, so you may as well meet it now on the first page, in a cold study, in the last spring of a century, in the shut brass mouth of a drawer.

---

I did not sleep in the house. I want to be honest about the size of what happened that night, which is that nothing happened, that I did not have a revelation or hear a voice or feel the pull of destiny, because destiny, in my experience, does not announce itself; it just quietly removes your other options while you are looking at a map. I made a bad sandwich of the stale bread because I was suddenly and violently hungry, the body insisting on itself the way it does, and I ate it standing up over the sink, and it was, for the record, an offence against bread, and I ate all of it. And then I sat back down and read every margin of that map by the two-bar fire until my eyes ached, and I did the thing I always do, the thing that is my flaw wearing the mask of my virtue: I started to work the problem.

The crosses were sites. That much I could read. The ringed one had a set of numbers beside it in my father's hand this time, unmistakably his, added later in a different ink, and they were coordinates, and they were precise to a degree that no treasure-map flourish ever is, precise the way you are precise when you actually intend to stand in a place, and I copied them into the back of my own field notebook, the current one, the one that lived in my coat with a pencil through its spiral, and I did not ask myself why I was copying them, because I already knew and did not want to hear myself say it.

There was a card, too, fallen loose in the bottom of the tube, a trade card, the small stiff advertising kind, foxed at the corners: an antique dealer, a name, a street in a town three hours up the coast from where I sat, and on the back, in my father's hand, one word and a question mark. *Fenn?* Just that. As if my father had held this same tube and read this same impossible note and gone looking, late, in the last spring he had, for the man who wrote it. As if the list he meant to start was the finding of August Fenn.

I would learn what had become of August Fenn. I would learn it from his own mouth, in a language I did not yet speak, on the far side of a thing I did not yet believe in, and by then the learning would cost me more than I can put in a first chapter and be worth, God help me, more than that again. But that night I knew only what the card told me, which was a name and a street and a town, and I sat with my father's coordinates in my notebook and a dead stranger's map in my lap and the glasses I could not reach folded in the dark of a drawer at my back, and I made the decision that I have never, in all the years since, been able to decide whether to forgive myself for.

I decided to go and find the shop.

I told myself it was for him. I told myself I would finish this one small thing my father had started, ring the man named Halloran about the permit that no longer mattered, drive up the coast to a dusty shop and learn that August Fenn was a swindler thirty years dead and his map a pretty forgery, and then I would come home and open the drawer and hold the glasses and be, at last, a person who could grieve in the ordinary way, in the black-veil way, instead of the furniture-dispute way. I told myself the trip was the price of the peace. I have always been able to tell myself anything, so long as it lets me finish the thing I should walk away from.

There was a Halloran to ring first. The name from the diary, the permit man, and I had no telephone in the house that worked past the hall extension, so late as it was I walked down to the box on the corner with a fistful of change and a scrap of paper, past the dark windows of neighbours who had brought casseroles and did not know what else to do with me. Two of those casseroles I had put straight in the bin, dish and all, without tasting them, and felt nothing whatever about it. That was true, and grief made me, in small ways, a worse person than I usually bother to be. The box smelled of cold metal and old rain. I fed it coins and dialled the number my father had written and got, after a while, a tired man in a university office three time zones off who had plainly been ringing my father, not the other way about, and who went quiet when I told him. He had been holding a permit for a survey season on the Campanian coast, he said, a favour to an old friend, the paperwork almost through. He asked, carefully, whether the family meant to send anyone in his stead. I said I would let him know. I stood in the lit box with the receiver warm against my ear and the pips going and the coastline folded in my bag eight feet away in the dark, and I did not say the thing I already half-knew, which was that there was no one to send but me.

I walked back and laid out my things on the study floor the way I lay out tools before a job, because a job is the only prayer I know. My field notebook with the coordinates in the back. The wristwatch he had given me, cheap, accurate, its second hand sweeping. My camera, the good manual one, with a roll of film in it I had shot months ago and never developed and could not now have told you the contents of. A Discman and the one disc I always carried and no longer really heard. I did not know I was packing. In fairness, I would have said, that night, that I was only tidying. But a person who lays out her kit at midnight the day before a drive up the coast to find a dead man's map-maker is not tidying. I set the camera down at the end of the row, square to the others, and then picked it up again and turned it over in my hands and set it down a second time, in the same place, for no reason I could have given you.

Before I left in the morning I tried the drawer one more time. I don't know why. I knelt and took the brass pull in both hands, gently this time, the way you handle a thing you mean to keep, and I asked it, out loud, in the grey light, to please just this once open, and it did not, and I heard myself, and I stopped. I put the map back in its tube and the tube in my bag with my field notebook and my father's copied coordinates, and I did not take the slides, and I did not take the datebook, and I left the ugly loved mug in the ring of its own old stain, and I told the empty house I would be back in a day or two, which was true, which is the terrible thing, because I did come back, once, briefly, for the last time, and by then I was not the woman who had promised it.

The drawer I never did open. It is still shut, I imagine, in whatever room that house has become, holding a pair of folded glasses in the dark, and somewhere below the level of language I am still kneeling in front of it with brass-marks in my palms, still asking, still eight hours from the coast and the shop at the end of it, still a lifetime from the door, still, God, still so sure that the dead were the ones who did not leave.
