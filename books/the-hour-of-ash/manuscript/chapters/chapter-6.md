# Chapter 6: Kallia's House

I woke on my second morning in the wrong year to the smell of woodsmoke and the sound of a woman sweeping, and for one merciful blank moment I did not know where I was. Then I knew, all at once, the way you know a bruise when you roll onto it. I lay still in the goat-warm straw with my eyes shut and did the thing I would do every morning for a long time: let the fact arrive, and settle, and be survived, before I opened my eyes to it. I was here. It was still here. The morning had come, as I had told myself on the rock it would, and it had brought the whole of the impossible with it, undiminished, waiting.

Kallia's broom stopped. "You are awake," she said. It was not a question. "The dead sleep less than you."

She had not turned round. I understood most of it, and I understood too that it was a summons, and I got up.

That was the first lesson, and she taught it to me before I had washed my face: that I would earn my place under her roof, from the first hour, in labour. Charity is a thing a poor woman cannot afford to give twice, and I had already had my one night of it. She did not say this. She did not have to. She put a bucket in my hand, pointed at the door, and said the word for water, which she had given me the night before, and waited to see whether I had kept it.

I had. "Water," I said back.

She nodded once. That nod was worth more to me that morning than any praise I had ever been given for anything, because it was the first thing I had gotten right in nineteen centuries.

I want to set down plainly what the next weeks were, because a story would montage them. It would run the passage of a season under a swelling music and hand you a woman who belonged. The truth was slower than that, and more humiliating, and the humiliation is the point, so I am going to sit in it the way I had to.

---

The language nearly broke me, because I had thought I owned it, and I owned nothing.

Understand the particular cruelty of my case. I was not an illiterate peasant who had never met the tongue. I was a scholar of it, and my scholarship was worse than useless. It was a trap. My mouth was full of the wrong Greek, the frozen classroom Greek of the page, and it kept offering that Greek up in the confident tones of a woman who knows a language, and every time it did, I marked myself. The living speech had moved on nineteen hundred years from my books the way a river moves on from its spring. I stood on the bank with a map of where the water used to be.

Kallia set out to fix my mouth the way a smith fixes iron. She would hold up a thing and name it and make me name it back, and when I got it wrong, which was always, she would not spare my dignity, because dignity was a luxury and we were poor.

"Bread," she said, the second morning, holding the loaf.

I gave it the pure open vowel my professors had taught me.

She tipped her head, the way a hen does at a thing it is not sure is food. "You ask for bread like a man reciting his grandfather's will," she said. She said the word again, and hers was short and swallowed and ran straight into the next sound, the way a real word does in a real mouth. "Bread. You are hungry. Ask like it."

"Bread," I tried.

"Better. Now the water."

"Water," I said.

"Like a priest again." She flicked the wet from her fingers into my face, not hard. "Ask like a thirsty woman, not a temple."

"Water," I said, and this time I let it be ugly, let it be fast and low and needful, and she threw the rest of the cup at me, the whole of it, and it caught me full in the face and ran down inside my collar.

"Better," she said.

I stood there dripping, and then I was laughing, the first time I had laughed since the drawer in my father's cold house, a whole life and a whole language ago. Something in the laughing broke a wall the fear had built, and after that it went a little easier, because I had stopped trying to be right and started trying only to be understood. That is a different skill, and it is the only one that matters.

She was a hard teacher and a fair one. When I got a thing wrong she told me. When I got it right she said nothing, because getting it right was the baseline expected of a grown woman, and I understood the compliment buried in the silence: that she was treating me not as a marvel or a portent but as a slow adult who would eventually catch up. I have been flattered by experts, in the life before. Nothing ever warmed me like Kallia declining to be impressed.

She caught me out, once, weeks in. I had scratched a tally on the hearthstone without thinking, in a hand far too fluent for a woman who could barely ask for bread, and I saw her see it. I saw her decide not to be frightened by it, and file it instead, under *later*, the way I file things.

"Where did you learn your letters," she said.

"Far away." It was the truth, and the size of lie I could manage. "My father taught me."

She looked at me a while. "A father who teaches a daughter her letters," she said, "either loves her too much or has no sons." Then she gave me a proverb about the uses of a clever wife, and got the ending backwards, so it meant nearly the opposite of what it should, and went back to the pot.

I learned that day that the way to survive Kallia's questions was to answer the small true part and let her build the rest. She would build it kinder than the truth, and she would never check her work.

---

The body was its own apprenticeship, and mine had a great deal to unlearn.

I had a scholar's hands, and a scholar's hands are soft, and softness is a thing that gets noticed and then gets asked about. So my hands had to be ruined, and the work ruined them. I carried water until the blisters broke and healed into the beginnings of honest calluses. I learned to card wool, which sounds like nothing and flays the fingers. I learned the goat, whose name I never got, because Kallia did not name a thing she meant to eat, and I learned to milk it, badly, and then less badly.

The loom was the worst of it and the making of me. It stood against the wall, the great upright frame with its clay weights hanging in their rows, and I was hopeless at it for a long time. One evening Kallia came and stood behind me and watched me work a while in silence, which was itself a verdict, and then she reached past my shoulder and pulled out an hour of my weaving with two fingers.

"No," I said, before I could stop myself. The modern woman in me, who had never been corrected without a form to appeal it.

"No," she agreed, mild. "Look." She took my hand in her hard one and moved it, weight and shed and beat, slower than I had been going, until the wrong of it and the right of it were the same motion at different speeds. "You are quick," she said. "Quick is the enemy here. The wool does not care how clever you are. It cares only that you do one true thing, and then that thing again, and then again, until the doing of it forgets you were ever clever." She let my hand go. "Again. And do not think while you do it. Thinking is where you go wrong."

"I don't know how to not think," I said, and it came out truer than I meant it, the confession of my whole life, and she heard the size of it and did not make a thing of it.

"Then the loom will teach you," she said. "It is a good teacher. It has taught worse than you." And she left me to it, without another word, and went back to her own work, and I did the one true thing, and then it again, and then again, and somewhere in the doing of it my clever ruined hands began, at last, to learn.

There is a particular humiliation in being forty years an expert at one thing and six weeks a fumbling child at everything. I felt it daily, the scholar reduced to a slow pair of hands, and it galled me past the telling of it. Some evenings I sat at the loom with my ruined fingers and hated the wool, and hated the goat, and hated, for one black hour, the kindly hard old woman who would not let me be the clever thing I had spent my whole life being. Then it would pass. The alternative to learning was dying, and I am, whatever else, a survivor, and a survivor learns the loom.

My clothes changed by degrees, one honest object at a time, the way I had known on the mountain they would. The impossible trousers went up the hill under the cairn with the buried jacket. I put on a coarse thing of Kallia's, a sleeveless tunic pinned at the shoulders and belted, and over it, as the weather cooled, a rough mantle. I kept the boots. God help me, I kept the boots, hidden under the hem, my one indulgence, my one refusal. A woman can lose everything and still keep one small mutiny, and mine was footwear.

My hair grew. It grew out of its impossible modern shape into something a scarf could honestly cover, and the day I no longer flinched when a stranger's eye passed over me at the fountain was the day I understood I had begun, actually begun, to disappear into the world. I did not know whether to be glad or to grieve. I was both, and I told no one, because the only person who would have understood was the woman across the room, and she was busy grieving her own disappeared.

---

The olives brought me the rest of the way in, because the olives belonged to everyone and no one, and a harvest does not care who you are, only whether you will work.

They came ripe in the late autumn, all down the terraces and the long sacred plain, the whole silver forest I had looked at from the road on my first morning and had not let myself name. Now I stood inside it. The gathering was communal, the way the truly old work is, the women and the children and the old men of the valley moving out along the terraces at first light with cloths and baskets and long canes, and Kallia took me with her, and put a cane in my hand, and told me what a poor woman's honour required, which was that I not shame her in front of the valley.

"You beat the high branches, and let them fall on the cloth," she said. "You do not climb; climbing is for the young and the foolish and I have decided you are neither. You do not eat the fruit; it is bitter as sin off the tree and only a child does it once. And you keep your mouth working the small talk and your eyes on the fruit, and if a woman asks you a thing you cannot answer, your mouth is full, always, of the hill country and its stones."

I beat the high branches. It is good work, honest and rhythmic and bruising, the cane heavy in hands still learning to be hard, the small black-purple fruit coming down in a rattle onto the spread cloths, the smell of it rising all around, green and bitter and clean, the smell that was oil and lamplight and the whole economy of that world before it was pressed. And the women talked. God, how the women talked, a river of it, over and under the work, and I stood in the middle of the river and let it run past me and picked meaning out of it the way I picked the odd leaf out of the fruit, and it taught me more living Greek in three days than the inn had taught me in a month.

A broad woman on the next cloth, whose name I came to know as Melita, took it on herself to draw me out, in the way of women who consider a new quiet one a project.

"You're the one at Kallia's," she said. "The niece."

"The niece," I agreed. My mouth, as instructed, half full.

"From the hills." She looked me over, frank, not unkind, the assessment women give each other across work. "You've soft hands for the hills."

The cold flat place opened under me, small and quick. But I knew by now that the answer to a dangerous question is very often another true small thing offered fast, so the asker feels answered and moves on. "My mother kept me at the loom," I said, "and off the goats. She had ideas." I made a face that every woman there had made about her own mother, and it landed, and Melita laughed.

"Ideas," she said, with enormous scorn and enormous fondness, the two feelings a mother earns. "Mine had ideas. Look where they got me. Beating trees with the likes of you." And she was off, onto her mother, and her mother's ideas, and the marriage the ideas had made, and I was forgotten as a subject and folded in as a listener, which is the exact transaction I had wanted and could not have engineered, and I understood, standing in the falling fruit, that I had just been accepted, provisionally, by the valley, on the strength of a face about a mother and half a mouthful of the truth.

At the day's end we pressed, or the men did, at the great stone press below the grove, and the first oil ran out green and cloudy and astonishing, and there was a cup of the new stuff passed with bread, and I ate the bread dipped in the oil of olives I had helped bring down, in a grove below Delphi in the reign of Vespasian, among women who had decided I was one of them, and it was, I think, the first hour of my exile in which I was not afraid. Not brave. Just, for one hour, in the falling dark with the oil and the bread and the tired talk, unafraid, folded into a thing older and larger than my catastrophe, a pair of hands the work had used and did not question. Kallia watched me eat. She did not smile; she did not do that. But she gave me the nod, the water-bucket nod, the *you did not shame me* nod, and I have kept few things from that whole long time as carefully as I have kept it.

---

The house was an inn, of a small poor kind, and that was the thing that saved me twice over. It gave my presence a reason, and it gave Kallia a use for my hands.

She let two rooms to the traffic of the sacred road: the pilgrims coming up to ask the god their questions, the traders following the pilgrims, the occasional official on the sanctuary's slow business. She fed them a pot and poured them a rough wine and took their coin. And a lone woman working an inn is the most invisible woman there is, a fixture, a pair of hands with a jug. I learned to be that. I learned the living language faster among the inn's traffic than any drill could have taught me, because I had to. A traveller wants his wine now, and does not slow his speech for a slow woman.

One evening early on, a trader stopped the night, a fat cheerful Corinthian with opinions, and he watched me pour and decided, in the way of such men, to be amused by me.

"You," he said. "You've a strange way of speaking, girl. Where are you from?"

The whole room seemed to slow. This was the question, the one that ended in a road or worse, and I had no answer ready, and my heart went to the flat cold place, and I opened my mouth not knowing what would come out of it.

Kallia answered from the hearth without looking up. "She's my sister's girl from the hill country," she said, in the flat bored voice of a woman stating the obvious. "They talk like they've stones in their mouths, up there. Marry your cousins long enough and see how you sound." She banged the pot. "Leave her be and eat your supper. The wine's paid for, the girl isn't."

The trader laughed, and the room laughed with him, and the danger went out of the air as quickly as it had come, and I filled his cup with a hand that had learned, in the last three seconds, exactly how thin the ice was that I walked on every hour of every day. Later, when he had gone up, Kallia kept her eyes on the pot.

"Hill country," she said. "If a man asks again. Backward, poor, far. It answers everything and invites nothing." She scoured the pot. "A lie is like a garment. The plainer it is, the fewer people remember they saw it."

"That is not a proverb," I said. I could tease her, by that point.

"It is now," she said.

---

The first time she sent me to the market alone, I understood that she was not being lazy. She was giving me an examination.

It was a small enough errand, a measure of barley and a twist of salt and a length of cheap braid for a mantle, and she gave me the coin for it counted into my palm and closed my fingers over it and looked at me the way you look at a thing you are about to drop and hope will float. "You can pass in my kitchen," she said. "The kitchen loves you. The market does not love anyone. Go and be no one and come back."

I went down to the little market below the sanctuary with my heart going and my scarf close, and I want to set down what that walk was, because a person from my old life cannot imagine it, the sheer nerve it took to be an ordinary woman buying barley. Every face was an examiner. Every exchange was an oral in a language I was failing forward in. By then I understood that the trick of passing is not to perform belonging, which reads at once as performance, but to withhold, to be dull, to give the world so little to catch on that its eye slides off you and goes to the next thing. So I was dull. I bought the barley from a man who barely glanced at me, and I bought the salt, and my confidence, which is always my undoing, began to rise.

It was the braid that nearly finished me. The woman selling it named a price, and I, forgetting myself, forgetting nineteen centuries, did the thing my whole competent modern life had trained me to do, which is that I heard a price I judged too high and I went to argue it on the merits, opened my mouth to say the braid was poorly dyed and the price a Corinthian's insult, in a burst of the fluent, structured, confident speech of a woman who has never in her life been afraid to complain about a purchase. Three words in, I heard myself. I heard the wrongness of it, the register, the fluency, the whole impossible educated woman rising up out of my mouth in a market below Delphi, and I saw the seller's face begin to change, begin to sharpen from boredom into attention, into the beginning of the question, *who talks like that,* and the cold flat place opened wide and black under my feet.

I did the only thing there was to do. I let the sentence collapse. I let the fluent woman fall down in the middle of her own clever complaint and I put a coughing peasant stammer over the top of her, ducked my head, went red, which by mercy I can do at will, and mumbled the hill-country shape of *too dear, too dear for me,* and made myself small and poor and slow and embarrassed, a backward woman who had reached above herself and been shamed for it. And the seller's face, which had been sharpening toward the dangerous question, softened back into contempt, which is a much safer thing than curiosity, and she knocked a little off the price the way you do for the simple, and I paid, and I got out of that market with my barley and my salt and my braid and my life, shaking.

I told Kallia. I thought she would be angry. She listened to the whole of it, the fluent burst and the collapse and the red face, and when I had finished she was quiet a moment, and then she did a thing she almost never did, which was that she reached out and touched my cheek, once, with the back of her hard fingers.

"You are going to live," she said. It was not praise, exactly. It was a diagnosis, delivered with something underneath it I had not heard from her before, something that in a less guarded woman would have been pride. "A fool would have won the argument. You lost it on purpose, in time. That is the whole art, girl. Not the not-slipping. Everyone slips. The falling down soft, in time, so the slip looks like nothing." She turned back to the pot. "The clever survive by being thought stupid. Remember it. It is the only wisdom I have that is worth the oil to say it by."

And then she said nothing more about it, ever, which was its own kind of mercy, and put me back to work, and let me keep the shame to myself until it had worn down into something I could carry. I have carried it since. It is the most useful thing anyone ever taught me, and she taught it to me by declining, for once, to dress it in a saying.

---

It was a pilgrim at the inn who first put the god into my head as a thing I might use, and I have wondered since whether that was the god's doing, which is the kind of thought you did not have in my old life and cannot help having in that valley.

He came up the road in the last good weather before the wet closed in, an old man with a bad leg and a good cloak, leaning on a stick, with a servant leading the mule, and he took the second room for two nights because a man does not climb to Delphi and ask his question the same day; a man rests, and washes at the spring, and composes himself, because you do not come before the god travel-stained and blurting. I served him his pot and his wine, and he was courteous to me in the offhand way of a man who has always had servants, and he wanted to talk, the way the old and frightened do, and I was a wall with a jug and safe to talk at.

"You'll not know what it is to want a thing answered," he said, more to the fire than to me. "Young. But there's questions that sit on a man's chest at night like a stone, and no priest in his own town can lift them, and no friend, because they've their own stones. So you come to the god. You come all this way to the one place the god still troubles to open his mouth." He drank. "They say it's less than it was. My grandfather came, and he said the whole plain was full, the road solid with folk and offerings, before the Roman took the bronzes." He said *the Roman* the way you say a weather that ruined a harvest. "But the god still speaks. Diminished is not silent. The Pythia still goes down."

"What will you ask him," I said, because he wanted to be asked, and because I had learned that a question offered is a coin that buys you an hour of listening and gives nothing of yourself away.

"That's between me and the god." He said it sharply, then softened, because he wanted, in truth, to say it. "A matter of an inheritance, and a grandson, and whether the boy is mine to trust or his mother's to fear. Ordinary. The god hears a hundred like it." He looked into the wine. "You ask plain, they say. You go up, you make your offering, you put your question to the priest, and the woman goes down into the place under the temple where the breath comes up, and she says what the god gives her to say, and the priest hands it back to you shaped into verse, and you carry it home and spend the rest of your life finding out what it meant." He laughed, without much in it. "That's the trick of the god. He never lies and he never quite tells you. He gives you the true thing folded so small you only open it after."

I stood there with the jug going cold in my hand, and something in me had gone very still.

Because I had a question. I had the largest question a human creature had ever carried up that road, and here was an old man telling me, over a pot, that there was a place, a walk of a morning from where I stood, where the god still troubled to answer, where a woman went down into a cleft in the rock and came up with the true thing folded small. I did not believe in the god. The modern woman in me did not, for one instant, believe that a priestess breathing a vapour in a cave could know anything of doors through time or the way back to a life nineteen centuries off. But I had also not believed, on a headland at the edge of the world, that a compass could turn in circles pointing at a north below the world, and I had knelt and watched it do exactly that, and I had learned, at great cost, to distrust the confident *cannot* as much as the confident *is.*

"Does he answer anything," I said, and I heard my own voice come out lower than I meant it. "Or only the ordinary stones. Inheritances. Grandsons."

The old man looked at me then, really looked, for the first time, some sharpness coming into his rheumy eye, as though the wall with the jug had said an unexpected thing. "They say," he said slowly, "that he answered a man once who asked how to get home. Odysseus, that was. Though that was a different god and a longer road." He drank. "Why. What would a girl at an inn ask the god of the whole world?"

"Nothing," I said, and made my face the hill-country face, dull and closed. "I was only wondering what it costs."

"Everything a man has, and never enough," he said, and thought it a joke about offerings, and laughed, and I refilled his cup and got away from the fire before my face could betray the thing that had woken in it, which was not faith, and was not quite hope, but was the first cousin of both: the knowledge that I had, at last, somewhere in this shut world, a door with a voice, and a morning's walk to reach it, and the whole rest of a winter to find out what a woman who did not exist might dare to ask a god.

I did not sleep well that night either. But it was a different not-sleeping. The planning kind.

---

I thought, in my ignorance, that the language and the loom and the scarf were the whole of the wall between me and Pompeii. I thought that once I could pass, I could go. I was wrong, and Kallia showed me how wrong, and she did it without meaning to, over a pot, on an ordinary evening. It was the worst hour of my early exile, because it was the hour the true size of the thing came clear.

I had asked her, carefully, in my improving Greek, how a person went from here to Italy. To the bay in the south, I said, under its mountain; I had a thing I must do there. I meant it lightly, a traveller's question. She set down her spoon and looked at me as though I had asked the way to the moon.

"A person," she said. "What person."

"A woman," I said. "Alone."

She laughed, once, hard, and then she saw my face, and stopped, because she saw that I did not know. That I had genuinely not known. Something in her went gentle and terrible at once, the gentleness of a person about to tell you a truth for your own good.

"Sit," she said. "You are a fool, and it is not your fault, and I would rather you learn it from me over a pot than from a road."

And she told me. She told me a woman did not travel. A woman was not a person who went places; she was a person who belonged to a place, and to the man who held it, and her right to be on any road at all came from that man, walking ahead of her or waiting at the end of it.

"A woman on the road with no man," she said, "is one of three things to every eye that finds her. A runaway. A whore. Or a thing no one owns yet." She held up a scarred finger for each. "All three end the same way, and the way is not kind."

"Then how far is it," I said. "To Italy. If I could solve the man."

"It is not a distance." She said it flatly. "It is a chain. Ship, road, ship, road, and at the joint of every link a man who asks you who you are and who holds you, and a woman who cannot answer him fast and plain is a woman who does not leave that link." She counted the rest off on the hard fingers. "The fare, which you have not got. The season, which is closing; no ship crosses till the spring. The roads, which eat even those with the right to walk them. And under all of it, the one thing." She looked at me. "You need a man's name to wear. Not for love. Not for his arm, though his arm too. For your *self*. A woman is real in the light a man throws on her, the way the moon is only the sun's light come back. A woman with no man is not free, girl. She is dark. And the dark gets walked into."

I sat with that. I had known it as history. I had lectured on it, in a warm room, to young women in trousers who could board an aeroplane alone with a document that said they were persons in their own right, and not one of us had felt the actual iron of it. I felt it now. The whole machinery of my exile came clear in that single hour over a pot. I was not a traveller temporarily stranded. I was a woman who did not exist, in a world that had no slot for what I was, and the road home ran not through a distance but through the one thing I had spent my modern life priding myself on not needing, which was a man to make me real.

"So," said Kallia, watching me take it. "Now you know the size of your mountain."

"Now I know the size of my mountain," I said.

"Good." She picked the spoon back up. "A woman who knows the size of her mountain has already walked the worst mile of it."

She got the proverb wrong, of course. The real one is grimmer and offers no such comfort. But she made it mean what I needed it to mean, and I took the comfort and asked no questions of it.

---

There was a night, past the middle of that first autumn, when the wine was in her a little more than usual and the inn was empty and the rain was on the roof, that she told me about the lamp.

I had never asked. I had a shut drawer of my own, a whole life away, and I knew a shut room when I lived beside one. And she had a shut room; the third and best room of the inn, dry, with a real window and a bed frame, worth double in a season, and it stood empty and swept and shut, and I had watched her sweep it though no one used it, air its bedding on the fine days and take it in before the damp. I had watched her set the faithful useless lamp, every night, in the window that faced down the valley, down the long road that ran west toward the sea and the ports and the whole wide swallowing empire a young man goes into.

"He went for a soldier," she said that night, not looking at me, mending in her lap. "Six years past. There was no work, and a recruiter with a full purse, and a boy's head full of eagles." She bit the thread. "The letters came, the first years. A scribe's hand; he never had his letters, I couldn't give him that. Then a long time. Then one more, two years gone, from a place I cannot say the name of, at the edge of the world. And then no more."

"He stopped writing," I said, carefully, the way you set your foot on ice.

"A poor soldier pays a scribe when he can, which is never." She smoothed the cloth on her knee. "It is nothing. Boys are careless. He will come up that road one evening with a beard I won't know, and eat me out of house, and be astonished I ever worried." She said it lightly, which was the most terrible way she could have said it.

"Yes," I said. Because there was nothing else to say, and because it was the kindness she was asking for. The agreement. Not the truth.

"A lit lamp," she said, "wastes no oil it wasn't going to burn." It was not any proverb I could find the true shape of, and it meant nothing, and it meant everything. She got up and filled the lamp though it was already full, and set it back in the window against the rain.

I thought of a drawer that stuck, and a small plastic sound below language, and a date written at the head of a page. "I had a father," I said, to the fire, not to her, because it was easier said to the fire. "I keep— there are things of his I cannot make myself put away. A drawer I cannot open and cannot empty."

Kallia was quiet a moment. "Then you know," she said. "It is not the oil. It is the filling of the lamp. The hands have to do a thing, or the heart does a worse one." She looked at me, once, straight, the only time that night. "We are the same animal, you and I. You have simply come a longer way to your grief than I have to mine."

We did not speak of it again for a long time. But I loved her from that night, in the plain enormous way you love the person carrying the same weight up the same hill, and not one word passed between us that either of us would have called by that name.

---

Then the winter came down off the mountain, and it came hard, and it taught me the last thing I needed to know about my life in that house, which was how much I had to lose.

Kallia took a fever in the black of the year, the wet-lung kind that carries off the old, and for four days and four nights I nursed her and ran the inn alone, and I did not have time, in the doing of it, to notice that I was doing the impossible thing, that a woman who three months before could not ask for water was now buying at the market and pouring for strangers and keeping the fire and the accounts and turning a suspicious muleteer's questions with a bored word, in a language and a body and a life not her own, and passing, wholly, unremarked, because no one was left to remark her and there was no time to be marked. Terror is a great teacher but drudgery is a greater one, and nothing had assimilated me like four days of having to be Kallia because Kallia could not.

She was a terrible patient. She fought the broth I made her and misquoted proverbs at me about the foolishness of the well tending the sick, and on the third night, when the fever was highest and I sat up with her wringing cloths, she caught my wrist in her hot hand and looked at me with eyes gone bright and far and said, "You'll not put the lamp out. Whatever comes. You'll fill it and set it in the window, every night, you hear me, girl. Every night."

"I hear you," I said. "I'll fill it. Lie still."

"He'll come up the road," she said, "and it'll be dark, and he'll not know the house without the light."

"I'll keep the light," I said. "I promise you. Now drink."

She drank, that time, because I had promised, and I filled the lamp before I slept that night and every night of her fever, set it in the window facing down the black valley toward the sea, and I understood as I did it that I had taken up her faithful useless devotion as my own, that it had passed to me across the fever like the illness itself, and that this was what it was to belong to a person: you took up their impossible vigil and kept it without believing in it, because they could not, and because the keeping was the love.

She lived. On the fifth morning the fever broke and she woke thin and furious and herself, and looked at the inn I had kept running around her, and looked at me, and did not thank me, because you do not thank family for family, and that not-thanking was the whole of my adoption. She only said, looking at the filled lamp in the grey morning light, "You kept it lit."

"I kept it lit," I said.

"Hm." She lay back. "The wrong niece would have let it go and told me the oil was dear." A long pause. "You are not the wrong niece." And she slept, healing, and I sat in the pale winter light in a house I had, against every likelihood in the turning of the world, come to belong to, and I let myself feel, for one moment, the thing I had been too busy and too frightened to feel, which was that I had found, in the middle of my catastrophe, a person I could not now bear to lose, and that this was a new kind of danger, the oldest kind, the kind the whole rest of my story would be made of.

---

I did not sleep, the night I learned the size of my mountain. I lay in the byre with the goat and the rain and the enormity, and I did what I do. Somewhere past the middle of the night the fear burned down to its embers, and the cold working part of me woke, and began, quietly, to plan.

If I needed a man's name to cross the world, I would have to acquire one, by whatever means the world allowed, and the world allowed a few. A woman could have a husband, or the memory of one. That was the crack in the wall: a widow. She was a woman a man had made real and then died and left real, the least unfree of unfree women, and she travelled, sometimes, on her dead husband's business, if she had coin and a hard face and a story that held. I had the hard face. I could build the story; I had spent my life reconstructing the plausible past of vanished people out of fragments, and now I would reconstruct my own. A dead husband. A reason to travel. A widow's grief to wear over the real grief like a mantle over a tunic.

And I would need coin, which meant work, real work, more than a widow's keep at an inn. And I would need, first, before any of it, the one road out of these mountains that a woman might buy her way onto, and the man who ran it. I had already heard the shape of him in the inn's talk, without knowing I was gathering it. A Roman, a freedman, scarred, who ran mules and horses down to the coast and back, and was said to be honest, or at least too dangerous to cheat, and who trusted no one and helped no one, and had once, on the road below the sanctuary, called off two men who meant me harm without troubling to look at my face.

I lay in the dark and turned him over, the big scarred wall of a man, and I thought of him as nothing but a door. That is all he was to me then: the first door in the long chain of doors between a byre in Delphi and a threshold under a mountain in Italy. I have laughed since at how completely I misjudged him. At the woman lying in the straw costing out a cold transaction with a man she had decided was a means, a thing to be used and left behind, the first in a chain of things between her and home. I would not go back and tell her, even if the length of time would let me reach into the goat-dark and try. She had a mountain to climb and needed the cold arithmetic more than she needed the truth, and I owe her the dignity of her ignorance.

So I will let her lie there in the straw doing her sums. I will let the rain come down, and the one faithful lamp burn in the window for a boy at the edge of the world, and the morning, which was coming, which always came, bring the first hard step of the rest of it.

In the morning I asked Kallia where a woman might find honest work that paid in coin and not in keep. She looked at me a long moment, and something moved behind her eyes. Approval, maybe. Or recognition. Or only the plain acknowledgement of a fellow creature who had decided to climb rather than lie down.

"So," she said. "The lost one has found her feet." She got the proverb wrong again, gently, mercifully wrong, and turned it toward me like the lamp toward the road. "Feet that have found the floor," she said, "do not thank the dark they were dropped in."

And she told me where to begin.
