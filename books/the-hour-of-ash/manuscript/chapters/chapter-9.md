There is a cold that comes an hour before light that is not the night's leftover but the day's arriving, the mountain clearing its throat, and I met it on the road above Kallia's with a borrowed cloak, a stranger's four beasts, and the particular courage a person can only manage before she is properly awake. He was there before me. Of course he was there before me. He had the mules strung and the loads squared and a lamp hooded down to a coal, and he looked at me the way you look at weather you have decided to travel in anyway.

"You brought too much," he said, to the near mule's girth.

"I brought a water-skin and a spare shift and my tablets."

"The tablets are for the widow. Everything else you'll carry on your own back or you'll leave in the ditch by the second bend, because the beasts carry the trade, not the passengers." He gave the girth a last pull, felt it with the flat of two fingers, and let it be. "There's the ditch. Choose."

I kept the tablets and the water and left the shift folded on a stone at the roadside like an offering to whoever came after, and I saw the scarred corner of his mouth do the thing it did when he approved of a thing and would rather have died than say so.

"Good," he said. "Walk on the off side. If a beast's going to put its foot wrong it'll do it downhill, and I'd sooner it stepped on the gorge than on you. Not for your sake. For the delay."

"For the delay," I agreed, and we went up into the dark.

Let me give the road its due, because the road is the whole of it. It went up off the valley floor by the old sacred way, the pilgrims' road, cut into the flank of the mountain in a stair of switchbacks a man laid out before Rome was anything, and it climbed the side of a gorge that fell away on the left into a black nothing you could hear water in but not see. Above us, when the light finally came grey and then rose, the two great cliffs stood up out of the mountain like the sawn ends of the world, pink and then gold at the top while we were still in shadow at the bottom, and there were tombs cut into the rock beside the road, and niches with small clay gods gone soft-faced with weather, and here and there a scatter of last year's votives, a cracked cup, a rag tied to a thorn, the litter of other people's hope. I catalogued it as we climbed because cataloguing is what I do with wonder the same as with fear; I could not have told you, that morning, which of the two I was having.

"Pilgrims leave the rags," Valens said, when he caught me looking. He had seen me look. He saw everything I did and remarked on almost none of it, and the ration of what he chose to remark on was how I knew what he was thinking. "For the fever. You tie a rag with the sickness in it to the god's thorn and walk on clean. Whole hedge of them below the spring."

"Does it work?"

"The walking works. Man too sick to climb a mountain stays home and dies in his bed; man well enough to climb it was going to mend anyway." He chirruped the lead mule on around a bend. "God takes the credit either way. It's a good trade. I'd have gone into it if I'd had the piety."

"You've no piety at all."

"I've exactly as much as has ever been any use to me," he said. "Which up this road is some. Keep it to yourself that I said so."

That was as close to easy as the two of us had been, and I knew even then to hold still inside it and not name it, the way you hold still when a wild thing comes to feed near your hand. We climbed. The beasts blew and leaned into the grade. The gorge fell away and filled with light from the top down, so that we walked up out of the night by degrees while the day had already happened to the peaks, and somewhere far below and behind a cock crowed in the valley we had left, thin and absurd and dear, and I thought, with the total irrelevance my mind keeps for the worst and best moments both, that the bread would be proving on Kallia's hearth by now and no one to knock it back, and it would go sour, and she would blame the god.

"You're smiling," Valens said, without turning round. "Nobody smiles on this grade. What is it."

"Bread," I said.

He let a silence go by that had a question in it and decided the question wasn't worth the breath, which was the most companionable thing he could have done, and we went on up.

---

The festival crowd was ahead of us and behind us and we were, for a while, held in the loose company of it, the way you are on a road that everyone is walking for one reason. There were pilgrims proper, in their good cloaks, a Corinthian family with a garlanded kid for the sacrifice that would not stop trying to eat the garland; there were traders like Valens with strings of beasts, and a litter with the curtains drawn that everyone had a theory about, and a knot of young men from somewhere down the coast who were making the climb a competition and would regret it by noon. Valens knew a third of them to nod to and none of them to talk to, and he set our pace to open a gap, not to close one.

"You don't like the crowd," I said.

"I don't like being in a line of men where I can't see the front of the line or the back of it." He said it evenly, a fact about roads, and I heard the other thing under it, the old vigilance that never fully slept in him, and I did not push at it. "Crowd's fine on the flat. On a road with one edge that's a cliff and one edge that's a rock, a crowd is just a great many people who can't get out of each other's way when something happens. We'll go steady and let the racers burn themselves out and pass the family at the spring. By the high stretch it thins. Everybody stops at the spring."

"Why does everybody stop at the spring?"

"Because you don't come before the god filthy," he said, "and because the water's the best on the mountain and it's free, and because after the spring the road gets mean and men would rather stand about drinking than climb the mean part, so they invent a piety about washing first." He glanced sidelong at me, that flat weighing look. "You'll want to wash too. Not for the god. For the widow's people, when they see you. A clean woman is a woman with somewhere to be clean. Remember whose you are up there."

"Caedicia," I said. "Widow of Lucius Caedicius, dyed-wool. Three years a widow. Chest complaint."

"Say it less well," he said. "You say it like a lesson. A widow of three years doesn't recite her husband; she mentions him by accident and changes the subject. Practice being sick of him."

I laughed, the cracked involuntary one, and a pilgrim ahead of us turned round at it, and Valens's face closed a half-inch, and I put the laugh away.

We walked a while with only the beasts to fill the quiet, and then, because the grade had a way of loosening a person's tongue when it had already taken all their breath for the climbing and left none for guarding, I asked him the thing I had been not-asking since the yard.

"Why the mountain," I said. "For you. Not the trade. You've a rival who waters his oats; you told me. You could let him have the festival week and keep your head down in the valley where nobody's asking after fighting men. You climb up into the one place on the mountain where all of Corinth's money is standing about looking at faces. That's a strange road for a man who wants to be nobody."

He drew the lead mule up a step over a broken place and set it down before he answered, which was his way, he ran a question the length of a knot before he'd trust it out loud.

"A man who hides is a man who's found," he said. "Every time. You put yourself in a hole and stay off the roads, and the day someone comes up your valley asking, there you are, the one Roman who's never at the market, and now you're the thing everyone knows a small strange story about. That's worse. That gets you talked of." He sent the mule on with a click of his tongue. "The way to be nobody is to be exactly the sort of somebody nobody looks at twice. A muleteer who's up at the festival every year like the tax and the weather. Dull. Reliable. The sort of man you forget you saw while you were looking at him. Present men are invisible; it's the absent ones that get remembered." He glanced at me sidelong. "You'd do well to learn it. You keep trying to disappear by being quiet. Quiet's loud. Be dull instead. Widows are dull. Be a dull widow and no one in Achaea will ever look at you again."

"You looked at me."

"I look at everything. It's a defect." He said it flat, but there was the ghost again at the corner of the scar. "I looked at you and I saw a woman being a widow so hard she was the only interesting thing in the market. That's the opposite of the trick. You don't act the widow. You bore people into forgetting to check."

"You're telling me to be boring."

"I'm telling you the safest thing to be, in a world that means you harm, is not worth the trouble of remembering." He said it lightly, to the road, but it came off the floor of him, off some old cold place I had begun to map the edges of without ever being shown it, and I understood that he was not, after all, giving me a lesson in passing for a widow. He was telling me how he had survived being the most interesting man in any arena in the world by learning, too late and at a cost I could only guess the size of, to want to be no one at all. "I've had a great deal of practice at it," he said, which was the nearest he came, on that whole road, to his own story, and he shut the door on it before the sentence had finished cooling. "Anyway. That's why the mountain. Because a man who skips the mountain is a man with a reason to. Walk on. You're slowing the string."

I was not slowing the string. I walked on.

We came up to the Castalian spring at the middle of the morning, where the water comes out of a cleft between the two cliffs cold enough to hurt your teeth, into a basin cut in the rock with steps worn into a curve by pilgrims' feet, and there was a press of people there doing exactly what he had said they would, washing and drinking and standing about being pious at each other, and above us the road went on up and around a shoulder of the mountain into the mean part, and it was, I saw, nearly empty. The racers had gone up. The family was still arguing with the kid. The litter had stopped.

Valens watered the beasts a little downstream of the basin, where their muzzles wouldn't foul the pilgrims' water and start a fight, and he did it fast, and he kept looking up at the shoulder of the road, and his free hand had found the end of the lead-rope and was working it through his fingers, over and over, the slow patient tell I had learned to read like weather.

"What," I said. Quietly. His register was catching.

"Nothing," he said. "The road's empty above the spring. It's always empty above the spring; I told you it would be." He coiled the rope. He uncoiled it. "It's empty the right amount. Wash your hands and your face and drink what you can hold. We're going up ahead of the family. I want daylight and no company on the mean stretch, and we won't get both if we wait."

I did not understand, then, why a man would choose the empty road over the crowded one when the empty road was the thing worrying him, and I asked him, and he gave me his answer.

"Because if it's coming," he said, "I'd rather it came where I can see all of it than where I can't see any of it. Crowds are for hiding in. I've nothing to hide from a mountain." He slung his own water-skin, freshly filled, across the lead mule's load and knotted it down, and I noticed the knot was one more than the load needed. "Drink. Don't dawdle. And Caedicia." He used the name flatly, as a drill, the first time he had ever used it. "If I tell you to do a thing on this next stretch, you do it in the doing of it, not after you've asked me why. Can you manage that."

"I can manage that."

"We'll see," he said, which was the thing he always said, and we went up past the spring onto the mean part of the road, the two of us and the four beasts, into the empty that was empty the right amount, and left the noise of the water and the people behind us at our backs where I could no longer tell one voice in it from another.

---

The mean stretch was a place where the road narrowed and the rock came out over it and the gorge on the left went sheer, so that you walked in a kind of stone corridor with a lid half on it, and the light came down in one long slot and left the sides in shadow, and I remember thinking it was beautiful in the way of a thing built to funnel you, and then I stopped thinking in sentences.

They came out of the rock on the right.

Three men. Two out of a fold in the cliff face where a goat-track met the road, one from behind a fallen block ahead, so we were between. The one ahead had a staff shod in iron. The two from the fold had knives, short, the working kind, not soldier's iron. None of them ragged enough. That was the thing I saw and set down without knowing why I was setting it down: not ragged enough. A man starving on a mountain does not have that cloak.

Valens put me behind him with his forearm, one motion, no words, and I went where the arm put me, the way I had said I would.

"The beasts and the goods," the staff-man said. Greek, but not his own. "Nobody hurt. Stand off the animals."

Valens did not answer him. He had gone to the stillness, the loud one, and his hands had let the lead-rope fall, which frightened me more than the knives did, because his hands never let a thing fall, and I understood that his hands had let it fall because they were about to be needed for something else.

Then it was fast and I have to give it to you slow, in the pieces, because pieces are all I kept.

The two from the fold came first. Knife-men come to your inside. Valens took the near one on the turn, took the knife-arm at the wrist with one hand and did something to the elbow with the other that made a sound I felt in my own arm, and the knife went down onto the road and the man went down after it folded the wrong way, and Valens had the knife before it stopped moving. He did not pick it up cleanly. That is a thing I kept. It skittered and he had to snatch and it cut his own palm as he closed on it and he closed on it anyway. Blood on the grey road. Not much. Enough to note.

Second man. The second man was already inside and Valens turned into him instead of away, which is the thing you cannot teach, and there was a short business of the two of them very close together that I could not read, arms and the one grunt, and then the second man stepped back off Valens with the wrong expression and sat down against the cliff with both hands at his middle and did not get up. The knife stayed in Valens's fist. I did not see it go in. I have been glad, on and off, for years, that I did not see it go in.

Staff-man. I had forgotten the staff-man. The mules had. The lead mule threw its head and swung its quarters and the whole string bunched and shied against the rock, four beasts and a wall and no room, and the staff-man used the shy, came round the back of the bunched animals along the cliff on Valens's off side while Valens was still turning off the second man, and I saw the iron cap of the staff go up, and I saw that Valens was going to be a half-beat late because the mule was between him and it, and the half-beat was going to be his head.

I was holding the picket-pin. I do not remember taking it off the load. It is an iron pin the length of my forearm that you drive to tether a beast, and it was in my hand, and I was on the off side because he had put me there for the delay, and the staff-man's back was to me because I did not count, I was the passenger, I was a woman standing off the animals as told.

I hit him where the neck goes into the shoulder. Not the head. I did not aim for the head; I have thought about that and I did not choose it, my hand chose the big soft place a body has there the way my hand chooses the sound part of a rotten hinge, by feel, without me. The pin went in with the whole weight of my arm and my fear behind it and he made a sound and the staff came down but not on Valens, on the mule's load, off the beast's rump, and he went sideways into the cliff and I hit him again because he was still upright and being upright he was still the thing that was going to kill the man in front of me, and the second time he went down.

Then it was quiet.

I want to be exact about the quiet, because it is the part no one tells you. It was a loud quiet. The mules were screaming, the flat awful bray of a frightened mule, and one of them was kicking the cliff over and over, iron shoe on stone, and the second man was making a wet sound against the rock, and somewhere very far away at the spring the pilgrims were still being pious and had heard nothing or had heard it and gone still. But at the centre of it, where Valens and I stood, it was quiet, because neither of us was making any sound at all, and the man at my feet had stopped.

Three men. Two down and not getting up. One folded against the far rock with a broken arm, gone grey, no fight left in him, watching us the way a beaten dog watches. Valens's palm cut, shallow, bleeding into the wet fist that still held the knife. A picket-pin in my hand with dark on the iron of it that I noted was already going tacky in the cold and did not let myself understand. My own breath, very loud, in and out, the loudest thing on the road.

I catalogued him first, out of habit, the way I catalogue everything I am afraid of. Condition: upright. Damage: left palm, minor, self-inflicted on the recovery. Blood not his own on the right forearm and the hem. Respiration: fast, controlled. Standing. Whole. Alive. I ran the inventory and it came back alive and I did not feel the thing I expected to feel at alive. I did not feel anything. That is the part I have never found the words for, not to anyone who was not on a road when it happened to them. At the moment it mattered most I was a list, and the list had no feelings in it, only conditions, and I stood there being a list and it was the calmest I have ever been in my life.

"Are you cut," I said. My voice came out flat and level and not mine, or more mine than my voice usually is, the true dead-flat under the surface. "Your hand. Show me your hand."

He did not show me his hand. He was looking at the man at my feet, the one I had put down, and then he was looking at me, and something was wrong with his face, and I did not yet have the sense to know what.

---

I got the mules quiet first. That is what you do; you make the loud thing stop being loud so you can hear the mountain, so you can hear whether there is a fourth man, so you can hear yourself. I went to the lead mule's head and put my hand on it flat and hard the way I had watched Valens do a hundred times in his yard and I said the nonsense you say, low, over and over, and the beast rolled its eye at me white and blowing and then it stopped kicking the cliff, and then the others came down off it one at a time the way beasts do, borrowing calm off the front of the string. My hands were entirely steady on the mule. I noted that they were steady the way you note the time.

The broken-armed man against the rock decided, correctly, that his day was over, and he got himself up one-handed and went off down the goat-track into the fold in the cliff without a word or a backward look, and I let him go, because there was nothing in my hand's list that said to stop him and I had no other authority working just then.

Then I went to Valens, because the mules were quiet and I could, and I said again, "Show me your hand," and he held it out, and I saw the trouble.

His hand was shaking.

Not the cut. The cut was nothing, a shallow slice across the pads below the fingers, already clotting, the kind of thing you wrap and forget. It was the hand itself. It had begun to shake, a fine hard tremor from the wrist, the knife still in it because his fingers had not managed the thought of putting the knife down, and it was shaking so that the blade of it caught the slot of light and threw it around the rock walls in a small jumping shard, and he was looking at his own hand doing this with an expression I had never seen him wear, which was the expression of a man watching a stranger use his body.

"Put the knife down," I said.

"Yes," he said, and did not.

There is an order in which a body lets go of a killing. Mine had gone dead-flat and stayed flat through the whole of it and the mules and the walk to him; the falling came later, that night, in the dark, alone, quietly, and was mine to manage. His had held perfectly through the doing, the two men, the turn, the recovery, held the way a drawn rope holds, taut and true and without a tremor, right up until the second it was over and there was nothing left to do with it, and then it had let go all at once, and it was letting go now, in the hand, because the hand had nowhere left to put what it had spent. He had killed two men with those hands inside a count of breaths and the hands had not shaken once while there was a use for them. It was the peace that undid him. It was the being safe.

His hands, that always found a strap. That found a knot the moment feeling came up in him, that had worked the lead-rope raw at the spring, that had tied one knot more than the load needed because a task was the only place he had ever learned to put fear. His hands went looking for the task now, I watched them go looking, the free one groping blind at the mule's load for a strap to true, for a buckle, for anything, and finding the pack-strap that the staff-man had knocked loose off the beast's rump when he went sideways, the strap hanging, wanting doing.

And they could not do it.

I watched him try. I watched the great careful hands that had turned a horse's lip up to read a fraud, that had worked a knot out of a young horse's mane with the animal leaning its whole weight into him, I watched them take the loose strap and fumble it and lose it and take it again and not be able to make the fingers close the buckle, because they were shaking too hard, and he stood there in the middle of the emptied road over the two men he had killed and could not fasten a strap, and I understood that I was watching the thing come apart that held the whole of him together, the belief that his hands, whatever else failed, would always have the task and always be equal to it.

I took the strap out of his hands.

I did not say anything. There was nothing that was not smaller than the moment, so I said none of it. I took the loose end of the pack-strap out from between his shaking fingers, and I threaded it, and I drew it up snug against the beast's flank the way I had learned it off him in the yard, off the corners of a squared muck-heap and the set of a girth, and I buckled it, and I gave the tongue the extra half-pull he always gave it, the one that said the man who tied this did not trust the world to stay tied, and I set my hand flat on the finished knot of it a moment the way you press a mend to be sure of it. My hands were steady. The conservator's hands that had failed me at my father's drawer, that had failed me on a shop counter three time zones from anyone who loved me; those hands were, on the one afternoon a man I was coming to love could not use his own, perfectly, miraculously steady, and they tied the strap, and the strap held.

He watched me do it. He watched me do the thing his hands could not, on his own beast, to his own standard, and for the length of it he stopped watching his hands and watched mine, and something in his face that had been coming apart stopped coming apart and simply grieved instead, quietly, without moving, the way I imagine a wall grieves.

"There," I said, when it was done. Flat. Level. Not comfort; he could not have borne comfort and I knew it now, I had learned it off him. A fact about the strap. "It's tied. It'll hold to the top."

"Yes," he said. His voice had the tremor his hand had. He heard it have the tremor and he stopped talking rather than let me hear more of it, and he turned half away toward the gorge and stood with the knife finally lowered at his side, and I stood at the mule's head with my hand on the strap I had tied, and neither of us looked at the two men on the road, and the mountain let us not look at them for a while, which was the only mercy going.

I broke first, but not into pieces. I broke into work, which is the family way, his and mine both, our whole marriage in one small figure though it was months from being a marriage: two people finding a task to stand inside while the feeling had its way with them out of eyeshot. I got the water-skin down off the lead mule and uncapped it and went to him and held it up, and he took it in the hand that wasn't cut and the hand still shook and the water went down his chin as much as his throat, and he wiped his mouth with the back of the shaking wrist and looked at the wet of it, blood and water, and said the first whole thing he had said since the road went empty.

"You were behind me."

"I was on the off side. You put me there."

"You were behind me and you had the pin." He said it slowly, working it out, a man re-tying a knot in his head to see how it had held. "He had the whole of my off side. The mule was between us. I was going to be late." He turned and looked at me, and the look had the thing in it his voice couldn't manage, and his hand came half up toward my face and thought better of it and went back down, because his hand was still not his to command and he would not touch me with a hand he didn't trust. "You were late by nothing. You put an iron pin through a man's shoulder because I taught you to muck a yard and read a girth and I never once taught you that, and you did it in the doing of it, not after, like I told you, and you didn't ask me why."

"You told me not to ask why."

"People say a thing," he said. "They don't do it." It was the same shape he had used at the very first, in the yard, about coming back on the third day, and I heard the callback in it and I do not think he did, and I said nothing of it to him, because some things you keep for yourself and take out later in the dark and turn over to find the maker's mark.

---

We did not stay long on the road with the dead. You cannot; the mountain is a road and roads have traffic, and the family with the argumentative kid would come round the shoulder inside the hour, and a widow of dyed-wool and a muleteer standing over two corpses would be a thing that traveled to Corinth faster than the wool would. Valens knew all this before I did. His hands had steadied enough, by then, to work, though they steadied last of all the parts of him and I saw him hate them for the delay.

He went through the two men. I did not want to watch and I made myself, because I had already decided I would not be a woman who let a man do the ugly parts out of my sight and then took the clean road he'd bought with them; I had done a version of that my whole life with the dead, handling them beautifully after other people did the dying, and I was done with it on that road if nowhere else. He was fast and unsentimental and he took nothing but a purse off the staff-man and a folded thing out of the staff-man's breast that he looked at longer than a purse warrants.

"What is it," I said.

He did not answer for a moment. He was looking at the folded thing, which was a scrap of leather with marks scratched into it, and then he was looking down the empty road the way he had looked up the empty valley the day the ordinary man on the hired mule rode off in the wrong direction, and I watched the same weather cross him, the bleak level weather, and the free hand find the end of the lead-rope again now that it could, and begin, quietly, to work it.

"Robbers take the beasts," he said. "That's the trade. You take the beasts and the goods and you let the people walk, because dead people are talked about and a lame mule is just a lame mule. I've been stopped on a road three times in my life and every time it was the beasts and the goods and stand off, no one hurt." He turned the leather scrap over. "These stood between us on a narrow stretch and they came for the inside, for the man, first, before the goods. The staff-man kept back for my head, not for the string. That's not robbers. That's men who were paid by the head and told which head." He folded the scrap back the way it had been and put it away inside his own breast, not into the purse, kept it separate, and I understood he meant to keep it and think about it and not to show it to me, and I let him, for the same reason I had let the ordinary man ride away nine days before without telling Valens what I was thinking. You do not put a thing like that into a man's grip in the first hour of his fear.

But I was already handing myself the thing, quietly, in the flat dead voice the road had put on me and had not quite taken off. A household in the south. An old debt. He'll not be the last, he had said, over the paddock rail, in another life, four evenings ago. Drusus somewhere under it, loud and soft, pricing his own ruin up and down a valley and naming a creditor to the open air. The ordinary man in the good cloak on the hired mule, riding off in the wrong direction. Then these three, on a narrow road, come for the man before the goods. I did not have it in an order yet. I want to be honest that I did not solve it on that road, I only felt the edges of it the way you feel the edge of a buried thing through a trowel before you have any idea what it is. I filed it, with the dark going tacky on the iron pin I had not yet been able to make myself set down, and I carried it up the mountain with everything else I carry.

"We leave them," Valens said. "The mountain has ways. Don't look back at them; a woman looking back at a thing on a road is a thing people remember. Look at the god's road ahead of you and walk it like a pilgrim who saw nothing, because that's the last true thing you'll say to anyone up there, that you saw nothing, and you'd best start believing it now."

"I saw nothing," I said.

"You saw nothing," he said, and for once since the rock his mouth did the almost-smile, terrible and grateful and gone at once. "God, but you're a bad liar and a worse widow, and you put a pin through a man like you were setting a tent. Wash your hands at the next runnel. There's a runnel above the shoulder. You've dark on your hands and it shows worse on a woman."

I looked at my hands then, because he told me to, and there was dark on them, and it did not shake them, and here was the fear that outweighed the men, that outweighed even his coming apart: that I could look at my own hands with a dead man on them and feel the flat calm hold, and know that whatever I had brought here to this place carrying, I had also brought this, the steadiness that does not care, the conservator who can handle anything as long as it has already stopped moving. I had come to the ancient world to finish being my father's daughter. I was learning up that mountain that I was something else besides, something the drawer and the shop counter had never asked to see, and it had just kept a man alive, and I could not tell whether to thank it or fear it, and I settled, the way I settle everything, for filing the question and washing my hands.

---

We walked the mean stretch out in silence at first, the beasts strung and steady again and the road climbing, and I let the silence be, because his silences were rooms he furnished and you did not walk into one uninvited. He furnished this one a long way before he opened the door of it.

"You'll want to know if it takes you like that every time," he said, out of nothing, to the road. "It doesn't. It takes you worst the first time, when the body hasn't a shelf to put it on. After that you build the shelf. That's the whole of what the arena teaches, that a person can be taught to have a shelf for it. It's not a thing to want to be good at."

"I wasn't going to ask you that."

"You were going to ask me nothing and think it very loud, which is the same as asking, only ruder." He shifted the lead-rope to the hand with the wrapped palm and worked the loose end with the other, easy now, a task and not a tremor, and I saw that the tell had come back to being only the tell, the thing his hands did instead of the thing his mouth wouldn't. "So I'll tell you the once, since we don't speak of it after. The shaking's not the fear. Fear's during, and it's useful; it's the thing that keeps you fast. The shaking's after, and it's not fear at all. It's the body finding out it's still got to go on being your body now that it's done that. It doesn't forgive you. You go on together anyway. That's all it is."

"You were ashamed of it," I said. It was not a question and I did not soften it, having watched him watch his own hand like a stranger's, and I would not pretend I hadn't. "In front of me. That's why you turned toward the gorge."

He was quiet the length of three of the mule's slow steps.

"I've kept everyone I've ever known at the far side of a thing they could pay me for," he said, at last. "It's cleaner. A man you're of use to doesn't get to see your hands come apart. That's the good of being useful and never anything else; nobody's owed the look." A step. "You saw the look. You've seen it twice now. The horse's teeth and this. I don't know what to do with a person who's seen the look, Caedicia. I've no shelf for it. I built shelves for the killing and left none for the other thing." He said it as flatly as he'd said everything, no heat in it at all, and the flatness of it was the measure of what it cost him, more than anything else he'd said, and then, because a man like that cannot leave a wound uncovered, he reached for the dry thing to lay over it. "So do me the kindness of being dull about it. Don't make it a speech. You're a terror for speeches. I can hear one building in you now."

"I wasn't going to make a speech."

"You were going to make a speech and think it very loud." But the ghost was all the way to a real thing that time, a short exhausted crooked thing that was almost the whole of a smile, gone before it could be held against him, and he faced forward and chivvied the string on up the grade, and I walked in his dust and did not make the speech, and not making it was the speech, and he knew it, and let it be said that way, which was the only way either of us ever managed to say the large things: sideways, and out of eyeshot, and disguised as their opposite, and true.

We came up out of the mean stretch onto the high shoulder in the early afternoon, and there was the runnel he'd promised, a thread of the same cold spring-water crossing the road, and I knelt and washed my hands in it until they hurt with the cold and were clean, and Valens washed his cut and let me tie a strip of the abandoned widow's-shift I had, after all, not left entirely at the roadside around his palm, and his hand was steady enough by then to have done it himself and he let me do it anyway, which was its own sentence, spoken in the only language he had.

I was hungry. I had not been hungry through any of it and now I was, standing over the runnel with the cold in my knuckles, and there was nothing to eat but the hard bread in his load and I did not ask for it, and I minded the hunger more than seemed decent for the day I'd had.

And then the road bent a last time around the shoulder of the mountain, and the sanctuary was below and above us both at once, spilled down the mountainside in terraces of stone the way I had walked into it the first day not knowing what year I was in, the treasuries and the long climbing sacred way and the columns of the temple standing up pale against the dark of the rising rock, and the smoke of the festival fires going up straight in the still high air, and the sound of it came up to us, a whole living city of the god at its business, and after the quiet of the road the noise of the living was almost more than I could bear.

Valens stopped the beasts at the bend and let me look, because he had seen my face and, for once, chose to remark on it by saying nothing and giving me the moment.

"There's your festival," he said, at last. "Widow. The factor's people will be down among the porticoes by the spring of Cassotis. You wash, you present yourself, you keep the books in front of their eyes, and if you hold you're theirs and you go south in the spring and I'm shot of you." He gathered the lead-rope. His hand did not shake. "That's the road I brought you up for. That's all of it. Go and be seen."

"Yes," I said.

But I was not looking at the porticoes where the factor's people would be, and I did not tell him where I was looking, because it was my errand and not his and some roads a person walks up alone even in company. Up above the treasuries, up past the temple where the smoke went up, up where the sacred way ran out at the top of the whole climbing terraced world, there was the place the pilgrim in Kallia's inn had told me of on a winter night, the place with a door and a voice, where a woman sat over a cleft in the rock and answered what you asked her. I had climbed a mountain and a man had killed two men on it to bring me here to keep another woman's accounts, and none of that was why I had come. I had come to ask the one question I had carried the whole way from another century, the question of a mountain by a Roman sea, and the only mouth in the living world that might answer it was drawing breath, that very afternoon, a quarter-mile up the god's road above my head.

"Go and be seen," he said again, because I had not moved.

I went. But I went up the sacred way and not down to the porticoes, with a dead man washed off my hands and a live one at my back not asking why, toward the smoke and the temple and the loose third step and the tired cold voice of the god, to ask it where my door home was, and to learn, before that day was out, that the god of that place answers the question you ask and keeps the one you need.
