# Chapter 5: Greece, 70 AD

I raised my head, and the first thing I did, before I looked at the world, was refuse to believe it. That refusal is the discipline of my trade. It is the only reason I am alive to set this down.

A conservator does not trust her own eyes. The eyes want a story. They will supply one, gladly, out of hope or dread, and hang it on the object like a coat on a peg, and call it seeing. So you learn to distrust the first reading. You go instead to the thing itself, the dumb mute stubborn thing, and you ask it small questions with hard answers. What are you made of. How were you worked. What has time done to you, and in what order. The object cannot lie, because it does not know it is being asked.

I had spent fifteen years learning to interrogate dead matter until it told the truth against my wishes. I brought the whole of that training to bear now, kneeling in the dust of a road that should not exist, because it was all I had. The alternative to using it was to start screaming. Screaming was a luxury my life had never afforded me, and I was not about to be granted it now.

So. The dumb mute thing. I did not think *ancient Greece.* I thought: gradient. The road under my hands ran downhill to my left and up to my right, cut into a slope. It was not made, not paved, not metalled. It was worn. A track beaten into the hillside by feet and hooves and narrow wheels over a very long time, polished in two ruts a wheel-span apart, loose with pale dust between them. Limestone dust. I rubbed it between my fingers. It was the fine bright chalky dust of a limestone country, and it carried that country's particular smell, hot stone and dry thyme and dung, and under all of it, far off, the green resinous breath of pine going up a mountain.

I kept my eyes down among the small honest facts a while longer. I was not ready for the mountain. You steady your hands on the rim of the bench before you lift a thing that might come apart in the air.

The light did not match the hour I had left, and I knew that and let it stand. The sun was high and hot and well into a morning. Behind me on the headland it had been going down into evening. But the sun tells you nothing you can date. The cicadas told me more. They were going in the pines above me, that dry continuous stridulation that is the actual sound of Mediterranean heat. I had heard it on three dig seasons and in no English autumn, and it placed me in a hot southern summer as surely as a thermometer would. A gull had been the last thing I heard on the rock. A thousand cicadas were the first thing I heard off it. The whole crossing could be told in those two sounds and nothing else, and a person who understood it would understand everything.

I got up. My legs held. I was giddy and my ears rang faintly and there was a metallic taste at the back of my throat that has never been explained to me and never will be. But I stood. The kit was on my back with the one strap fallen, and I settled it, both straps, the old animal comfort of a weight distributed right. Then I turned slowly all the way round to take in the whole of it, and I made myself do it as a survey and not as a swoon.

I was on the side of a mountain. It rose behind me in tiers of grey rock and holm oak and pine to a summit hidden by its own shoulder. It fell away in front of me in olive terraces, a long spill of silver-green trees dropping to a river valley. The low ground was one soft dull-silver forest of olives running west between two ranges, and at the end of it, far off, lay a bright wedge that I knew, before I would let myself know anything, was the sea.

Olives to the sea. A sacred plain of olives filling a valley between mountains, below a great grey peak, above a hidden gulf. There is one such place. I knew the shape of it the way you know a face across a room before you have named the friend, and my knowledge outran my permission. I had to stop, and set it down, and refuse it, and come at it the slow way, through the evidence. If I let myself simply *recognise* Delphi, I would also have to simply believe the rest, and I was going to believe nothing that had not paid for its passage.

The evidence, then. I turned uphill. Three hundred yards along the contour of the slope, above a scatter of low buildings with pantiled roofs, stood a wall. A retaining wall of grey polygonal masonry, the great irregular blocks fitted without mortar, in the way that is older than the classical and unmistakable once you have handled it. Above the wall rose the pale verticals of columns, and among them, along the terraces, the small bright shapes of roofs and pediments climbing the mountainside in the crowded disorder of a place built up over centuries by people adding to a holy thing. A sanctuary. A terraced sanctuary climbing a mountain above an olive plain above a gulf, with the smoke of morning fires going up from the houses of the people who served it. I still would not say the name. A name was a conclusion, and I had not finished the proof.

I finished the proof with the plinths.

I walked up toward the buildings, slowly, on legs that steadied by the minute. Motion is a sedative to a certain kind of terror, and I am that kind. As I came level with the lowest terrace, I passed a series of stone bases beside the road. Statue bases. Rectangular blocks of worked limestone and marble, set where a person entering the precinct would pass them. Each one was cut on its top face with the shallow sockets and the poured-lead dowel channels that had held the bronze feet of a standing figure. Each one bore, in incised Greek letters, weathered but perfectly legible, the name of the man dedicated, the men who had raised him, the god to whom he was given.

And the bases were empty.

Not fallen. Not broken. Not thrown down by earthquake or war or the slow work of the centuries I knew. Empty in the particular obscene way of a robbery. The statues had been lifted clean off, the lead sawn or levered, the sockets left raw, the dowel holes gaping, the inscriptions abandoned to name men who were no longer there. Row upon row of them. A whole vanished population of bronzes, and the plinths still warm-coloured at the wounds where the metal had been taken. The exposure was recent. A season old, two, no more. Someone had stripped this sanctuary of its bronze on an industrial scale, and lately.

And I knew when that was.

This is the thing I have never made anyone feel who was not raised the way I was raised, on a Sunday floor beside a man who taught me the past as other children are taught scripture. So I will simply state it. I knew, kneeling by a robbed plinth on a mountain road, reading the name of a dead athlete over the empty place where his likeness had stood, that Nero stripped Delphi. In the last years of his reign the emperor had come through Greece playing the tourist, the artist, the liberator, and had carried away from this one sanctuary something on the order of five hundred bronze statues. For his golden house in Rome. For his own vast appetite. It was one of the great documented lootings of the ancient world, and it had happened, in the count I had learned as a girl and never thought to stand inside, in the year sixty-seven.

The bronzes were gone and the wounds were fresh. So it was after sixty-seven. And it was after Nero himself, because a man in a plain toga was directing two others at the base of a column further up the terrace, unhurried, proprietary, and freedmen do not resume the ordinary maintenance of a plundered holy place while the plunderer still reigns and might come back for the rest. Nero had fallen. I knew when Nero fell. I knew the year of the four emperors the way I knew my father's birthday. So I was past sixty-seven and past the wreck of sixty-eight, into the settling, into the reign of the hard old soldier who had come out on top of the ruin. Vespasian.

I was standing in the sacred plain of Delphi in the reign of Vespasian. In or about the year of the Christian count seventy. One thousand nine hundred and twenty-eight years, give or take, before the afternoon I had knelt on a headland at the edge of the world and laid my kit down on the lid of it.

I sat down on the empty plinth of a vanished bronze and put my head between my knees. Not from faintness, though I was faint. A person who has just done that arithmetic correctly needs, for a moment, not to be looked at.

---

I want to tell you about the wonder, because I have given the fear a great deal of room in this account, and will give it more, and it would be a lie by omission to let you think there was only fear.

There was, in that first hour on the plinth, folded all through the terror, a joy so enormous and so shameful and so total that it frightened me worse than the arithmetic had. I have spent my life among the fragments of this world. I have knelt over a shard of a Greek cup no bigger than a thumbnail and rebuilt from the curve of it the whole vessel, and the hand that threw it, and the party at which it broke. I have breathed on the corrosion of a bronze and watched a letter swim up out of two thousand years. And I have wanted, with a wanting I never confessed to a living soul because it is the wanting of a madwoman, not to study the past but to stand inside it. To stand in the plain of olives with the paint still bright on the statues, and hear the language spoken, and smell the smoke of the actual fires.

And I was there. The impossible, unpurchasable, unrepeatable thing I had wanted since I was a child on a floor had happened to me. Was happening to me. Was the hot stone under my legs and the cicadas in the pines and the incised name under my hand, a man whose bronze self had stood on this very block within the living memory of the people cooking breakfast forty feet away. I sat on the plinth and wept, at last, the tears I had not spent on my father in the drawer or on the headland, and I was never able to say then, and cannot say now, how much of the weeping was grief and how much the purest joy of my life. I have made a sort of peace with not knowing. The truest things arrive like that, I find, in a currency you cannot break into coins.

The wonder got me up off the plinth before the fear could stop it. I should have gone straight back into the oaks and hidden. Instead I went up. The pull of the thing was stronger than the sense in me, the same forbidden appetite that had knelt me over a turning compass on the headland, and it drew me a little way up the sacred road into the precinct, because I could not stand that close to it and not see.

And I saw it. I will set down what I saw, plainly, because no one alive will ever see it again, and I did.

The way climbed in switchbacks between the terraces, paved here with worn slabs, and it was lined on both sides with the small stone buildings the books call treasuries, the little templed strongrooms the cities of Greece had raised to hold their offerings. I had known them as foundations. I had known them as a plan on a page, a grid of numbered rectangles, a model under glass. They stood around me now roofed and painted, their pediments picked out in red and blue gone dusty, their doors shut, and some of them had been broken into and stood open and stripped, and some were whole and tended, and between them the god's ground rose crowded and bright and shabby and alive. There were offerings still, the ones too poor or too pious or too awkward to carry off: bronze tripods green with age, a bronze bull, stone slabs thick with the incised decrees of cities, a gold-and-ivory something behind a grille that I did not get close enough to read. And everywhere, between the standing dedications, the empty plinths, the sawn lead, the raw sockets, the god's house robbed and going on being a god's house anyway.

Above it all stood the temple. The temple of Apollo, on its great terrace, on the polygonal wall I had recognised from below, six columns wide and long down the flank, its stone the warm grey of the mountain it was quarried from, its ramp worn hollow at the centre by the feet of the faithful. Smoke went up thin and blue from an altar before it. A priest, or a man dressed as the sanctuary's business, crossed the terrace without haste. And there were people. Not a crowd; Delphi in its genteel decline was not the Delphi of the histories, and Nero's men had been through it like a plague of surveyors. But pilgrims, in ones and twos, a family in travelling dress with a goat for the knife, a lame man on a stick who had come a long way to ask a question, a knot of the sanctuary's own people about their work. The oracle still spoke here. The god was diminished and plundered and still, that summer, at home.

I stopped at the edge of the terrace in my scarf and my impossible boots and looked at the front of the temple of Apollo with the smoke going up, and somewhere behind it, cut into the living rock, I knew there was a cleft and a spring and a woman who breathed the ground and spoke for the god, and I knew, with the flat certainty of my trade, exactly where the Castalian water ran, because I had washed my own dusty student hands in it on a coach tour in a summer nineteen centuries from this one, at a stone basin they had roped off, with a guide talking through a small loud speaker about a civilisation that was, in front of me now, cooking its lunch. I had touched this place dead. I was standing in it living. And the gap between those two touchings was the whole of my exile and the whole of my wonder, and it opened under me there on the terrace like the pull under the rock, and I had to look away from the temple and down at my own wrong boots to keep my feet.

It was the boots that saved me, in the end, because looking at them I remembered what I was, and looked up, and saw that a man near the altar had stopped his crossing and was looking back at me. Not yet alarmed. Curious. A person noticing a person who did not fit. And I understood that the same appetite that had drawn me up here to see would keep me here to be seen, and that being seen was the one thing I could not afford, and I made myself turn, unhurried, a pilgrim who has looked her fill, and go back down the sacred way between the robbed treasuries, down out of the god's ground toward the houses, where the ordinary smoke of ordinary hearths promised the one thing wonder could not give me, which was bread, and where the next lesson was waiting.

It was waiting at the first house. A woman came out of it with a basket on her hip and threw a double handful of scraps to a scatter of hens, and called something back over her shoulder, and the language went into me like cold water and ended the wonder. Because I understood perhaps one word in five of it. And one word in five was enough to tell me the size of the thing I was in.

It was Greek. Of course it was Greek. I had known it would be Greek. But it was not the Greek I had. I had Greek the way a person has a dead language, off the page, reconstructed, pronounced in the mongrel classroom fashion no Greek who ever lived would have owned. I could parse a line of Homer. I could read an inscription cold. I had, God help me, a little tourist modern Greek off a fortnight on an island with a man I no longer spoke to. And what the woman spoke was none of these. It was the living speech of a Greek province under Rome, fast and swallowed and full of the small hard change of daily use, the vowels gone their own way, the endings worn like the ruts in the road, shot through with words I half-knew wearing pronunciations I did not. I stood in the road below the houses and listened to her scold her hens and understood the whole of my situation. I could read the name of a dead athlete carved in stone with perfect ease, and I was in no position to ask that woman for a cup of water without marking myself, inside the first sentence, as something with no business being alive.

That was the moment the survey ended and the survival began. I have dated it as precisely as I have ever dated anything. It was the hens.

---

I did the calculation a frightened competent person does. I inventoried my assets and found them worthless.

I sat back down, out of the woman's sight, and went through the kit and through myself, and costed the whole of it against the world I was in. The costing is the ugliest and most useful thing I did that day, so here it is.

I was dressed wrong. Not exotically wrong; catastrophically wrong. A woman in stout modern boots, in trousers, in a technical jacket the colour of no dye that existed, with a rucksack of a fabric that did not exist, cannot walk into a first-century town and be thought merely foreign. Foreign was a Syrian, a Gaul, a Jew. I was not foreign. I was *impossible*. And impossible, in a superstitious age at a holy place, is not a curiosity. It is a portent. Portents are dealt with by the frightened in ways that end badly for the portent.

My money was worse than useless. I had a handful of coins that would not be struck for nineteen centuries, milled, alloyed, stamped with a queen not yet even an ancestor's dream. If they were found on me, they were proof of something no one would frame kindly. The kit itself was a catalogue of anachronism, each object a small death sentence if shown. The camera. The meters. The compass with its floating oil-damped needle. The watch on my wrist. The very pencil, aluminium-ferruled, machine-made, bound into a notebook of a paper no mill of this age could dream of.

And worse than any object, there was me. A woman alone, past thirty, with no husband walking ahead and no father behind and no slave beside her, no veil, no story. In a world where a woman was a possession that belonged to some man's household, and drew her whole right to walk down a road from that belonging. A woman who belonged to no one was not free. She was unclaimed, which is a different thing and a far more dangerous one. I knew this. I had taught it, in a manner, to undergraduates who wrote it down and did not feel it. I felt it now, on a plinth, with the sun climbing. A lone unattached foreign-seeming woman was, in the plain reading of the place, either a whore, a runaway, or a witch. All three were solved by force.

So I could not be seen as I was. That was the first fact with a hard answer.

I went off the road, up into the holm oak above the sanctuary, where the ground broke into rocks and gave me cover, and I made myself small among the stones and began, methodically, to reduce the evidence of myself. The kit I could not abandon. It was anachronism entire, but it was also the only thing I owned that might get me home, and Fenn's papers were in it, and I was not yet frightened enough to throw away the one map through this that any human being had ever drawn. But I could hide the worst of it. I emptied the technical jacket, turned it, rolled it small, and buried it with the modern coins under a cairn of stones. I marked the cairn against three landmarks, a split oak, a white boulder, the corner of the polygonal wall, the way you record a find spot, because a conservator does not merely hide a thing. She records where, so that she remains a person who intends to come back.

I kept the boots. I could not cross this country barefoot and would not, and I could keep them hidden under a long skirt, when I had a long skirt. Over my trousers I had one square of cloth that was not impossible: a plain undyed length of linen I had carried against the sun. A real thing. The sort of undistinguished cloth a poor woman of any century might own. I took it off my neck and looked at it a long while, this single scrap of my kit that could exist here, and I saw the whole shape of my survival in it. I was going to have to expand the surface of that one honest square, one true object at a time, until it covered me completely, until there was nothing left of the impossible woman for the world to see and destroy.

I drew it over my hair and crossed the ends under my chin and knotted them behind, the way I had seen ten thousand times on ten thousand painted vases and carved stelae, without ever thinking I would do it to save my life. It changed everything and nothing. It hid my hair, which was cut wrong, short, shaped by a hand and a machine that did not exist. It gave my head the outline the world expected. It did nothing for the trousers or the boots or the impossible jacketless torso. But it was a beginning. It was the linen becoming the world. I have kept that first length of cloth through everything, through nine years, through the very end. It is one of the few things I carried all the way through, and will carry, I think, out the far side, if there is a far side.

---

Hunger drove me down out of the oaks by the middle of the afternoon. Hunger, which I have said is the most human thing I know, and which arrives at the worst moment without apology. I had eaten nothing since a foreign headland the day before and nineteen centuries hence, and the body does not care about the paradox. The body wants bread. And there was bread. I could smell it, coming up from the houses below the sanctuary on the afternoon air, the flat hot smell of barley loaves baked on a hearth, and it pulled me down the slope against every calculation I had just made about being seen.

I had no plan. I had a scarf and a hunger and a fragment of dead-language Greek. I went down meaning to trade, to beg, to steal if it came to that, to do whatever a starving woman does. The world I went down into taught me its first real lesson inside a hundred yards, and taught it hard.

There was a fountain-house where the road widened below the sanctuary, a covered stone basin fed by a lead spout, and there were women at it, and a knot of men in the shade of a plane tree beyond. Muleteers, by their gear, resting animals out of the heat. I went to the water, because water is the one thing a stranger may approach. I cupped my hands under the spout and drank. It was cold and hard and tasted of the mountain, the first thing of that world to go into my body, and I have never forgotten the taste of it.

When I straightened, the women at the basin had gone quiet. They were looking at me. At the boots below the rucksack, at the wrongness of me, with the flat frightened hostility of people at a holy place who have just seen a thing that does not fit. One of them said a word to another behind her hand. I did not need the language for the word. The word was the shape that *witch* makes in every tongue, the drawing-back, the two fingers, the old sign against the eye that our animal has made at what it fears since before it had cities. And the men under the plane tree had turned to look.

I did the arithmetic very fast. Four women drawing back and pointing. Five or six men, idle, curious, with the particular idleness of men who have time and an unclaimed woman suddenly in front of them. A stranger who could not explain herself in the first sentence, and whose second sentence would be worse than her first. Nothing good could come of standing there. I turned to go. Not fast. Fast is prey. Steadily, back up the road the way I had come.

Behind me I heard the scrape of a man getting up off his heels. A laugh. A word thrown after me in the swallowed living Greek. I did not understand the word and I understood it completely, and the whole length of my spine understood it. I walked and did not run. My catalogue-mind, bless it and damn it, was noting even then the exact quality of the laugh, and the number of footfalls that had come loose from the group to follow. Two. And thinking, with the awful flat clarity that comes over me at the worst moments: *this is how it happens. This is the ordinary way it happens to a woman alone. Not as a story. As an afternoon. And no one in nineteen centuries knows my name.*

It was the big scarred man who ended it, and he ended it by doing nothing, which was the truest introduction the world could have given me to Valens. I did not have his name for weeks. That afternoon he was only a wall that happened to stand in the right place.

He was at the edge of the muleteers' ground, a little apart from the rest, a big man gone still in the way that very dangerous men go still. His hands were working a strap on a pack-saddle while his eyes did something else entirely. He was scarred. Even at that distance I could see the ridged pale seams on his forearms, and one down the side of his jaw that pulled his short beard askew. Not the scarring of accident. It had a pattern to it, a repetition, the signature I would later learn to read as the arena, the way I read tool-marks on a bronze. As I came up the road with the two men loosening behind me, he did not look at me. He looked past me, at them. He did not speak. He did not move to help me. He did not move at all, except to go on working the strap through his big scarred hands, a knot tied and loosed and tied again.

But something in the set of him reached those two men before I did. The stillness. The flat, unhurried attention he had turned on them. I heard the footfalls behind me stop, and hesitate, and after a moment turn back toward the plane tree with a muttered word and a second laugh gone thin and false. He had called them off without a word or a gesture. He had done it simply by being a thing they did not want to find out about. And he had done it, I understood even then, not for me. He never looked at my face. He had decided, long before I walked up his road, that trouble was to be kept at exactly the length of his own outstretched arm and no nearer. I was trouble, plainly, impossibly. He stood between me and worse trouble for precisely as long as it cost him nothing, and not one second longer. When the two men had gone back to their fellows, he shouldered his saddle, turned his broad scarred back, and walked off toward the picket line of his animals.

That was the whole of it. Saved and dismissed in one motion. Kept alive and unseen in the same breath by a man who would, in the fullness of a great many hard years, weep at my own table over a child mispronouncing his name, and who could not, that first afternoon, be troubled to ask me mine.

I have turned that over a great deal since. That the man who would come to matter most to me first came to me as a shut door I was grateful merely not to be crushed against. It is not the meeting a story would invent. But it was the truest thing the world could have shown me about him at the start. That his kindness, when it finally came, would always arrive like that. Sideways. Unlooked-at. Costing him something he would rather not admit to spending. Dressed up as indifference, so that no one, least of all himself, could accuse him of having a heart still soft enough to break.

---

Night is the enemy of the unclaimed, and the afternoon was going, and I had a scarf and a drink of well-water and nothing else in the world. It was a woman who saved me, as it is nearly always in the end a woman who saves a woman, and her name was Kallia.

She found me at the grey end of the day. I had gone back up above the sanctuary, to the broken ground, to be alone while the dark came on. She came up the same path with a goat on a string and a bundle of cut broom on her back. An old woman, or a woman made old, broad and bent and quick-eyed. She stopped when she saw me huddled against a rock with my impossible boots drawn up under me and my one scarf over my hair. She looked at me a long time, with the frank unfrightened assessment of a person who has seen a great deal and been surprised by little of it. She did not make the sign against the eye.

She spoke. I spread my hands, the old gesture, *I do not understand,* which needs no tongue. Something moved in her face. Not pity, exactly. Something drier and more useful than pity. A kind of recognition. She tried again, slower, and pared it down to a single word I did know, the Greek for *lost*, and made a question of it. I nodded. And then, to my own horror, I began to cry, the second time that day. She watched me cry with her head tipped to one side like a bird. Then she said a whole sentence, of which I caught only the shape, a proverb by its cadence, one of the round worn sayings a people keeps in its mouth. And she got it wrong.

I did not know then that she always got them wrong. That misquoting the wisdom of her people was the whole of her armour against a life that had used her hard. But I heard the wrongness in it, even through my ignorance, the small deliberate slippage of it. Then she jerked her head down the path, *come*, and turned the goat and went, not looking back to see whether I followed. Exactly as the scarred man had not looked back. I understood that I was being offered a thing, and that the offer would be withdrawn the instant I made a performance of taking it. So I got up on my stiff cold legs and followed a strange old woman and a goat down a mountain into the dark, because nothing else in the world was on offer. It was the best decision of my life, and I made it because I was too frightened and too hungry to make a worse one.

Her house stood below the road, bigger than it looked from the path, the low main room of grey polygonal stone with a hearth at one end and a byre partitioned off where the goat went and a sleeping-loft above, a floor of beaten earth swept clean, one window shuttered against the coming cold, and doors giving off it to the two cold rooms she let, in season, to the traffic of the sacred way. It was poorer than any dwelling I had entered in my old life, and it was warm, and it was safe, and I could have wept again for the plain animal relief of a roof.

She fed me. She put into my hands a piece of the barley bread I had smelled from the mountain, and a bowl of something hot, a mess of boiled greens and oil and a few beans, coarse and bitter and sharp with some salted thing. I ate it with my fingers off the flat of the bread, the way she showed me with a jerk of her chin. I have eaten at good tables since, in this life and in the one before it, and I have never eaten anything my body remembers the way it remembers that bowl of bitter greens, in a firelit room, on the first night of my exile, given by a woman who knew neither my name nor my language nor my century, and gave it anyway. Because I was cold and lost and a woman. And because, as I would come to understand, feeding a stranger in the dark was the nearest thing she had left to feeding the one she could not.

She had a few words of a trader's Latin, I discovered as I ate, dredged up slow and rusty from some earlier season of her life. I had better Latin than Greek. So we met there, in the servants' tongue of the empire that ruled us both, a handful of words at a time, a coarse ungrammatical pidgin of two women's need. She touched her own chest. *"Kallia,"* she said, and waited.

I touched mine and gave her the nearest true thing my mouth could make of my name. She tried it, and made a Greek shape of it, and I let the Greek shape stand, because the woman I had been was buried up the hill under a white boulder with the coins, and a new name was only the truth.

She held up the bread between us. *"Panis,"* she offered, reaching for the Latin because she had already seen that I leaned on it, and then she gave the Greek word after it and made me say both back. What my mouth did to the Greek made her laugh once, short, not unkind. She pointed at the fire, the water, the goat, the door, and gave me each its word, slow, twice, and had me say it back, and shook her head at my mouth, and had me say it again. It was the first lesson of a thousand. I did not know that either. I only knew that a wall had a name now, and a fire had a name, and that a person with the names of things is a little less impossible than a person without them.

For there was a lamp. I noticed it while I ate. A small clay lamp set in a niche by the door, filled and trimmed and lit, its little flame steady in the draught, set apart from the hearth-light, deliberate, tended. It burned all that night. I would learn that it burned every night, in the window where a window's ledge could hold it and in the niche when the shutter was closed, and that Kallia filled it last thing before she slept, with an oil she could ill spare, and lit it, and said nothing. And that if you were fool enough to ask a poor woman why she burned good oil all night for no one, she would tell you, with a shrug and a wrongly-remembered proverb, that a lit lamp wastes no oil it was not going to waste. Which means nothing. Which is not how the saying goes. Which was the exact size and shape of the hole in her that she would let no living person look into.

There was a son. I did not know it that first night; I knew only that an old woman kept a light burning in the dark for something, and tended it like a wound. And that I had crossed the whole width of time and come to rest, of all the roofs in the turning world, under the one belonging to a person who understood, without a word we could yet share, precisely what it was to have loved someone you could not reach, and to keep the small ritual going anyway. The drawer. The lamp. The date at the head of the page. The useless faithful gesture toward the one who is not coming back down the road.

She made me a place in the straw of the byre and gave me a rough blanket that smelled of goat and woodsmoke, and she banked the fire, and she filled the lamp and trimmed it and lit it again though it was already burning, a doubling, a devotion. Then she lay down in her own place across the room. The house went quiet but for the goat shifting and the small collapse of the embers.

I lay in the warm dark in a poor woman's byre in the reign of Vespasian and could not sleep. After a while, because I could not sleep, and because I had to know whether I was the only one this had ever happened to in the whole of history, I did the thing I had been not-doing all day. I got Fenn's papers out of the kit.

I could not read them by the banked fire. But the lamp was there, Kallia's faithful useless lamp, burning for her lost one, and I crept to it and knelt under it and turned the little sheaf of a dead man's writing to its light. The pages I had bought at an estate sale in another world for the price of a curiosity. The notes of a self-proclaimed treasure hunter everyone had thought a fraud. And I found, some pages in, past the coordinates and the dated ledger-columns of attempts I had already learned by heart, an entry in his cramped numbering hand. I had read it a dozen times in the hotel. I understood it, that night, for the first hour of my life, on my knees under a widow's lamp with the actual dust of Delphi on my boots.

*Item,* he had written, in the flat bookkeeping voice the man used even for the end of his world. *Item, the second: I, too, woke in the wrong year.*

I read it and stopped and read it again.

*Let no one who finds this think I did not know where I was. I knew it to the reign. The knowing is not the mercy. I have set down elsewhere what the knowing costs. Here I set down only the fact, for the next one, whoever the next one is, because there will be a next one. The door does not open for the deserving. It opens for the curious, and the curious are never rare. To you, then, reading this in your own wrong year: you are not mad, and you are not the first, and that is the whole of the comfort I have, and I give you all of it, and I am sorry that it is so small.*

I knelt under the lamp with the page in my hand. I did not weep; I was done weeping for one day. But something in me that had been braced since I raised my head on the road let go a little. Not into hope. There was no hope in it. Fenn had not come home. I already suspected Fenn had never come home; the estate sale had been the estate of a man who vanished. It let go into something plainer and older than hope. The mere ancient comfort of company. That a human being had knelt in a wrong year before me, and had thought, at the end of whatever it cost him, not of himself but of the next one, and had left a light burning in the window of his terrible accounts for a stranger he would never meet.

Two lamps, then, in that low room. The widow's, for the son who would not come. The dead man's, for the woman who had. I folded the page away. I went back to the straw.

And lying in the goat-warm dark under a Greek mountain, nineteen hundred years before my own birth, unclaimed, impossible, hunted by nothing yet and by everything soon, with a scarf over my hair and a buried jacket up the hill and a bowl of a poor woman's greens warm inside me and a dead man's small sorry courage folded in my kit, I understood the shape of what lay in front of me. It was very simple. And it was enormous.

No one was coming to rescue me. No one could. The nearest human being who had known what happened to me had written his last accounts and vanished a lifetime and a language away. If I meant to live, I was going to have to *become,* patiently, humiliatingly, one honest object and one swallowed vowel at a time, a woman who existed in this world. A woman with a story, and a claim, and a right to walk its roads. And I was going to have to manage it before the wrong clothes or the wrong money or the wrong sentence got me killed. And I was going to have to do all of it while carrying, hidden, the impossible kit that was my only road home, toward a door whose *where* I did not yet know and whose *when* I could not yet let myself imagine. In a country I could read and could not speak. Among people who fed strangers in the dark and pointed at witches in the daylight, and who were, in this as in everything, exactly as complicated and exactly as dangerous as people have always been and always will be.

The lamp burned. The goat sighed. Somewhere down the valley a dog spoke once to the dark and was answered and let it go. I lay still, and began, because there was nothing else to begin, to learn the first of it, which was simply to breathe in a wrong year and let the morning come, and to be, when it came, a fraction less impossible than I had been at noon.

I did not sleep for a long while. But at the last I slept. And that, too, was a thing I had to learn was allowed.
