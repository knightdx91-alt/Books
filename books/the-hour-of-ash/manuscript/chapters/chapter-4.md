# Chapter 4: The Threshold

I went back on the fourth day.

I have tried, in the telling, to give myself a longer interval than that, three or four sensible days of resistance, a week of a reasonable woman weighing a thing and deciding against it and being talked round only slowly. It was the fourth day. I spent the three before it not deciding, which is a skill of mine, the not-deciding that is a decision wearing a coat. I ate in the town and walked the harbour. I read Fenn's ledger in the hotel with the lamp on until the dated columns had gone as familiar as a psalm. And the whole time I was not deciding to go back, I was buying the film I would carry and having the hire car's oil checked for a longer drive I had told myself I was not going to make. The body knows before the mouth admits it. My body had known on the rock, with the compass going round, that it would climb the headland again, and it spent three patient days provisioning the woman it was going to overrule.

So: the fourth day, the early afternoon, the tide going out. I climbed the spine of the headland in good light with the kit on my back, and this time I did not tell myself I was surveying anything. There was no pretence in it, and I will not put one in now. I had come up to stand in the saddle of rock where the meters had lied and my father's watch had strained against the shape of the hour, and to feel again what I had felt kneeling there, the forbidden joy. Because in four days of not-deciding I had missed it the way you miss a person, and there is no reasonable name for that, and I have stopped hunting one.

The saddle was as I had left it. Grey hollow of stone between the two shoulders of the headland, the sea loud on both sides, a warm wind off the water carrying thyme and salt and the white noise of a great deal of nothing. I stood at the lip of it a while with the kit still on my back, and I did the sensible smallest thing, the thing I had promised the frightened part of me on the drive up: I would not lay out the kit, I would not kneel, I would not settle in over the instruments and lose an hour. I would step down into the hollow and stand in the exact place and count ten breaths and climb back out. If the light lay steady on the rock while the sun moved, if the wrongness was still there and still real and not a thing four days had let me talk into a magnetic seam, then I would know. And knowing, I would go down and find a telephone and begin the enormous patient business of being a person who has found something. Ten breaths. That was the bargain. I have kept worse ones and broken better.

I stepped down into the saddle.

I will tell you what I felt, in order, in the language I trust, because it is the last inventory I took as the woman I had been. My ears went first. Not a sound, not the pop of altitude, but a change in the quality of the silence, a deepening, as though the sea had moved a great distance off without getting quieter. Then my skin. The warm wind was still on my face and my arms and I could see the thyme moving with it three feet away, and it had stopped touching me; I stood in moving air that did not move against me, in a pocket of stillness the shape of my own body. Then the light, which I already knew about, the false gold steady brightness lying flat on the rock. And then, last, low in me, behind the breastbone, in the place the compass had pointed at under the world, a pull. That is the only word. A pull. Not a wind and not a slope and not a hand; a direction, added to the three the world already has, opening downward and inward at once, gentle as the drag of deep water on the legs, patient as the tide that had been going out all afternoon while I climbed.

I counted one breath. I did not count a second.

I have gone over the next part more than any hour of my life, and I cannot make it longer than it was. It was very short. That is the whole horror I have to give you, that the thing which took everything took almost no time at all: a woman changes her mind in a hollow of rock, and steps back, and does not arrive where she stepped.

The pull deepened, or I leaned into it without meaning to, the way you sway toward a great height. And the frightened animal that had bought the film and checked the oil and set the bargain of ten breaths came up all at once through the joy and took the decision clean out of my hands. Get out. I did not think it in words. My body refused the place the way a hand refuses a stove. I turned on the ball of my foot to climb out of the saddle the way I had come, and I put my weight forward onto my leading foot to carry me up and out and home, and the rock was not under it.

There was no rock under it. There was the pull. I had leaned my whole weight into a step meant to take me up the near shoulder of the headland, and the ground I stepped onto gave the way the world gives in the half-second before you understand you are falling. Except that I did not fall. There was no down to fall in. I went the other way, along the new direction, the inward one; and it was not falling and it was not walking, and my inner ear could make nothing of it, and neither, twenty years on, can I. Pressure. A pressure that was everywhere and had no edge, on the eyes from the inside, on the teeth, in the long bones, the deep even squeeze of very cold water closing overhead without the cold and without the water. My leading foot went down through a step that had no bottom. My trailing foot never left the rock, and then there was no rock for it to have left. The light went out; not to dark, to nothing, to the flat grey behind the eyes when you press them; and the sea's deep-moved silence closed the last of the way over me, and I heard, very clear and very far and gone as soon as heard, one gull, and then not even that.

And then air.

Different air. That is what I came back to, before sight, before the ground, before my own name: the air was different. It had a weight to it and a green wet warmth and a smell I did not know and would spend nine years learning, resin and dust and crushed herb and woodsmoke and the animal nearness of a great many living things, and none of it, not one strand of it, was the thyme and salt of the headland. I was down. I was on my hands and one knee on something that was not the grey stone of the saddle. It was warm and gritted and gave slightly. Packed earth. A path, the packed earth of a path worn by feet. And there was sun on the back of my neck, real sun, the honest weight of it, laid at an angle it had no business owning after the afternoon I had climbed out of. My kit was still on my back. One strap had come off my shoulder. My palms had taken the fall and were printed with grit and the small pain of it was the realest thing in the world and I held onto it.

I did not look up. This is the join between the woman I was and the woman I became, and I have never dressed it up. I knelt on a warm path in an unknown light with strange air in my lungs, and I did not look up to see where I was. I already knew. Behind me the grey saddle of rock was gone; there was no headland, no sea on two sides, no small white hire car at the top with the moths still to come. I had felt the way back go as I came through it, felt it close the way a held breath closes a throat. Looking would only cost me the last half-minute in which I could still tell myself I had not felt it. So I knelt and looked at my own two hands on the ground, my ordinary hands, grit-printed, steady even now, the only instrument I had carried through that I could still be sure of, and I made myself, before anything else, before I raised my head to the thing that was waiting to be the whole of the rest of my life, take the reading that mattered and take it honestly.

The light was wrong for the hour. The air was wrong for the coast. The ground was wrong for the rock. Three independent instruments. And three independent instruments do not share a fault.

Somewhere off to my right, close, a voice said something in a language I had never heard, and a goat bell answered it. And I understood, kneeling in the dust with the sun on my neck and my palms full of a road that had not been built yet, that I had done the thing Fenn did. That I had stepped off the lid of the world at the interesting part. That the only question I had refused to write in the notebook, the *when*, had just been answered, and that I was not going to like the answer, and that it was too late, by the whole width of a slammed door, to ask it any other way.

I raised my head.
