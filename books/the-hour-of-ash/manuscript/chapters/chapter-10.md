Sweet. The god's breath rose sweet out of the cleft, a thin high sweetness like fruit a day past its best, and I had braced myself the whole climb for something that would sting the eyes, for sulphur, for the reek of the underworld the pilgrims promised, and I got orchards. That was the first thing the place taught me and it taught it before a word was spoken: that a thing built to unmake you can smell like a thing you would want to eat.

I have gone up that sacred way in my head so many times since that I can no longer be sure which stones I truly remember and which I have paved in after. But I remember the smell, because the body keeps a smell the way it keeps nothing else, and I remember that I stood at the top of the world with a dead man washed off my hands not four hours gone, and a live one at my back who had killed the two of them for me, and I gave a boy in a stained tunic the widow's good coin for a goat, and I waited to find out whether a god I did not believe in would agree to answer a question I had carried across seventeen hundred years.

Let me set it in order, because the order is the whole of what happened to me up there, and I have spent a long time since getting the order right.

Valens came with me as far as the god would take a muleteer, which was the low court below the temple, where the porters and the beast-boys and the men who were only paying for other men's questions stood about in the cold shadow of the great retaining wall and waited. He had left the string with his boy down at the porticoes and come up empty-handed, which sat wrong on him, a man without a rope in reach, and I watched his hands go looking twice for something to true and find only his own belt and settle on the buckle of it, working the tongue in and out of the same hole while he looked the sanctuary over the way he looked over any ground he had not chosen.

"They won't let me past the steps," he said. It was not a complaint. He was telling me the edge of the map. "A freedman with a slave name and no toga goes to the low court and no further. That's as far up the god as my kind climbs." He worked the buckle. "You go up alone from the fountain there. Don't let them hurry you and don't let them slow you. Priests are the same everywhere; they get paid by the delay and they call the delay reverence. You've a question and a coin. Ask it, pay it, come down."

"And if the god won't answer."

"Then you've bought a goat and learned nothing, which is most of what religion is." He said it flat, to the wall, but his eye came back to my face for a half-beat, that weighing look, and something in it was gentler than the words. "You've come a long way up a bad road for this. I saw your face at the bend. It's not the factor's books that put it there. I'm not asking what it is. But you'll go up in there and they'll frighten you on purpose, the smoke and the dark and the woman's voice, it's a machine for frightening, and you'll want to give them more than the question because frightened people do. Give them the question. Nothing else. Not your name, not your true one." He let the buckle be. "Come down and I'll be here. I'm not going anywhere with a dead man's leather still to think about."

That was as near as he came to saying he was afraid for me, and I took it the way I had learned to take everything he gave me, which was to say nothing back and let the not-saying be the thanks. I left him against the wall with his hand on his own belt for the want of a rope, and I went up alone to the fountain.

---

A priest met me at the basin of Kassotis, where the second spring of the mountain runs into the sanctuary itself, and he was old, and bored, and kind under the boredom the way a doctor is kind, which is to say without wasting any of it. He had a beard gone the yellow-white of old ivory and a way of not looking directly at the people he served, as if their questions were a light too bright to face, and he told me what I would give and in what order and how I would stand, all of it in the sing-song of someone who had recited it ten thousand times and no longer heard the words in his own mouth.

The cake first. The pelanos, the god's honey-cake, and it cost more than I want to tell you it cost, more than a family below in the valley ate in a week, and when the boy brought it out on a board I looked at it the way I look at everything, and I saw that it had not risen right, that whoever had baked the god's own cake had let the leaven go over and knocked the life out of the dough and baked a dense sad thing with a grey crumb, and I thought, standing at the mouth of the oracle of the whole classical world with a knife-death on my hands, *that is a badly made cake, you could get a better rise off Kallia's third-best batch,* and then I put the thought away where I keep them, uninvited and unexplained, the small dry litter my mind throws down at the worst doors it has ever stood at.

Then the goat. This is the part no one had told me, and it is the part that nearly ended the day before it began, and I set it down plainly because I have never read it plain anywhere and I lived it.

The god does not answer merely because you have paid. First the goat must consent for him. They led out a young she-goat, white with a brown face, and the priest took a jug of the cold spring water and poured it over her, over the head and along the spine and down the legs, and everyone went still, the boy and the second priest and the old one and me, because if the goat shivered, if she trembled through the whole of her body, head to hoof, at the cold of the water, then the god was willing that day and the woman would go down. And if the goat only flinched at the cold the way any beast flinches, if the shudder did not run the whole length of her, then the god was not in, and there would be no oracle, and I could pay again on the next day the calendar allowed and come back up the mean stretch of the road past the place where two men were lying under stones.

The water went over her. She threw her head and stamped, and my heart went down, because that was a flinch, that was only cold. The old priest stood with the empty jug and watched her the way a man watches a coin still spinning, and I understood that he did not know either, that for all the ten thousand times he was reading the beast honestly and did not know how it would fall, and that was somehow worse than if it had all been a fraud he was working. And then the goat's legs went, all four, a long hard ripple ran up out of the wet hide from the hocks to the poll, and she shuddered her whole self, ears and tail and the loose skin of the throat, and shook off a spray of the cold water into the low sun, and the old priest let his breath out through his nose and said, without any wonder in it at all, in the voice you would use to say the tide was in, "The god consents. She'll go down."

That goat has stayed with me longer than almost anything else on the mountain. I wanted the god to consent; I had climbed a killing road to make him consent; and when the beast shivered I was glad, God help me, glad the way you are glad of a thing you have paid for arriving safe. I did not, standing there, let myself weigh what I was being glad of. The goat shivered. The door of the god opened. They made ready to take her below and cut her throat, and to take me down to the edge of the place where the answer lived.

---

They do not let you all the way in. I want to be exact, because the machine that frightens you works by exactly measured distances, and the distance is the machinery. There is the temple, the great house of Apollo with its columns, and inside it, sunk into the living rock below the floor of the ordinary world, there is the adyton, the not-to-be-entered place, the small dark chamber where the cleft is and the tripod over the cleft and the omphalos, the navel-stone of the earth, and there the woman sits. The consultant does not go into the adyton. The consultant is brought into the temple and set at a rail in a kind of antechamber above and to the side, in the dark, where you can hear but not properly see, and the god's answer comes up to you already handled, folded into verse by the priest who stands nearer, so that you never in the ordinary way of things hear the Pythia's own mouth make the sound the god makes through it.

That is the ordinary way. I did not go up an ordinary road and I did not, in the end, get the ordinary way, and I have never decided whether that was the old priest's small mercy or his small cruelty or merely that the sanctuary in that thin late year had too few hands to keep the distances it had kept in its greatness.

They took me down the temple in near-dark, the old priest ahead of me with a lamp cupped in his hand and the second priest behind, and I went the way you go into a place you have been told not to go, small in yourself, minding my feet. There were laurel leaves crushed underfoot; I felt them turn to slick under my sole before I smelled them, the sharp green bitterness of them coming up bruised, Apollo's own tree strewn on the floor of his own house for the feet of the god to grind. The lamps were few and set low, in niches at the level of the knee, so that the light came up off the floor and left the roof of the place in a smoke I could not see the top of. And there was another smoke on top of the lamp-smoke, resined, drugging, sweet, laid over the sweetness already coming up out of the rock, and the two sweetnesses together thickened the air until I was breathing it shallow and high in the chest and my head had begun to go light and far in a way I did not like and could not stop and did not, after a moment, entirely want to.

I will be honest about this because I have never read it told honest. I do not know how much of what came to me down there came from the god and how much came from a low sealed room full of a smoke that was meant to do exactly what it did, and an empty stomach, and a woman who had watched two men killed on the road that same morning and had not slept and had not eaten and had climbed out of the reach of her own ordinary sense on purpose, hand over hand, one bad step at a time, to get here. I have turned that question a long while and set it down still open. Both are true at once. The truth of the place lives in the seam where the two of them will not come apart, and if you pull them apart to hold one you have dropped the thing itself.

There was the navel-stone, the omphalos, a pale rounded block wound about with the carved likeness of a woollen net, the middle of the whole earth by their reckoning, set close beside the tripod. There was the tripod itself, the three-legged bronze seat standing over a dark slot in the rock floor that the lamplight would not go down into, and the god's cold sweet breath rose out of that slot and moved the little flames. And on the tripod, above the slot, wrapped white, with a sprig of the god's laurel held in one fist and her bare heels drawn up on the rung of the seat like a girl's, was the woman, though I did not see her clearly yet, and did not want to, and was not, in the ordinary way of it, ever meant to.

And she spoke before I had made sense of the white shape as a person. A dry voice, an old woman's voice, coming up out of the floor of the world, and it was not chanting and it was not screaming, none of the raving the pilgrims had sold me on a winter night. It was low and even and tired, and it was speaking, at first, to a cat.

"Off the stone," the voice said. "Off it. You know you're not to sit the stone." A small scuffle, a soft affronted sound, the particular put-upon chirrup of a cat moved against its will. "There. Now. What have they sent me."

The priest beside me at the rail leaned to the dark below and said, in the formal Greek, the widow's question I had given him at the fountain, the question I had built in the flat careful way Valens had taught me to build a lie, so that it hid the size of itself: *a woman asks the god the way of her homegoing. She is far from the place she was born and does not know the road back to it. She asks the god where her door home lies.*

I had thought I was clever, wording it so. I had thought I had hidden the strangeness of it inside the ordinary grief of any traveller who wants to die where she was whelped. I know now that you cannot hide the shape of a true thing from the thing in that room, whatever the thing in that room was, and that the wording bought me nothing, because the voice below did not answer the widow's homesick question. It answered mine.

There was a silence. My eyes had learned the dark enough now to find the cat, which had gone straight back up onto the forbidden stone the instant she stopped watching it, and lay curled and indifferent on the navel of the earth, asleep, while the god came into the room over its head.

The voice changed. It is the only thing I will swear to. Whatever else was smoke and hunger and my own frightened head, the voice changed, went from the tart old woman scolding a cat to something flat and level and plural, a voice with no person in it, second-person and many-mouthed, and it did not sound like possession, it sounded like recitation, like a thing being read off a wall by someone who has read it before and is sorry.

"You came the wrong way to go the right way," the voice said. "You came down to go up and back to go forward, and you'll do it again, and it is the only honest thing about you." A pause. The cat's tail moved. "You ask the road home. There is a door under a mountain by the Roman sea. You knew that; a dead man told you that; you did not come up my mountain to be told what you knew. So hear the part he did not know. The mountain has a town at its foot with a wall the colour of old blood and a forum full of the wrong gods, and a smell of fish left in the sun to rot into gold, and a bakers' street where the loaves are marked with the maker's stamp. That is your door. Not the coast, not the province, not a hundred mountains. That one. The green one that has forgotten it is a mountain. The town at its foot. Go there and your door is there."

I gripped the rail. I had come for a name and the god had given me a place I could walk to, a red-walled town, the fish-sauce reek, the stamped bread, a green mountain that had forgotten what it was, and I could have wept with it, the sheer answered fact of it, after a winter of nothing but a mountain, a sea, and no name. I had the where. I had it at last, and exactly.

"When," I said.

I said it aloud, into the dark, over the priest, which you are not to do, and I heard him draw a breath to be scandalised, and I did not care, because the god had opened a door in the world and I was going to put my whole arm through it before it shut.

"When does it open," I said. "The door. You've given me the place. Give me the time. When can I go home."

And the voice, the flat plural god-voice, said one more thing in its recitation register, and it is the thing I carried down the mountain and could not put down, the thing I have taken out in the dark of every year since and turned over looking for the maker's mark, and never found it, and it is this.

"You will keep nothing you can carry."

Then, without any seam in it at all, in the same breath, the register fell out of the voice like a dropped cloth and it was the tired old woman again, tart and near and entirely present, and she said, to the priest, to the cat, to the dark, to me, "Mind the third step going up; it's loose. Somebody's to see to it and nobody has since Nero's men, and one of you pilgrims will go down on it and break your neck and then I'll never hear the end of the god's displeasure."

---

*You will keep nothing you can carry.*

I turned it over there at the rail, in the smoke, the way you turn a coin in the dark to feel which face is up, and I made it mean the small thing, because the small thing I could bear. A traveller's caution. Robbers on the long road south; the two dead men that morning who had come for the goods; a warning to go poor and grip poor. Keep nothing you can carry, because a road like the one I had walked up will take from you anything worth the taking, and I had learned that lesson already this very day with a picket-pin in my hand. I even felt the conservator's flat gratitude for it. *Very well,* I thought. *I'll carry little and hold it loose.*

I did not weigh it against the other thing. I did not, that afternoon, hear that a thing you can carry is a thing small enough to take through a door, and a thing you cannot carry is a thing gone too large and too alive and too much its own to be carried anywhere by anyone. The line sat in me like a swallowed stone, whole and undissolved, and I let it sit, because I had the where at last, my green mountain and my street of bakers, and a woman with the where does not go looking for what the when will cost her. I was not a woman to be sent off a mountain with half of what she climbed it for, and I meant to have the other half, and I was already leaning back to the rail to take it.

"That's not an answer," I said. My voice had gone flat, the road-voice, the dead-level one that came on me now under any strain, and I heard myself use it on a god and did not stop. "When. I asked you when. You told me a green mountain and a bad-smelling town and a loose step. You did not tell me when the door opens. I'll ask it plainly since plainness offends you. Is it a year. Is it ten. Am I to grow old in that town waiting on a door, or is it soon. When."

The cat woke, stretched, and resettled on the omphalos with its back to me. The old woman below did not put the god-voice back on. She stayed the tired old woman, and that, I think now, was itself the answer, that she would not dress this part in the god because the god would have made it beautiful and she would not let it be beautiful.

"You ask for the day," she said, "the way they all ask for the day, as if the day were the mercy." She shifted on the seat; the laurel rustled in her fist. "I have given days. I gave a man a day once, a good clear day, the truest thing I ever said in this hole, and he heard it and knew it and went home and hanged himself in the knowing, because a day is a wall you spend your life walking toward, and the walking is worse than the wall. You do not want the day. You think you want the day. You want to go home to that green mountain and keep your books and be a respectable nothing and live, and you think the day would let you live, and it is the one thing that would not." Her voice had no comfort in it, none; it was her law and she kept it. She would not comfort. She would only stop lying. "There is a when. There is a when the way there is a bottom to a well; it is real and it is dark and you do not want to be standing on it. I have seen it. I will not hand it to you. Not to spare the god's dignity. To spare you the walk."

"Then tell me a season," I said. I had begun to shake, but the voice held, the flat road-voice held. "Not the day. Give me a season and a sign. You owe me a sign; I paid the cake, I paid the goat, the beast shivered, the god consented. He consented to answer me and you're the mouth he answers through and you're keeping half of it back. Is that allowed. Can the mouth keep back what the god gave."

There was a sound below that I understood, after a moment, to be laughter, dry and short and not unkind, an old woman laughing in a cave at a thing she had heard a thousand strangers say.

"The god gives me more than I say to any of you, every time, all of it at once, and I give you the piece you can stand and eat the rest," she said. "That is the whole of my trade, girl. I am not a well you lower a bucket into. I am a woman who has swallowed a great deal of the truth so that other people need not choke on it, and it has cost me my teeth and my sleep and every friend I ever made, and I will not add you to the reckoning for the price of a goat." The cat stirred on the omphalos; she reached, without looking, and set two fingers on its back, and it settled, and I saw that she knew that cat the way I knew a hinge, by feel, in the dark, wholly, and knew nothing of Rome at all. "You want a sign. Here is your sign, and it is more than I gave the last three who stood at that rail. When the time is on you, you will know it, because the mountain you are going to will tell you itself, in a voice, and you will not be able to pretend you did not hear it, and on that day you will remember an old woman told you to keep nothing you can carry, and you will finally understand what a small kind thing that was to say. Now that is your season and your sign and it is all of it, and it is too much, and go."

"That's for me to choose," I said. "The walk. Mine to choose. Not yours."

And for the first time the old voice had something in it that was almost warm, and the warmth was the worst thing yet, because it was pity, and she had sworn somewhere in herself never to give pity, and it leaked.

"Yes," she said. "It is yours to choose, and you'll choose it, I can hear that you're the kind that chooses the walk, you came up a road that killed two men this morning to ask a question you could have died not asking. So I'll give you the only true thing that helps. You have time to build the thing you'll lose. That is the mercy in the not-knowing; it is the only mercy, and it is a real one, and I am giving it to you instead of the day, and you will curse me for it and then, at the end, you will understand that I loved you a little, which I am not supposed to do, and now you have had more of me than anyone has had in thirty years, so go up. Mind the third step. And send someone to fix it."

The priest beside me was rigid; I understood that whatever had just happened was not the way of it, that the woman had said too much and said it wrong and said it to me and not through him, and that he would have things to answer for it. He put his hand on my arm to draw me back from the rail. I let him draw me. I went up out of the dark of the god's house into the ordinary blinding cold of a mountain afternoon, and I minded the third step, which was loose, exactly as loose as she had said, rocking under my foot on its bad mortar. I stood on it a moment too long, with my hand flat on the cold wall, and of everything she had said to me down in the smoke there was precisely one thing I had understood, and it was the one I had not asked for and could not un-know: the where.

I had the where, whole and exact and unasked-for. Everything else she had said I carried down the mountain not understanding, and set to one side, and did not look at.

The shaking that Valens had promised me on the road came in the temple porch, in the clean cold air, the way he had said it would, when the danger was done and the body found it out. It started in the backs of my knees and went up. I stood in the colonnade with my hand on a fluted column older than my whole country and let it have me, because there was nowhere private to take it and no one watching a widow but the god, and the god had seen worse. The old priest had gone back down to whatever accounting he owed for the woman speaking out of turn. A boy went by with the goat over his shoulders, the little white she-goat, dead now, her consenting shudder spent, her head hanging and swinging with his stride, and I looked at her because I look at everything, and I thought, out of nowhere, of my father's hands turning a potsherd to the window light and saying *feel the weight of it, Mara, the past has a weight, it's the one thing they never fake right,* and I had not thought of his hands in weeks, and there they were, on a cold porch in the reign of Vespasian, holding a fragment of a bowl in a kitchen that no longer existed in any century I could walk to. I stood with the tremor going through me and the dead goat swinging away down the steps and my father's voice in my ear, and I did what I always do: I put the wonder and the grief into the same drawer as the fear, all of it filed under the one heading, *unbearable, handle later,* and I straightened off the column and went down to find the living man who was waiting for me.

---

Valens was where he had said he would be, against the great wall in the low court, and he had, in the end, found himself a rope; one of the porters' lines had been left coiled on a bollard and he had taken it up without asking and was working a splice back into a frayed eye of it, mending another man's rope for the want of anything of his own to hold, and he looked up when I came down the steps and read my face the way he read a horse, all at once and without seeming to.

"You got something," he said.

"I got a town," I said. "The exact town. A mountain by the sea with a town at its foot, red walls, a whole street of bakers." My voice came out level, which surprised me, and I was glad of it. "I know where I'm going now. To the stone. Not to a province, not to a coast. To one town I could put my finger on a map if this world had maps worth the name."

He set the last of the splice and did not smile, but the scar at the corner of his mouth did its thing, and he set the borrowed rope neatly back on its bollard the way you leave a tool better than you found it. "That's more than most get for a goat," he said. "That's a good day's oracle." He looked at me a moment longer. "And you asked it the other thing. The when. It's on your face still, the thing you didn't get."

"It wouldn't give me the when."

"No," he said. "They never give the when. That's the one they keep. A god that told you the when would be out of custom inside a year; nobody comes back to a god that's finished with them." He said it as a fact about trade, which was how he understood the world, and there was no scorn in it, only the flat competence of a man who has watched every kind of person take every kind of loss. He gathered himself off the wall. "You'll want to eat. You've the shakes coming; I can see it starting now the god's done with you, same as after the road. It waits till you're safe, the shaking. Come down to the porticoes and eat and then you present yourself to the factor's people before the light goes, because a town's no good to you if you can't afford the road to it, and the road to it is the next thing, and it's worse than the god."

He fed me before he let me go and present myself, which was not in his charge and he did it anyway. He got me down to the porticoes and bought a bowl of the festival stuff off a stall, barley and beans gone claggy in the pot, and put it in my hands and stood in front of me while I ate it so that his back was between me and the crowd and no one saw the widow's spoon rattle against the bowl, and he did not mention the rattle, and he did not look at it, he looked out over the crowd being a wall. The barley was underdone and stodgy and I ate all of it and was grateful past what the barley deserved. When the shaking had gone out of my hands enough to trust them, he took the empty bowl back to the stall himself rather than watch me carry it across the crowd and betray the tremor, and he called it fetching his own bowl back for the deposit, and it was the deposit, and it was not the deposit.

"Better," he said, when he came back. Not a question. He had decided I was better and so I was to be better. "Go and be a book-keeper. Bore them into keeping you. I'll be about the beasts a day or two yet; the boy's gone down with a fever and I'll not leave him the string alone in a festival crowd. If they take you on, you find me before I go down the mountain, because we've a winter to arrange and I don't do arrangements twice." He had his hand on the near mule's lead by then, his own rope, his own beast, and I saw the whole set of him settle back into itself the moment the leather was in his fist. "Go on. You're steady enough."

I was not steady enough, but he had said it, and saying it was how he handed a thing over, and I took the steadiness the way I had taken the bowl, because it was offered and because refusing it would have cost him more than keeping it cost me. I went and presented myself to the factor's steward with the tremor almost gone and the god still loud in my ears, and I was, thank God and Kallia's drilling, taken on.

That night I did not sleep. When the wrong year sat too heavy on me to lie under, there was one place I went, and I went to it now: I got the dead man's papers out of the kit and read Fenn by a stub of lamp in the corner of the factor's crowded outer room, where I had been given a mat among the other travelling nobodies. And I found the third of the entries I had first read in a hotel in my own century and not understood, and I understood it that night no better, only differently, the way a shadow makes plainer sense once you have finally seen the thing that stands to throw it.

*Item, the ninth,* he had written, in the cramped bookkeeper's hand, the flat voice he kept even here. *I have tested the doors I could find. There are more than one. I have set down the eleven attempts elsewhere with their dates. Here I set down only the law I bought with them, for the next one, whoever you are, reading this in your own wrong year: the door does not carry two. Whatever you learn to love on the far side, you cannot bring it through. The doors only open for the one they brought. I have proved this. I will not write how I proved it. Do not make me have written it. Carry nothing living to the door and it will not be able to break your heart, and if you have already something living, then you already know the shape of the ruin coming, and I am sorry, and I number this regret among the others and give it no more room than the rest, because if I gave it the room it wants there would be room for nothing else in me at all.*

I read it, and I set it down, and I did not put it beside the other thing. That is the whole of my confession about that night. I had the god's *keep nothing you can carry* in one hand and Fenn's *the doors only open for the one they brought* in the other, the promise and the law, two entries in two hands saying one thing to me across two rooms of the same house, and I did not lay them together and read what they spelled. I was too full of the where. I folded Fenn away with the coordinates and told myself the old hunter had lost his people to robbers and grief and gone strange in the counting of it, a lonely man numbering ghosts. Then I lay down on the borrowed mat among the sleeping strangers and thought about a town I had never seen, its red walls and its street of bakers and its green forgetful mountain, and I let myself be simply, stupidly glad, and I took it, and I would take it again. A person can refuse a sum whose answer is the end of everything. I refused it. It waited. It was patient as a mountain, and it went nowhere, and it is waiting still in every line I have set down here.

---

The road south, it turned out, was the god's real riddle, and there was no cake would open it.

I learned it in pieces over the next three days at the porticoes, some of it from the factor's steward, once I had the place and a corner of his ledger-room to work in, and most of it from Valens, who understood roads the way I understood corrosion, as a set of true conditions that did not care what you wanted.

"You've a town," he said, the third evening, when I had told him I meant to be gone south the moment the factor's people would release me. We were in the lee of the porticoes, out of the wind, and he had the day's harness across his knees and was working a stiff strap soft with his thumbs, the task his hands did while his mouth said the hard flat things. "Say the town's real. Say it's every stone the god said. It's the far side of the world from this rock, widow, and you're a woman, and a woman is not a thing that travels."

"Women travel. The road up here was full of them."

"For a day. To a festival. On their own mountain, in their own tongue, under the eye of a father or a husband or a village that knows them." He turned the strap. "You're talking about the width of Greece and then the sea and then the width of Italy, the whole spine of it, a season of roads, and every mile of it the same windfall I named you weeks ago, only there's no one up the valley to come asking after you now. You cannot buy that road with the widow's coin. You cannot buy it with three widows' coin. It isn't a matter of the fare."

"Then what is it a matter of."

The bread there was bad and I ate it anyway, waiting on his answer, because I had learned by then that he made you wait for the true ones.

"A man," he said, at last, and hated saying it, I could see him hate it, the flat honest disgust of a man who knew how little justice there was in the thing and told it anyway because a lie would get me killed. "A name to travel under that isn't your own strangeness. Papers if you can get them and a story if you can't. You need to be somebody's, going somewhere for a reason a hard man on a road will believe. That's the road, widow. It's long, and it's the sea shut till spring so you're wintering here whether you like it or no, and it's dear past your purse, and the last of those three you might beat with a season's hard work and the factor's wages. The other two you don't beat with money. You don't beat them at all. You go around them or you don't go."

"I've come a long way already," I said. "A day up a mountain today. I wasn't stopped."

"You were with me and four beasts and a widow's story, and it was your own mountain, and you were stopped, only the men who stopped you meant to take everything you had and you happened to survive it." He said it without heat, which was worse than heat. "You cross a province line, there are stations on the road. Toll-houses. Soldiers set to watch the roads, the stationarii, bored men in the middle of nowhere with the emperor's leave to ask anyone anything. They see a caravan, they wave it through for a coin, because a caravan is a thing they understand and a thing they can tax. They see a woman on her own two feet with a full purse and no husband at her shoulder, and now they've a whole afternoon's entertainment: whose are you, where's your master, why are you not where a woman's kept, and the true answers all end with you in a room you don't walk out of the same. There's no writ against a woman walking a road. There doesn't need to be one. A woman on it alone is either a man's, and they'll send to him, or she's no one's, and no one's is a thing you can pick up off a road and keep, and no clerk anywhere will write down that you were ever there." He turned the strap over, once. "That's the law you're up against, and it isn't in any code, and that's what makes it law. You can't argue it in front of a magistrate because it never comes before one. It happens on a road at dusk and the road forgets you."

I sat with that. The cold of the porticoes had got into me and the shaking had gone from the god's kind and settled into the ordinary kind, and I minded it less than the picture he had drawn, a room at the end of a toll-road, a clerk who would not write me down. My whole working life had gone among people the record had kept. It had not once occurred to me that a person could simply fall out of it entirely, the way a sherd falls out of a spoil-heap and is trodden back into the ground, and here I was one careless province-line from being exactly that.

"Around them how," I said.

He worked the strap. He worked it a long time, longer than the strap needed, and I watched his hands do the thing his hands did when a feeling came up in him that he had no shelf for, and I did not push, because that, too, I had come to understand: you did not put a thing into his grip before his hands were ready to take it. When he spoke again it was not an answer. It was the shape of one, laid down sideways, the only way he ever laid the large things down.

"There's a man I trade with goes to Italy," he said, to the strap. "Roman. Drusus. Loud, and in debt to the wrong sort, and worth more than he lets anyone see. He crosses in the spring with goods and a household and he owes me a favour I've never called. A woman travelling in a Roman trader's household, with the trader's word over her, going to the trader's markets in the south, that woman isn't a windfall on a road. She's baggage with a name on it. Nobody stops the baggage." He set the strap down, finished, and did not pick up another. His hands, for once, had nothing, and he let them have nothing, open on his knees in the cold, and he looked at the porticoes and not at me. "It's four months off, the spring crossing. You'd winter here and work, and I'd get you a place in it, and it would cost you the whole of the winter and the whole of your pride and a lie you'd have to hold across a sea. And you'd get to your green mountain."

"You'd do that," I said. "Get me a place in it."

"I'd do that," he said, and then, because he could not leave a thing standing that warm, he reached for the cold cloth to lay over it, the same as always. "A favour called is a favour spent; I'd sooner spend it shifting you off my mountain than leave it sitting owed. Nothing to me either way." He said it to the dark, the old lie, the one he had first told me in the yard and told a knot instead of my face, and this time there was no knot to tell it to, his hands were empty on his knees, and the lie had nowhere to hide and stood there between us plainly not true, and he heard it not be true, and I heard it, and neither of us said so.

"For the delay," I said.

"For the delay," he agreed, and the scar did its thing, and that was the whole of what we let ourselves say that night about the four months, and the winter, and the sea we would cross together into the country where my door was, and where his old ruin was waiting, and where a green mountain that had forgotten it was a mountain stood over a red-walled town, keeping, with the god's own patience, the one thing neither of us had asked it: the when.

I had gone up the sacred way with a question and come down with a place and a road, and the place was a gift and the road was a wall, and I set myself, that cold night among the sleeping strangers with a dead god-cake's bad crumb still souring in my mouth, to spend a winter learning to climb the wall. I would work the factor's books till my eyes crossed. There was a widow's lie to hold across a sea. I would put myself into a loud indebted Roman's baggage-train and be baggage with a name. I would do the long illegal unaffordable impossible thing, because a god had told me where my door was, and I was my father's daughter, and I had never in my life been able to leave an excavation half-finished, and the deepest one of my life had just been opened, four months' hard road south, under a beautiful green mountain by the Roman sea.

I did not know its name yet. I would learn to love the town before I ever learned to fear the mountain. That is the order the god gave me, and I have come to believe he gave it to me on purpose, and gently, and that it was the cruellest thing anyone ever did to me, and the kindest, and that the two were, up on that loose third step at the top of the world, the same act.
