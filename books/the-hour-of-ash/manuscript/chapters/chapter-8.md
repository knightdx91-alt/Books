# Chapter 8 — Earning the Yes

<!-- Word count: ~7,950 | Target: 7,900 | Anchor: Valens turning the horse's lip up with his own thumb to read what she'd read, and finding it there, and saying nothing — competence witnessed, the yes he can't take back -->

I went back in three days, as he had told me to and as I would have done whether he told me or not, and I found out at once that a man who says *come back* is not the same as a man who says *yes*. He had left the door on the latch, that was all. He had not opened it. He meant to make me open it myself, with my own hands, over a week of days I had not planned to spend, and I have thought since that this was the most honest courtship in the history of the world, though neither of us would have used the word, and one of us would have priced it.

The yard was as I had left it and the man was as I had left him, which is to say he was doing something to leather and did not look up.

"You came," he said, to the strap.

"You said three days."

"I said three days or don't." He drew the awl through, set it, drew the waxed thread after it in two even pulls. "People say a thing to be rid of you. Then they're surprised when you turn up counting on it." He tied off, bit the thread, and only then looked at me, that flat appraisal that took the whole of me in one draught and gave nothing back. "Sit down. Not there. The bench takes the sun off the muck-heap by midmorning and you'll not thank me. There."

---

The catch was this, and he laid it out for me plainly, because plainness was the only currency he trafficked in that was not a beast: the road I had asked for was not his to give, and the road he had offered was not his either. The factor's-widow place ran through a man named Drusus, who was Valens's partner in the trade in the loose way that a rope is partner to the bucket, and through the factor himself, a Corinthian who came up twice a year with money that answered to Corinthian money, and neither of those men was going to seat a stranger at the widow's elbow to keep her accounts and her confidence on a three-day road because a muleteer said she seemed steady.

"They'll want you shown," he said. "Not sworn for. Shown. Can she keep a tally that holds when a factor's clerk goes over it. Is she close-mouthed, or only quiet until she's comfortable, which is a different animal and worse. Does she know a thing's worth or only its price." He said that last as though it were a distinction he cared about, and I filed it, because he was a man who let very little out and I gathered the leaks. "I told the widow's people you could do the work. Now you have to do some, where they can see, so that what I told them stops being my word and starts being a thing they watched."

"So it's a trial."

"It's a trial." A ghost of something crossed the scarred side of his mouth and did not commit to being a smile. "You wanted to buy your way south with coin and a hard face. Turns out the coin they want is your afternoons. Drusus is bringing a consignment up at the week's turn, wool and some beasts, and there's tallying in it that wants doing right, and if you do it right in front of the right eyes you'll not need my string or anyone's. You'll have a place. That's better than a fare. A fare ends. A place travels twice a year and pays."

I understood that he was, in his sideways graceless way, giving me exactly the thing I had walked down the mountain to buy, and giving it in a form that cost him nothing to say and would cost me a week of labour to earn, and that this was not stinginess. It was the opposite. He had found the one road on which I would owe him nothing, and set my feet on it, and stepped back so I could not thank him for it. I had made a whole trade of reading intention off the shape of what people left behind them. I read this off the shape of what he refused to take credit for.

"All right," I said. "Show me the tallying."

"Not today. Today you muck out, because the boy's hands are torn and the beasts don't wait on your trial." He stood, and the yard rearranged itself around him the way a room rearranges around the largest piece of furniture. "You said your hands were what you claimed. We'll see. The dung's honest, whatever else isn't."

---

I mucked out. I want it set down that the first thing I ever did for the man I would marry was shovel the leavings of a dozen mules into a squared heap to a standard he did not have to explain to me because I had read it off the corners of the heap before I picked up the fork.

I was not good at it and I did not pretend to be. My hands, which had gone from a conservator's soft steadiness to Kallia's calluses over a winter, found a fresh set of complaints. But I was fast, and I was tidy, and tidiness was a language he spoke; I saw him clock the squared corners of my heap without appearing to look at anything but the horse he was gentling, and I saw him decide not to remark on it, which from him was a paragraph.

The smell of the place got into me those first days and never left. Dung and warm hide and the sweetish rot of old hay and the clean bite of neat's-foot oil off the harness under the eaves; I have smelled it since in other yards in other countries and been ambushed by it every time, put down in the churned mud below Delphi with my heart doing something I would not name. I loved it before I loved him and I have never told him so, because to say *I loved the smell of your muck-heap first* is not a thing a woman says to a man, though it is truer than most of what she does say.

We worked near each other and did not talk much, and the not-talking was its own conversation. He gave me tasks the way another man might have made small talk, testing what I could do by handing me the doing of it. Water the string. Sort the harness that's for mending from the harness that's for the tip. Hold this. He said *hold this* to me on the second morning, a young horse's headstall while he worked a knot out of its mane with those enormous careful fingers, and the horse leaned its whole warm weight into my chest and blew, and I stood there holding a Roman horse in a Greek valley nineteen hundred years from anyone who had ever loved me, and I laughed, out loud, at nothing, the cracked involuntary laugh that comes up in me at the wrong moments and has never once asked my permission.

He looked over. Not alarmed. Assessing.

"She lean on you?"

"She leaned on me."

"She does that." He went back to the knot. "Means she's decided you're furniture. It's the best you'll get from her; she doesn't like anyone. Neither do I." And that was the closest thing to a joke he had made, and he made it to a horse's mane, and I understood that I was being let, very slightly, in.

The boy watched all of this with the frank suspicion of the young. He was called nothing in my hearing that week; Valens spoke to him in Latin, short, and the boy answered in a Greek-Latin mash and did the thing asked. On the third day the boy dropped a bucket, a full one, over his own feet, and stood in the cold slop of it with his ears going scarlet and his jaw set against the humiliation of it, and Valens did not laugh and did not scold, only said, "Again, and slower," and the boy filled it again and carried it slower and the thing was over. I catalogued that too. A man who trusts nothing he hasn't secured himself, teaching a boy the only way such a man can, which is *do it until it holds.* I was in a school and had not enrolled.

---

Drusus came up on the fifth day, ahead of his consignment, on a mule that was too good for him and a temper that was too loud for the hour, and I heard him before I saw him, which I would learn was the whole of the man.

"Valens! *Valens.* Tell me you've wine that isn't vinegar, tell me you've something a Roman can drink without weeping for the Republic, I've come up a road that's cost me a shoe off this beast and a half-day I'll never bill anyone for, and if you offer me that resined Greek pitch you keep for the goatherds I'll take my factor to Timarchos and you can run your mules to the sea empty." He swung down, a soft heavy man gone prematurely jowled, in a tunic with a stripe of dye at the hem that cost more than the mule and a great deal more than he had, and he saw me, and stopped, and his whole face did a swift sum. "Ah. And who's the expense."

"She's the tallying," Valens said.

"The tallying's a woman."

"The tallying's a woman."

Drusus looked at me the way a man looks at a bill he wasn't expecting. Not unkindly; Drusus was almost never unkind, it would have cost him effort and he saved his effort for money. He looked at me as an item that had appeared in a column and had to be accounted for before he could rest. "Does she read? Does she cipher? A woman who keeps books, that's either a marvel or a disaster, there's no middle rate on it, I've paid both. What's she cost you?"

"Nothing. She's earning it."

"Nothing." Drusus said the word the way another man says *ghost.* He turned to me directly for the first time, and there was, under the venal froth of him, a pair of quick unhappy clever eyes that had seen the bottom of things and climbed back up talking. "You'll forgive him, he's Spartan by conviction and Roman by accident, he thinks *nothing* is a price. There's no such price. Everything runs you something. This yard runs him his whole life and he calls it *nothing to me either way,* which is the most expensive lie in Achaea, I've watched it beggar him of every soft thing a man's allowed." He said it lightly, tossing it off toward the wine Valens had gone to fetch, and he did not see, because he was not the sort of man who saw such things, that he had said the exact words Valens had said to me across a knot four days before, and that I had gone still holding them.

I did not go still where he could read it. I had learned that much.

"I read," I said. "I cipher. I've kept the books of a business or two." True enough, if you counted an estate's worth of finds catalogued to the last bent pin, which he did not need to know I was counting.

"A business or two." He settled himself on the sun-bench, the one Valens had warned me off, and did not notice the muck-heap breathing at his shoulder because his nose had long ago made its peace with worse. "Well. We'll see what a business-or-two comes to when there's a Corinthian's clerk across the board from you with a wax tablet and a grudge. The factor's a decent enough article, for a man who'd sell his mother's shroud off her if the wool was sound, but his clerk's a weasel and the widow's particular, and if you make her look a fool for taking on a companion who can't add, that's not your loss, that's mine, because I'm the one who swore to the factor there was a clever discreet woman going spare in the hills." He drank. He winced at the wine and drank again, because winced-at wine was still wine and he was not a man to leave a cup. "So be clever. And be discreet. The last time a hire went sour on me it ran me eight in silver and a season's..." He stopped. He looked into the cup. "The vintage before this one didn't hold either," he said, to the wine, and I understood I had just watched him swerve off the edge of something and price the wine instead of naming the drop, and that this was how the man walked: always changing the subject to money before the subject could change him.

---

The consignment came up the next morning, and with it the thing I had crossed a winter to be good for, though I did not know yet that it would be the making of me in that yard.

It was wool, chiefly, forty-odd bales of it graded and pressed, and I was set to tally it against the factor's manifest with the clerk himself at my elbow, a narrow young man with ink-stained fingers and the exact watchful discourtesy Drusus had promised. This part I could do in my sleep. A tally is a tally; you count, you weigh where weighing's called for, you mark, you reconcile the marks against the writing, and you do not let the man beside you hurry you off a number because his hurrying is the tell. I counted the bales and weighed the sample bales and found the manifest honest to the bale and nearly honest to the pound, off by a hair in the factor's favour that I noted in the margin without accusing anyone, because a margin note is a mirror and an accusation is a wall, and I wanted the clerk to see his own face and not mine. He read my margin. His mouth did something. He said nothing, which was the most respectful thing he had it in him to say.

But the consignment was not only wool. Drusus, who could not pass a bargain the way a drunk cannot pass a tavern, had folded into it a string of beasts he meant to sell on down at the coast, six mules and a pair of horses bought cheap off a coper out of Amphissa two days before, and it was these that Valens was walking the line of when I finished the wool, going down the string with his hands on each animal in turn, slow, and his face gone to the particular blankness I had learned meant he was not pleased.

I came to stand at the rail. I did not mean to interfere. I meant to watch, because watching him handle animals was the nearest thing to pleasure I had that spring and I took it where I found it.

"They trot sound," Drusus was saying, from the bench, not troubling to get up. "I walked them myself. Every one of them trotted sound as a bell, I'm not a fool, Valens, I know what a lame beast is worth, which is a fine and a funeral and the coper's grin, I've bought lame, I've *paid* for lame, I'd sooner set fire to my own strongbox. They trotted sound."

"They trot sound," Valens agreed, which was not agreement. He had a hand on the near horse's neck, and he was frowning, and he could not say why, and I saw the exact moment a working man comes up against a wrongness he can feel and cannot name. His hands had begun, quietly, to work the lead-rope through his fingers.

And I, who had spent thirty years being paid to know the difference between a thing's true age and the age it was dressed to look, felt the old cold click of attention come on behind my eyes.

"Show me the grey," I said.

Both men looked at me. Drusus with the offence of a man whose purchase is being questioned; Valens with something flatter and more careful.

"The grey trots sound," Drusus began.

"I heard you. Show me his mouth."

Valens looked at me a beat longer, then turned the grey's head and put his thumb in behind the lip and drew it up off the teeth without a word, and held it, and let me come to the rail and look, because he was a man who, when he did not know a thing, would let anyone who might know it look, and this, I think, is the rarest quality I have ever found in a human being and I found it first in a mule-yard over a horse's gums.

The teeth were wrong. Not obviously. Obvious would have been caught by the coper's own conscience if he'd had one. But I had passed thirty years reading surfaces that had been made to lie, false patina laid over new bronze, a worn-looking edge filed to counterfeit an age the metal did not have, and a horse's tooth is only another surface a dishonest man has learned to age. Someone had taken a tool to these. The cups that a young horse's teeth carry and lose with the years had been burned back in, a dark false hollow drilled and stained into a tooth that had already worn smooth; the slope of them had been rasped to flatter the story the burned cups told. Whoever had done it was good. He had aged the horse *up*, which is the coper's cleverer trick, taking a beast too young to have done the work its price implied and giving it the mouth of a settled seven-year-old, because a green four-year-old sold as green is cheap and a made horse sold as made is dear, and the buyer who checks the mouth for a *young* fraud never thinks to check it for an old one.

"This horse is four," I said. "Maybe five. Somebody's bishoped him to read seven. The cups are burned in; look at the colour of the hollow against the colour of the tooth, it's too even, a real cup stains uneven because the horse ate uneven. And the wear on the table's been rasped, you can feel it, it's smooth the wrong way, it's smooth like a floor and not like a mouth." I stepped back. "He's not lame. You're right, he's sound. He's just half the age you paid for and green as grass, and when your factor's man puts him to real work at the coast he'll come apart, and it'll be Drusus's brand on the bill of sale."

There was a silence in the yard of the particular quality that follows a thing being seen that everyone had agreed not to see.

Drusus got up off the bench. That, more than anything, told me I had landed a true blow; he had left money's company to come and look. He came to the rail and peered into the grey's mouth with the expression of a man reading his own death-sentence in a language he half-knew. "That could be. That could be, the light's poor, the... Valens, could that be?"

Valens did not answer him. Valens was looking at me.

Then he did the thing I have turned over more nights than I can count, the thing that told me the yes was already given whatever any of us said after, though it would take days more of playing at business to spend it. He turned the grey's lip up again, with his own thumb, and put his own eye to the tooth I had named, close, the way a man checks a thing he has been told and means to know for himself and not on anyone's word, because he trusted no one to be right for him. He looked a long time. His breath went out slow through his nose. And he found it there, the thing I had read, the burned false hollow and the floor-smooth table, and I watched him find it, and he said, at last, not to me and not to Drusus but to the horse, quietly, "You poor doctored bastard," in a voice with something in it I had not heard from him, something almost like grief, for the animal or for the state of the world that files a young horse's teeth, I could not tell.

He let the lip down. He smoothed the grey's face once with the flat of his hand.

"The mule too," I said, because I was in it now and could not stop, the completionist itch that has been my ruin and my rescue in equal measure. "The dark one, third from your end. He was walking too well when I came up. A beast that's been on a coper's rope two days doesn't walk that fresh unless he's been given something for it. Ginger, or worse. Check under his tail and check his eye; if he's been peppered to carry himself sound over the sale you'll know it by tomorrow when it wears off and he's dead lame in the yard and the coper's three valleys gone."

Valens went to the dark mule. He lifted its tail. He looked at its eye, which even I could see, once I was looking, was too bright, too wide, a beast running on something a man had put in it. He straightened.

"Ginger," he said. "And something else. He'll be lame by dark." He looked down the line of the string, all of it, the whole cheap clever fraud of it that Drusus had walked past sound-trotting animals and bought with his eyes and never once thought to distrust, and he said a short thing in Latin that I did not need Latin to translate.

Drusus had gone the colour of the wine he'd complained about. "How much," he said, which I would come to understand was the man praying. "How much of it's sound. How much have I lost. Valens, put a figure on it, I can't... if it's the string, if the whole string's copered, that's..." and he named a sum, and named the interest on the sum, and named the man the interest was owed to, a name that meant nothing to me then and would mean a great deal later, and he named it the way you'd flinch from a hot pan, fast, and then he was pricing his own ruin aloud on the sun-bench because pricing it was the only way he had ever found to hold it far enough from himself to keep breathing. "Grief I can manage. Grief's cheap. It's the *carrying charge* on grief that ruins a man, it's the compound on it, it's the man you owe it to..."

"Drusus," Valens said.

Drusus stopped.

"Two are sound," Valens said. "The grey's young, not ruined; walk him green, sell him green, at a green price, and no one's cheated. The dark mule's a loss. Two of the others want looking at. You'll take a beating on it, not a killing. Sit down before you cost yourself a tooth grinding them." And he came back up the line to where I stood at the rail, and he stopped in front of me, and for the second time in our knowing each other he looked at my face and let the look mean something, and what it meant this time was not appraisal. Appraisal was the first day. This was after appraisal. This was a man looking at a thing he has appraised and found, against his settled expectation of the world, to be exactly what it claimed.

"Where did you learn a horse's mouth," he said.

"I didn't. I learned bronze. And a comb, and a hinge, and a false green patina laid over new metal to make a fool pay for centuries he isn't buying." My voice went up at the ends and I let it, this once. "A doctored tooth is just a doctored surface. I've never mended a horse in my life. I've just spent thirty years being lied to by objects, and this horse was lying to you, and I read it off him the way I read it off everything, because it's the only thing I've ever been any good at and I couldn't stop doing it if you paid me, which no one ever has, adequately."

He looked at me. Something in the yard had changed and neither of us was going to say so.

"Huh," he said.

It was the least a man has ever said to me and it undid me more than a speech.

---

He was harder to be near, after that, not easier, and it took me days to understand why, and when I understood it I understood the whole of him.

A charming woman he could have refused. A charming woman was a known danger; he had been ruined by a story once and he could smell a story being built at forty paces, and charm is only a story told with the body. But I had not charmed him. I had been *right*, in front of him, about a thing he cared about and could not do himself, and being right is the one thing there is no armour against in a man who trusts no one to do a thing correctly but himself. Charm he could refuse. Competence walked straight past the guard and sat down at the fire. He had built a whole life on being the only one in any yard who could be relied upon to see the true thing under the plausible one, and I had stood at his rail and seen it faster than he had, and he did not know where to put that, and so he put it where he put everything, into work, and gave me more of it.

"You'll do the beasts on the manifest," he told me, the morning after the coper's string. "Not the wool. The clerk can do wool. You do the beasts and the tack and anything with a maker's mark on it, and you tell me what you see, and you tell me before you tell Drusus, because Drusus hears a loss and starts pricing it and can't add straight for an hour after." He said it looking at a buckle. His hands were working. "That's not me needing you. That's me not wanting to be cheated. There's a difference."

"There's a difference," I agreed, and did not smile, and it cost me not to.

There is a thing that happens between two people who work well near each other, and it is not friendship and it is not the other thing either, or not yet; it is a kind of fitted silence, the silence of two hands that have learned the same task from opposite sides. He would need a thing before he asked for it and find it already coming, because I had watched him need it the day before and learned the shape of the wanting. I would reach for a strap and find it already in the air between us, held out, because he had watched me reach the day before. We did not remark on this. To remark on it would have been to make it a thing, and he did not make things, and I was learning not to. The boy remarked on it, once, in his blunt young way; he watched me hand Valens a hoof-pick before Valens had turned to ask for it, and he looked from one of us to the other with the flat suspicion of a child who has sensed a weather changing in a house and does not have the word for it, and he said something in Latin, and Valens said one word back that shut him up, and the tips of the boy's ears went scarlet again, and I understood I had been discussed and would not be told what was said. I minded less than I let on. It is a strange thing to be gossiped about by a twelve-year-old in a dead language on the far side of the world; it was, I think, the first ordinary thing that had happened to me in that country, and I hoarded it.

We worked the trade for the rest of that week, the three of us and the boy, getting the good beasts and the sound wool ready to go down with the factor's party, and it was the nearest thing to happiness I had had since the door shut, and I did not let myself know that either. I know it now. I knew it then and turned the knowing toward the window like Kallia and her lamp, out of habit and hope and the refusal to say the hope aloud. I catalogued tack. I found a saddle with a Roman legionary's stamp on the tree, sold on a dozen times since some quartermaster wrote it off, and I told Valens whose it had likely been and how it had likely got there, and he listened to the whole of it with the leather still in his hands, and at the end he said, "You do that with everything," and I said, "Yes," and he said, "It must be very loud in there," and went back to work, and I stood in the sun with my heart doing the thing again.

---

The man came on the ninth day, in the afternoon, when the light was long and the beasts were fed and I was in the stable with a wax tablet doing the last of the tally, and I would not have known he mattered at all except for what Valens's hands did.

He was ordinary. That is the first thing and the worst thing; I have turned him over in memory a hundred times and he refuses to be more than ordinary. A traveller's cloak, a good one, road-dusted the honest amount. A mule, hired not owned, I could tell by the way he sat it. He came into the yard the way men came into the yard, calling for the master, wanting to hire, and Valens went out to him the way he went out to anyone, and I went on with my tally and half-listened the way you half-listen to weather.

The man wanted passage. That was the shape of it. Passage down to the coast, a beast, the usual. And then, easy, folded into the talk of roads and rates the way a bad coin is folded into good ones, he said a name.

"They tell me down the valley you're the man to ask for," he said. "Roman, they said. Come up from the south a few years back. Fighting man, once, is what they say. Would that be you?"

I kept my eyes on the wax. I had learned not to look up. But I heard the yard change.

There is a stillness I have described to you already, his stillness, the loudest thing he did. I heard it come down over the yard now, and I heard, in the gap where his voice should have been, nothing, and then the small dry sound of leather, his hands finding a strap, the mule's lead he still held, working it, working the buckle of it back and forth, back and forth, once, twice, a metronome for a fear he would not otherwise show.

I catalogued. It is what I do when the floor tips. Man, middle years. Cloak: Campanian weave, I thought, though I could not swear it, southern anyway, not local. Speech: a townsman's, careful, the carefulness of a man saying less than he knows. He had asked *would that be you* about a fighting man from the south, and he had asked it lightly, and light was wrong, light was the tell, a real hirer does not care what his muleteer used to be. He cared. He had come up a valley to care, and dressed the caring as small talk.

"Lot of Romans come up into the hills," Valens said. His voice was level. It was too level; I knew the register now, it was the register he used on mortal danger, the one that flattened a body in the poppies to a tradesman's aside. "I run mules. That's all I am up here. Where is it you're bound?"

"Kirrha, in the end. Corinth after." The man smiled; I heard it in the shape of the words. "I'm asking after a particular man, is the truth of it. An old matter. A household I served kept a fighting man, years back, before there was some trouble, and the family's minded to settle an old debt, if the man could be found. Debts owed, not owed." A pause the length of a coin turning over. "You'd not know him. Big man. Scars. Went by a fighting name."

"I'd not know him," Valens said.

"No," the man agreed, pleasantly. "No, I don't suppose. Well. The passage, then."

And they went on talking passage, rates, the day after tomorrow, a beast that could carry a pack, and I sat in the stable with my tally gone still under my hand and my heart doing a thing that was not the good thing, the other thing, and I did not move until the man had mounted and ridden back up the valley the way he had come, which was not toward Kirrha at all.

Valens stood in the yard a while after the sound of the mule was gone. His hands had stopped. They had gone still on the lead-rope, open, the way I had seen them go open and still that first day when there was nothing left to work, and I had learned by now what open still hands meant on him, and I got up and went out and stood near him and did not touch him and did not ask, for that much the winter had taught me.

"You heard that," he said. Not a question.

"I heard it."

"A household in the south. An old debt." He rolled the lead-rope up, slow, methodical, a task where there was no task, and set it on its peg. "Settling old debts. That's a kind way to say a thing." He looked up the empty valley where the man had gone. "He'll not be the last. That's the trouble with being the only steady clerk on the mountain; word travels, and word doesn't care who's listening for it." He turned and looked at me, and there was something bleak and level in it. "You want to be gone from here before words like that turn into men like that. So do I. It's the one thing you and I have wanted the same since you walked into my yard."

I did not tell him what I was thinking, which was that the man had asked *would that be you* about a fighting name, and that Drusus that first day had priced his own ruin aloud and named a creditor and never once thought about who might be listening to a loud soft man name his debts up and down a valley. I kept it back because I did not yet know it mattered, and because his hands had only just gone still, and because there are things you do not hand a man in the first hour of his fear. I filed it. I have a great capacity for filing.

---

The yes, when it finally came, came the way everything came from him, sideways, disguised as its opposite, handed over while he looked at a knot.

"The factor's people want you seen once more before they'll seat you," he said, that evening, over the wall of the paddock, the two of us watching the light go off the poplars. "Not here. Up top. There's a festival day coming at the sanctuary; the factor's man comes up to it, and the widow, and half of Corinth's money, and the trading's done up there in the week of it. You go up, you keep the widow's books in front of her people for the festival's trade, they watch you do it, and if you hold, you're hers, and you go south with the wool in the spring and I never have to think about you again." He said the last part flatly, a man reciting a relief he did not feel. "That's the road. Up first, then down. You go up to Delphi."

"Up to Delphi," I said, and something moved in me, because I had two locks to open and he had just, without knowing it, set me on the road to the first. Before the widow, before the factor, before the ship, I had my own errand up that mountain, to the one voice in the world that might give a name to a mountain by a Roman sea. He thought he was sending me to a trial. He was sending me to an oracle. I kept that to myself as well.

"It's a day's climb," I said. "More, with the road the way it is."

"It is."

"A day's climb up the mountain," I said, "for a woman alone, with no man behind her and none at the far end. You told me yourself what that is. A liability with legs. A body in the poppies. You said it to me across that bench nine days ago and it was the truest thing you'd said, your words." I was not fighting him. I want that understood. I was handing him back his own logic to see what he would do with it, because I had begun to know the man, and I knew he could not stand an unsecured thing.

His hands were on the top rail of the paddock. They found a loose splinter of it and worked it, back and forth.

"The festival road's crowded," he said. "You'd not be alone on it."

"Crowded's not safe. It's survivable. I've had a whole winter of the difference; you taught me the word yourself." I let that sit the way he let things sit. "And I'll have the widow's books on me by then, and a factor's name to travel under, which is worth more than any crowd. So I'll be all right, most likely. Most likely. You needn't trouble yourself. It's nothing to you either way."

I gave him his own lie back to him, gently, the largest one he'd told me, *it's nothing to me either way,* and I watched it land, and I watched him hear himself in it, and I watched the splinter come off the rail in his fingers.

He was quiet a long time. The light went from the poplars and left them grey.

"I'm running four beasts up to the sanctuary for the festival trade," he said, at last, to the poplars, to the loose rail, to anything but me. "Have to. The trade's up there that week; a man who isn't up there that week loses the whole season's custom to Timarchos, who waters his oats and I won't say what he does to a girth. So I'm going up regardless. And a man taking four beasts up an empty mountain road has room for a woman to walk in his dust, if she keeps up and doesn't talk the whole way, and it costs him nothing, because he was going anyhow, and it's not a favour, it's just the road being the road." He set the broken splinter down on the rail with a care the splinter did not warrant. "Come up with me and the beasts. Day after tomorrow, before light. Don't make it a thing."

"I won't make it a thing," I said.

"Good," he said. "Because it isn't one."

It was, of course. It was the largest thing, and he built it out of four beasts and a rival who watered his oats so that I could not thank him for it, and I did not thank him for it, and we stood at the rail in the last of the light not thanking each other, which was, I have come to think, the truest conversation the two of us ever had, and we had a great many after, in the years we were given.

---

I climbed the three miles home in the dark that night with a lamp Kallia had made me carry, and the mud had gone from clinging to merely cold, and I turned the day over the whole way up, the way I turn everything.

I had gone down that mountain nine days before to buy a fare, and I had failed to buy it, and in failing I had earned something a fare could never have bought, which was a place, and a road, and a man taking four beasts up a mountain for reasons that had nothing to do with me and everything to do with me. I had done it by being what I was, and not by being the widow, and not by charm, and not by any of the plans I had lain awake building. I had done it by knowing a horse's mouth off a bronze's false face, by reading a lie the way I had read lies my whole life, and by being right in front of a man who had no defence against right.

And under the good of it, cold and small and steady, the other thing. A man in a good cloak, on a hired mule, riding away up the valley in the wrong direction, asking after a fighting name and an old debt in a household in the south. He'll not be the last, Valens had said, and I had believed him, and I carried that up the mountain too, the way I carried everything, filed against a day I could not yet see the shape of.

Kallia was awake when I came in, or pretended she had only just woken, and she looked at my face in the lamplight the way she read hinges, and she did not ask *what did he say.* She asked the other thing.

"You're going up the mountain with him."

"Day after tomorrow. He's running beasts up for the festival. I walk in his dust." I sat on the bench and my legs told me about the six miles. "It's business. He was going anyhow."

Kallia looked at me a long moment. Then she reached up to the niche and turned the lamp, the lit one, the one that burned every night for the road her son would not come up, and she turned it a little toward the window, out of the habit that was her whole heart, and she did not misquote a single proverb, which was how I knew she was serious.

"Take the good cloak," she said. "Not the widow's. Yours." She lay back down in the dark, and I heard her settle, and I thought she was done, and then she said, from the dark, the last thing, and she said it not unkindly, the way you tell a traveller the third step is loose. "He's going up that mountain for the beasts. And you're going up it for the god. And the two of you will walk up it together telling each other neither of those is the road you're on. Mind you don't fall in step so easy you forget your own errand at the top." A breath. "The oil burns whether you name it or not, girl. But the god at the top of that road answers what you ask him, not what you're carrying. Ask him the right thing."

I lay awake in the byre with the goat and the cold and the lamp's small light coming under the door, and I did not plan the yard anymore. The yard was behind me now, earned. I planned the road, and the mountain at the top of it, and the voice I meant to put my one true question to, and under all of it, unplanned and unasked-for and lit low and steady like a lamp set in a window for someone still a long way down a dark road, the man who would be walking it beside me before light on the day after tomorrow, saying it was nothing, saying it to a beast, unable to help himself, coming.
